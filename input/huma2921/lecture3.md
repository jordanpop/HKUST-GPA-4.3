## Slide 2

Moral Relativism

Theory of Right Conduct

An action A, performed by a member of C, is obligatory iff, according to the basic moral norms of C, A is required.

An action A, performed by a member of C, is wrong iff, according to the basic moral norms of C, A is prohibited.

An action A, performed by a member of C, is optional iff, according to the moral norms of C, A is neither required nor prohibited.


## Slide 3

Context Sensitivity Thesis

MR: What is right and what is wrong for the members of a culture depend on (are ultimately determined by) the basic moral norms of their culture.

MO: There are at least some moral norms whose correctness or truth is independent of the moral norms a culture does or might accept. Such norms express objectively true moral standards for all cultures regardless of whether they recognize or heed them.


## Slide 4

Context Sensitivity Thesis

CS: what is right or wrong for someone to do often depends on one’s circumstances, including facts about one’s culture.

CS is not equivalent to MR. Why?

Because CS is (while MR is not) compatible with MO.


## Slide 5

Context Sensitivity Thesis

Suppose you are an expert swimmer and one day while walking along a deserted beach, you see a young child struggling in the water, obviously in immediate danger of drowning. What should you do?

Suppose I am in the same situation, but I can’t swim. What should I do?


## Slide 6

Context Sensitivity Thesis

While what you ought to do and what I ought to do are different, they may well be determined by an objective moral principle, i.e.: One ought to help those in need of help when one can do so while avoiding a serious risk of life and limb to oneself.

The rightness or wrongness of an action partly depends on non-moral facts that hold in the context in question—facts concerning agents and their circumstances.


## Slide 7

Moral Diversity Thesis

MD:

The moral codes of some cultures include basic moral norms that conflict with the basic moral norms that are part of the moral codes of other cultures.

Such fundamental conflicts are widespread.


## Slide 8

Moral Diversity Thesis

Question: If MR is true, does it follow that MD must also be true?

Even if it turns out (now or sometime in the future) that all cultures accept the same set of basic moral norms, moral relativism might still be true.

Analogy: It could be universal to drive on the right-hand side and still be a matter of convention.


## Slide 9

Anthropologist’s Argument – ver. 1

Different cultures have different moral codes. (=MD)

If MD is true, then there are no objectively true moral norms applying to all cultures.

If there are no universally valid moral norms applying to all cultures, then what is right and what is wrong for the members of a culture depend on the basic moral norms of their culture (= MR).

Thus, MR is true.


## Slide 10

Challenges

Against P2

The mere difference in belief over some issue does not imply that there is no single correct belief about that issue.

Take for example the historical dispute in belief about the shape of the earth.


## Slide 11

Challenges

Against P1

It is possible for there to be differences across cultures in their moral beliefs without fundamental moral disagreements.

## Slide 12

Nonfundamental Moral Disagreements

Imagine we hold contrary views regarding the morality of capital punishment. Your line of reasoning:

A society ought to employ only those punishments that have overall uniquely beneficial consequences for society.

Capital punishment does not have overall uniquely beneficial consequences for society.

Therefore, capital punishments ought to be abolished.


## Slide 13

Nonfundamental Moral Disagreements

My line of reasoning:

A society ought to employ only those punishments that have overall uniquely beneficial consequences for society.

Capital punishment does have overall uniquely beneficial consequences for society.

Therefore, it is not the case that capital punishments ought to be abolished.

Non-fundamental: disagreement due to differences in non-moral beliefs


## Slide 14

Fundamental Moral Disagreements

My line of reasoning:

A society ought to employ only those punishments that involve inflicting harm on the wrongdoer that is equivalent to the harm that the wrongdoer inflicted on his or her victim.

Capital punishment inflicts harm on the wrongdoer that is equivalent to the harm that the wrongdoer inflicted on his or her victim.

Thus, capital punishment ought to be employed.


## Slide 15

MD Revisited

If intercultural moral disagreements can almost always be explained in terms of intercultural differences in the relevant non-moral beliefs, then the second claim of the MD thesis is false.

MD requires evidence of widespread fundamental disagreements, which is far from being firmly established.

Difference of the two types of disagreements: resolvability by rational means


## Slide 16

Different cultures have different moral codes. (=MD)

Such conflicts cannot be resolved by rational means.

There are widespread and rationally irresolvable intercultural conflicts over basic moral norms.

The best explanation for such irresolvable conflicts is that there are no objectively true moral norms applying to all cultures.

Anthropologist’s Argument – ver. 2


## Slide 17

Anthropologist’s Argument – ver. 2

If there are no objectively true moral norms applying to all cultures (= denial of MO), then what is right and what is wrong for the members of a culture depend on the basic moral norms of their culture (= MR).

Thus, what is right or wrong depends on the basic moral norms of the culture in which the action is performed (MR).


## Slide 18

The individual shares the same non-moral beliefs about animals.

## Slide 21

Problem of Intercultural Evaluation

If MR is true, the basic moral norms of a culture set the very standard for right and wrong, then it makes no sense to question the correctness of those norms—they just are the ultimate standards for the relevant culture.

But surely it makes sense.

## Slide 25

Restricted Moral Relativism

What is right and what is wrong for the members of a culture depend on  the basic moral norms of their culture, but only in cases where those norms are consistent with those universal constraints on an adequate morality that properly reflect the functions of morality as well as facts about human nature.


## Slide 26

Facts about human nature
