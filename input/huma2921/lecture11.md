## Slide 4

What Is Moral Particularism?

Principle Eliminativism:
There are no correct or true moral principles that represent moral criteria and which are needed to explain the deontic status of particular actions.
Principle Abstinence:
A morally committed agent who wants to come to correct verdicts about particular cases and act accordingly should not just rely on moral principles for guidance.
Possible to have PE without PA or PA without PE

## Slide 5

What are Moral Principles?

5

Absolute Principle
An AP says a certain kind of action is always right or always wrong, regardless of circumstances.
Once a certain feature is present, the verdict is fixed. (decisive)
Contributory Principle
A CP says only that a feature counts for or against an action.
The contribution of the feature can be outweighed by other considerations. (defeasible)
The particularist can attack both.


## Slide 6

Why do we find MG attractive?

## Slide 7

The Generalist Picture

7

The Generic Generalist Thesis
There is a small set of fundamental morally relevant features.
These features explain the rightness and wrongness of actions.
Moral principles express these explanatory features.
The Universal Relevance Thesis
If a feature is morally relevant in one case, it is morally relevant wherever it appears.
The Polarity Thesis
If a feature counts in favour in one case, it always counts in favour.


## Slide 8

The Generalist Picture

8

Reasons Atomism
The moral contribution of a feature is fixed independently of its context.
If a feature has a positive (or negative) moral charge, then it has that charge across all contexts.
If a feature counts against (or for) an action, it counts against (or for) that action across all contexts.
Atomists can allow reasons to outweigh each other.


## Slide 9

Reasons Holism

9

The particularists hold that the moral contribution of a feature may depend on context.
A feature that is a reason in one case may be no reason in another case.
A feature that counts in favour in one case may count against in another case.
This is also called the variability of reasons.


## Slide 10

Reversal

10

“A government is considering reintroducing hanging and drawing and quartering in public for terrorist murders. If reactions to public hangings in the past are anything to go by, a lot of people may enjoy the spectacle. Does that constitute a reason in favor of reintroduction? Is the fact that people would enjoy it here a reason for its being right? It would be perfectly possible to take just the opposite view. The fact that spectators might get a sadistic thrill from the brutal spectacle could be thought to constitute an objection to reintroduction. Whether the fact that an action causes pleasure is a reason for or against doing it is not something that can be settled in isolation from other features of the action. It is only when we know the context in which the pleasure will occur that we are in a position to judge.” (McNaughton 1988, 193)


## Slide 11

Reversal

11

A feature has reversed when its moral polarity changes. 
Reversal ≠ Outweighing
A Rossian would describe it as a case where pleasure counts positively but is outweighed by cruelty.
The particularist: The pleasure here does not count positively at all.
The point is not that rules have exceptions. The point is that the moral contribution of a feature itself changes.


## Slide 12

Silencing

12

Ordinarily, if I promise to do something, the fact that I promised gives me a reason to do it.
Suppose I promise someone that I will help them cheat on an exam. Does the fact that I promised give me a reason to help them cheat? No. 
Silencing occurs when a feature that usually matters becomes morally irrelevant in a particular context.

## Slide 15

Generalist Strategy

15

But the consequence of reasons holism seems to be grave: 
If reasons vary by context, do we lose moral theory? Do we lose moral knowledge? Do we lose moral guidance?
Generalists: The particularist has shown that simple principles are vulnerable to counterexamples. But we can make principles more complex.


## Slide 16

Atomistic Specification
When a principle faces a counterexample, add the conditions needed to avoid the counterexample.
Take promising as example: 
If one has promised to do something, and the promise was not coerced, and the promised act is not immoral, and the promise has not been cancelled, and keeping it would not violate anyone’s rights, then one has a moral reason to keep the promise.

## Slide 17

Particularist Challenge

17

When we add all these conditions, are we still identifying a moral reason?
We are urged to distinguish between
Reasons 
Considerations that count for or against an action
Enablers
Conditions that allow a consideration to function as a reason
Disablers
Conditions that prevent a consideration from functioning as a reason


## Slide 18

The specified principle of promise contains different kinds of things. 
The reason, if there is one, is simply that I promised. 
The other conditions are not reasons. They are enablers or disablers. They determine whether the promise functions as a reason at all. 
It would be odd to say: “I should go because my promise was not extracted under duress.”

## Slide 20

Holistic Generalism

20

A holist principle has the following form: 
Feature F counts in a certain way when the surrounding context allows it to do so, and when no contextual factor silences, reverses, disables, or outweighs it.
If an action is an instance of killing a rational agent, and no other feature of the situation explains why this fact fails to be a reason against the action, and no opposing reasons collectively outweigh this reason, then the action is wrong in virtue of being an instance of killing a rational agent.

## Slide 22

Holistic Generalism

22

Why is it not just particularism?
Principles still play an explanatory role. 
The principle-based explanation works by subsumption. 
For the particularist, to explain why an action is wrong is to show how the morally relevant features of this concrete situation add up here – the explanation works by narration.

## Slide 24

The Particularist Claim

24

The particularist need not deny the truth of moral generalizations. 
But they are not the foundation of moral explanation. The practical consequence of this thesis is Principle Abstinence.
The reason for PA is that principles can distort attention and cause premature closure. 
Moral deliberation requires sustained attention to the particulars.


## Slide 25

Principles can be useful in offering guidance. 
In professional ethics, they may even be (practically) necessary: 
Medical ethics: informed consent, non-maleficence, confidentiality, and respect for patient autonomy
Public servants: fairness, accountability, transparency, and integrity
But they should not replace discernment.

## Slide 27

Practical Grammar
In learning a language, we do not always learn a single definition that can be applied mechanically. 
We learn how the word is used across contexts. We learn its grammar, tone, boundaries, and flexibility etc. 
Moral concepts may work similarly. 
We recognize obvious cruelty, subtle cruelty, justified harshness that is not cruelty, apparent kindness that is actually cruel, and so on.
