## Slide 3

Punishment and Retributive Justice

3

Suppose a series of grave, unsolved crimes has unsettled a town, and the residents are outraged, demanding that the perpetrator be identified and brought to justice; failing this, they threaten to riot. However, the police have no viable suspects. One option is to frame an innocent individual—ideally someone socially isolated, without close ties, and with a prior criminal record—against whom a credible case could be fabricated. The chief happens to know of such a person. Utilitarianism appears to entail that he is morally obligated to frame and punish the innocent person.


## Slide 4

Medical Sacrifice and the Right to Life

A physician with a strong utilitarian commitment faces the following situation. A patient in otherwise excellent physical health has been admitted for alcohol abuse. The physician is familiar with his background: he has no family, is homeless, and is socially isolated. Apart from some liver damage, his organs are in very good condition. At the same time, three other patients under the physician’s care each require a transplant of a different organ, and all are in urgent need, with little time remaining. Utilitarianism appears to require the physician to sacrifice the one to save the three.

## Slide 6

Promising

6

Suppose Jones, who is terminally ill, has privately entrusted me with a substantial sum of money, which I have promised to pass on to his daughter when she turns twenty-one. Now imagine that, in the intervening years, the daughter wins a massive Powerball lottery and comes into considerable wealth; by the time she reaches twenty-one, she has no need whatsoever for the money her father set aside. Meanwhile, my own circumstances have deteriorated drastically and it becomes painfully clear that the money entrusted to me would effectively rescue us.


## Slide 7

Demandingness

7

Suppose one of my favourite activities is gardening. From the standpoint of my own “partialist” concerns, I have a perfectly good reason to spend my spare time tending to my garden. But now imagine that, on a Sunday afternoon, I instead have the option of doing volunteer work for the city—work that would clearly produce greater overall benefit. In that case, I am pulled in two directions: on the one hand, my personal interests give me reason to garden; on the other, the impartial standpoint associated with utilitarianism gives me reason to set that aside and devote my time to volunteering instead.


## Slide 8

Personal Relationships and Special Obligations

8

Consider a case in which I can either use some of my savings to put my son (an average student) through college, or I can use that money to put a neighbour's more promising student through college whose parents cannot afford the cost. If the consequences of my using the money to send the neighbour's child to college would be better than using the money for my own child’s education, then (assuming there is no better way to use the money from the utilitarian perspective) I am obligated to use the money for the education of the neighbour's child.

## Slide 13

Bold Denial and Remote Effect

13

Katarzyna de Lazari-Radek and Peter Singer discussed the following case: 
“[A] surgeon has to do a delicate brain operation on a patient who happens to be the ideal organ donor for four other patients in the hospital, each of whom will die shortly unless they receive respectively, a heart, a liver, and—for two of them—a kidney. The doctor is highly skilled, and is confident of her ability to carry out the brain surgery successfully. If she does, the patient will lead a more or less normal life. But because the operation is a delicate one, no one could blame her, or have any reason to suspect anything, if the patient were to die on the operating table...” (2010, 40)
They suggested that the consequentialist must accept that the right thing for the surgeon to do would be to kill the one to save the four.


## Slide 14

Bold Denial and Remote Effect

14

Utilitarian: the right thing to do would be to kill the one to save the four.
But the action would not be recommended – for its possible damage of social order should it come under public attention.


## Slide 15

Satisficing Consequentialism

15

Replace “maximizing” with “satisficing”
To satisfice = to perform an action whose consequences are “good enough” even if some alternative action would have even better consequences
SC: An action A performed in circumstances C is morally right (not wrong) iff the utility of doing A in C meets or exceeds the specified threshold utility.

Unacceptable Implications

17

There are two groups of campers, 6 in one group and 2 in another, both groups camping in a ravine but at some distance from one another. Heavy rains have caused a nearby dam to break and water is flooding into the ravine. Ian, a helicopter pilot, knows the location and size of each group, but he can save only one of the groups before the floodwaters reach the campers. The members of the group who aren’t rescued will drown. According to SC, if Ian chooses to save the 2 instead of the 6 he is doing nothing wrong.

## Slide 19

Rule Consequentialism

19

RC: An action A is right (not wrong) iff A is not prohibited by an ideal code of rules.
How do we know which rules are good? 
Look at the overall level of utility that would result if individuals were to internalize the rule in question
An ideal code would be a set of rules whose value would be at least as great, if not greater, than alternative codes

Rule Consequentialism

R4: Whenever one has made a promise, one will keep it unless there is some alternative action open to the agent which, in the situation, would produce a greater amount of value.

## Slide 25

Alternative Theories of Value

25

Desire Fulfillment Consequentialism 
An action A in C is right iff doing A in C would result in at least as much general desire fulfillment as any alternative action that the agent could perform instead.
But not all fulfilments of desires contribute to one’s welfare. 
Also, the fulfilment of horrendous desires does not seem to be of intrinsic value.
