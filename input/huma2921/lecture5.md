## Slide 5

Theory of Right Conduct

An action A performed in circumstances C is obligatory iff doing A in C would produce a higher level of utility than would any other alternative action that the agent could perform instead.

An action A is wrong iff doing A in C would produce less utility than would some other alternative action that the agent could perform instead.

An action A is optional iff in terms of utility production it is tied for first place with at least one other action.


## Slide 6

Generic Principle of Utility

An action A in C is right iff doing A in C would produce as high a utility as would any alternative action that the agent could perform instead.


## Slide 7

Hedonistic Utilitarianism

Jeremy Bentham and J. S. Mill accept the following two claims:

One’s (how one is faring in life) is a matter of one’s level of happiness.

Happiness is constituted by experiences of pleasure (and the absence of experiences of pain).

Pleasure: intrinsically good; pain: intrinsically bad.


## Slide 8

Hedonistic Utilitarianism

Ethical Hedonism (EH):

Experiences of pleasure are intrinsically good, and experiences of pain are intrinsically bad, and they are the only items of intrinsic value with which ethics is concerned.

Principle of Hedonistic Utilitarianism (PHU):

An action A in C is right iff doing A in C would produce at least as high an overall balance of pleasure versus pain as would any other alternative action open to the agent.

## Slide 10

Mill’s Utilitarianism

John Stuart Mill (1806-1873) refined the position by considering qualitative facts about the nature of pleasure.

“It is quite compatible with the principle of utility to recognize the fact, that some kinds of pleasure are more desirable and more valuable than others. It would be absurd that while, in estimating all other things, quality is considered as well as quantity, the estimation of pleasures should be supposed to depend on quantity alone. (Mill [1863] 1979, 8)”


## Slide 11

Mill’s Utilitarianism

“If I am asked, what I mean by difference in quality of pleasures, or what makes one pleasure more valuable than another…there is but one possible answer. Of two pleasures…If one of the two is, by those who are competently acquainted with both, placed so far above the other that they prefer it, even though knowing it to be attended with a greater amount of discontent, and would not resign it for any quantity of the other pleasure which their nature is capable of, we are justified in ascribing to the preferred enjoyment a superiority in quality, so far outweighing quantity as to render it, in comparison, of small amount. (Mill [1863] 1979, 8–9)”


## Slide 12

Two Objections to Qualitative Hedonism

Obj. 1

Either quality has to do with the intrinsic nature of mental states of pleasure, or it does not.

If it does, then the only basis for distinguishing various pleasures is their intensity and duration. → back to pure quantitative hedonism.

If it does not, then Mill has actually forsaken hedonism.


## Slide 13

Two Objections to Qualitative Hedonism

Obj. 2

Presumably, to be competent in judging matters of quality, one must have the developed capacities to enjoy the types of pleasures to be ranked.

But how can Mill be so sure that “expert judges” will reach universal or nearly universal agreement over matters of quality?


## Slide 14

Mill’s “Proof”

Although Mill agrees that “questions of ultimate ends do not admit of proof” (Mill [1863] 1979, 34), we may still present considerations that determine the intellect to give assent to the doctrine.

Mill’s three-stage proof:

Each person’s happiness is intrinsically good to that individual.

The general happiness (the happiness of all individuals) is an intrinsic good to the aggregate of individuals.

Happiness is the only intrinsic good.


## Slide 15

Mill’s “Proof”

Stage 1:

People desire their happiness for its own sake.

If something is desired for its own sake, then it is desirable as such.

Each person’s happiness is desirable as such to that person.

If something is desirable as such, then it is intrinsically good (by definition).

Therefore, one’s happiness is an intrinsic good to oneself.


## Slide 16

Stage 2:

One’s happiness is an intrinsic good to oneself (conclusion of stage 1).

If each person’s happiness is an intrinsic good to that person, then general happiness is intrinsically good to the aggregate of persons.

Therefore, general happiness is an intrinsic good to the aggregate of persons.

Mill’s “Proof”


## Slide 17

Mill’s “Proof”

Merely saying that happiness is an intrinsic good is not sufficient to secure the utilitarian position.

Happiness is the only thing of intrinsic value.

Stage 3:

All other things besides happiness that are desired for themselves are only desired as parts of the end of happiness.

Therefore, happiness is the sole intrinsic good.

## Slide 20

Stage 3:

It is not clear how things that are desired in and for themselves are a part of the end of happiness.

“The ingredients of happiness are very various, and each of them is desirable in itself, and not merely when considered as swelling an aggregate. The principle of utility does not mean that any given pleasure, as music, for instance, or any given exemption from pain, for example, health, is to be looked upon as means to a collective something termed happiness and to be desired on that account. They are desired and desirable in and for themselves; besides being means, they are a part of the end. ## Slide 26

SPU: An action A performed in circumstances C is (presumptively) subjectively right if (not iff!) the agent believes that A has as high an expected utility as does any of the available alternative actions that she believes are open to her in C.

The action judged to have the highest expected utility is subjectively obligatory, all others are subjectively wrong.

## Slide 28

SPU formulates only a sufficient condition, not a necessary one.

Without beliefs about expected outcomes, the principle will not suffice as a guide.

It provides only a presumptively sufficient condition for an action’s being subjectively right.

In some cases, the presence of beliefs about expected outcomes does not warrant an act’s being subjectively right.

## Slide 29

Constraints

Suppose by closing gate 3 Phil can save only 50 of the 100 miners (regardless of where they are).

The expected utility associated with closing gate 3 would then become +50 [(0.5×50)+(0.5×50)=50], equal to the expected utilities of the other two options.

Does it mean that all three acts are subjectively right?

Intuitively, closing gate 3 is still the subjectively right act.
