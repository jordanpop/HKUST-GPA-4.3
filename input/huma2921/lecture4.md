## Slide 4

Aquinas’s Perfectionism

Aquinas connects a thing’s essence with its purpose.

The value of a thing consists in the perfection of its state to perform its function (to achieve its purpose).

A teleological worldview— all things in nature have purposes.


## Slide 5

Aquinas’s Perfectionism

The goodness of a human being is a matter of its realizing the purposes characteristic of human beings.

Human beings are rational agents with free will.

It is up to us to understand our essential nature and our good using reason.


## Slide 6

Value Perfectionism

VP: The goodness of a thing is a matter of that thing fully developing those capacities that are essential to it. Aquinas’s theory of value is also a version of perfectionism.

MP: A perfectionistic account of value is central in understanding the nature of right and wrong actions.


## Slide 7

Perfectionist Theory of Intrinsic alue

Some state of affairs S is intrinsically good concerning human beings iff the realization of S is part of what perfects human nature.

Some state of affairs S is intrinsically bad concerning human beings iff S involves the hindrance or destruction of those states of affairs that perfect human nature.

Some state of affairs S is intrinsically value-neutral concerning human beings iff S is neither part of what perfects human nature nor involves the hindrance or destruction of those states of affairs that perfect human nature.

## Slide 12

Principle of Double Effect

PDE: Whenever an action would produce at least one good effect and one evil effect, then one is permitted to perform the act iff all of the following conditions are met:

Intrinsic permissibility

Non-intentionality

Proportionality


## Slide 13

Principle of Double Effect

The action, apart from its effects, must be intrinsically permissible.

The bad effect must not be intended by the agent. Two ways an effect can be intended:

As a chosen end of action

As a means for bringing about some intended end

There is a proportionally grave reason for bringing about the evil effect.

## Slide 15

Moral Absolutism

But moral absolutism yields a negative answer to our question.

## Slide 16

NLT distinguishes between direct and indirect violations of the basic values:

Direct violation:

The action will seriously hinder or destroy a basic value, and

That action cannot be justified by PDE.

Indirect:

Actions that satisfy the first clause but not the second (and hence can be justified by PDE) count as indirect violations

Theory of Right Conduct


## Slide 17

Theory of Right Conduct

An action A is obligatory iff failing to perform A would result in a direct violation of one or more of the basic goods.

An action A is wrong iff performing A would result in a direct violation of one or more of the basic goods.

An action A is optional iff neither performing A nor failing to perform A would result in a direct violation of one or more of the basic goods.


## Slide 18

Craniotomy

Suppose a pregnant woman with a heart condition will die unless her unborn child is aborted. In such cases, the method of abortion involves performing a craniotomy—crushing the skull of the child—so that it can be removed.

In this case, the death of the unborn is a means of saving her life, and so this bad effect is intended. – Direct violation

## Slide 23

Evaluation – Is PDE innate?

Bystander case:

A trolley is out of control and heading down a track towards five workers. A bystander sees what happens and can pull a lever that will send the trolley onto a sidetrack, saving the lives of the five workers. But there is a lone worker on the sidetrack. Should the bystander pull the lever?


## Slide 24

Footbridge case:

There is a footbridge over the track located between the incoming trolley and the five workers. Two men are standing on the bridge, one very large and the other fairly slight. The large man is large enough to stop the trolley. Should the other man push the large man onto the track?
