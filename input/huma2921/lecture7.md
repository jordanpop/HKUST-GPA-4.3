## Slide 3

Theory of Right Conduct

3

An action A performed by person P in circumstances C is obligatory iff P’s performing A in C would produce a higher agent utility than any other action P could perform instead.
An action A performed by P in C is wrong iff P’s performing A in C would produce a lower agent utility than some other action P could perform instead.


## Slide 4

Theory of Right Conduct

An action A performed by P in C is optional iff P’s performing A in C would produce at least as much agent utility as would any other action P could perform instead, and there is at least one other action P could perform that would promote at least as much agent utility as would A.

## Slide 5

Ethical Egoism

5

EE: An action A performed by person P in circumstance C is right (not wrong) iff P’s performing A in C would produce at least as much agent utility as would any other action P might perform instead.

## Slide 6

Forms of Egoism

6

Ethical

## Slide 7

Rational Egoism

7

RE: An action A performed by person P in circumstance C is rational (in the sense of being rationally permissible) iff performing A in C would produce at least as much agent utility as would any other action P might perform instead.
While RE and EE address different questions, they are cousins:
What makes an action rational or irrational?
What makes an action right or wrong?


## Slide 8

Rational Egoism

8

Their distinction (and relation) is addressed in response to the complaint that EE is not a moral theory. 
It is a common assumption that it is always rational to do what is morally right. In other words, a moral theory attempts to capture one kind of rational action. 
EE competes with any theory that denies that all morally right action is necessarily self-interested action.

## Slide 12

Argument from PE to EE

12

If there are any actions that one is morally obligated to perform, then one can perform them (“ought” implies “can”).
If an action is one that one (psychologically) can perform, then it is an action whose performance is ultimately motivated by one’s self-interest (from PE). 
If there are any actions that one is morally obligated to perform, they are actions whose performance is ultimately motivated by one’s self-interest (from 1 and 2).


## Slide 13

Argument from PE to EE

13

There are actions that one is morally obligated to perform.
All obligatory actions are are ultimately motivated by one’s self-interest.
If all obligatory actions are ultimately motivated by one’s self-interest, then all obligatory actions serve to maximize one’s self-interest.
All obligatory actions serves to maximize one’s self-interest. (= EE)

## Slide 19

Is Psychological Egoism True?

19

The Satisfaction Argument
The result of having one’s desired goals or aims met (or getting what one wants) is that the desire is satisfied.
But satisfaction is an experience that involves some degree of positive feeling—if not pleasure then at least relief.
Whenever one acts, one’s actions are ultimately motivated by the pleasure (or relief) one gets from satisfying the desire.


## Slide 20

The Pleasure Argument
All desires are ultimately based on the motive of bringing about as much pleasure for oneself as possible and must be so based.
If one’s fundamental motive is necessarily a desire for one’s own (maximal) pleasure, then PE is true.
PE is true.

Evaluation

21

Consistency
Apparently at odds with the Principle of Non-Interference:
It is morally wrong for one to knowingly interfere with someone else doing their duty.
Resolvable by rejecting PNI or qualifying it, e.g.: 
It would be morally wrong to knowingly interfere with someone else’s performance of duty iff such interference would not be in one’s own best interest.
