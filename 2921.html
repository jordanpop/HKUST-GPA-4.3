Lecture 3: Moral Relativism

Moral relativism (道德相對主義, MR) says that what is right or wrong for the members of a culture is ultimately determined by the basic moral norms of that culture. The lecture builds up MR from the moral diversity thesis, examines two versions of the anthropologist's argument and the objections they face, distinguishes context sensitivity (CS) from MR, and ends with a restricted version of MR plus its relationship to tolerance.

1. Relativist Theory of Right Conduct

For a member of culture C: action A is obligatory iff A is required by the basic moral norms of C; wrong iff A is prohibited by those norms; optional iff A is neither required nor prohibited by those norms.

The rightness/wrongness of A is defined entirely by what C's moral code says. There is no further "culture-independent" standard.

2. Context Sensitivity vs Moral Objectivism

Context Sensitivity (CS): what is right or wrong for someone to do often depends on circumstances, including facts about their culture. The expert-swimmer and non-swimmer example shows different agents in different situations may be required to do different things.

Moral Objectivism (MO): at least some moral norms are correct independently of what any culture accepts; they apply to all cultures.

Exam trap: CS is NOT equivalent to MR. CS is compatible with MO — a single objective principle ("help those in need when you can do so without serious risk") can prescribe different actions to different agents because the non-moral facts differ.

3. Moral Diversity Thesis (MD)

MD (道德多樣性): the moral codes of some cultures include basic moral norms that conflict with basic moral norms of other cultures, and such fundamental conflicts are widespread.

MR does not entail MD. Even if all cultures happened to share the same basic norms, MR could still be true (analogy: everyone driving on the right is still a matter of convention).

4. Anthropologist's Argument — Version 1 and Its Problems

Version 1: (P1) Different cultures have different moral codes (= MD). (P2) If MD is true, there are no objectively true moral norms applying to all cultures. (P3) If there are no universally valid norms, MR is true. Therefore MR is true.

Challenge to P2: mere difference of belief about an issue does not show there is no single correct belief about it. Compare historical disagreement over the shape of the earth.

Challenge to P1 — nonfundamental disagreement: two parties can share the same basic moral norm and still disagree because they hold different non-moral beliefs. E.g., both accept "society ought to use only punishments with uniquely beneficial consequences," but disagree about whether capital punishment in fact has such consequences.

Fundamental disagreement: a disagreement traceable to different basic moral norms (e.g., one party endorses a retributive principle that the other rejects). The two types differ in resolvability by rational means: nonfundamental disagreements can in principle be settled by gathering empirical evidence; fundamental ones cannot.

Exam trap: MD requires widespread fundamental disagreement, not just observed differences. Most cross-cultural disputes can be re-described as nonfundamental, so MD is far from firmly established.

5. Anthropologist's Argument — Version 2

Version 2: (P1) Different cultures have different moral codes (= MD). (P2) Such conflicts cannot be resolved by rational means. (P3) The best explanation for widespread, rationally irresolvable conflicts is that there are no objectively true moral norms applying to all cultures. (P4) If there are no such norms (denial of MO), then MR is true. Therefore MR is true.

Objectivist response (the "chicken pull" case): if someone shares all the non-moral beliefs about animals yet his code lacks any norm prohibiting cruelty, the appropriate response is to insist he is mistaken and urge him to exercise sympathy. Disagreement does not show there is no fact of the matter.

6. Problems of Formulation

Consistency: Susan belongs both to the Roman Catholic Church (forbids abortion) and the Democratic Party (permits abortion). On MR her abortion would be both right and wrong — contradictory.

Usability & Determinacy: MR presupposes a determinate account of what counts as a "culture." Is culture fixed by language, ethnicity, religion, political affiliation? Without an answer, MR cannot tell us what makes any action right or wrong.

7. Intercultural & Intracultural Evaluation; Moral Reformer

Intercultural evaluation: on MR, a culture's basic norms set the standard, so it makes no sense to question their correctness from outside. But it clearly does make sense.

Intracultural evaluation: we routinely say morality has improved (e.g., on race and gender) or declined. MR cannot easily make sense of these comparative judgments because the standard of evaluation itself changes with the code.

Moral reformer problem: anyone who publicly opposes their culture's practice in order to reform it must, on MR, be in the wrong — yet reformers such as abolitionists are paradigmatically right.

Exam trap: The intercultural-evaluation problem, the intracultural-evaluation problem, and the moral-reformer problem are three distinct objections to MR, even though they all turn on the fact that MR makes a culture's current norms the final standard.

8. Internal Support, Explanatory Power, Restricted MR

A culture's basic moral norms may conflict with one's own considered moral beliefs (e.g., Nazi Germany's antisemitism). MR's failure to provide internal support is at the same time a failure of explanatory power.

Restricted Moral Relativism (受限道德相對主義): what is right and wrong for the members of a culture depends on its basic norms, only insofar as those norms are consistent with universal constraints that reflect the functions of morality and facts about human nature.

Culture-independent constraints come from: (a) the intrapersonal and interpersonal functions of morality; and (b) facts about human nature — satisfaction of physical needs; goods of intimacy, sociability, and social status; knowledge of the physical and human world.

The interpersonal function yields a norm of reciprocity (returning good for good), encourages other-regarding behaviour, leaves room for personal freedom, and requires norms to be justifiable. Restricted MR is pluralistic: different codes can be equally correct provided they satisfy the constraints — but not anything goes.

9. Relativism and Tolerance

Principle of Tolerance: it is morally wrong for any culture to interfere with the moral practices of another culture.

MR is often thought to commit one to tolerance, but unrestricted MR is actually incompatible with it — because tolerance is presented as a universally applicable norm, which MR cannot accommodate. Only restricted MR is compatible with the principle. Also note: rejecting MR does not commit one to intolerance.

Exam trap: "MR supports tolerance" is a common mistake. Unrestricted MR cannot endorse a universal principle of tolerance without contradicting itself.
Exam Traps Summary — Lecture 3
  • CS ≠ MR; CS is compatible with MO.
  • MR does not entail MD, and MD does not entail MR.
  • MD requires widespread fundamental disagreement; mere difference of belief is not enough.
  • The objectivist can respond to "irresolvable disagreement" by insisting that the other party is mistaken (chicken-pull case).
  • MR generates inconsistency for individuals in multiple cultures, blocks intercultural and intracultural evaluation, and makes the moral reformer always wrong.
  • Only restricted MR is compatible with a universal principle of tolerance.
Top things to memorize
  1. Definitions: MR, MO, CS, MD.
  2. The two versions of the anthropologist's argument and the specific challenges to each premise.
  3. Nonfundamental vs fundamental disagreement and the resolvability criterion.
  4. The three "evaluation" objections (intercultural, intracultural, reformer) plus the consistency and determinacy problems.
  5. The two sources of constraints in restricted MR (functions of morality + facts about human nature) and the reciprocity norm.

Lecture 4: Natural Law Theory

Natural Law Theory (自然法理論, NLT), in the Thomistic tradition, grounds morality in human nature understood teleologically. Its three components are a perfectionist theory of value, the principle of double effect (PDE) for resolving cases of moral conflict, and a commitment to moral absolutism. The lecture sets out the theory, applies it to hard cases (hysterectomy, craniotomy, euthanasia, self-defence), then evaluates each component.

1. Two Senses of Law and the Three Components

Law has a descriptive sense (regularities in nature) and a normative/prescriptive sense (norms for how people are supposed to behave). NLT calls moral laws "natural" because they are grounded in human nature, not because they describe physical regularities.

Three components of NLT: (i) a perfectionist theory of value; (ii) the Principle of Double Effect (PDE); (iii) commitment to moral absolutism (MA).

2. Aquinas's Perfectionism

Aquinas links a thing's essence to its purpose: the value of a thing consists in the perfection of its state in performing its function. This is a teleological (目的論) worldview: all things in nature have purposes.

Human beings are rational agents with free will, so it is up to us to use reason to understand our essential nature and our good. The goodness of a human being is a matter of realizing the purposes characteristic of humans.

3. Value Perfectionism & Intrinsic Value

VP (價值完美主義): the goodness of a thing is a matter of that thing fully developing the capacities essential to it. MP: a perfectionist account of value is central to understanding right and wrong actions.

A state of affairs S is intrinsically good (concerning humans) iff realizing S is part of what perfects human nature; intrinsically bad iff S hinders or destroys what perfects human nature; intrinsically value-neutral iff S neither perfects nor hinders human nature.

4. Four Basic Human Goods

The four basic goods are Life, Procreation, Knowledge, Sociability. The Fundamental Principle of Right Conduct in NLT: these are to be preserved and promoted; their hindrance and destruction are to be avoided.

Implications: an obligation to protect and promote one's own life and others' (Life); to have and raise children (Procreation); to pursue knowledge (Knowledge); to form friendships, marriage, and civil society (Sociability).

5. Moral Conflict & the Principle of Double Effect

NLT faces conflict cases (self-defence killing, hysterectomy, lying to a would-be murderer). It rejects pure consequentialism: intentions also count, not just how much good or evil would result.

Principle of Double Effect (PDE) (雙重效果原則): whenever an action would produce at least one good effect and one evil effect, it is permitted iff all of the following hold:

  1. Intrinsic permissibility: the action, apart from its effects, must be intrinsically permissible.
  2. Non-intentionality: the bad effect must not be intended — neither as a chosen end nor as a means for bringing about the intended end.
  3. Proportionality: there is a proportionally grave reason for bringing about the evil effect.

Hysterectomy: removing a cancerous uterus from a pregnant patient. (i) The operation itself is not wrong; (ii) the death of the unborn is neither the purpose of the operation nor an intended means to saving the mother; (iii) saving the mother's life is proportionally grave. PDE permits it.

Exam trap: All three PDE conditions must be met. The bad effect being unintended is necessary but not sufficient; you still need intrinsic permissibility and proportionality.

6. Moral Absolutism and the Theory of Right Conduct

MA (道德絕對主義): certain very general types of action are always morally wrong to perform, even when performing them would bring about good results. Paradigm case: the intentional taking of innocent human life is absolutely prohibited.

NLT distinguishes direct from indirect violations of basic values. Direct: the action seriously hinders or destroys a basic value AND cannot be justified by PDE. Indirect: hinders/destroys a basic value but CAN be justified by PDE.

Right conduct in NLT: A is obligatory iff failing to perform A would result in a direct violation of one or more basic goods; wrong iff performing A would result in a direct violation; optional iff neither performing nor failing to perform A would result in a direct violation.

7. Applications: Craniotomy, Euthanasia, Self-Defence

Craniotomy: crushing the skull of a child to save the mother. Here the death of the unborn is the means of saving the mother's life — so the bad effect is intended. PDE fails on Non-intentionality → direct violation, morally wrong.

End-of-life: active euthanasia (intentional killing) is morally wrong. But prescribing a life-shortening drug that eases the patient's pain can be permissible — the shortening is a foreseen but unintended side effect.

Self-defence: using force against an aggressor is permissible (perhaps even required), but intending the aggressor's death is morally wrong. The death must remain a side effect.

Exam trap: Hysterectomy and craniotomy can have the same physical outcome (mother lives, child dies), yet NLT permits the first and forbids the second. The difference lies in whether the child's death is a means or a side effect.

8. Evaluation — Initial Appeal, Absolutism, PDE Innateness

Initial appeal: NLT fits common beliefs about morality (grounded in human nature, principles are objective), arguably satisfies consistency and determinacy, but usability requires understanding the elements of PDE.

Against MA: is there any exceptionless moral rule? Cases like active voluntary euthanasia, assisted suicide, and the case of Tony Bland strike many as morally justified, putting pressure on absolutism.

Is PDE innate? The bystander case (pull a lever to divert a trolley onto a sidetrack with one worker — judged permissible) and the footbridge case (push a large man off a bridge to stop the trolley — judged wrong) differ in intuitive verdict even though both kill one to save five. Harman (1999) suggests the responsible principle (PDE-like) is "built into" people's morality in advance.

9. Evaluation — The Core

The Grounding Project — Aquinas: all things to which man has a natural inclination are naturally apprehended as good. Two problems: (i) logical gap between non-moral facts and value statements; (ii) circularity — not all inclinations are good, and to identify the "good" inclinations you must already presuppose what the human good is.

Moral conflict without MA/PDE: two options — (i) find a non-arbitrary rank ordering of rules; (ii) appeal to the details of the case and use good moral judgement to decide which rule applies.

Moral perfectionism as a separable thesis: one can agree that considerations of value come before considerations of rightness without holding that the morally central value is perfectionistic (e.g., welfare/well-being could play that role instead).

Exam Traps Summary — Lecture 4
  • All three PDE conditions are required; failing any one is sufficient for impermissibility.
  • Hysterectomy vs craniotomy turns on means vs side effect, not on the outcome.
  • Self-defence: force is permitted; intending the aggressor's death is not.
  • MA prohibits the intentional taking of innocent human life — unintended killing can still be permissible via PDE.
  • The bystander/footbridge contrast is used as evidence that PDE-like reasoning is innate (Harman).
  • The grounding project faces the is-ought / fact-value gap and a circularity worry.
Top things to memorize
  1. The three components of NLT: perfectionism, PDE, MA.
  2. The four basic goods: Life, Procreation, Knowledge, Sociability.
  3. The three PDE conditions: intrinsic permissibility, non-intentionality (end or means), proportionality.
  4. Direct vs indirect violation of a basic value.
  5. Application verdicts: hysterectomy permitted, craniotomy forbidden, active euthanasia forbidden, life-shortening pain relief permitted, force in self-defence permitted but intending death forbidden.

Lecture 5: Utilitarianism

Lecture 5 introduces classical utilitarianism as the leading form of consequentialism. The right action is the one whose consequences produce as high a utility as any alternative. The lecture distinguishes hedonistic and qualitative versions, examines Mill's "proof" of the principle of utility (and its three fallacies), and addresses practical objections by introducing distinctions between actual vs expected consequences and between subjective and objective rightness.

1. Three Theses of Consequentialism

Consequentialism's three theses: (i) value is independent of the right — value can be specified without reference to rightness; (ii) explanatory priority of value — the right is to be explained in terms of the good; (iii) personal value alone is relevant — the value that matters morally is value for persons. To make consequentialism precise we must answer: which theory of value? and how is right action related to value?

2. Classical Act Utilitarianism

Classical Act Utilitarianism combines: act utilitarianism (each act evaluated directly), a maximizing conception of right action (right = greatest total amount of value), welfarism (welfare is the fundamental value), universalism (the welfare of all affected individuals counts), and impartialism (agent-neutral — no special weighting for oneself or one's group).

Utility = the values of the consequences of actions: the positive value of effects on those positively affected, minus the negative value on those negatively affected.

3. Theory of Right Conduct & the Generic Principle of Utility

For A performed in C: A is obligatory iff A in C would produce a higher level of utility than any alternative; wrong iff A would produce less utility than some alternative; optional iff in terms of utility production A is tied for first place with at least one other action.

Generic Principle of Utility (PU): A in C is right iff doing A would produce as high a utility as any alternative the agent could perform instead.

4. Hedonistic Utilitarianism (Bentham)

Bentham and Mill accept: (i) one's welfare is a matter of one's level of happiness; (ii) happiness consists in experiences of pleasure and absence of pain. Pleasure is intrinsically good; pain intrinsically bad.

Ethical Hedonism (EH): experiences of pleasure are intrinsically good and pain intrinsically bad, and they are the only items of intrinsic value relevant to ethics. PHU: A in C is right iff doing A produces at least as high an overall balance of pleasure over pain as any alternative.

Bentham's felicific calculus: features for calculating utilities — intensity, duration, certainty/uncertainty, propinquity/remoteness, fecundity, purity, extent.

5. Mill's Qualitative Hedonism & Two Objections

Mill refines Bentham by recognizing that some pleasures are higher in quality, not just quantity. Test of quality: if those competently acquainted with both prefer pleasure P1 to P2 even when P1 involves more discontent, P1 is superior in quality.

Objection 1: either quality concerns the intrinsic nature of pleasurable mental states or it does not. If it does, the only basis for distinguishing pleasures is intensity and duration — back to quantitative hedonism. If it does not, Mill has abandoned hedonism.

Objection 2: to judge quality competently one must have developed capacities for both types of pleasure — but how can Mill be sure such "expert judges" will reach universal agreement?

6. Mill's "Proof" and Its Diagnosis

Mill grants that "questions of ultimate ends do not admit of proof" but offers a three-stage consideration:

  1. Stage 1: each person's happiness is intrinsically good to that individual.
  2. Stage 2: the general happiness is an intrinsic good to the aggregate of individuals.
  3. Stage 3: happiness is the only intrinsic good.

Diagnosis of Stage 1 — Fallacy of Equivocation: "desirable" can mean "able to be desired" or "worthy of being desired." Mill slides from the descriptive sense to the normative sense. Analogy: philosophy is an art; art is studied by art historians; therefore philosophy is studied by art historians — equivocation on "art."

Diagnosis of Stage 2 — Fallacy of Composition: a machine made of parts each weighing less than five pounds need not itself weigh less than five pounds. Likewise, that each person's happiness is good to that person does not entail general happiness is good to the aggregate.

Stage 3: Mill says things like music or health are desired as part of the end of happiness. But it is unclear how things desired in and for themselves are parts of happiness rather than independent goods.

Exam trap: Stage 1 is equivocation; Stage 2 is composition; Stage 3 is a separate worry about how individually-desired ends could all be "parts" of happiness. Don't confuse the three.

7. Practical Objections; Actual vs Expected Consequences

Problem of ignorance: to apply PHU one would have to figure out the utilities of all alternatives. Without access to the future and to others' experiences, this is not feasible. Response: PU is a moral criterion of rightness, not a decision procedure.

ECU (Expected-Consequences Utilitarianism): A in C is right iff A has as high an expected utility as any alternative. ECU contrasts with ACU (Actual-Consequences Utilitarianism) which evaluates actual outcomes. Each version exerts pull in different scenarios.

Mine Shafts case (Parfit): 100 miners trapped, equally likely in shaft A or B. Closing gate 1 saves 100 if A, 0 if B; gate 2 saves 0 if A, 100 if B; gate 3 saves 90 either way. Expected utilities: gate 1 = 50, gate 2 = 50, gate 3 = 90. Intuitively Phil ought to close gate 3 — but if the miners were actually in A, he later judges "I ought to have closed gate 1."

8. Subjective vs Objective Rightness

SPU (Subjective Principle of Utility): A performed in C is presumptively subjectively right if (not iff!) the agent believes A has as high an expected utility as any alternative she believes is open to her. The action judged to have the highest expected utility is subjectively obligatory; all others subjectively wrong.

SPU states only a sufficient condition, and only a presumptively sufficient one — without beliefs about expected outcomes, SPU won't guide action, and sometimes having such beliefs still doesn't warrant subjective rightness. Example: if closing gate 3 would save only 50 regardless of shaft, all three options have expected utility 50, yet closing gate 3 is still intuitively the subjectively right act.

Exam trap: SPU uses "if," not "iff," and the condition is only presumptively sufficient. Watch for this in MCQs.

9. Blame, Praise & Two Levels of Moral Thought

Blame-worthiness/praise-worthiness ≠ objective wrongness/rightness. Praise and blame are sensitive to how morally conscientious the agent was. In Mine Shafts, Phil is not blameworthy even though he performed an objectively wrong act — a case of blameless wrongdoing. Symmetrically there can be praiseless rightdoing.

Two levels of moral thought: intuitive level — proceeds by moral rules learned early; critical level — where ordinary moral rules conflict and we must deliberate. Not all situations allow for deliberation, and this two-level structure helps utilitarianism accommodate ordinary moral life.

Exam Traps Summary — Lecture 5
  • Classical AU is direct + maximizing + welfarist + universalist + impartialist.
  • Utility = positive minus negative effects across all affected.
  • The principle of utility is a criterion of rightness, not a decision procedure.
  • Mill's proof: Stage 1 = equivocation; Stage 2 = composition; Stage 3 = parts-of-happiness problem.
  • ACU vs ECU: in Mine Shafts ACU says gate 1 (in shaft A); ECU says gate 3.
  • SPU has "if" not "iff," and is only presumptively sufficient.
  • Blameless wrongdoing and praiseless rightdoing are possible.
Top things to memorize
  1. The five features of classical AU (act, maximizing, welfarist, universalist, impartialist).
  2. The Generic Principle of Utility and the obligatory/wrong/optional definitions.
  3. Bentham's seven calculus features and Mill's qualitative-hedonism test.
  4. The three stages of Mill's proof and the corresponding fallacies.
  5. The Mine Shafts numbers and the ACU/ECU contrast.
  6. SPU's logical form ("if," presumptively sufficient) and the blameless-wrongdoing / praiseless-rightdoing distinction.

Lecture 6: Objections to Utilitarianism

Classical utilitarianism — direct, maximising, and impartialist — faces powerful objections drawn from cases where its verdicts clash dramatically with our moral intuitions about justice, rights, personal relationships, and what morality can reasonably demand of us. Understanding these objections, and the utilitarian responses to them, is the core skill of this lecture.

1. Cases Clashing with Intuitions about Justice and Rights

The punishment case (懲罰): police can prevent riots by framing an innocent, socially isolated person with a criminal record. Utilitarianism appears to require (功利主義看似要求) this because framing maximises utility.

The medical sacrifice case (醫療犧牲): a utilitarian physician should sacrifice one healthy patient to harvest organs for three dying patients. Utilitarianism appears to require this.

The distributive justice case (分配正義): utilitarianism prefers a scheme with higher total utility even if it leaves some people below the poverty line, because it ignores how utility is distributed across persons.

Exam trap: The objections are not just about the outcomes being bad — they reveal structural features of utilitarianism (impartialism, maximising) that generate systematic conflicts with intuitions about rights.

2. Cases Clashing with What Morality Can Demand

The promising case (承諾): if the beneficiary of a deathbed promise no longer needs the money and the agent is impoverished, utilitarianism may require breaking the promise to use the money better.

The demandingness objection (要求過高): utilitarianism requires the agent to volunteer rather than garden on Sunday whenever doing so produces more overall utility — it demands the sacrifice of personal projects.

The special obligations case (特殊義務): utilitarianism requires funding a more promising neighbour's child over one's own son if the consequences are better, ignoring parental obligations.

Exam trap: The demandingness objection and the special obligations objection target the impartiality of utilitarianism, not just its maximising character.

3. Direct Responses: Bold Denial and Remote Effects

Bold denial (大膽否認): accept the counterintuitive verdict and challenge the reliability of moral intuitions. Joshua Greene argues that strong moral feelings are emotional responses dressed in rational clothing — not reliable guides.

Remote effects (遠期效應): challenge the stipulated facts. Is it really true that framing the innocent maximises utility? Realistically, such behaviour would erode public trust and produce catastrophic long-term disutility. De Lazari-Radek and Singer acknowledge the surgeon should kill the one to save the four (bold denial), but note the action would not be recommended publicly due to its effects on social order.

Exam trap: Bold denial and remote effects are distinct responses. Bold denial questions the moral intuition; remote effects questions the empirical premise that utility is actually maximised.

4. Satisficing Consequentialism

Satisficing consequentialism (SC) (滿足後果主義) replaces "maximising" with "satisficing" (滿足): an action is right if its utility meets or exceeds a specified threshold, even if an alternative would produce more utility.

Problems with SC: (1) A charity collector who keeps some donations but still exceeds the threshold acts permissibly under SC — but this is theft. (2) A helicopter pilot who saves 2 instead of 6 when saving 6 costs no more does nothing wrong under SC — but this is counterintuitive.

5. Rule Consequentialism

Rule consequentialism (RC) (規則後果主義): an action is right iff it is not prohibited by an ideal code of rules — a set of rules whose acceptance utility (效用接受值) is at least as great as any alternative code.

RC handles the cases: a rule permitting medical sacrifice has lower acceptance utility than a prohibiting rule. A rule of strict impartiality is too costly to internalise. RC allows partiality indirectly.

The collapsing objection: when rules are made sufficiently complex to handle all exceptions, they become extensionally equivalent (延伸等價) to act consequentialism — agreeing on every verdict. This undermines RC's distinctiveness.

Exam trap: RC retains maximising at the level of rule-selection but not at the level of individual action — it is indirect, not direct, consequentialism.

6. Alternative Theories of Value

Nozick's experience machine (體驗機器) challenges hedonism: if welfare is just subjective experience, we should plug in — but most of us would not. This suggests welfare is not reducible to experience.

Desire fulfillment consequentialism (慾望實現後果主義): an action is right iff it maximises general desire fulfillment. Problems: not all desire fulfillment contributes to welfare; fulfilling monstrous desires has no intrinsic value.

Perfectionist consequentialism (完善主義後果主義): certain objective states make life good independent of welfare — bringing act consequentialism closer to Aquinas's natural law theory.

Exam Traps Summary
  • Distinguish bold denial (questioning intuitions) from remote effects (questioning empirical premises).
  • SC has two independent counterexamples: the theft case and the helicopter case.
  • RC's collapsing objection: maximally complex rules become extensionally equivalent to act consequentialism.
  • Extensional equivalence means same verdict in every case — not same justification.
  • The structural features objected to are: direct, maximising, impartialist — different objections target different features.
Top things to memorize
  1. Three structural features: direct, maximising, impartialist.
  2. Three types of response: rebut directly, alternative welfare theory, reject structural feature.
  3. Bold denial (Greene) vs remote effects (empirical challenge).
  4. SC formula and its two counterexamples.
  5. RC and the collapsing objection (extensional equivalence).
  6. Experience machine → challenges hedonism → desire fulfillment → perfectionism.
  7. Acceptance utility: utility from internalising the rule across society.
Lower-yield / skip if short on time
  • De Lazari-Radek and Singer's exact wording of the surgeon case.
  • Technical details of perfectionist consequentialism's link to Aquinas.
  • Specific threshold values in the distributive justice table (+500, 0).

Lecture 7: Ethical Egoism

Ethical egoism holds that an action is right if and only if it maximises the agent's own utility. It is consequentialist and maximising like utilitarianism, but agent-relative (代理人相對) rather than impartialist. This lecture also covers psychological egoism — the empirical claim that all deliberate action is ultimately self-motivated — and the argument from psychological to ethical egoism.

1. Ethical Egoism (EE)

EE: An action A by P in C is right iff P's performing A would produce at least as much agent utility (代理人效用) — utility accruing to the agent P — as any alternative.

EE is: consequentialist, welfarist, agent-relative (only the agent's own welfare counts), maximising, and prudentialist (what is good for the agent determines rightness).

The definition of EE closely resembles the definition of an optional action. The difference: obligatory actions produce strictly more agent utility than all alternatives; optional actions are those where multiple alternatives tie for best.

Subjective EE (SEE): right iff the agent believes it maximises agent utility. Contrasts with objectivist EE where actual consequences determine rightness.

Exam trap: EE is not the same as "always do what you feel like." It requires maximising agent utility, which may require sacrificing short-term pleasure for long-term self-interest.

2. Rational Egoism and the Complaint That EE Is Not a Moral Theory

Rational egoism (RE): An action is rationally permissible iff it maximises agent utility. RE asks what is rational; EE asks what is morally right — they are distinct but related questions.

A common assumption: it is always rational to do what is morally right. This makes RE directly relevant to the defence of EE — EE competes with any theory that claims morally right action need not be self-interested.

3. Psychological Egoism (PE) and Altruism

PE: Any deliberate action a human agent performs always has self-interest as its ultimate (終極) motive. PE is a descriptive, empirical claim — it says how people in fact are motivated.

PE differs from psychological hedonism (心理享樂主義): PE allows ultimate motives beyond pleasure (e.g., power, legacy). PE does not require conscious awareness of self-interest, and distinguishes short-term from long-term motivation.

Three pro-egoistic explanations for apparently altruistic helping: (1) aversive-empathic arousal reduction — helping eliminates one's own discomfort at another's suffering; (2) self-punishment avoidance — helping avoids guilt or shame; (3) social esteem/self-rewards attraction.

Batson's experiments: participants who could escape witnessing suffering without helping still chose to help. This suggests genuine altruistic motivation — contra PE.

The argument from PE to EE: (1) ought implies can; (2) PE — all deliberate actions are ultimately self-interested; therefore (3) all obligatory actions are self-interested actions (EE). This commits the is-ought fallacy (是非謬誤) — a normative conclusion cannot be derived from a purely descriptive premise.

Exam trap: PE is descriptive; EE is normative. Batson's empirical findings challenge PE, but do not directly refute EE (a normative claim).

4. Evaluation of EE

Consistency: EE appears to conflict with the Principle of Non-Interference (PNI) — that it is wrong to interfere with someone performing their duty. EE may require such interference whenever it serves agent utility. Resolvable by qualifying PNI.

Determinacy: EE faces the same uncertainty problem as utilitarianism. Response: operate on expected agent utility; apply critical vs. intuitive levels of moral reasoning.

External support: a theory should accord with well-established findings from other fields. Batson's findings challenge PE, but this does not directly refute EE since PE is descriptive and EE is normative.

Exam trap: The "What You Most Want" argument confuses the liking sense of "want" with the explanatory sense. Acting on a desire to help another is not self-interested just because it is what the agent "wants."
Exam Traps Summary
  • PE is descriptive; EE is normative — Batson refutes PE, not EE directly.
  • EE vs. optional action: EE says right iff at least as much agent utility as any alternative — this is how optional actions are defined, not obligatory ones. Know the precise difference.
  • Psychological hedonism is narrower than PE: pleasure vs. self-interest.
  • RE concerns rationality; EE concerns morality — they are related but answer different questions.
  • The is-ought fallacy: PE alone cannot entail EE.
Top things to memorize
  1. EE formula and the distinction from optional actions.
  2. Five features of EE: consequentialist, welfarist, agent-relative, maximising, prudentialist.
  3. PE definition and its three pro-egoistic explanations of helping.
  4. Batson's experimental design and finding.
  5. Three arguments for PE (What You Most Want, Satisfaction, Pleasure) and their flaws.
  6. Is-ought fallacy blocking the PE-to-EE argument.
  7. Three evaluation criteria: consistency (PNI), determinacy, external support.
Lower-yield / skip if short on time
  • SEE (subjective ethical egoism) details beyond its basic definition.
  • The exact wording of the Satisfaction Argument vs. the Pleasure Argument.
  • Detailed mechanics of the critical vs. intuitive distinction for EE.

Lecture 8: Kantian Ethics

Kant grounds morality not in consequences or desires but in pure practical reason (純粹實踐理性). The categorical imperative — unconditional moral command — has two main formulations: the Formula of Humanity (respect persons as ends) and the Formula of Universal Law (act only on universalizable maxims). These generate a system of perfect and imperfect duties and culminate in postulates about the soul and God required for moral coherence.

1. Duty and Practical Reason

Moral law must hold with absolute necessity for all rational beings (理性存在者) — including non-human rational beings if they exist. Its ground must be in pure reason, not in contingent facts about human nature.

Practical reason (實踐理性): the capacity to deliberate and make free choices. It is both the source and the authority of the moral law.

Categorical imperative (CI) (定言命令): unconditionally binding — commands regardless of what ends one happens to adopt.

Hypothetical imperative (HI) (假言命令): conditionally binding — "If you want E, do A." Only valid given certain ends. Morality cannot be a system of HIs because moral duty cannot be escaped by giving up one's ends.

Exam trap: Morality cannot be a doctrine of personal happiness because happiness is contingently desired — making obligations hypothetical (dependent on wanting to be happy). Kant requires unconditional obligations.

2. Formula of Humanity (FH)

Kant distinguishes ends adopted based on desire from ends in themselves (自身目的): things with unconditional, non-instrumental value. Rational nature is the paradigmatic end in itself.

Autonomy (自律) — the capacity to give oneself the moral law through reason — is the ground of human dignity (尊嚴) and unconditional worth.

FH: "Act so that you use humanity, in your own person as well as in the person of any other, always at the same time as an end, never merely as a means." (G, 4:429)

Negative requirement: do not treat persons merely as means. Positive requirement: promote self-perfection and the happiness of others. Using someone as a means is permissible (e.g., friend gives you a lift); treating them as merely a means is not (mugger coercing victim).

The gangster who regards the coffee seller as a vending machine acts with a bad attitude but not wrongly in the transaction — what matters for FH is the action, not only the attitude.

Exam trap: "Treating as a means" is not the same as "treating as merely a means." All daily transactions involve using others as means — this is not automatically wrong.

3. Formula of Universal Law (FUL) and the Universalizability Test

FUL: "Act only according to that maxim through which you can at the same time will that it become a universal law." (G, 4:421)

FLN (variant): act as if the maxim were a universal law of nature.

A maxim (準則) is a subjective principle of volition: "I will X, when/if Y, in order to Z." FUL is a decision procedure; FH is a fundamental criterion.

The false promising case: a maxim of making false promises when in need cannot be universalised — the institution of promising would collapse, contradicting the maxim's own presupposition.

The selfishness case: a maxim of never helping others can be imagined as universal without logical contradiction, but it cannot be rationally willed — we simultaneously will to receive help when we are in need.

Exam trap: FUL is not rule consequentialism. The universalizability test checks logical/practical consistency, not utility maximisation.
Exam trap: False negative — FUL can prohibit morally neutral actions (stock market withdrawal). False positive — a hyper-specific maxim may be universalizable yet intuitively wrong.

4. Kant's System of Duties

Perfect duties (完全義務): must always/never be performed without exception. Imperfect duties (不完全義務): must be fulfilled sometimes and to some extent, with latitude in how.

Duties to oneself: self-perfection (developing talents and powers). Duties to others: promoting their happiness. These derive from the positive requirement of FH.

Casuistry (決疑法): the procedure for applying general principles to specific cases, required when moving from abstract principles to concrete moral judgments.

Lizbeth's lie: lying to murderers to protect an innocent person. Kant's principle: "the stronger ground of obligation prevails." Saving innocent life outweighs honesty here.

5. Moral Worth and the Highest Good

Moral worth (道德價值): requires (1) a good will — disposed to act from duty as the sole motive; and (2) inner strength to consistently resist temptation. Rational nature gives all persons equal dignity, even a vicious person retains some respect as a human being.

The highest good (最高善, summum bonum): virtue plus happiness proportionate to virtue. Nothing in the natural world guarantees this, requiring two practical postulates:

Immortality of the soul (靈魂不朽): perfect virtue requires endless moral progress, possible only if the soul continues indefinitely. God (上帝): a cause adequate to ensure happiness proportionate to virtue. Both are postulates — practically necessary but not theoretically cognisable.

Exam Traps Summary
  • FH criterion vs. FUL decision procedure — different roles.
  • Means vs. merely means — the key distinction for FH.
  • FUL ≠ rule consequentialism — different criteria (consistency vs. utility).
  • False negative and false positive objections to FUL.
  • Perfect vs. imperfect duties — imperfect allows latitude, perfect does not.
  • Practical postulates: immortality + God — practically necessary, not theoretical proofs.
Top things to memorize
  1. CI vs. HI and why morality must be categorical.
  2. FH exact formulation and its negative + positive requirements.
  3. FUL exact formulation; FLN as its variant.
  4. Maxim semantic form: "I will X, when Y, in order to Z."
  5. False promising: contradiction in the universalisation of the practice.
  6. Perfect vs. imperfect duties.
  7. Highest good = virtue + proportionate happiness → requires immortality + God as postulates.
Lower-yield / skip if short on time
  • Exact CPR citations for immortality and God postulates.
  • Detailed casuistry mechanics beyond Lizbeth's lie.
  • The gangster example's finer points about attitude vs. action.

Lecture 10: Virtue Ethics and Care Ethics

Virtue ethics (德性倫理學) shifts moral focus from right action to virtuous character: what kind of person should I be? Aristotle's account grounds virtues in human function and eudaimonia. Care ethics (關懷倫理學), inspired by Carol Gilligan, grounds morality in relationships of care and dependency, offering a distinct picture of the self and moral reasoning.

1. What Is Virtue?

A virtue (德性) is a relatively fixed character trait involving dispositions to think, reason, feel, and act in certain ways across varied circumstances. It has three components: (1) intellectual — correct beliefs about when the trait is appropriate; (2) affective — appropriate emotional responses; (3) motivational-behavioural — disposition to act accordingly.

Virtues are acquired by practice and exercise — not naturally endowed. Cardinal virtues (主德) are not dependent on other virtues; Greek cardinal virtues: wisdom, courage, temperance, justice. Christian adds: faith, hope, and love.

2. Aristotle on Virtue and the Good Life

The Function Argument: all activities aim at some good; the highest good is most choice-worthy (unconditionally complete and self-sufficient); the distinctive function of humans is rationality; the good of a thing is performing its function well; therefore the human good is rational activity of the soul guided by virtue — eudaimonia (幸福/繁盛).

Eudaimonia is a moralized concept: it requires virtue (unlike ordinary happiness which is subjective). For Aristotle, virtue is necessary but not sufficient — external goods are also required. For the Stoics, virtue is both necessary and sufficient.

Doctrine of the Mean (中道): virtue aims at the intermediate between excess and deficiency in feelings and actions — not a fixed numerical midpoint but what is appropriate for the person in the circumstances. E.g., courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness.

Phronesis (實踐智慧, practical intelligence): the higher-order virtue that determines the right manner of feeling and action in particular circumstances. Comes only with life experience.

Unity of Virtues (德性統一): virtues mutually require each other. Example: being truly just in rationing requires also being temperate — without temperance one is disposed to take more than one's share.

3. The Formal Theory of Virtue Ethics (VE)

VE holds that facts about virtuous agents are more basic than facts about right conduct and explain why actions are right or wrong. Uses a qualified agent standard — an ideally virtuous agent who possesses all morally significant virtues and consistently acts on them.

Formal definition: A in C is obligatory iff any ideally virtuous agent would not fail to do A; wrong iff no ideally virtuous agent would do A; optional iff some ideally virtuous agent might or might not do A.

VE generates virtue rules (e.g., "be kind") but features multiple cardinal virtues, so conflicts arise when virtue considerations are incompatible.

4. Evaluation of VE

The mendacious person counterexample: a compulsive liar who decides to reform must take concrete steps (tracking lies, rewarding honesty) that an ideally virtuous person would never need. VE's standard cannot guide moral improvement.

Modified VE: an action is obligatory iff any ideally virtuous agent would advise one to do it. The obstinate person counterexample: a virtuous agent might strategically advise the wrong action to prompt the right one in an obstinate person — generating the wrong verdict.

Exam trap: The two counterexamples target VE in different ways — the first targets action-guidance in self-development; the second targets the advisory modification.

5. Care Ethics

Inspired by Carol Gilligan's In a Different Voice (1982). The justice vs. care contrast: justice sees self as individual, inequality as central moral problem, and uses impartial reasoning; care sees self as constituted by relationships, disconnection/abandonment as central problem, and uses contextually sensitive reasoning.

Two senses of care: caring about (being invested in someone/something — an attitude); caring for (actively meeting needs — an action).

Tronto's five stages: (1) Attentiveness / Caring About — recognising need, overcoming moral blindness; (2) Responsibility / Taking Care Of — moving from "someone should" to "I must"; (3) Competence / Care-Giving — care actually meets the need; (4) Responsiveness / Care-Receiving — attend to care-receiver's perspective, alert to power imbalances; (5) Solidarity / Caring With — care as collective political responsibility.

Exam trap: Not all forms of care are morally valuable — care that stifles development or autonomy is problematic.

6. Care Ethics and Moral Theory

Stephanie Collins: care ethics is not a complete moral theory — it does not cover all of morality (e.g., duties not to harm, promise-keeping). It is complementary to, not a replacement for, broader theories.

Dependency principle (依賴原則, Collins): when an important interest is unfulfilled, you are capable of fulfilling it, and doing so is not too costly, you have a responsibility to fulfill it. Extends care obligations beyond existing personal relationships.

Principle of subsidiarity (輔助原則, Engster): care should be delivered at the most local and personal level possible; we should enable self-care wherever possible. Both principles are guided by emotional attunement.

Exam Traps Summary
  • Eudaimonia requires virtue (Aristotle) but also external goods — unlike Stoics for whom virtue is sufficient.
  • Doctrine of the Mean: the intermediate is relative to the person, not a fixed midpoint.
  • VE's two evaluation problems target different modifications (direct and advisory).
  • Care ethics is not just about close relationships — the dependency principle extends globally.
  • Tronto's five stages: know them in order and what each involves.
Top things to memorize
  1. Three components of a virtue: intellectual, affective, motivational-behavioural.
  2. Function Argument steps (6 steps).
  3. Eudaimonia: moralized concept; Aristotle (necessary not sufficient) vs. Stoics (necessary and sufficient).
  4. Doctrine of Mean: excess, intermediate (virtuous), deficiency.
  5. VE formal definition (obligatory / wrong / optional).
  6. Tronto's five stages in order.
  7. Dependency principle + principle of subsidiarity.
Lower-yield / skip if short on time
  • Christian cardinal virtues beyond the Greek four.
  • Detailed Collins quote about care ethics not being a complete theory.
  • Exact wording of the mendacious person regimen steps.

Lecture 11: Moral Particularism

Moral particularism (道德特殊主義) challenges the generalist assumption that morality depends on principles. By showing that the moral contribution of features can vary, reverse, or be silenced across contexts, particularism challenges both the existence and the practical primacy of moral principles. The lecture traces generalist responses and the resulting holistic generalism as a middle ground.

1. Generalism vs. Particularism

Generalism (普遍主義): morality depends on principles. Particularism (特殊主義): morality depends on the morally relevant features of particular cases, and these features may not behave in a fixed, principle-like way.

Particularism does not mean: (1) moral judgment is just personal feeling; (2) all moral generalizations are false.

2. Forms of Particularism

Principle Eliminativism (PE): there are no correct moral principles — a metaphysical/semantic thesis about the existence of true principles.

Principle Abstinence (PA): a morally committed agent seeking correct verdicts should not rely on principles for guidance — a practical/methodological thesis.

PE and PA are logically independent: one can hold PE without PA or PA without PE.

3. The Generalist Picture and Types of Principles

Absolute principles (絕對原則): a feature always generates a fixed, decisive verdict (exceptionless). Contributory principles (貢獻性原則): a feature counts for or against but can be outweighed (defeasible).

The Generalist Picture has three theses: (1) Generic Generalist Thesis (GGT) — a small set of fundamental features explains right/wrong; (2) Universal Relevance Thesis (URT) — a feature morally relevant in one case is relevant wherever it appears; (3) Polarity Thesis (PT) — a feature that counts in favour always counts in favour.

Reasons atomism (理由原子論): the contribution of a feature is fixed independently of context — its moral charge is intrinsic. Atomists allow reasons to outweigh but not reverse each other.

4. Reasons Holism, Reversal, and Silencing

Reasons holism (理由整體論) / variability of reasons: the moral contribution of a feature depends on context. A feature that supports an action in one context may oppose it or be irrelevant in another.

Reversal (逆轉): a feature's moral polarity changes across contexts — not merely outweighed but actively contributing in the opposite direction. Example (McNaughton): in the public execution case, sadistic pleasure may count against reintroducing executions, not merely be outweighed.

Reversal vs. outweighing: in outweighing, the feature retains its positive charge but is defeated. In reversal, the charge itself flips.

Silencing (沉默): a feature that normally gives a reason becomes entirely morally irrelevant in a particular context. Example: promising to help someone cheat — the promise is silenced and gives no reason to help.

The case of lying: the same feature (lying) can count in favour (protecting from violence), against (job application fraud), be irrelevant (harmless game), or context-dependent (witholding harsh truth from a patient).

Exam trap: Reversal is not the same as outweighing. The particularist says the pleasure here does not count positively at all — its polarity has changed. A Rossian would say it counts positively but is outweighed.

5. Generalist Responses

Atomistic Specification: when a principle faces a counterexample, add conditions to avoid it (e.g., "keep promises unless coerced, unless the act is immoral, unless..."). Makes principles more complex.

Particularist reply: the added conditions are not reasons — they are enablers (conditions allowing a consideration to function as a reason) or disablers (conditions preventing it). The specified principle conflates reasons with enabling/disabling conditions. It would be odd to say "I should go because my promise was not extracted under duress."

Holistic Accommodation: accept reasons holism but insist principles are still possible. Yields holistic generalism (HG).

6. Holistic Generalism

A holistic principle has three components: (1) a morally significant feature; (2) a holism clause — acknowledging potential silencing, reversal, disabling; (3) an overriding clause — allowing outweighing by opposing reasons. This is called double defeasibility (雙重可廢除性).

HG explains by subsumption (涵攝) — showing that the action falls under a holistic principle. Particularism explains by narration — showing how the features of this concrete situation add up here.

HG's key claim: reasons holism (RH) does not entail principle eliminativism (PE). RH undermines atomistic principles but not holistic ones — the particularist needs a stronger argument.

Exam trap: Double defeasibility: holistic principles are defeasible in two ways — by context altering the feature's contribution (holism clause) and by outweighing (overriding clause).

7. The Particularist Claim and Moral Discernment

The particularist does not deny all moral generalizations. But principles are not the foundation of moral explanation. The practical consequence is Principle Abstinence: principles can distort attention and cause premature closure (過早結論) on the wrong verdict.

Principles are useful as heuristics in professional contexts (medical ethics: informed consent, non-maleficence; public service: fairness, accountability) but should not replace discernment.

Moral discernment (道德辨識力): a trained capacity to notice morally relevant features and understand their relations, developed through experience, comparison, reflection, and moral education.

Practical Grammar metaphor: like learning a language, moral concepts are learned through exposure to cases across contexts — their grammar, tone, and flexibility — not through a single mechanical definition.

Case Comparison: placing a case alongside structurally related cases with different verdicts illuminates which features are doing the moral work, without mechanically determining a verdict.

Exam trap: Particularism does not reject moral learning. It endorses learning patterns of relevance from cases — a practical grammar model rather than a rule-memorisation model.
Exam Traps Summary
  • PE (no true principles) and PA (don't rely on principles) are logically independent.
  • Reversal ≠ outweighing: polarity flip vs. being overridden while retaining polarity.
  • Enablers and disablers are not reasons — the particularist's key reply to atomistic specification.
  • RH does not entail PE — holistic generalism uses this to resist particularism.
  • HG's double defeasibility: holism clause + overriding clause.
Top things to memorize
  1. Generalism vs. particularism: principle-based vs. case-based.
  2. PE (metaphysical) vs. PA (methodological) and their independence.
  3. Three generalist theses: GGT, URT, PT.
  4. Reasons atomism vs. reasons holism.
  5. Reversal vs. silencing vs. outweighing.
  6. Reasons, enablers, disablers — the distinction.
  7. Holistic principle structure: feature + holism clause + overriding clause = double defeasibility.
  8. HG explanation by subsumption vs. particularist explanation by narration.

  • McNaughton's exact wording of the public execution example.
  • The exact promised-act conditions in the atomistic specification example.
  • Detailed legal/professional ethics examples beyond the general point.
