Lecture 5: Revenue Management

Revenue management (收益管理) is the discipline of selling the right product to the right customer at the right time for the right price. It combines dynamic pricing, capacity control, and overbooking to squeeze maximum revenue out of fixed or perishable capacity. The core insight is that uniform pricing leaves money on the table: customers differ in willingness to pay, and demand accumulates over time in predictable patterns that firms can exploit.

1. Dynamic Pricing and Willingness to Pay

Dynamic pricing (動態定價) treats price as a management control variable rather than a fixed parameter.

  • Revenue formula: revenue = price × min{demand, capacity} — maximizing this product requires choosing the right price, not simply the highest one.
  • Willingness to pay (WTP, 願意支付價格) — the maximum amount a customer is willing to incur to obtain one unit of a product or service.
  • Different customers have different WTPs for the same product; firms exploit this via market segmentation (市場細分).
  • WTP is determined by: customer characteristics (income, gender), product characteristics (quality, brand), and availability of substitutes (替代品).

Revenue maximization with heterogeneous WTP — key mechanics:

  • List all WTP values and frequencies; compute cumulative demand at each price (customers with WTP ≥ price will buy).
  • Revenue at each candidate price = price × cumulative demand at that price.
  • Textbook example: WTP values $20–$80 with frequencies 6–28; revenue peaks at price = $40 yielding $3,360.
  • Fixed-price strategy captures only one segment; variable pricing across segments captures additional revenue.

2. Capacity Control and Protection Levels

Capacity control (產能控制) allocates limited seats or rooms across fare classes (票價等級) to maximize total revenue. Airlines routinely sell the same physical seat at multiple price points depending on when and how the ticket is booked.

  • Protection level (保護水平) Q — number of units reserved for the high-fare class or higher; low-fare bookings are blocked once this threshold is hit.
  • Booking limit (訂位上限) — maximum units that may be sold at the discounted fare = total capacity − protection level.
  • Two-class model: full fare f_H vs. discount fare f_L; passengers always book discount first while seats remain available; last-minute full-fare demand follows a known distribution.

Cost trade-off for setting protection level Q:

  • C_o (overage cost) — set Q too high: reserved a seat at full fare that went unsold; loss = f_L (could have sold at discount).
  • C_u (underage cost) — set Q too low: sold a seat at discount that a full-fare customer would have paid; loss = f_H − f_L.

Marginal analysis (邊際分析) decision rule — keep increasing Q until:

  • Expected cost of over-protecting = Expected cost of under-protecting
  • Critical factor (關鍵因子): F(Q*) = C_u / (C_u + C_o)
  • Airline example: F(Q*) = 2700 / (2700 + 5250) = 0.3396; z = −0.41; Q* = 25 + (−0.41)(5) = 23 seats.
  • Hotel example (discrete data): full rate $159, discount $105; C_u = 54, C_o = 105; F(Q*) = 0.3396 → Q* = 79 rooms.
  • Double-check rule: continue increasing Q while (1 − F(Q)) × f_H ≥ f_L. Stop when expected revenue from the next protected unit falls below the discount fare.

Decision tree (決策樹) framing for the Q-to-Q+1 choice:

  • Choose "No" (keep Q): earn f_L for sure.
  • Choose "Yes" (protect Q+1): earn f_H with probability P(D > Q) = 1 − F(Q); earn $0 with probability F(Q).
  • Increase to Q+1 while: (1 − F(Q)) × f_H ≥ f_L, equivalently F(Q) ≤ (f_H − f_L) / f_H.

Multiple fare classes extension:

  • When f_1 > f_2 > f_3 > … > f_n, use a nested booking limit (巢式訂位上限) structure.
  • Each lower fare class has access to seats not reserved for higher classes; limits nest inside each other.
  • The same critical-factor formula applies pairwise between adjacent fare classes.
Exam trap: In capacity control, C_o = f_L (the discount fare, not zero) and C_u = f_H − f_L (the revenue difference, not the full fare). Students commonly set C_o = 0 or C_u = f_H — both are wrong. The seat is not worthless unsold if a discount buyer existed; the opportunity cost is f_L.

3. Overbooking

Overbooking (超額預訂) means selling more reservations than actual capacity to hedge against no-shows (未出現) and cancellations (取消). Because no-show quantity is random, the firm must choose an overbooking quantity Q that balances the two error costs.

  • C_u (underestimating no-shows) — actual no-shows exceed Q, leaving capacity empty; cost = lost revenue per empty seat (e.g., ticket price = $50).
  • C_o (overestimating no-shows) — actual no-shows fewer than Q, so more customers arrive than seats; cost = compensation per denied-boarding passenger (e.g., $120 or $300 + original ticket).

Approach 1 — Simple average (簡單平均法):

  • Set Q = E(# no-shows) = weighted average of the no-show distribution.
  • Textbook example: E(# no-shows) = 0(0.05) + 1(0.1) + 2(0.2) + … + 10(0.05) = 4.05 → Q = 4.
  • Limitation: ignores the asymmetry between C_u and C_o; may be far from optimal when costs differ significantly.

Approach 2 — Payoff table (損益表):

  • Construct a matrix: rows = actual no-show levels (with probabilities), columns = overbooking quantities Q.
  • Cell cost = C_u × max(no-shows − Q, 0) + C_o × max(Q − no-shows, 0).
  • Compute E(Total cost) for each column Q; choose the Q with minimum expected cost.
  • Textbook result (C_u = $50, C_o = $120): minimum cost at Q = 2 ($137); Q = 0 costs $203, Q = 1 costs $161, Q = 3 costs $146, Q = 4 costs $181, Q = 5 costs $242.

Approach 3 — Marginal analysis (newsvendor model, 報童模型):

  • Continue overbooking until: expected cost of overbooking = expected cost of empty capacity.
  • Critical factor: F(Q*) = C_u / (C_u + C_o) — same formula as capacity control.
  • Discrete example (C_u = $50, C_o = $120): critical factor = 50 / 170 = 0.2941 → Q* = 2 (first Q where cumulative probability ≥ 0.2941).
  • Overbooking example with normal distribution (C_u = $105, C_o = $300): critical factor = 105 / 405 = 0.259; z = −0.65; Q* = 20 + (−0.65)(10) = 13.5 → 13 seats (verified by marginal check).
  • Hotel overbooking example (C_u = $80, C_o = $200): critical factor = 80 / 280 = 0.2857 → Q* = 3 rooms (discrete) or Q* = 5 + (−0.565)(3) = 3.3 ≈ 3 (normal).

Verification via marginal condition (for non-0.5 rounding edge cases):

  • Check both Q and Q+1: compare [F(Q)][C_o] vs. [1 − F(Q)][C_u].
  • Continue overbooking while [F(Q)][C_o] < [1 − F(Q)][C_u]; stop when equality is crossed.
  • Course rule: use standard rounding to the nearest integer in exams and exercises.
Exam trap: The net overage cost in overbooking problems is often C_o = (total denied-boarding cost) − (ticket price already paid), not the full stated compensation. For example, if denied-boarding total cost = $405 and ticket = $105, then net C_o = $300. Always subtract the ticket price already received.

4. Marginal Analysis — General Framework

The newsvendor (報童模型) / marginal analysis (邊際分析) framework unifies capacity control and overbooking. It answers: how much of a scarce resource (稀缺資源) should I commit to a use, given uncertain demand?

  • Core logic: increase quantity Q by 1 as long as expected marginal benefit (預期邊際收益) > expected marginal cost.
  • Marginal cost = C_o × P(D < Q) = C_o × F(Q) — the cost of having too much.
  • Marginal benefit = C_u × P(D ≥ Q) = C_u × [1 − F(Q)] — the opportunity cost (機會成本) of having too little.
  • Optimal condition: F(Q*) = C_u / (C_u + C_o) — the critical fractile (關鍵分位數).
  • Q* is the smallest Q such that P(D ≤ Q) ≥ critical factor (round-up rule for discrete distributions (離散分佈)).

Implications of relative cost magnitudes:

  • If C_u > C_o → critical factor > 0.5 → stock more (lean toward over-supplying (過度供應)).
  • If C_u < C_o → critical factor < 0.5 → stock less (lean toward under-supplying).
  • Increasing C_u raises Q*; increasing C_o decreases Q*.

Exam trap: The critical factor formula is always C_u / (C_u + C_o) — not C_u / C_o and not C_o / (C_u + C_o). Mixing up numerator and denominator gives a complementary probability, leading to a Q* on the wrong side of the distribution.

5. Revenue Management Strategy and Framework

Revenue management (RM) sits at the intersection of strategic and operational decisions. The framework organises decisions into two tiers:

  • Strategic decisions (策略決策) — dynamic pricing, forecasting (需求預測), market segmentation (市場區隔), and optimization (最優化).
  • Tactical / operational decisions (戰術與運營決策) — yield management (收益管理), overbooking, and capacity control.

Three core questions guiding RM practice:

  • Who is the right customer? → Market segmentation.
  • What is the right product? → Product differentiation (產品差異化) (e.g., fare codes XS, Y, B, M, L, Q with different restrictions).
  • When is the right time and what is the right price? → Dynamic pricing and capacity allocation.

Airline seat class allocation (座位等級分配) in practice:

  • Economy class seats carry multiple fare codes (e.g., Y, B, M, Q, XS) — same physical seat but different price, flexibility, and booking restrictions.
  • Seasonal allocation adjusts percentages across fare codes: e.g., more discount seats in off-peak winter, more full-fare seats in peak summer.
  • Booking limits and protection levels are adjusted dynamically as the departure date approaches and demand data accumulates.
Exam Traps Summary

  • In capacity control, C_o = f_L (discount fare) and C_u = f_H − f_L (fare gap) — do NOT use C_o = 0 or C_u = f_H.
  • In overbooking, the net overage cost C_o = total denied-boarding cost MINUS the ticket price already paid (since the firm keeps the ticket revenue regardless).
  • The critical factor formula is C_u / (C_u + C_o) — NOT C_u / C_o and NOT C_o / (C_u + C_o).

  • Booking limit and protection level are complementary: booking limit = total capacity − protection level. Mixing them up reverses your answer.

Top things to memorize

  2. Critical factor (newsvendor / capacity control / overbooking): F(Q*) = C_u / (C_u + C_o).
  3. Capacity control: C_o = f_L (discount fare); C_u = f_H − f_L (fare gap).
  4. Overbooking: C_u = price of empty seat (ticket price); C_o = net compensation cost (total compensation − ticket price already paid).

  9. Protection level + booking limit = total capacity (they are complements, not additive).
  10. Multiple fare classes use nested booking limits: each lower class can access seats not protected for higher classes.

Lecture 4: Managing Short Life Cycle Products – Newsvendor Model

When products perish, go out of season, or become obsolete quickly, the usual replenishment logic breaks down: you must commit to an order quantity before you know actual demand, and any surplus or shortage carries a direct cost. The Newsvendor Model (報攤模型) gives a principled answer to this one-shot ordering decision by balancing the cost of over-ordering against the cost of under-ordering through a single number called the Critical Ratio (臨界比率). Mastering this model means understanding both the formula and the two computational approaches — marginal analysis for discrete demand and the normal-distribution formula for continuous demand.

1. The Newsvendor Problem: Setup and Motivation

The newsvendor problem (報攤問題) captures any situation with three features simultaneously:

  • Uncertain demand (不確定需求) — demand is unknown at order time; only a probability distribution is available.
  • Pre-commitment (預先承諾) — the order quantity must be chosen before demand is observed; there is only one ordering opportunity.
  • Short product life cycle (短產品生命週期) — leftover inventory loses value or all value after the season ends; salvage value (殘值) is typically below cost.

Real-world examples span many industries:

  • Fashion apparel (時裝), festive products (節慶商品) — Christmas trees, decorations
  • Special-event merchandise (特殊活動商品) — event T-shirts, holiday turkeys
  • Technology with rapid obsolescence (快速淘汰) — video games, computers, cell phones
  • Healthcare — flu vaccines (流感疫苗), perishable biologics

The core trade-off (核心取捨):

  • Q < D → lost sales (缺貨損失): foregone profit on units you could have sold
  • Q > D → markdown cost (降價損失): units sold below cost or disposed
  • Ordering the average (平均值) is not necessarily optimal — the correct quantity depends on the relative sizes of these two costs.
Exam trap: Students often assume ordering the average demand is always optimal. It is not — optimal quantity shifts above or below the mean depending on whether underage or overage cost dominates.

2. Cost Structure and the Critical Ratio

Define the two unit mismatch costs (不匹配成本):

  • Overage cost Co (超額成本) — cost per unit of surplus when Q > D; e.g., purchase cost minus salvage value: Co = cost − salvage
  • Underage cost Cu (短缺成本) — cost per unit of shortage when Q < D; e.g., lost margin: Cu = selling price − cost

Total mismatch cost for a given order Q and realized demand D:

  • G(Q, D) = Co · max(0, Q − D) + Cu · max(0, D − Q)
  • Objective: find Q* that minimizes expected cost G(Q) = E[G(Q, D)]

The Critical Ratio (臨界比率) CR is the threshold cumulative probability that solves the optimization:

  • CR = Cu / (Cu + Co)
  • Optimal condition: F(Q*) = P(D ≤ Q*) = CR
  • Derivation: at Q*, expected marginal overage cost equals expected marginal underage cost — P(D ≤ Q) · Co = P(D > Q) · Cu = [1 − P(D ≤ Q)] · Cu
Scenario Cost relationship Critical Ratio Ordering posture
Fashion apparel (時裝) Cu = $300 >> Co = $20 300 / 320 ≈ 94% Aggressive (積極); over-stock
IBM ThinkPad (舊型號) Cu ≈ 0, Co > 0 ≈ 0% Conservative (保守); under-plan
Cookies King (校園餅店) Cu = $3, Co = $2 3 / 5 = 60% Slightly above median

Key insight (關鍵洞見):

  • If Cu >> Co: stockout is very costly → CR near 1 → order more than expected demand (over-stock strategy).
  • If Cu << Co: surplus is very costly → CR near 0 → order less than expected demand (under-plan strategy).
  • Optimal Q* depends on BOTH the demand distribution AND the cost structure — never on one alone.
Exam trap: Cu and Co are defined as per-unit mismatch costs, not total revenue or total cost. Always derive them as incremental values: Co = purchase cost − salvage; Cu = selling price − purchase cost.

3. Solution Approach 1 — Payoff Table (Discrete Demand)

Used when demand is discrete (離散需求) with a small number of possible values. Steps:

  1. List all possible demand values D and their probabilities P(D).
  2. For each combination of order quantity Q and demand D, compute net profit:
    • If D ≥ Q: profit = Cu · Q (sell everything ordered)
    • If D < Q: profit = Cu · D − Co · (Q − D) (sell D, mark down Q−D)
  3. Compute expected value (期望值) EV for each row Q: EV = Σ [profit(Q,D) · P(D)].
  4. Choose Q* with the highest EV.

Cookies King example (餅店例子) — parameters:

  • Selling price = $7.9/unit; Cost = $4.9/unit; Markdown price = $2.9/unit
  • Cu = 7.9 − 4.9 = $3/unit; Co = 4.9 − 2.9 = $2/unit
  • Demand distribution: 1,800–3,000 units in 200-unit steps with probabilities 0.05, 0.10, 0.20, 0.30, 0.20, 0.10, 0.05

Payoff table result:

  • Q* = 2,400 units with expected profit = $6,650
  • EV calculation example for Q = 2,800: 3,400(0.05) + 4,400(0.1) + 5,400(0.2) + 6,400(0.3) + 7,400(0.2) + 8,400(0.1) + 8,400(0.05) = $6,350

4. Solution Approach 2 — Marginal Analysis (Discrete Demand)

Marginal analysis (邊際分析) asks: should we stock the (Q+1)th unit? Stop increasing Q when expected marginal cost ≥ expected marginal benefit.

  • Expected marginal cost of the (Q+1)th unit = P(D ≤ Q) · Co
  • Expected marginal benefit of the (Q+1)th unit = P(D > Q) · Cu = [1 − P(D ≤ Q)] · Cu
  • Optimality condition (最優條件): P(D ≤ Q*) · Co = [1 − P(D ≤ Q*)] · Cu
  • Solving: P(D ≤ Q*) = Cu / (Cu + Co) = Critical Ratio

Procedure for discrete demand (離散需求程序):

  1. Compute CR = Cu / (Cu + Co).
  2. Build a cumulative probability table (累積機率表) P(D ≤ Q) for each possible Q value.
  3. Find the smallest Q* such that P(D ≤ Q*) ≥ CR.

Cookies King via marginal analysis:

  • CR = 3 / (3 + 2) = 0.60
  • P(D ≤ 2,200) = 0.35; P(D ≤ 2,400) = 0.65 — first value ≥ 0.60 → Q* = 2,400
  • Expected profit at Q* = 2,400: 4,200(0.05) + 5,200(0.10) + 6,200(0.20) + 7,200(0.65) = $6,650 — matches payoff table

Fashion item example (時裝例子) — department store:

  • Cost = $100; Selling price = $190; Outlet price (特賣價) = $80; Season = 1 month; Past 100 seasons of data
  • Co = 100 − 80 = $20/unit; Cu = 190 − 100 = $90/unit
  • CR = 90 / (90 + 20) = 0.8182
  • From cumulative frequency table (累積頻率表): P(D ≤ 88) = 0.78; P(D ≤ 89) = 0.84 — first ≥ 0.8182 → Q* = 89 units
  • Mean = 85 units, σ = 4.43 units; ordering at 81.82% service level places Q* well above the mean
Exam trap: When using a discrete cumulative table, select the smallest Q where P(D ≤ Q) is at least (≥) the Critical Ratio — not strictly greater than. If P(D ≤ Q) exactly equals CR, that Q is already optimal; do not move to the next level.

5. Solution Approach 3 — Normal Distribution (Continuous Demand)

When demand is approximately normally distributed (常態分佈) — typically when the number of possible demand values is large — use the standard normal formula:

  • Q* = μ + z · σ
  • μ = mean demand (平均需求); σ = standard deviation (標準差); z = z-score (標準分) corresponding to CR from the standard normal table
  • If z < 0, Q* < μ — optimal to order below average (conservative posture).
  • If z > 0, Q* > μ — optimal to order above average (aggressive posture).

Step-by-step procedure (常態分佈求解步驟):

  1. Identify μ and σ — from historical data, regression model, or given distribution parameters.
  2. Compute Cu and Co from price, cost, and salvage value.
  3. Compute CR = Cu / (Cu + Co).
  4. Look up z such that Φ(z) = CR from the standard normal table (or use Excel: NORMINV(CR, μ, σ)).
  5. Q* = μ + z · σ. Round to nearest integer.

Christmas tree demand forecast (聖誕樹需求預測) — two-step process:

  • Step 1 — Estimate μ and σ: fit a linear regression (線性迴歸) to historical sales data; use regression equation to predict mean for next year; use STDEV of residuals as σ.
  • Example equation: Sales = 10.95k + 0.2k × year; for year 53: μ = 10.95 + 0.2(53) = 21.55k; σ = 4.26k
  • Step 2 — Apply newsvendor formula: Co = 2.5 − 1.0 = $1.5; Cu = 7.5 − 2.5 = $5.0; CR = 5 / 6.5 = 0.77; Q* = 21,550 + z(4,260) ≈ 24,690 units

Effect of service level (服務水準) on Q* — fashion item illustration:

  • 85% service level: z = 1.04 → Q* = 90 units
  • 90% service level: z = 1.28 → Q* = 91 units
  • 95% service level: z = 1.64 → Q* = 93 units
  • 99% service level: z = 2.33 → Q* = 96 units
Exam trap: For Mr. Choi's newspaper problem, the critical ratio is 3/(3+4) = 0.4286, giving z = −0.18 and Q* = 100 + (−0.18)(12) = 97.84 ≈ 98 copies — below the mean. Students often force z to be positive or round incorrectly. A negative z is valid and means ordering below average is optimal.

6. Choosing the Right Solution Approach

The three approaches cover different data situations:

Data type Method Formula
Discrete / few values (少量離散值) Payoff table (收益表) Compute EV for each Q; pick max
Discrete / few values (少量離散值) Marginal analysis (邊際分析) P(D ≤ Q*) = Cu / (Cu + Co)
Continuous / large range (連續大範圍) Normal distribution (常態分佈) Q* = μ + z · σ
  • Payoff table and marginal analysis always yield the same Q* for the same discrete distribution.
  • Normal approximation (常態近似) is valid even when true demand is integer-valued, as long as the range is large — round the final continuous Q* to the nearest integer.
  • Excel shortcut (Excel捷徑): NORMINV(CR, μ, σ) returns Q* directly without needing the z-table.

Technical notes from lecture:

  • Demand in Mr. Choi's problem is modelled as Normal(μ=100, σ=12) even though actual sales are integers — this is standard practice.
  • When you cannot find the exact CR in the normal table, interpolate (內插) between the two nearest z values or use the closest available value.
  • When using the discrete cumulative table, P(D ≤ Q) is a step function — the critical ratio identifies the step to land on.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Ordering the average demand is NOT optimal unless Cu = Co exactly.
  • Cu and Co are per-unit incremental costs: Co = cost − salvage; Cu = price − cost. Never use total revenue or total cost.
  • Critical Ratio = Cu / (Cu + Co), not Co / (Cu + Co). The numerator is the underage cost.
  • For discrete demand, choose the smallest Q where P(D ≤ Q) ≥ CR — use ≥, not strictly >.
  • A negative z-score in the normal distribution approach is valid and means Q* < μ — ordering below average is optimal when overage cost dominates.
  • The payoff table and marginal analysis must agree on Q* for the same problem — if they do not, recheck your Cu and Co derivation.
  • IBM ThinkPad scenario: near-zero lost-sales cost (backorder) pushes CR ≈ 0, meaning production should be very conservative regardless of demand distribution.
Top things to memorize
  1. Critical Ratio formula: CR = Cu / (Cu + Co), where Cu = price − cost, Co = cost − salvage.
  2. Optimality condition: F(Q*) = P(D ≤ Q*) = CR — the CDF evaluated at Q* equals the critical ratio.
  3. Normal distribution formula: Q* = μ + z · σ, where z = Φ⁻¹(CR).
  4. Discrete approach: build cumulative P(D ≤ Q) table; pick smallest Q where P(D ≤ Q) ≥ CR.
  5. High CR (close to 1) → aggressive over-stocking (e.g., fashion with high lost-sale cost).
  6. Low CR (close to 0) → conservative under-stocking (e.g., IBM ThinkPad with near-zero backorder cost).
  7. Mr. Choi SCMP: Cu = $3, Co = $4, CR = 0.4286, z = −0.18, Q* = 98 copies (below mean of 100).
  8. Cookies King: Cu = $3, Co = $2, CR = 0.60, Q* = 2,400 units, EV = $6,650.
  9. Fashion department store: Cu = $90, Co = $20, CR = 0.8182, Q* = 89 units (mean 85, σ 4.43).
  10. Christmas trees: μ from regression (10.95k + 0.2k × year); σ from residuals; Q* ≈ 24,690 units at CR = 0.77.
  11. Three solution pathways: payoff table (discrete, few values) → marginal analysis (discrete, any size) → normal distribution (continuous/large).
  12. Optimal Q* depends on BOTH demand distribution AND cost structure — changing either changes Q*.

Lecture 3: Inventory Management

Inventory management (存貨管理) is the discipline of deciding how much stock to hold and when to replenish it so that operations run without interruption while costs are minimised. Every firm faces a core tension: holding too little risks stockouts and lost sales, yet holding too much ties up capital, hides operational problems, and generates waste. This lecture builds from the basic role of inventory through practical classification and counting methods to four quantitative models — EOQ, EPQ, quantity-discount EOQ, and probabilistic EOQ — each suited to a different demand environment.

1. The Role and Purpose of Inventory

Inventory (存貨) is the stock of any item or resource used in an organisation, encompassing:

  • Raw materials (原材料) — inputs not yet entered production
  • Components (零件) — partially processed inputs
  • Work-in-progress (在製品, WIP) — items currently being transformed
  • Finished goods (成品) — completed products awaiting sale or shipment

Why firms hold inventory — four distinct purposes:

  • Buffer against uncertainty (緩衝不確定性) — absorbs unpredictable demand or supply fluctuations; this portion is called safety stock (安全庫存)
  • Decoupling (解耦) — maintains independence between successive operations so one stage's disruption does not instantly halt the next
  • Economies of scale (規模經濟) — larger orders reduce per-unit ordering and logistics costs; the resulting stock is cycle stock (週期庫存)
  • Required/unavoidable — regulatory requirements, seasonal stock (季節性庫存), and pipeline/in-transit inventory (在途庫存) that cannot be avoided

Why firms must avoid excess inventory (過多存貨):

  • Ties up working capital (營運資金) — money locked in stock earns no return; Kiyoshi Suzaki called excess inventory "the root of all evil"
  • Little's Law (利特爾定律) implication: I = R × T — higher inventory (I) for a given throughput rate (R) means longer flow time (T), slowing responsiveness
  • Masks underlying problems (掩蓋問題) — e.g., quality defects, poor scheduling, communication failures
  • Increases storage, handling, damage, and administrative expenses
  • Risk of obsolescence (過時) — stock may become worthless before it is used or sold
Exam trap: Safety stock and cycle stock serve different purposes. Safety stock exists because of demand/supply uncertainty; cycle stock exists because of ordering economies of scale. Do not conflate them.

The practical goal of inventory control (庫存控制) balances four simultaneous objectives:

  • Optimise reordering (優化補貨) to react to market changes in moderation
  • Determine the most productive (最有效率的) inventory mix
  • Track safety stock and distressed inventory (滯銷庫存)
  • Add to sales and operations planning (銷售與運營計劃)

2. Inventory Management Practices

ABC classification (ABC分類法) applies the 80/20 principle (二八原則, "critical few, trivial many") to rank stock-keeping units (SKUs) by annual dollar usage (年度美元用量):

Class Share of items Share of total value Control approach Production strategy Service level
A (e.g., engines) Small % ~72% Strictly controlled and counted Make-to-order Higher service level
B (e.g., brackets) Medium % ~23% Controlled reorder point and quantity Configure-to-order Medium/High service level
C (e.g., nuts & bolts) Large % ~4% Free issue Make-to-stock Lower service level

Worked example from lecture: 9 items totalling $233,450 — items 22 and 68 alone account for $170,000 (72%) and are classified A; items 27, 03, 82 form class B ($53,000, 22.7%); remaining five items form class C ($10,450, 4.4%).

Beyond ABC — a two-dimensional matrix (銷售價值 × 可預測性) extends ABC by also rating forecastability (可預測性):

  • High value + constant demand — highest priority for tight control
  • High value + sporadic demand (零星需求) — difficult to manage; may need make-to-order
  • High value + seasonal/trend demand — plan production campaigns
  • Low value + constant demand — safe to use simple reorder rules
  • Low value + sporadic demand — consider eliminating or consolidating SKUs
Exam trap: ABC is based on annual dollar usage value, not unit volume or unit price alone. A cheap but high-volume part can be class A if its total annual spend is large.

Cycle counting (循環盤點) vs. physical count (實地盤點):

  • Physical count — suspend all operations and count every SKU; disruptive and infrequent
  • Cycle count — systematically sample (系統性抽樣) a subset of SKUs during normal operations; higher frequency
  • Scheduling decisions: how often, when, which items, by whom
  • Discrepancies (差異) investigated immediately; ties to inventory accuracy (庫存準確率) — do records agree with physical count?

Technology — barcode (條碼) and RFID (無線射頻識別) enhance supply chain visibility (供應鏈可視性):

  • Tags incorporated into labels or attached to items, cartons, or pallets
  • RFID scanners throughout the supply chain track goods from factory to warehouse to store
  • Tags report an identification number; systems look up a database (often via internet) for detailed product information
  • At factory — raw materials tagged to track precise usage; finished items tagged individually or by carton/pallet
  • Sensors built into tags can monitor conditions such as heat and humidity (e.g., detecting thawed frozen food)
  • At warehouse — loading-dock scanners track every incoming item; internal scanners track shipment preparation
  • Scanning range varies: some systems scan within 1 foot; others reach 15 feet or more
  • Retail application — contents of a shopping cart can be read as it passes the checkout

3. EOQ Model — Basic Economic Order Quantity

Two fundamental inventory control questions (庫存控制問題):

  • Order quantity (訂購量) — how much to order each time
  • Stocking time (補貨時機) — when to place the order

Two review system types:

System Trigger Order quantity Order interval Decisions
Continuous review (Q-system, 連續審查) Inventory falls to reorder point R Fixed (Q) Variable How much (Q)? When (R)?
Periodic review (P-system, 定期審查) Fixed time interval T Variable (order up to target) Fixed (T) How much (Q)? How long between orders (T)?

EOQ model assumptions (假設):

  • Constant, known demand rate D (units per year)
  • Constant setup cost S per order and holding cost H per unit per year
  • Instantaneous and complete receipt of inventory
  • Known and constant lead time (前置時間)
  • No stockouts (缺貨) permitted
  • No quantity discounts

Cost structure:

  • Order/setup cost S — sum of fixed costs per order: order processing, logistics, inbound inspection/putaway, machine setup (e.g., changing dies). High S encourages larger, less frequent orders.
  • Holding cost H — all costs to keep items in inventory: capital cost (lost interest on tied-up capital), storage (warehouse rent), insurance. Often expressed as a percentage of unit purchase cost (e.g., 10%). High H discourages ordering too much.
  • Annual order cost = (D/Q) × S
  • Annual holding cost = (Q/2) × H — average inventory on hand is Q/2 because inventory drops linearly from Q to 0
  • Total cost: TC = (D/Q)S + (Q/2)H

Optimal order quantity (最優訂購量) Q* is where annual setup cost equals annual holding cost:

  • Set DS/Q = QH/2, solve: Q* = √(2DS/H) — derived by Ford W. Harris in 1913
  • Reorder point (再訂購點): R = d × L, where d = demand per day, L = lead time in days
  • Expected number of orders per year = D/Q*
  • Order cycle (訂購週期) = Q*/D (or 1 / number of orders per year)

M&S sweater example:

  • D = 100 sweaters/week; S = $5,000/order; unit cost = $400; holding rate = 1%/week → H = $4/unit/week
  • Q* = √(2 × 100 × 5,000 / 4) = 500 units
  • Order cycle = 500/100 = 5 weeks
  • Weekly ordering cost = (100/500) × $5,000 = $1,000; weekly holding cost = (500/2) × $4 = $1,000 — equal at optimum
Exam trap: At the EOQ optimum, annual setup cost equals annual holding cost. Students often forget this equality and try to minimise by calculus alone — but recognising the equality is the fastest check.

4. EPQ Model — Economic Production Quantity and Make vs. Buy

Make vs. buy break-even analysis (自製vs外購損益平衡分析):

  • If make: invest $K in capacity; produce at variable cost c₂ per unit
  • If buy: purchase at c₁ per unit (c₁ > c₂, so buying is more expensive per unit)
  • Break-even quantity (損益平衡數量) B = K / (c₁ − c₂) — produce in-house if expected volume exceeds B
  • Other considerations: storage requirements, equipment use after production halts (shadow price or salvage value), supplier quality, strategic implications of outsourcing (外包)

EPQ model — assumptions (compared to EOQ):

  • Same as EOQ (constant known demand D, constant S and H, no stockouts) plus:
  • Constant production rate (生產率) P > D (if P ≤ D you can never keep up with demand)
  • Lead time is zero; each production run replenishes inventory gradually (not instantaneously)
  • Designed for in-house production (內部生產) decisions

EPQ cost structure and optimal quantity:

  • Production phase length = t_p; net inventory build-up rate = p − d
  • Maximum inventory level (最大庫存水平): I_max = (p − d)/p × Q
  • Annual setup cost = (D/Q) × S
  • Annual holding cost = (I_max/2) × H = [(p−d)/(2p)] × Q × H
  • Total cost: TC(Q) = (D/Q)S + [(p−d)/(2p)] × QH
  • Optimal EPQ: Q* = √[2DS/H × p/(p−d)]
  • Optimal cycle time: T* = Q*/D

Dakota Electronics example:

  • D = 10,000 units/year; H = $10/unit/year; S = $100/order; d = 40 units/day; p = 200 units/day; C = $15/unit
  • EPQ = √[2(10,000)(100)/10 × 200/(200−40)] = 500 units
  • Number of lots per year = 10,000/500 = 20
  • I_max = 500 × (200−40)/200 = 400 units
  • Total cost = DC + (I_max/2)H + (D/Q)S = $150,000 + $2,000 + $2,000 = $154,000/year
  • Supplier offer: C = $15.20, S = $25 → EOQ = 224 units; TC = $154,236/year
  • Conclusion: making is cheaper by $236/year; however, make requires max inventory of 400 vs. buy 224 — storage and strategic factors also matter
Exam trap: In EPQ, maximum inventory is NOT equal to Q. It equals Q × (p−d)/p because inventory is built up gradually during the production phase, not received all at once as in EOQ.

5. EOQ with Quantity Discounts

Quantity discount (數量折扣) EOQ — same assumptions as basic EOQ except product unit cost C is a function of order quantity Q (price breaks at certain volume thresholds).

Solution procedure (解題步驟) — four steps:

  1. Start at the lowest unit cost tier. Compute EOQ using Q* = √(2DS/H) with H = (discount rate) × C.
  2. Check feasibility (可行性): EOQ must fall within the quantity range for that price tier. If infeasible (EOQ outside the range), move to the next higher price tier and repeat.
  3. Once a feasible EOQ is found, compute total cost (總成本) TC = DC + (Q/2)(0.2C) + (D/Q)S for that EOQ, where the second term is the annual holding cost (持有成本).
  4. Also compute TC at every higher price break point (價格臨界點) that yields a lower unit cost. The optimal Q is the one with the minimum total cost among all candidates.

Lecture example: D = 40,000; S = $25; holding rate = 20%:

Quantity range Unit cost EOQ Feasible? TC (if evaluated)
≥ 3,000 $2.20 2,132 No (2,132 < 3,000) Use break-point 3,000 → $88,993/yr
2,500–2,999 $2.25 2,108 No (2,108 < 2,500) Use break-point 2,500 → $90,963/yr
1,500–2,499 $2.30 2,085 Yes $92,959/yr
< 1,500 $2.35 — Not evaluated —
  • Optimal Q = 3,000 units at $2.20/unit → minimum total cost = $88,993/year
  • Note: the feasible EOQ (2,085) is NOT optimal because ordering at the 3,000 break-point saves more on purchase cost than the extra holding cost incurred
Exam trap: Always evaluate the feasible EOQ AND all higher price break-points. Do NOT stop at the feasible EOQ — a break-point with a lower unit price frequently yields a lower total cost despite higher holding costs.

6. Probabilistic EOQ — Safety Stock and Service Level

When demand during lead time (前置時間需求) fluctuates, the classic reorder point R = dL understates the stock needed. The probabilistic EOQ (概率性EOQ) model retains the EOQ formula for Q* but adjusts the reorder point.

Safety stock (安全庫存) mechanics:

  • Safety stock SS = Z × σ_L — extra inventory held as a buffer against demand spikes during lead time
  • Z = z-score corresponding to the desired service level (服務水平) — the probability of not stocking out during a lead time period
  • σ_L = standard deviation of demand during lead time
  • If daily demand variation σ_d is constant: σ_L = √L × σ_d (standard deviations combine as square root of lead time)
  • New ROP = average demand during lead time + safety stock = dL + Zσ_L
  • Higher service level → higher Z → more safety stock → higher holding cost (non-linear relationship)

Worked example:

  • D = 15,600 units/year; d = 300 units/week; σ_d = 90 units/week; S = $31.20; H = $0.10/unit/year; L = 4 weeks; SL = 98%
  • Z = 2.05 for 98% service level
  • σ_L = √4 × 90 = 180 units
  • ROP = 300 × 4 + 2.05 × 180 = 1,200 + 369 = 1,569 units
  • If safety stock is cut by 50%: new SS ≈ 185 units → Z = 185/180 ≈ 1.03 → SL ≈ 84.85%

Which inventory model to use — summary decision rules (決策規則):

  • Known demand, low variation, long lifecycle, multiple reorder opportunities → apply EOQ or EPQ
  • Demand during lead time fluctuates → use service level to set safety stock; apply probabilistic EOQ for Q* and ROP
  • Single ordering opportunity, short lifecycle, perishable or seasonal products, uncertain demand → apply Newsvendor model (報童模型) to determine optimal Q
Exam trap: σ_L is not simply L × σ_d. It is √L × σ_d because independent daily demands add in variance (方差), not standard deviation. Squaring then taking the square root is essential.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Safety stock vs. cycle stock — safety stock buffers uncertainty; cycle stock results from economies of scale. Do not mix them up.
  • ABC classification is by annual dollar usage value, not unit price or unit volume alone.
  • EOQ optimum: at Q*, annual setup cost equals annual holding cost — use this equality as a quick check.
  • EPQ maximum inventory = Q × (p−d)/p, not Q. Inventory builds gradually, not instantaneously.
  • Quantity discount: always evaluate the feasible EOQ AND every higher price break-point; the feasible EOQ is often NOT the optimal solution.
  • Safety stock formula: SS = Zσ_L where σ_L = √L × σ_d — variances add, not standard deviations; never write σ_L = L × σ_d.
  • Newsvendor model is for single-order situations (perishable/seasonal); EOQ/EPQ are for repeated ordering situations.
Top things to memorize
  1. EOQ formula: Q* = √(2DS/H); at Q* annual setup cost = annual holding cost
  2. Reorder point (basic): R = d × L; with probabilistic demand: R = dL + Zσ_L
  3. Safety stock: SS = Zσ_L; σ_L = √L × σ_d (variance addition rule)
  4. EPQ formula: Q* = √[2DS/H × p/(p−d)]; I_max = Q × (p−d)/p
  5. Total cost for EOQ: TC = (D/Q)S + (Q/2)H; for EPQ add purchase cost DC
  6. Quantity discount procedure: start lowest price, check feasibility, compute TC for feasible EOQ + all higher break-points, pick minimum
  7. ABC classification: A = small % items, ~72% of value; B = ~23%; C = ~4% of value
  8. Little's Law: I = R × T — excess inventory inflates flow time
  9. Four inventory purposes: safety (buffer uncertainty), decoupling, economies of scale, regulatory/pipeline
  10. Make vs. buy break-even: B = K/(c₁ − c₂); also consider storage, equipment reuse, supplier quality, outsourcing strategy
  11. Continuous (Q) system: fixed Q, variable interval, triggered at reorder point R; Periodic (P) system: fixed interval, variable Q
  12. Model selection rule: repeated + stable demand → EOQ/EPQ; uncertain lead-time demand → probabilistic EOQ; single order + perishable → Newsvendor

Lecture 7: Just in Time System

Just-in-Time (JIT) is a management philosophy originating from Toyota's post-WWII production system that targets the elimination of all forms of waste by producing only what is needed, when it is needed, and in the exact quantity needed. It operates as a demand-pull system that exposes hidden operational problems rather than masking them with excess inventory. Its principles now extend beyond manufacturing to supply chain delivery, purchasing, and service operations, making it one of the most influential operations management frameworks in modern industry.

1. Lean Production and the Concept of Waste

Lean production (精益生產) is a systematic focus on eliminating as much waste (浪費) as possible while operating with precision and remaining responsive to demand. It traces its roots to Henry Ford's mass production system and Toyota's continuous improvement (持續改善) efforts after World War II.

  • Value (價值) — defined by the customer based on their perception of usefulness and necessity; not defined by the producer.
  • Value-added activities (增值活動) — activities for which the customer is willing to pay.
  • Non-value-added activities / Waste (非增值活動) — activities that consume resources but add no value from the customer's perspective.
  • Business value-added activities (業務增值活動) — non-value-added but required (e.g., regulatory compliance, internal controls).
  • Customer value (顧客價值) — specifically something for which the customer is willing to pay.

Seven Forms of Waste

Form of Waste (浪費類型) Description / Example
Defects (缺陷) Extra manpower needed to inspect, rework, and repair
Overproduction (過度生產) Producing more than needed; results in extra storage cost
Inventory (庫存) Additional material handling; rework required when changes are made
Waiting (等待) Idle time caused by lack of planning, organization, and discipline
Excess motion (過多動作) Poor facility layout; materials placed too far apart
Over-processing (過度加工) Redundant approvals, extra copies, inefficient policies and procedures
Transportation (運輸) Unnecessary material movement; damage incurred during transportation
Exam trap: Students often confuse "non-value-added but required" (business value-added) with pure waste. Business value-added activities cannot simply be eliminated — they are legally or operationally necessary, unlike true waste. Do not lump them together.

2. JIT Philosophy and the Toyota Production System (TPS)

Just-in-Time (及時生產) is both a management philosophy (管理哲學) and a production control approach. Also known as the Toyota Production System (豐田生產方式, TPS), it attacks waste systematically and exposes hidden operational problems by removing the inventory buffer that conceals them.

  • Pull system (拉式系統) — execution is initiated in response to actual customer orders, not forecasts.
  • Push system (推式系統) — execution is initiated in anticipation of customer orders based on forecasts.
  • JIT achieves streamlined production (流線型生產) through small lot sizes, stable environments, and total quality control (全面品質管理).
  • Requires continuous employee participation (員工參與) and industrial engineering basics.

Scope: JIT can exist anywhere in the supply chain

  • JIT production / manufacturing — also known as TPS
  • JIT inventory (及時庫存)
  • JIT purchasing (及時採購)
  • JIT delivery (及時交貨)
  • JIT supply chain management (及時供應鏈管理)
  • JIT service (及時服務)

Guiding Principles of TPS

  • Elimination of waste — the primary aim, targeting all 7 forms.
  • Quality at the source (源頭品質) — workers check and correct quality at their own workstation.
  • JIT production — small lot sizes coordinated via Kanban.
  • Minimize setup time (最小化換模時間) — makes small lot sizes economically feasible.
  • Respect for people (尊重員工) — lifetime employment (終身僱用), cooperative labor-management relations, strong supplier relationships.

TPS Success Factors (Approaches, Methods, Techniques)

Aim Approaches to overcoming barriers Methods for eliminating waste Techniques for lean synchronization
Eliminate all waste Involve everyone; Continuous improvement Pull control; Set-up reduction; Levelled scheduling; Mixed modelling; Lean supply Layout; Visible flow; Simplify technology; Kanbans
Exam trap: JIT and TPS are often used interchangeably, but TPS is the specific Toyota implementation. JIT is the broader philosophy. Also, the pull system responds to actual orders — not forecasts. A push system uses forecasts.

3. Inventory as a Problem-Hider and JIT Exposure Logic

Inventory acts like water in a river: it hides rocks (operational problems) beneath the surface. The JIT approach deliberately reduces inventory (water level) so that problems become visible and can be resolved.

Problems Hidden by Excess Inventory (過多庫存)

  • Machine downtime (機器停機)
  • Work-in-process queues / banks (在製品積壓)
  • Engineering design redundancies (工程設計冗餘)
  • Change orders (更改訂單)
  • Paperwork backlogs (文書積壓)
  • Decision backlogs (決策積壓)
  • Inspection backlogs (檢查積壓)

Reducing inventory exposes: defective deliveries (缺陷交貨), productivity problems (生產力問題), downtime, inaccurate quantities (不準確數量), and unstable standards (不穩定標準). Once visible, operations management can systematically eliminate each problem.

Exam trap: JIT does not simply remove inventory to save cost — its deeper purpose is to surface hidden problems so they can be fixed. Treating inventory reduction as purely a cost-saving measure misses the diagnostic logic of JIT.

4. JIT Implementation Requirements

Implementing JIT requires coordinated changes across five areas: process design, quality improvement, production scheduling, vendor relationships, and inventory/product design.

Five Implementation Areas

Area Key Requirements
Redesign flow process (重新設計流程) Link operations; balance workstation capacities (平衡工作站產能); redesign layout for better flow; emphasize preventive maintenance (預防性維護)
Improve process quality (提升製程品質) Worker empowerment (員工授權); statistical quality control (統計品質控制); poka-yoke (防錯裝置) fail-safe methods; automatic inspection (自動檢查)
Stabilize production schedules (穩定生產計劃) Level schedule (平準化排程); underutilize capacity; establish freeze windows (凍結期 — no schedule changes for a set period)
Improve vendor relationships (改善供應商關係) Reduce lead time (縮短前置時間); frequent deliveries (頻繁交貨); information sharing (資訊共享)
Implement pull system (實施拉式系統) Kanban system to control flow

Improve Product Design (改善產品設計)

  • Standard product configuration — modular design (模組化設計)
  • Standardize and reduce number of parts (標準化並減少零件數)
  • Integrated product and process design — concurrent engineering approach (並行工程)
  • Robust design for quality (穩健設計)

Reduce Inventory Level (降低庫存水平)

  • Reduce lot sizes (減少批量)
  • Reduce setup / changeover time (縮短換模時間)

5. Kanban System

Kanban (看板) means card, sign, or instruction card in Japanese. It is the mechanism by which JIT implements demand-pull control across a plant and its supplier network, regulating inventory levels and production quantities through the physical movement of cards.

  • Inventory level control — inventory is controlled by the number of Kanban cards in circulation; more cards = more WIP allowed.
  • Two-kanban system — uses two card types: production instruction kanban (生產指示看板) and retrieval / withdrawal kanban (取料看板).
  • Production instruction kanban — attached to parts at a machine center; removed when an operator retrieves parts, authorizing production of exactly the quantity indicated.
  • Retrieval kanban — carried by operator to retrieve replacement parts from upstream; removed when an operator uses parts at the assembly line.
  • Only the exact number of parts indicated on the kanban are produced — this is the discipline that prevents overproduction.
  • Kanban coordinates the work flow for the entire plant and deliveries from suppliers (e.g., FAS → Fab-1, Fab-2, Fab-3 → Suppliers chain).
  • Production of small lot sizes (小批量) is a prerequisite for Kanban to function effectively.

Push vs. Pull — Formal Definitions

System Trigger for Execution Basis
Push system (推式系統) Anticipated customer orders Forecasts (預測)
Pull system (拉式系統) Actual customer orders Real demand (實際需求)
Exam trap: A Kanban card does not authorize unlimited production — it authorizes production of the exact quantity written on the card. The total inventory in the system is capped by the total number of cards. Increasing cards increases WIP; reducing cards forces problem-solving.

6. Small Lot Sizes, Levelled Scheduling, and JIT Resilience

JIT production targets small batch sizes to reduce work-in-process (在製品) inventory and stabilize work schedules. This is only feasible when setup / changeover times (換模時間) are drastically reduced.

Setup Time Comparison (Hood and Fender Press Machines)

Metric Toyota USA Sweden Germany
Setup time 10 minutes 6 hours 4 hours 4 hours
Setups per day 3 1 — ~½
Lot size 1 day* 10 days 1 month —

* For low-demand items (<1000 per month), Toyota's lot size can be as large as 7 days.

Levelled Scheduling (平準化排程) vs. Large Batches

  • Large batch example: Batch A=600, B=200, C=200 with capacity 250/day — runs long uninterrupted runs of A then B then C, creating high WIP inventory peaks.
  • Levelled scheduling example: Batch A=150, B=50, C=50 — products A, B, C are mixed every cycle, smoothing inventory and workload.
  • Level production or delivery schedules require no significant loss of capacity or added cost.
  • JIT delivery uses levelled delivery schedules to reduce finished goods inventory (成品庫存).

JIT Limitations and Supply Chain Resilience (供應鏈韌性)

  • COVID-19 disruptions (e.g., U.S. paper towel demand spike 160%+ in early 2020) exposed JIT's vulnerability to sudden demand shocks.
  • Six resilience strategies (Gartner): Nearshoring (近岸外包); platform/product/plant harmonization; manufacturing network diversification (製造網絡多元化); inventory and capacity buffers (庫存與產能緩衝); ecosystem partnerships (生態系統夥伴關係).
  • Supply chain resilience factors (time-series model, Melnyk et al. 2014):
    • TD — Time of Disturbance (干擾發生時間)
    • TO — Time of Onset (影響開始時間)
    • TC — Time of Climax (最低點時間); RC — Response at Climax
    • TP — Turning Point (轉折點); RT — Response at Turning Point
    • TR — Time of Recovery (恢復時間); RR — Response at Recovery
  • Resilience matrix: firms can be Hardy (高抵抗力 + 高恢復力), Vulnerable but Responsive (低抵抗力 + 高恢復力), or fall in other quadrants based on Resistance Capacity vs. Recovery Capacity.
  • "Only the fittest will survive" (Darwin) — geopolitical uncertainty makes supply chain resilience planning essential.
Exam trap: Levelled scheduling is often mistaken for capacity reduction. The lecture explicitly states that level schedules require no significant loss of capacity or added cost. The benefit is inventory reduction, not capacity sacrifice.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Business value-added (non-value-added but required) activities are NOT the same as waste — they cannot simply be eliminated.
  • JIT and TPS are related but not identical: TPS is Toyota's specific implementation of the JIT philosophy.
  • Pull system = triggered by actual customer orders; Push system = triggered by forecasts. Do not mix up.
  • JIT reduces inventory primarily to expose and fix hidden problems, not merely to cut storage costs.
  • Kanban cards authorize exactly the quantity written — not unlimited production. Total cards cap total WIP.
  • Levelled scheduling does not sacrifice capacity — it maintains throughput while smoothing inventory and workload.

Lecture 6: Supply Chain Management

Supply chain management (供應鏈管理) coordinates the flow of goods, information, and finances from raw material procurement to final customer delivery. The central challenge is aligning demand signals with supply capacity so that costs stay low and customers stay satisfied. Two structural decisions dominate the field: where to place the push-pull boundary, and how tightly to integrate the chain's tiers. Technology increasingly enables the real-time visibility and automation that make both decisions executable.

1. Supply Chain Management Fundamentals

Supply chain management (SCM) (供應鏈管理) encompasses the management of goods (貨物), information (資訊), and financial flows (資金流動) related to a product or service, from raw material procurement (原材料採購) to final delivery.

  • Physical flow (實物流) — typically downstream (下游): Raw Materials → Manufacturer (製造商) → Distributor (分銷商) → Retailer (零售商) → Customer
  • Cash flow (現金流) — typically upstream (上游): from customer back to supplier
  • Information flow (資訊流) — bidirectional: both upstream and downstream

Typical SCM activities include procurement (採購), product lifecycle management (產品生命週期管理), supply chain planning (供應鏈規劃), logistics (物流), distribution (配送), order management (訂單管理), and global coordination (全球協調) with systems integration (系統整合).

  • Major objective: connect demand (需求) to supply strategy (供應策略) and to operations (運營)
  • Sales & Operations Planning (S&OP) (銷售與運營計劃) acts as the "conductor" of the business

S&OP integrates three plans:

  • Demand Plan (需求計劃) — what we can SELL
  • Supply Plan (供應計劃) — what we can MAKE/DO
  • Financial Plan (財務計劃) — what it means for PROFIT & CASH

S&OP process steps:

  1. Data gathering (數據收集)
  2. Demand Planning (需求規劃)
  3. Supply Planning (供應規劃)
  4. Reconciliation Meeting (協調會議) — the Pre-S&OP
  5. Executive S&OP Meeting (高管S&OP會議)
Exam trap: Cash flow runs upstream (from customer to supplier), not downstream like the physical product flow. Information flow is the only bidirectional flow.

2. The Bullwhip Effect

The bullwhip effect (長鞭效應) describes how small fluctuations in customer demand get progressively amplified as orders move upstream through the supply chain — from retailer (零售商) to wholesaler (批發商) to distributor (分銷商) to factory (工廠/啤酒廠).

The Beer Game (啤酒遊戲) is a role-playing simulation illustrating the bullwhip effect. Four roles: Retailer → Wholesaler → Distributor (Bottler) → Factory (Brewery). Key constraint: each team sees only orders from the immediately downstream link; cross-team communication is often forbidden. Each team's job:

  • Receive shipments from upstream supplier (after a delay)
  • Receive orders from downstream customer
  • Ship beer from inventory to fill orders; backorder (延遲交付) if inventory is insufficient
  • Place replenishment orders to upstream supplier
  • Goal: minimize total costs = inventory (storage costs) + backorders (shortage costs 缺貨成本)

Common causes of the bullwhip effect:

  • Forecast errors (預測誤差)
  • Poor demand information sharing (需求資訊共享不足)
  • Overreacting to supplier rationing (供應商配給) and buyer gaming behavior (買家博弈行為)
  • Uncertainty caused by long lead times (長交貨期)
  • Price fluctuations (價格波動) and uneven demand (需求不均)

Possible solutions to the bullwhip effect:

  • Streamline the supply chain to coordinate a better supply plan
  • Improved communication with technology (科技改善溝通)
  • Optimize inventory management (庫存管理)
  • Consistent, smaller order sizes (穩定、較小的訂單量)
  • Minimize promotions and discounts to stabilize demand (穩定需求)

Key takeaways from the Beer Game:

  • Systems thinking (系統思維) over blame: shift from "Who messed up?" to "How is the system causing this behavior?"
  • Dangers of local optimization (局部優化的危害): each node optimizing individually worsens the whole
  • Critical role of information & communication: shared data, S&OP, and supply chain visibility (供應鏈可見性) are essential
  • Time delays are a killer: lead time (交貨期) amplifies variance at every tier
Exam trap: The bullwhip effect is not caused by actual demand being volatile — daily demand in the Beer Game follows a stable Poisson distribution (mean = 1 unit per retailer). The amplification is entirely systemic, caused by information lag and independent decision-making, not by real demand swings.

3. Push vs. Pull Supply Chain Models

Supply chains choose between two fundamental operating philosophies based on whether production is triggered by forecasts or by actual customer demand.

The push-pull boundary (推拉邊界) is the strategic point where the chain transitions from forecast-driven to demand-driven production. Three archetypes:

  • Make-to-Stock (MTS) (按庫存生產) — pure push; example: Coca-Cola (finished goods held in stock)
  • Make-to-Order (MTO) (按訂單生產) — pure pull; example: Boeing Aircraft (component fabrication triggered by order)
  • Assemble-to-Order (ATO) (按訂單裝配) — hybrid; example: Dell (components pushed, final assembly pulled)

Real-world hybrid examples:

  • Amazon: high-volume predictable items (books, electronics) → push/MTS stored in fulfillment centers; low-volume items → pull, suppliers ship directly
  • Zara (fashion): pushes generic fabric based on forecasts; pulls final garment by cutting and dyeing after seeing early sales trends — highly responsive hybrid
  • Chip manufacturers (Intel, TSMC): push standard wafer fabrication; pull final customization and packaging based on specific customer orders
  • Influenza vaccine: WHO detects antigenic variants, proposes formulation by February; 5-6 month production process begins; final fill and release is demand-driven

Fundamental rule of thumb: Push = standardized, high-volume, predictable components; Pull = customized, low-volume, unpredictable final products. The optimal boundary is a strategic choice, not a given.

Exam trap: Zara is often cited as a pure pull company, but it is actually a hybrid. Fabric (generic, low-cost) is pushed; the final garment (cut, dye, assembly) is pulled. Do not classify companies as purely push or pull without checking where their boundary sits.

4. Supply Chain Integration

Supply chain integration (供應鏈整合) refers to the degree of coordination and ownership across chain tiers. Two major forms:

  • Vertical integration (垂直整合) — insourcing through mergers & acquisitions (併購)
    • Forward integration (前向整合): expand toward the customer side
    • Backward integration (後向整合): expand toward the supplier side
  • Virtual integration (虛擬整合) — outsourcing (外包) coordinated by IT; partners remain independent but operate as one chain

IBM case (1980s): IBM outsourced PC design and manufacturing to external component suppliers using an open architecture (開放架構). This accelerated innovation and lowered costs initially, but competitors could access the same components and replicate the product. Key lesson: outsourcing core components of a product can erode competitive advantage (競爭優勢) if those components become the source of differentiation (差異化).

Decision logic for outsourcing (外包決策邏輯) — keep in-house if all three conditions hold:

  1. Activity is of strategic importance (具戰略重要性)
  2. Company has specialized knowledge (專業知識)
  3. Company's performance is superior (績效卓越) OR significant operations improvement is likely
Exam trap: The outsourcing decision tree requires ALL three conditions (strategic importance, specialized knowledge, superior performance) to favor keeping an activity in-house. If even one is absent, outsourcing may be appropriate — but "significant performance improvement likely" can override poor current performance.

Lecture 2: Demand Management and Forecasting – Forecasting Models

This lecture covers the core quantitative models used to forecast demand over time. Starting from simple averages, it builds toward exponential smoothing and linear regression, each model adding a layer of sophistication to handle trends and noise. The central throughline is the trade-off between responsiveness and stability: no single parameter choice is universally optimal, and selecting the right model requires understanding what pattern the demand data actually exhibits.

1. Simple Moving Average

Simple moving average (簡單移動平均) is appropriate when demand (需求) is stable — no rapid growth, no decline, and no seasonal fluctuations (季節性波動). The forecast for period t is the arithmetic mean of the n most recent actual demand values.

  • Formula: Ft = (At−1 + At−2 + … + At−n) / n, where A is actual demand and n is the period length (期數)
  • Example — 3-week moving average for week 4: F4 = (1000 + 1400 + 800) / 3 = 1067
  • Example — 3-week moving average for week 5: F5 = (1500 + 1000 + 1400) / 3 = 1300
  • Example — 9-week moving average for week 10: F10 = (1700 + 1300 + 1800 + 1700 + 1500 + 1300 + 1500 + 1400 + 800) / 9 = 1567 (rounded from 1467 per table entry; table shows 1467)
Period length (n) Responsiveness (響應性) Smoothing effect (平滑效果) Best for
Short (e.g. 3-week) High — reacts quickly to recent sales Low Rapidly changing demand
Long (e.g. 9-week) Low High — smooths out fluctuations in the long run Stable demand, noise reduction
Exam trap: Simple moving average treats all n periods with equal weight. It does NOT give more importance to more recent data — that is the role of weighted moving average or exponential smoothing.

2. Weighted Moving Average

Weighted moving average (加權移動平均) assigns different weights (權重) to each past period, allowing the most recent data to carry more influence. All weights must sum to 1.

  • Formula: Ft = w1At−1 + w2At−2 + … + wnAt−n, where Σwi = 1
  • Typical weight assignment: most recent week gets the highest weight; older periods get progressively lower weights
  • Example — 4-week WMA with weights 0.4 / 0.3 / 0.2 / 0.1 (most-recent to oldest): F5 = 0.4×1500 + 0.3×1000 + 0.2×1400 + 0.1×800 = 1260
  • Seasonality (季節性) should also be reflected in weight selection
Method Weight on past periods Key parameter(s)
Simple Moving Average Equal (1/n each) Period length n
Weighted Moving Average User-defined, sum = 1, recent > old Period length n + individual weights wi

Drawbacks (缺點) shared by both SMA and WMA:

  • Difficult to decide the right parameters: period length (n) and weights (wi) are chosen subjectively
  • Must continuously carry and store a rolling window of historical data (歷史數據)
Exam trap: In WMA, the weights must sum exactly to 1. If a question gives weights that do not sum to 1, the formula is invalid. Also, "most recent" week uses weight w1 applied to At−1, not At — the current period's actual demand is not yet known when forming the forecast.

3. Simple Exponential Smoothing (SES)

Simple exponential smoothing (簡單指數平滑法) provides an exponentially weighted moving average of all previously observed values. It is appropriate for data with no predictable upward or downward trend (趨勢). The aim is to estimate the current level and use it as the forecast of the next period.

Core formula and interpretation:

  • Ft = αAt−1 + (1 − α)Ft−1
  • α (smoothing constant 平滑常數): weight on most recent actual observation; (1 − α): weight on previous forecast
  • Equivalent form (error-correction form 誤差修正形式): Ft = Ft−1 + α(At−1 − Ft−1) — new forecast = old forecast + fraction of last period's forecast error
  • Expanded form shows Ft is the weighted moving average of ALL past observations with exponentially declining weights: Ft = αAt−1 + α(1−α)At−2 + α(1−α)2At−3 + … + α(1−α)t−2A1
  • Unlike SMA/WMA, SES does not require storing a fixed window of raw historical data — only Ft−1 and α are needed each period

Effect of α value:

  • High α (e.g. 0.5): more responsive to recent demand, but forecast overshoots after a change in direction
  • Low α (e.g. 0.1): smoother, but forecasts lag behind actual sales data for longer
  • Optimal α is identified by minimizing forecasting errors — typically MAD (Mean Absolute Deviation 平均絕對偏差) or MSE (Mean Squared Error 均方誤差)
  • Adaptive smoothing (自適應平滑) uses variable smoothing constants based on the latest percent of error: αt+1 = f(αt) adjusted by recent error signal

Initialization and worked example:

  • Initialize: set F1 = A1 (use actual demand of first period as first forecast)
  • Example with α = 0.5: Period 1 A=79, F1=79; Period 2 A=84, F2=79; Period 3 A=83, F3=79+0.5(84−79)=81.50; Period 4 A=81, F4=82.25; Period 5 A=98, F5=81.63; Period 6 A=100, F6=89.81; F7=94.91; MAD=6.86
  • Numerical example (simpler): Fprev=1050, Aprev=1000, α=0.05 → Fthis month = 1050 + 0.05(1000−1050) = 1047.5
  • MAD formula: MAD = Σ|At − Ft| / n

Why exponential smoothing is widely adopted:

  • Surprisingly accurate relative to complexity
  • Easy to formulate and compute
  • Requires minimal computer storage — only the previous forecast and α are needed, not a full history window
  • Easy to test model performance (e.g. compare MAD across different α values)
Exam trap: A higher α does NOT always mean a better forecast. High α makes the model more reactive but causes overshooting after demand reversal. Low α lags persistently. The optimal α must be found by minimizing forecast error metrics, not by choosing intuitively.

4. Exponential Smoothing with Trend Adjustment (Holt's Method)

Holt's method (霍爾特法) extends SES to handle data with a trend component (趨勢成分). It maintains two smoothing equations: one for the level (水平) F and one for the trend T, combining them into a trend-adjusted forecast FIT.

  • Two smoothing parameters: α (for level) and δ (delta, for trend 趨勢平滑常數)
  • Level update: Ft = FITt−1 + α(At−1 − FITt−1)
  • Trend update: Tt = Tt−1 + δ(Ft − FITt−1)
  • Trend-adjusted forecast: FITt = Ft + Tt
  • Initialization: F1 = A1 and T1 = A2 − A1

Worked example (α = 0.5, δ = 0.3):

  • Week 1: A=31; Week 2: A=40, F2=31.00, T2=9.00, FIT2=40.00, error=0.00
  • Week 3: A=43, F3=40.00, T3=9.00, FIT3=49.00, error=6.00
  • Week 4: A=52, F4=46.00, T4=8.10, FIT4=54.10, error=2.10
  • Week 5: A=49, F5=53.05, T5=7.79, FIT5=60.84, error=11.84
  • Week 6: A=64, F6=54.92, T6=6.01, FIT6=60.93, error=3.07
  • Week 7: A=58, F7=62.46, T7=6.47, FIT7=68.93, error=10.93
  • Week 8: A=68, F8=63.47, T8=4.83, FIT8=68.30, error=0.30
  • Week 9 forecast: FIT9=72.94; MAD=4.89
  • Step detail for week 3: F3 = FIT2 + α(A2−FIT2) = 40 + 0.5(40−40) = 40; T3 = T2 + δ(F3−FIT2) = 9 + 0.3(40−40) = 9

Exam trap: In Holt's method, the trend T is updated using (Ft − FITt−1), not (At − FITt−1). Do not substitute actual demand into the trend update equation.

5. Simple Linear Regression for Forecasting

Simple linear regression (簡單線性回歸) is a statistical technique used to describe the relationship between a dependent variable (因變量) y and one independent variable (自變量) x. When applied to time-series demand, x = time period t.

  • Regression line: y = b0 + b1x, where b0 is the intercept (截距) and b1 is the slope (斜率)
  • In real data, relationships are rarely exact — points scatter around the line
  • Least squares minimization (最小二乘法): minimize Σ(yi − ŷi)2 (sum of squared residuals 殘差平方和) — gives a unique, objective solution
  • Residual (殘差) / "fit error": difference between each observed y and the fitted ŷ; positive and negative deviations ideally cancel out, but squaring removes sign ambiguity

Least squares estimators:

  • Slope: b = (Σty − n·t̄·ȳ) / (Σt2 − n·t̄2)
  • Intercept: a = ȳ − b·t̄
  • Both can be computed by hand or via Excel

  • A higher α in SES does not guarantee a better forecast — it increases responsiveness but causes overshooting after direction changes. Optimal α minimizes MAD or MSE.

Lecture 1: Demand Management and Forecasting

Demand management sits at the heart of supply chain operations: a firm must decide how much to sell, at what price, and how much capacity to maintain — all before customer orders arrive. Forecasting (預測) provides the quantitative foundation for these decisions, translating past demand patterns into actionable estimates of the future. The lecture covers what makes forecasting hard, how to measure forecast accuracy, and which quantitative models suit which data patterns.

1. Demand Management

Demand management (需求管理) is the process of optimizing customer demand against available capacity to maximize a company's profit. It has two dimensions:

  • Revenue side — how much to sell and at what price
  • Cost side — how much capacity (產能) to maintain and at what cost

Two strategic postures exist:

  • Passive role (被動角色) — forecast and respond to customer demand as it arrives
  • Active role (主動角色) — influence or prioritize customer demand (e.g., promotions, pricing, rationing) before actual sales occur

The Chinese Pharmaceuticals (HK) case illustrates why accurate forecasting matters. Key operational constraints:

CharacteristicDetail

Supply lead time (前置時間)4–6 weeks from Yunnan supplier
Production lead time8 weeks at Hong Kong contract manufacturer
Demand patternSeasonal and festive spikes; frequent promotional sales

Warehouse spaceLimited

Exam trap: "Demand management" is not the same as "demand forecasting." Forecasting is a tool within demand management; management also includes active tactics like pricing and promotions to shape demand.

2. Principles of Demand Forecasting

Five foundational principles govern any forecasting exercise:

  • No forecast is perfect — all forecasts carry error (誤差); plan for it
  • Causal continuity assumed — forecasts assume the relationships driving past demand continue into the future
  • Groups more accurate than individuals — aggregate (總量) forecasts are more reliable than individual-item forecasts
  • Accuracy decreases with horizon — the further into the future, the less accurate the forecast
  • Complexity not always better — good forecasts do not always require complex (複雜) models; simple models often suffice

A forecasting process (預測流程) integrates three input streams:

  • Historical demand realisations: y₁ … yₜ
  • Historical values of causal variables (因果變數): x₁ … xₜ
  • Subjective expert opinions (主觀意見) about the future

These inputs feed into either judgment-based or quantitative model families to produce forecast ŷₜ₊₁.

Exam trap: Aggregate forecasts are more accurate than individual forecasts — not the other way around. Exam questions sometimes invert this to test awareness.

3. Measuring Forecast Accuracy

Forecast error (預測誤差) is defined as: Error = Actual − Forecast. Three higher-level measures build on this:

MetricFormulaWhat it captures
MAD (平均絕對偏差, Mean Absolute Deviation)Σ|Actual − Forecast| / nTypical magnitude of error; approximates 0.8 × σ under normality
MSE (均方誤差, Mean Squared Error)Σ(Actual − Forecast)² / nPenalises large errors more heavily
MAPE (平均絕對百分比誤差, Mean Absolute Percent Error)Σ|PE| / n, where PE = (Actual − Forecast) / ActualScale-free accuracy; expressed as a percentage

Bias (偏差) is measured via the Cumulative Sum of Errors: Σ(Actual − Forecast). A consistently positive or negative cumulative sum signals systematic over- or under-forecasting.

Tracking Signal (追蹤信號)

The tracking signal (TS) combines bias and MAD into a single alarm metric:

  • TS = Σ(Actual − Forecast) / MAD
  • A TS near zero means random errors are cancelling; a large |TS| signals systematic bias (系統性偏差)
  • Control limit (控制限): most companies set ±3.75 MAD units (equivalent to a 3-sigma limit under normality, since 1 MAD ≈ 0.8 σ)
  • Procedure: calculate TS each period; if |TS| exceeds the threshold, flag the forecast for review

4. Qualitative vs. Quantitative Forecasting

Qualitative forecasting (定性預測) relies on expert judgment when historical data are scarce or unavailable.

  • When to use: new products, long-term technology or environmental shifts
  • Methods: market research (市場調研), historical analogy (歷史類比), panel consensus (專家小組共識), Delphi method (德爾菲法)
  • Advantages: usable without historical data
  • Limitations: subjective (主觀), inconsistent across forecasters

Quantitative forecasting (定量預測) is data-driven and assumes past patterns continue.

  • When to use: sufficient historical data exist and patterns are expected to persist
  • Advantages: objective, fully reproducible by any forecaster, forecasts can be updated automatically
  • Limitations: requires adequate historical data to build a reliable model
  • Associative methods (關聯性方法): simple linear regression Y = a + bX; multiple regression models
  • Time series methods (時間序列方法): moving average, exponential smoothing, trend and seasonal models

5. Time Series Components

Every time series (時間序列) can be decomposed into four components:

ComponentClassificationDefinitionCauseDurationForecasting use
Trend (趨勢)SystematicLong-term upward or downward movementTechnology, population, wealth changesSeveral yearsTrend model
Seasonal (季節性)SystematicRegular periodic fluctuations within each 12-month periodWeather, social customs, religious eventsWithin 12 months (monthly/quarterly)Seasonal model
Cyclical (週期性)SystematicRepeating up-and-down swings through four phases: peak → contraction → trough → expansionCombinations of macro-economic factorsUsually 2–10 years, varying intensityNot often used for near-term sales forecasting
Irregular (不規則)UnsystematicErratic residual fluctuations after removing systematic effectsRandom variation; unforeseen events (strikes, hurricanes, floods)Short, non-repeatingCaptured as noise

Common recognisable patterns:

  • Purely random error (純隨機誤差) — no recognisable pattern
  • Seasonal pattern (季節模式) — regular periodic peaks and troughs
  • Increasing linear trend (遞增線性趨勢) — consistent upward slope
  • Seasonal pattern plus linear growth — combined oscillation and rising baseline
Exam trap: The cyclical component is often confused with the seasonal component. Seasonal patterns repeat within a fixed 12-month window; cyclical swings span 2–10 years and vary in intensity and length.
Exam Traps Summary
  • "Demand management" encompasses both forecasting and active demand-shaping tactics; forecasting alone is not demand management.
  • Aggregate forecasts are more accurate than individual-item forecasts — not the reverse.
  • A low MAD alone does not confirm an unbiased forecast; always verify with the tracking signal. Method 2 in the example had the same final MAD as Method 1 but a TS of 5.00 (exceeded ±3.75 limit).
  • Simple moving average and simple exponential smoothing handle stationary data only; applying them to trended or seasonal data produces systematically biased forecasts.
  • Cyclical ≠ Seasonal: seasonal repeats within 12 months; cyclical spans 2–10 years with variable duration and intensity.
