Lecture 19: Cell Division Cycle

The cell cycle is a tightly regulated process controlling when cells divide, driven by coordinated activation and inactivation of cyclin-dependent kinases (Cdks) bound to cyclins (细胞周期蛋白). Cells make critical decisions at checkpoints—particularly the G1/S restriction point and the G2/M checkpoint—where they assess readiness for DNA replication and mitosis. Failure of these checkpoints contributes to cancer development, making cell cycle control central to understanding both normal development and malignant transformation.

Overview of the Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of four phases: G1 (gap 1), S (synthesis), G2 (gap 2), and M (mitosis). G1, S, and G2 together comprise interphase, during which the cell grows and duplicates its DNA. The cell spends most of its life in G1, where it accumulates the size and resources needed for DNA replication. Cells can also exit the cell cycle into a quiescent state called G0 (G-zero), where they remain metabolically active but not preparing to divide.

Key terms: interphase (分裂间期), G1/S transition, S phase checkpoints, ploidy (细胞核DNA含量), doubling time.

Cyclin-Cdk Regulation

Cyclins (细胞周期蛋白) are regulatory proteins whose levels oscillate during the cell cycle, while Cdks (细胞周期依赖激酶) are catalytic subunits that become active only when bound to cyclins. Different cyclin-Cdk complexes drive each transition: Cyclin E-Cdk2 drives G1/S, Cyclin A-Cdk2 drives S phase, and Cyclin B-Cdk1 drives G2/M. Cdk activation requires not only cyclin binding but also phosphorylation by the Cdk-activating kinase (CAK) and dephosphorylation by Cdc25 phosphatase. Cdk inhibitors (Cki proteins) block Cdk activity and create checkpoints.

Exam trap: Cyclin presence alone doesn't activate Cdks—phosphorylation and dephosphorylation events are required.

Key terms: cyclin-Cdk complexes, CAK (Cdk-activating kinase), Cdc25 phosphatase, CDK inhibitors (Cki).

The G1/S Checkpoint (Restriction Point)

Once a cell passes the restriction point (限制点) early in G1, it becomes committed to completing S phase and cell division, regardless of external signals. This checkpoint is controlled by the retinoblastoma (Rb) protein and the E2F transcription factor. Rb binds E2F in G1, repressing S-phase genes. When Cyclin E-Cdk2 phosphorylates Rb, E2F is released and activates genes needed for DNA replication, including DNA polymerase and thymidine kinase. Mutations that inactivate Rb or p16 (a Cdk inhibitor) cause cells to bypass this checkpoint, leading to uncontrolled proliferation.

Exam trap: The restriction point is in early G1, not at the G1/S boundary—cells can still respond to stop signals before reaching it.

Key terms: restriction point, Rb protein (视网膜母细胞瘤蛋白), E2F transcription factor, p16 (CDK inhibitor).

The G2/M Checkpoint and Mitosis

The G2/M checkpoint ensures DNA has been fully replicated and undamaged before mitosis begins. Cdc25 phosphatase activates Cyclin B-Cdk1 by dephosphorylating it; this complex drives entry into mitosis. If DNA is damaged, p53 accumulates and activates p21, a Cdk inhibitor, halting progression. In mitosis, Cyclin B-Cdk1 coordinates events including nuclear envelope breakdown, chromosome condensation, spindle assembly, and cytokinesis. After metaphase, the anaphase-promoting complex (APC) ubiquitinates Cyclin B, targeting it for degradation, which allows Cyclin B-Cdk1 activity to drop and mitosis to complete.

Key terms: G2/M checkpoint, Cdc25 phosphatase, APC (anaphase-promoting complex), p21, p53.

Checkpoint Control and DNA Damage Response

When DNA damage is detected, sensor proteins (ATM, ATR) activate p53, a transcription factor that induces p21 expression. p21 binds to Cyclin-Cdk complexes, inactivating them and pausing the cycle. This delay allows DNA repair machinery to fix lesions. If damage is irreparable, p53 can trigger apoptosis through genes like BAX. Cells with mutated p53 lose this checkpoint control and can divide despite DNA damage—a hallmark of cancer. Cancer-causing mutations in BRCA1, BRCA2, and mismatch repair genes also compromise checkpoint integrity.

Exam trap: p53 mutations don't cause tumors directly; they prevent detection and arrest that would block tumors from forming.

Key terms: ATM/ATR kinases, p53 ("guardian of the genome"), BAX, BRCA1/BRCA2, mismatch repair.

Mitosis and Cytokinesis in Detail

Mitosis is divided into prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase. In prophase, chromosomes condense, the centrosome duplicates and migrates to poles, and the nuclear envelope breaks down. Sister chromatids are held together at the centromere by cohesin proteins (粘聚蛋白). In metaphase, chromosomes align at the cell equator. Anaphase begins when separase cleaves cohesins, allowing sister chromatids to segregate toward opposite poles. Cytokinesis (usually completing in telophase) divides the cytoplasm through a contractile ring of actin and myosin. Errors—such as cohesin failure or aneuploidy—can lead to chromosomal instability and cancer.

Key terms: prophase, metaphase, anaphase, telophase, separase, cohesins, centromere, aneuploidy.

Summary of Exam Traps:
  • Cyclin presence alone doesn't activate Cdks—phosphorylation/dephosphorylation is essential.
  • The restriction point is in early G1, not at G1/S.
  • p53 mutations prevent checkpoint control, they don't cause tumors directly.
  • Cohesin cleavage by separase is required for sister chromatid separation in anaphase.
  • Cells can remain in G0 indefinitely; it is reversible with appropriate signals.
  • CDK inhibitors like p16 and p21 create checkpoints by preventing Cdk activation.

Top Things to Memorize

  1. Cyclin E-Cdk2 + Rb phosphorylation drives G1/S transition by releasing E2F.
  2. Cyclin B-Cdk1 is the master regulator of mitosis; its degradation by APC ends mitosis.
  3. p53 activation by DNA damage leads to p21 induction, which inhibits Cyclin-Cdks and halts the cycle.
  4. Cdc25 phosphatase activates Cyclin B-Cdk1; Wee1 kinase inactivates it (dual control).
  5. The restriction point (early G1) is where cells commit to division; once passed, external signals no longer block S phase.
  6. Cohesin proteins hold sister chromatids together; separase cleavage in anaphase allows segregation.
  7. ATM and ATR kinases sense DNA damage and phosphorylate p53.
  8. G0 phase is reversible quiescence; cells can re-enter the cycle with appropriate signals.
  9. Cancer mutations often target checkpoint proteins (Rb, p53, Cki) or DNA repair genes (BRCA1/2).
  10. Aneuploidy results from cohesin failure or checkpoint defects; it drives tumor evolution.

Exam-style Questions (Preview)

Q1: How does Rb inactivation lead to uncontrolled cell division?
A: Inactivated Rb cannot bind and repress E2F, so S-phase genes are constitutively active.

Q2: Why is p53 called the "guardian of the genome"?
A: p53 detects DNA damage and triggers either cell cycle arrest or apoptosis to prevent division of damaged cells.

Q3: What is the role of separase in mitosis?
A: Separase cleaves cohesin proteins, allowing sister chromatids to separate during anaphase.

Q4: How does a cell distinguish G1 from G0?
A: G1 cells are preparing for S phase; G0 cells are quiescent but can re-enter if stimulated. G0 is reversible; G1 is not.

Q5: Why can Cyclin B-Cdk1 activation be called "point of no return"?
A: Once Cyclin B-Cdk1 is active, the cell is committed to mitosis and cannot reverse even if damaged.

Lecture 20: Sexual Reproduction & Genetics

Sexual reproduction generates genetic diversity through meiosis, a specialized cell division that produces haploid gametes from diploid germ cells. Meiosis combines two key mechanisms—recombination (交叉互换) through crossing-over and independent assortment of homologous chromosomes (同源染色体独立分配)—to shuffle genetic material. These processes, governed by Mendelian principles, create variation that fuels evolution. Errors in meiosis—such as nondisjunction—cause aneuploidy and genetic disorders, illustrating how fundamental this process is to human health and heredity.

Asexual vs. Sexual Reproduction

Asexual reproduction produces offspring genetically identical to the parent through mitotic cell division. Sexual reproduction combines genetic material from two parents, generating genetically unique offspring. Asexual reproduction is efficient and allows rapid population growth, but sexual reproduction, despite its metabolic cost, creates variation. This variation provides raw material for natural selection and adaptation. Most eukaryotes use sexual reproduction, or alternate between sexual and asexual phases. The evolutionary advantage of generating diversity outweighs the cost of producing fewer offspring per parent.

Key terms: cloning (无性繁殖), gamete (配子), germ cells (生殖细胞), genetic diversity.

Meiosis I: Reduction Division

Meiosis I is the first meiotic division, resulting in two haploid cells from one diploid cell. It begins with a prolonged prophase I during which homologous chromosomes (同源染色体) pair up in a process called synapsis (联合). During synapsis, non-sister chromatids of homologous chromosomes exchange DNA segments through crossing-over (交叉互换), creating recombinant chromosomes and new allele combinations. This exchange is mediated by the synaptonemal complex, a protein scaffold that holds homologs together. After recombination events are complete, chromosomes condense further. In metaphase I, bivalents (paired homologous chromosomes) align at the metaphase plate. During anaphase I, homologous chromosomes separate (not sister chromatids), moving to opposite poles. Telophase I completes, yielding two haploid cells.

Exam trap: Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes; sister chromatids remain together until Meiosis II.

Key terms: homologous chromosomes, synapsis (联合), bivalent, synaptonemal complex, crossing-over (交叉互换).

Meiosis II: Sister Chromatid Separation

Meiosis II resembles mitosis—it separates sister chromatids—but begins with haploid cells from Meiosis I. Meiosis II is therefore sometimes called "mitosis of haploid cells." In prophase II, chromosomes (still consisting of two sister chromatids joined at the centromere) condense. In metaphase II, chromosomes align at the metaphase plate. In anaphase II, sister chromatids separate when cohesin proteins are cleaved by separase, and individual chromatids (now called chromosomes) move to opposite poles. Telophase II and cytokinesis complete the division, producing four haploid cells (gametes). These four cells are not necessarily identical—each contains a unique combination of parental and recombinant chromosomes due to independent assortment and recombination in Meiosis I.

Key terms: sister chromatids, anaphase II, cohesin (粘聚蛋白), gamete (配子), haploid.

Genetic Recombination Mechanisms

Crossing-over occurs during prophase I when the synaptonemal complex facilitates strand invasion and exchange between homologous chromosomes. The RecQ helicase unwinds DNA strands, and the Dmc1 protein catalyzes strand invasion. Double-strand breaks created by Spo11 are repaired in a way that produces new allele combinations. Recombination frequency (measured in map units or centiMorgans) depends on chromosomal distance—loci far apart recombine more frequently (up to 50%) than loci close together. Three-point crosses (测试交叉, using three genetic markers) reveal gene order and interference (the phenomenon where one crossover affects the probability of another nearby). Gene conversion can also create new combinations without reciprocal exchange.

Exam trap: Recombination frequency plateaus at 50% because loci far apart assort independently regardless of crossing-over.

Key terms: crossing-over (交叉互换), Spo11, Dmc1, map units (centiMorgans), gene conversion, interference.

Chromosome Reassortment and Nondisjunction Errors

Independent assortment (自由组合) occurs when homologous chromosome pairs orient randomly at the metaphase I plate. With n pairs of chromosomes, there are 2^n possible combinations in gametes. In humans (n=23), this produces over 8 million distinct haploid combinations, even without recombination. Nondisjunction (不分离) is the failure of chromosomes (in Meiosis I) or sister chromatids (in Meiosis II) to separate, producing aneuploid gametes with too many or too few chromosomes. When these gametes are fertilized, the resulting zygotes are aneuploid—most are inviable, but some survive with developmental abnormalities. Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome), Trisomy 18 (Edwards syndrome), and Trisomy 13 (Patau syndrome) are common examples in humans. Maternal age increases nondisjunction risk, likely because oocytes arrest in prophase I for years or decades before ovulation, allowing cohesin proteins to deteriorate.

Key terms: independent assortment (自由组合), nondisjunction (不分离), aneuploidy (非整倍体), trisomy, monosomy.

Mendelian Genetics and Inheritance Patterns

Mendel's laws emerge from meiosis. The law of segregation (性状分离律) states that allele pairs separate during gamete formation, so each gamete receives only one allele of each gene. The law of independent assortment (自由组合律) states that alleles of different genes assort independently (provided they are on different chromosomes or far apart on the same chromosome). Dominance determines the phenotype when a dominant allele is present. Recessively inherited traits appear only in homozygous individuals. Pedigree analysis, Punnett squares, and testcrosses reveal genotypes and predict offspring ratios. Linkage (连锁继承) occurs when genes are on the same chromosome—they assort together unless recombination separates them. Incomplete dominance and codominance modify simple Mendelian ratios. Sex-linked inheritance follows different patterns because males are hemizygous (only one allele on the X chromosome).

Exam trap: Recombination can break linkage; genes on the same chromosome don't always assort together.

Key terms: law of segregation (性状分离律), law of independent assortment (自由组合律), dominance, recessiveness, linkage (连锁), hemizygous.

Genetics as a Research Tool and Biological Principle

Genetic analysis reveals gene function and networks. Forward genetics (正向遗传学) starts with a phenotype and maps the responsible gene. Reverse genetics (反向遗传学) disrupts a specific gene and observes the phenotype. Quantitative genetics (数量遗传学) studies polygenic traits (多基因性状) controlled by many genes. Linkage disequilibrium (连锁不平衡) and genome-wide association studies (GWAS) identify disease-associated variants. Population genetics (群体遗传学) uses Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium to predict allele frequencies. All of these rest on Mendelian principles and the mechanisms of meiosis. Understanding genetic inheritance is central to evolutionary biology, medicine, plant breeding, and conservation biology.

Key terms: forward genetics, reverse genetics, quantitative genetics, linkage disequilibrium, Hardy-Weinberg equilibrium.

Summary of Exam Traps:
  • Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes; Meiosis II separates sister chromatids.
  • Recombination frequency plateaus at 50% for distant loci because they assort independently.
  • Recombination can break linkage between genes on the same chromosome.
  • Nondisjunction in Meiosis I produces gametes with both copies of a chromosome; Meiosis II nondisjunction produces some gametes with both and some with none.
  • Maternal age increases nondisjunction risk due to cohesin deterioration during the extended prophase I arrest.
  • Hemizygous males on X-linked traits express the allele regardless of dominance.
  • Independent assortment applies only to genes on different chromosomes or far apart on the same chromosome.
  • Genetic diversity from sexual reproduction, despite its cost, provides variation for evolution.

Top Things to Memorize

  1. Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes; Meiosis II separates sister chromatids.
  2. Crossing-over during prophase I creates recombinant chromosomes; independent assortment shuffles whole chromosomes.
  3. Synaptonemal complex holds homologous chromosomes together during prophase I and enables recombination.
  4. Recombination frequency reflects map distance; 50% is the maximum (independent assortment limit).
  5. Nondisjunction produces aneuploid gametes; maternal age increases risk due to cohesin deterioration.
  6. Law of segregation: allele pairs separate during gamete formation (from Meiosis I).
  7. Law of independent assortment: alleles of different genes assort independently if unlinked.
  8. Linkage violates independent assortment when genes are on the same chromosome.
  9. Sex-linked inheritance: males are hemizygous; a single recessive allele on X is expressed.
  10. 2^n rule: n chromosome pairs produce 2^n possible haploid combinations from independent assortment alone.
  11. Three-point cross reveals gene order and recombination interference.
  12. Polyploidy (whole genome doubling) is tolerated in plants but usually fatal in animals.

Exam-style Questions (Preview)

Q1: Why does maternal age increase the risk of trisomy 21?
A: Oocytes arrest in prophase I for years; cohesins deteriorate over time, increasing nondisjunction risk in Meiosis I.

Q2: What is the maximum recombination frequency between two loci, and why?
A: 50%; loci far apart assort independently regardless of crossing-over, mimicking independent assortment.

Q3: How do forward and reverse genetics differ?
A: Forward genetics: phenotype → gene. Reverse genetics: disrupt gene → observe phenotype.

Q4: In a testcross of AaBb × aabb, what ratio is expected if A and B are unlinked?
A: 1:1:1:1 (equal frequencies of all four phenotypes).

Q5: Why does nondisjunction in Meiosis II produce a different outcome than nondisjunction in Meiosis I?
A: Meiosis II nondisjunction produces two gametes with the extra chromosome and two with none; Meiosis I nondisjunction produces all products with either two copies or zero.
