Lecture 19: The Cell Division Cycle

Lecture 19 explores fundamental biological processes essential for life: how cells divide and maintain genetic information. Understanding these mechanisms is critical because they underpin growth, reproduction, inheritance, and disease—including cancer, which arises from failures in cell cycle control.

Core Concept Overview

All living organisms depend on cell division for growth, repair, and reproduction. The cell cycle is tightly regulated by checkpoints and molecular control systems (cyclins and CDKs) that ensure accurate DNA replication and chromosome segregation. Sexual reproduction introduces genetic variation through meiosis and recombination, providing evolutionary advantages.

Key processes: The cell cycle (G1, S, G2, M phases), checkpoint control, mitosis, cytokinesis, meiosis, homologous recombination, and Mendelian inheritance patterns.

The Cell Cycle and Control Checkpoints

The cell cycle consists of four distinct phases. G1 phase (Gap 1) is a growth phase where the cell accumulates mass and proteins. S phase (Synthesis) involves DNA replication—the cell duplicates its entire genome. G2 phase (Gap 2) is another growth phase preparing for mitosis. M phase includes mitosis (nuclear division) and cytokinesis (cytoplasmic division), producing two daughter cells.

Three critical checkpoints regulate progression:

  • G1/S checkpoint: Verifies cell readiness for DNA replication; blocks progression if DNA is damaged or conditions are unfavorable.
  • G2/M checkpoint: Confirms DNA replication is complete and damage is repaired before mitosis begins.
  • Metaphase/Anaphase checkpoint: Ensures all chromosomes are properly attached to the spindle before separation.

Exam trap: Students often confuse which events happen in which phase. S phase = DNA replication ONLY, not mitosis. M phase = division, not replication.

Cyclins and CDKs: The Molecular Switches

Cell cycle progression is controlled by cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs), regulatory proteins that phosphorylate target proteins to drive cell cycle events. CDKs alone are inactive; they require binding to cyclins—regulatory proteins whose concentrations oscillate throughout the cell cycle.

Different cyclin-CDK complexes trigger different events:

  • Cyclin E-CDK2 drives G1/S transition (DNA replication initiation)
  • Cyclin A-CDK2 maintains S phase
  • Cyclin B-CDK1 triggers G2/M transition (entry into mitosis)

Cyclins are synthesized and degraded in a tightly regulated pattern. The anaphase-promoting complex (APC), an ubiquitin ligase, targets cyclins for destruction via the proteasome, ensuring CDK activity drops and allowing progression to the next phase.

Exam trap: "CDKs are always active" — FALSE. CDKs require cyclins to be active. Cyclin degradation turns OFF CDK activity.

Interphase: DNA and Centrosome Duplication

During interphase (G1, S, G2), the cell grows, replicates its DNA, and duplicates its centrosome (the microtubule organizing center). Chromosomes are not yet visible as condensed structures—instead, DNA is packaged loosely as chromatin. If DNA damage is detected during G1, checkpoint control can arrest the cell cycle and trigger repair or apoptosis (programmed cell death).

Key events:

  • Chromosome duplication (DNA replication in S phase)
  • Centrosome duplication
  • Organelle and cell size increase
  • Protein and lipid synthesis

M Phase: Mitosis and Cytokinesis

M phase is when the duplicated chromosomes and cellular contents are actually divided. Mitosis has several visible stages:

Prophase: Chromosomes condense from chromatin into visible structures. The cell must compact ~2 meters of human DNA into a nucleus ~10 µm in radius. Condensins facilitate this extreme condensation. Cohesins glue sister chromatids together. The mitotic spindle (made of microtubules) begins to assemble from the centrosomes.

Metaphase: Chromosomes align at the cell's equator. Kinetochores—protein structures on centromeres—attach chromosomes to spindle microtubules. The cell checks that all chromosomes are properly attached before proceeding.

Anaphase: APC triggers destruction of cohesins, allowing sister chromatids to separate and move toward opposite spindle poles. The spindle pulls chromatids apart and pushes poles further apart.

Telophase: Sister chromatids arrive at spindle poles and decondense. New nuclear envelopes reassemble around each set of chromosomes. A contractile ring of actin and myosin forms at the cell's equator.

Cytokinesis: The contractile ring tightens, dividing the cytoplasm and producing two daughter cells. In plant cells, a phragmoplast guides cell wall formation instead.

Exam trap: "Chromosomes separate during metaphase" — FALSE. Sister chromatids separate during anaphase after cohesins are destroyed.

Meiosis: Creating Genetic Diversity

Sexual reproduction requires meiosis, a specialized cell division that produces haploid gametes (sperm and egg) from diploid germ-line cells. Unlike mitosis, which produces two identical daughter cells, meiosis involves two sequential divisions producing four genetically distinct cells, each with half the chromosome number.

Meiosis I: Homologous chromosomes (maternal and paternal pairs) pair up to form bivalents. They then undergo crossing-over (homologous recombination), exchanging genetic material at points called chiasmata. Cohesins at chiasmata ensure proper chromosome segregation. After crossing-over, homologous chromosomes separate, producing two haploid cells.

Meiosis II: Sister chromatids separate, similar to mitosis, producing four haploid gametes.

Exam trap: "Meiosis has one DNA replication followed by one cell division" — FALSE. One replication is followed by TWO divisions. That's how you get four haploid cells from one diploid cell.

Homologous Recombination and Genetic Variation

During meiosis I, crossing-over between homologous chromosomes creates new combinations of alleles on individual chromosomes. Independent assortment randomly distributes maternal and paternal chromosomes into gametes. Together, these mechanisms generate enormous genetic diversity.

Each human with 23 chromosome pairs can produce 2^23 ≈ 8.4 million genetically distinct gametes. When fertilization occurs, sperm and egg unite randomly, further increasing genetic variation in offspring.

Clinical relevance: Errors in chromosome segregation cause aneuploidy. For example, nondisjunction (failure of homologs or sister chromatids to separate) can produce gametes with extra or missing chromosomes. A child inheriting three copies of chromosome 21 (trisomy 21) develops Down syndrome.

Mendel's Laws of Inheritance

Gregor Mendel discovered predictable patterns of inheritance by breeding peas. His experiments established two fundamental laws:

Law of Segregation: Each organism carries two alleles (versions) for each trait. During meiosis, alleles segregate so each gamete receives one allele. At fertilization, gametes fuse randomly, restoring two alleles in the offspring. A cross between two heterozygotes (Aa × Aa) produces a 3:1 phenotypic ratio in offspring (3 dominant, 1 recessive).

Law of Independent Assortment: Different genes (on different chromosomes) segregate independently during meiosis. In a dihybrid cross (tracking two traits simultaneously), four phenotypic classes appear in a 9:3:3:1 ratio.

Exam trap: "Independent assortment applies to all genes" — FALSE. It applies to genes on different chromosomes. Genes on the same chromosome show linkage.

Genetics as a Tool for Research and Medicine

Classical genetic approaches use random mutagenesis to identify genes controlling specific traits. Temperature-sensitive mutants are especially valuable—they function normally at permissive temperatures but are defective at restrictive temperatures, helping identify essential genes.

Next-Generation Sequencing (NGS) revolutionized human genetics by enabling rapid, cost-effective DNA sequencing. Applications include:

  • Identifying mutations associated with genetic diseases
  • Detecting chromosomal abnormalities
  • Tracking disease outbreaks through pathogen sequencing
  • Developing personalized therapeutics

Example: Sickle-cell anemia results from a single nucleotide mutation in the β-globin gene, causing abnormal hemoglobin polymerization and red blood cell sickling under low oxygen.

Common Exam Traps

  • G1/S checkpoint evaluates readiness for DNA replication; G2/M checkpoint confirms replication is complete.
  • Sister chromatids separate in anaphase (AFTER cohesin destruction), not metaphase.
  • Meiosis = one replication + two divisions → four haploid cells (not two).
  • Cyclin degradation INACTIVATES CDKs, allowing cell cycle progression to next phase.
  • Independent assortment applies only to genes on different chromosomes (linkage exception).
  • Homologous recombination occurs during meiosis I, not meiosis II.

Top Things to Memorize

  1. Four phases of the cell cycle: G1 (growth), S (DNA replication), G2 (growth), M (mitosis + cytokinesis).
  2. Three cell cycle checkpoints: G1/S, G2/M, and metaphase/anaphase transition.
  3. Cyclin-CDK complexes drive cell cycle progression; cyclin degradation by APC shuts off CDKs.
  4. Meiosis produces four haploid cells from one diploid cell (via two divisions after one replication).
  5. Homologous recombination and independent assortment create genetic variation in sexual reproduction.
  6. Mendel's segregation law: alleles separate during meiosis; 3:1 ratio in F2 monohybrid crosses.
  7. Errors in chromosome segregation cause aneuploidy (e.g., Down syndrome = trisomy 21).

Exam-Style Questions (Preview)

Q1: What is the primary function of the G1/S checkpoint? A: To verify DNA is undamaged and cell conditions are favorable before DNA replication begins.

Q2: Why does cohesin degradation trigger anaphase? A: Sister chromatids are held together by cohesins; their destruction allows chromatids to separate and move to opposite poles.

Q3: How does meiosis generate four haploid cells instead of two? A: Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes; meiosis II separates sister chromatids—two divisions total.

Q4: What is the relationship between independent assortment and the 9:3:3:1 ratio? A: Genes on different chromosomes assort independently; in a dihybrid cross, this produces four phenotypic classes in a 9:3:3:1 ratio.

  • Detailed molecular mechanisms of spindle microtubule dynamics and kinetochore attachment.
  • Specific proteins involved in chromosome condensation (condensins) and sister chromatid cohesion (cohesins)—names matter less than their functions.
  • Mathematical calculation of genetic variation: 2^n gametes (memorize concept, not the formula).
  • Detailed NGS technology workflow and bioinformatics—high-level understanding sufficient.
  • Temperature-sensitive mutant screening protocols—concept more important than methodology.

What is the primary purpose of the G1/S checkpoint in the cell cycle?

Which cyclin-CDK complex drives the G1/S transition?
Cyclin E-CDK2 is responsible for pushing the cell from G1 into S phase and initiating DNA replication. Cyclin D-CDK4/6 acts earlier in G1.
Question 3 of 30
What triggers sister chromatids to separate during anaphase?
Sister chromatids are held together by cohesin proteins. The APC ubiquitin ligase targets cohesins for degradation, allowing sister chromatids to separate and be pulled to opposite poles.
Question 4 of 30
How much DNA must be compacted during prophase in human cells?
Human DNA totals approximately 2 meters in length but must fit into a nucleus with a radius of only ~10 µm. Condensins facilitate this extreme compaction.
Question 5 of 30
What is the result of homologous recombination during meiosis I?
Crossing-over between homologous chromosomes during meiosis I allows maternal and paternal DNA to recombine, creating new allele combinations and increasing genetic diversity.
Question 6 of 30
How many meiotic divisions are required to produce four haploid cells?
Meiosis I separates homologous chromosomes (producing two diploid cells), and meiosis II separates sister chromatids (producing four haploid cells). Two divisions total.
Question 7 of 30
What is the primary evolutionary advantage of sexual reproduction?
Sexual reproduction mixes parental chromosomes and introduces recombination, creating genetic variation. This diversity helps populations adapt to environmental changes.
Question 8 of 30
Which statement about Mendel's law of segregation is correct?
The law of segregation states that the two alleles for each trait separate during meiosis, with each gamete receiving one allele. They reunite randomly during fertilization.
Question 9 of 30
What is a consequence of nondisjunction during meiosis?
Nondisjunction is the failure of homologous chromosomes or sister chromatids to separate. This produces some gametes with extra chromosomes and others with missing chromosomes. Fertilization with such gametes causes aneuploidy.
Question 10 of 30
Down syndrome results from which chromosomal abnormality?
Down syndrome (trisomy 21) occurs when an individual inherits three copies of chromosome 21 instead of the normal two, usually due to nondisjunction during meiosis.
Question 11 of 30
What is the function of kinetochores during mitosis?
Kinetochores are protein structures at centromeres that attach chromosomes to spindle microtubules, allowing the spindle to pull sister chromatids apart during anaphase.
Question 12 of 30
During which phase of mitosis do chromosomes first become visible under a light microscope?
Chromosomes condense during prophase, making them visible as distinct structures for the first time. Prior to prophase, DNA is loosely packaged as chromatin.
Question 13 of 30
What is the consequence of a G2/M checkpoint failure?
The G2/M checkpoint prevents entry into mitosis if DNA replication is incomplete or damage is detected. Failure of this checkpoint allows damaged DNA to be segregated into daughter cells.
Question 14 of 30
Which protein complex triggers cyclin degradation?
The APC is an ubiquitin ligase that polyubiquitinates cyclins, marking them for proteasomal degradation. This removes cyclins and inactivates CDKs, allowing cell cycle progression.
Question 15 of 30
What is the relationship between cyclins and CDKs?
CDKs are catalytically inactive without cyclin binding partners. When cyclins accumulate and bind, they activate CDKs, allowing them to phosphorylate target proteins and drive cell cycle events.
Question 16 of 30
What is the key difference between mitosis and meiosis I?
In mitosis, sister chromatids separate, maintaining ploidy. In meiosis I, homologous chromosomes separate, reducing ploidy from diploid to haploid.
Question 17 of 30
How many unique gametes can a human produce theoretically?
With 23 chromosome pairs, each of which can assort independently into gametes, humans can produce 2^23 ≈ 8.4 million genetically unique gametes.
Question 18 of 30
What is the purpose of cohesins during meiosis I?
Cohesins hold sister chromatids and homologous chromosomes together. Chiasmata (the sites of crossing-over) are maintained by cohesins, ensuring homologs don't separate prematurely.
Question 19 of 30
What is a bivalent in meiosis?
During meiosis I, homologous chromosomes synapse (pair up) to form a bivalent. Each bivalent consists of four chromatids (two sister chromatids from each homolog).
Question 20 of 30
During which stage of meiosis do sister chromatids separate?
Sister chromatids remain together through meiosis I. They separate during anaphase II, similar to mitotic anaphase.
Question 21 of 30
What does the law of independent assortment describe?
Independent assortment refers to the random, independent segregation of different genes (especially those on different chromosomes) during gamete formation.
Question 22 of 30
What is the significance of chiasmata in meiosis?
Chiasmata are the visible points where crossing-over has occurred between homologous chromosomes. They represent sites of genetic recombination and are held together by cohesins.
Question 23 of 30
A monohybrid cross between two heterozygotes (Aa × Aa) produces which ratio in offspring?
In an Aa × Aa cross, the F2 generation shows a 3:1 phenotypic ratio (three dominant to one recessive), reflecting the segregation of alleles according to Mendel's first law.
Question 24 of 30
What is the function of condensins during mitosis?
Condensins are protein complexes that compact chromatin into visible condensed chromosomes during prophase, allowing the ~2 meters of human DNA to fit within a ~10 µm nucleus.
Question 25 of 30
What event marks the start of cytokinesis in animal cells?
Cytokinesis begins when a contractile ring of actin and myosin forms at the cell's equator. Tightening of this ring divides the cytoplasm, producing two daughter cells.
Question 26 of 30
Which checkpoint ensures that all chromosomes are properly attached to spindle microtubules?
The metaphase/anaphase checkpoint (also called the spindle checkpoint) verifies that all kinetochores are attached to spindle microtubules before allowing anaphase to proceed.
Question 27 of 30
What is aneuploidy?
Aneuploidy is the condition of having an abnormal number of chromosomes, usually resulting from nondisjunction. Examples include trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) and monosomy X (Turner syndrome).
Question 28 of 30
In the dihybrid cross YyRr × YyRr, what phenotypic ratio is predicted?
In a dihybrid cross between two heterozygotes for two independently assorting genes, the predicted F2 phenotypic ratio is 9:3:3:1 (nine dominant for both traits, three dominant for each single trait, one double recessive).
Question 29 of 30
What is sickle-cell anemia caused by?
Sickle-cell anemia results from a point mutation that changes one amino acid in β-globin, causing hemoglobin polymerization and red blood cell sickling under low oxygen conditions.
Question 30 of 30
What occurs during the S phase of the cell cycle?
S phase (synthesis phase) is when DNA replication occurs, doubling the genetic material in preparation for cell division.

What is the role of germ-line cells in sexual reproduction?
Germ-line cells are specialized cells (diploid) that undergo meiosis to produce haploid gametes. Somatic cells, by contrast, undergo mitosis for growth and repair.
Question 27 of 30
What is the primary outcome of sexual reproduction compared to asexual reproduction?
Sexual reproduction combines genetic material from two parents and involves recombination, creating offspring with novel genetic combinations unavailable in asexual reproduction.
Question 28 of 30
During meiosis II, which cellular structures separate?
Meiosis II is similar to mitosis. Sister chromatids, which were held together by cohesins, separate and move to opposite poles.
Question 29 of 30
What is the primary function of the APC during mitosis and meiosis?
The anaphase-promoting complex (APC) is an ubiquitin ligase that marks cohesins and cyclins for degradation, triggering anaphase and regulating cell cycle progression.
Question 30 of 30
What evolutionary advantage does meiosis provide over asexual reproduction?
Meiosis creates genetic diversity through recombination and independent assortment. This variation allows populations to adapt to environmental changes and increases evolutionary fitness.
