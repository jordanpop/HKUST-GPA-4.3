Lecture 1: Overview of the Endocrine System

The endocrine system (內分泌系統) is the body's slow but far-reaching messaging network. Specialized cells release hormones (荷爾蒙) into the blood; those chemicals travel to distant tissues and bind specific receptors to shape metabolism, growth, reproduction, water balance, and mood. Lecture 1 lays the scaffolding: what counts as a hormone, the four chemical classes (amino-acid derivatives, polypeptides, steroids, prostaglandins), and the two big signaling categories — lipid-soluble hormones acting on nuclear receptors versus lipid-insoluble hormones acting through surface receptors and second messengers. Master this scaffold and every later lecture is just hanging examples on it.

1. What is a hormone?

Working definition for classical hormones (古典荷爾蒙): (1) secreted in very small amounts by specialized cells or glands; (2) carried in the bloodstream to distant tissues; (3) bind specific receptors in target tissues to produce a biological response. Ernest Starling coined the word in 1905 — from Greek meaning "to arouse or excite."

Endocrine glands (內分泌腺) are not the only source — many tissues contain endocrine cells: heart (atrial natriuretic peptide), kidney (renin, calcitriol), liver (somatomedin/IGF), stomach (gastrin), duodenum (secretin), adipocytes (leptin, estrogen), bone (FGF-23, RANKL, sclerostin), and the enteroendocrine system (GLP-1, GLP-2).

Three categories of stimuli that trigger hormone secretion:

  • Humoral (體液性) — changes in blood Ca²⁺, glucose, osmolarity.
  • Neural (神經性) — sympathetic control of the adrenal medulla.
  • Hormonal (荷爾蒙性) — hypothalamic neurohormones controlling anterior pituitary release.
Exam trap: Hormones are secreted in VERY SMALL amounts. High concentrations are pathological. Also: hormones travel in BLOOD — not via ducts (that is exocrine).

2. Endocrine vs nervous system

Both coordinate physiology, but on different timescales. The nervous system is fast and brief; the endocrine system is slow and sustained (minutes to days). They overlap structurally — the hypothalamus contains neurosecretory cells that release hormones, and the adrenal medulla is essentially modified sympathetic ganglion. The same molecule can act as both transmitter and hormone (e.g. norepinephrine, 去甲腎上腺素).

4. Mechanisms of hormone action

Solubility determines mechanism:

Lipid-soluble (steroids + T4)Lipid-insoluble (peptides + most amino-acid derivatives + prostaglandins)
Receptor locationNucleus or cytoplasmCell surface
MechanismHormone-receptor complex binds DNA; modifies transcriptionSecond messengers (cAMP, IP3/DAG, Ca²⁺) OR intrinsic enzymatic activity (tyrosine kinase, guanylate cyclase)
Speed / durationSlow onset; long-lasting (hours-days)Rapid; short-lived

Surface receptor types (細胞表面受體):

  • GPCRs with cAMP signaling — epinephrine (β), TSH, vasopressin (kidney), glucagon, serotonin, PGI (stimulatory); epinephrine (α2) inhibitory.
  • GPCRs with IP3/DAG signaling — vasopressin (liver), growth factors, TRH.
  • Receptor tyrosine kinases — insulin, growth factors. Bind ligand → autophosphorylate (positive feedback) → phosphorylate tyrosines on cytoplasmic proteins.
  • Receptor guanylate cyclase — ANP receptor; binding generates cGMP.

Elwood Jensen (1958): first described the estrogen receptor; later identified the entire nuclear hormone receptor superfamily.

Exam trap: Insulin uses a receptor TYROSINE KINASE, NOT a G-protein coupled receptor. Yet vasopressin uses different mechanisms in different tissues (cAMP in kidney, IP3/DAG in liver).

5. Practical implication — solubility in lab work

To prepare a concentrated cortisol stock solution (steroid, lipid-soluble), use ethanol as solvent — not water/saline. Steroids dissolve in organic solvents because they cannot dissolve in aqueous solutions at high concentration.

  • Insulin uses receptor tyrosine kinase — NOT GPCR.

  • Vasopressin uses cAMP in kidney but IP3/DAG in liver.

Top things to memorise

  1. 3 features of a classical hormone (small amount, blood, specific receptor).
  2. 4 chemical classes + solubility of each class.
  3. Tyrosine derivatives: thyroxine, epinephrine, norepinephrine. Tryptophan → melatonin.
  4. Lipid-soluble → nuclear receptor → transcription → slow but long.
  5. Lipid-insoluble → surface receptor → 2nd messenger or kinase → fast but brief.
  6. Prostaglandins behave like lipid-insoluble hormones despite being lipid-soluble.
  7. Insulin = receptor tyrosine kinase, autophosphorylation, positive feedback.
  8. 3 types of hormone-secretion stimuli: humoral, neural, hormonal.

  5. Which prostaglandin signaling pathway? — Surface receptor + cAMP.

Lecture 2: Neuroendocrine Systems (Hypothalamus & Pituitary)

The neuroendocrine system (神經內分泌系統) is where brain meets bloodstream. The hypothalamus contains neurosecretory cells (神經分泌細胞) that release hormones — either directly into the bloodstream (posterior pituitary route) or into a portal system that delivers them to the anterior pituitary (前葉腦垂體). The anterior pituitary then secretes its own glandular hormones — some direct-acting (GH, prolactin, MSH) and some tropic (ACTH, TSH, FSH, LH) that regulate other endocrine glands. Lecture 2 maps this two-tier control circuit and its key feedback loops.

1. Neurosecretory cells

Neurosecretory cells are specialized neurons that release hormones (neurohormones, 神經荷爾蒙) into blood. They are found in two organs:

  • Hypothalamus (下視丘) — two populations:
    • Magnocellular neurons in the supraoptic nucleus (SON) and paraventricular nucleus (PVN) → project axons to the posterior pituitary.
    • Parvocellular neurons → project to the median eminence → release hormones into the hypophyseal portal capillaries.
  • Adrenal medulla (腎上腺髓質) — chromaffin cells (same ectodermal origin as neurons).

Exam trap: Neurosecretory cells ARE excitable neurons. Saying "non-excitable" is FALSE.

2. Pituitary anatomy

The pituitary gland (hypophysis, 腦下垂體) sits at the base of the brain, attached to the hypothalamus by the infundibulum. Two functionally distinct lobes:

The hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system (下視丘-腦垂體門脈系統) is two capillary beds joined by portal vessels — concentrates parvocellular neurosecretions before they reach the anterior pituitary.

Exam trap: The anterior pituitary is NOT composed of neurosecretory axons — that is the posterior pituitary. The anterior is true glandular tissue.

Lecture 3: Metabolic & Developmental Hormones

Lecture 3 is the metabolic-hormone tour. Six hormones cover all the big jobs: epinephrine for fight-or-flight, glucocorticoids (cortisol) for sustained stress and gluconeogenesis, thyroid hormones (T3/T4) for basal metabolic rate, insulin and glucagon for blood-sugar control, growth hormone for body growth and lean mass, and leptin for appetite/energy balance. Every disease story you need (Cushing's, Addison's, goiter, diabetes, gigantism/acromegaly, obesity) lives here.

1. Epinephrine and norepinephrine

Catecholamines (兒茶酚胺) secreted by the adrenal medulla (腎上腺髓質, inner layer). Epinephrine ~80%, norepinephrine ~20%. NE is the synthetic precursor of E.

The adrenal medulla is essentially modified sympathetic ganglion — chromaffin cells are analogous to postganglionic sympathetic neurons. Activated by the same stimuli (cold, stress, hemorrhage, exercise) and reinforces/prolongs the fight-or-flight response.

Adrenoreceptors (腎上腺素受體):

ReceptorCouplingAction

α2Gi → ↓cAMPInhibits insulin release

β2Gs → ↑cAMPBronchodilation, vasodilation in skeletal muscle

Pheochromocytoma (嗜鉻細胞瘤) — adrenal medulla tumor causing episodic hypertension, palpitations, sweating, headaches. Hematidrosis (blood sweat) can occur from extreme adrenaline-driven capillary rupture.

Anaphylaxis treatment: epinephrine is first-aid drug — vasoconstrictor prevents laryngeal edema/hypotension, bronchodilator, positive inotropy/chronotropy.

Exam trap: Adrenal medulla does NOT secrete glucocorticoids (those are from cortex). Adrenal medulla secretion is NOT regulated by ACTH — only by direct sympathetic neural input.
Exam trap: Epinephrine INHIBITS insulin secretion (via α2) but STIMULATES glucagon — both push blood sugar UP.

2. Glucocorticoids (cortisol)

Steroid hormones from the adrenal cortex (外層). Major form in humans = cortisol (= hydrocortisone, 氫化可的松). Others: cortisone, corticosterone.

Regulation:

  1. Stress (including hypoglycemia) + diurnal rhythm (peak in morning).
  2. Hypothalamus → CRH → anterior pituitary → ACTH → adrenal cortex → cortisol.
  3. Negative feedback: cortisol inhibits CRH and ACTH.

Major actions:

  1. Gluconeogenesis (糖質新生) — primary effect. Sustains glucose supply to brain during fasting/stress by generating glucose from amino acids and fats.
    • Liver: ↑gluconeogenesis
    • Skeletal muscle: ↓protein synthesis, ↑protein degradation, ↓glucose uptake
    • Adipose: ↓glucose uptake, ↑lipid mobilization
  2. Enhance vascular reactivity.
  3. Suppress inflammation and immune system (higher doses) — basis for using glucocorticoids as immunosuppressants and anti-inflammatories.

Cushing's syndrome (庫欣氏症候群) — excess cortisol. Rapid weight gain (moon face, buffalo hump), moodiness, muscle weakness, immune suppression, thin skin, baldness. Causes: glucocorticoid drugs, or tumors producing cortisol/ACTH/CRH.

Addison's disease — primary adrenal failure → low cortisol → no negative feedback → high CRH/ACTH → high MSH → hyperpigmentation. Often TB-induced historically.

Exam trap: Glucocorticoids INCREASE blood glucose (via gluconeogenesis and ↓muscle/adipose glucose uptake). Hydrocortisone given for SARS to suppress immune system, not for glycemia.

3. Thyroid hormones (T4, T3)

Formed from two iodinated tyrosine precursors in the thyroid. Iodine (碘) deficiency in diet causes goiter (甲狀腺腫).

Major form released = thyroxine (T4) (tetra-iodothyronine). In target tissues, T4 → more potent T3 (tri-iodothyronine).

Nuclear receptors → transcription factors. T3/T4 also have non-genomic actions.

Regulation: hypothalamus (CNS inputs: cold, pregnancy energy needs) → TRH → pituitary → TSH → thyroid → T3/T4. Negative feedback on hypothalamus and pituitary. TSH also stimulates thyroid growth.

Actions (whole-body):

  1. Stimulate cellular respiration → ↑basal metabolic rate (BMR), heat production. Acts on ALL tissues EXCEPT brain, spleen, testes/uterus, and thyroid itself.
  2. Sensitize tissues to epinephrine — explains tachycardia in hyperthyroidism.
  3. Required for skeletal and nervous system development/maturation (synergistic with GH).
Exam trap: Hyperthyroidism causes increased heart rate by SENSITIZING tissues to epinephrine, not by directly stimulating catecholamine secretion. Brain BMR is NOT affected by T3/T4.

4. Insulin and glucagon

Both peptide hormones from the islets of Langerhans in the pancreas (胰島).

Insulin actions:

  1. Carbohydrate: ↑glucose uptake/storage (as glycogen) in liver, muscle, adipose.
  2. Fat: stimulates lipogenesis in liver and adipose.
  3. Protein: stimulates aa uptake and protein synthesis in liver and muscle.

Glucagon actions: ↑glycogenolysis in liver; ↑gluconeogenesis in liver.

Insulin uses receptor tyrosine kinase — NOT GPCR.

Diabetes mellitus (糖尿病):

  • Type 1 (10%): loss of β cells → no insulin (insulin-dependent).
  • Type 2 (90%): defective insulin receptors → insulin resistance.
  • Gestational: appears in pregnancy.

Acute complications: hyperglycemia → glycosuria → polyuria → dehydration. In type 1, low insulin → ↑glucagon → ↑ketones → ketoacidosis → coma/death.

Chronic complications: macrovascular (stroke, MI, limb loss), microvascular (retinopathy, nephropathy), neuropathy (autonomic and peripheral sensory).

Diabetes insipidus (尿崩症) is different — caused by ADH deficiency, no sweet urine.

Exam trap: Insulin uses RTK, not GPCR. Insulin is from β cells (NOT α). Type 1 diabetes has HIGH glucagon (not low) because insulin's inhibition is gone.

5. Growth hormone (GH)

Peptide (191 aa) from anterior pituitary. Overall effect: promote tissue growth (anabolic). Has both insulin-like and anti-insulin effects.

Regulation:

  • Stimulators: hypoglycemia, amino acids (especially arginine), deep sleep (peak ~1h after sleep onset), stress (trauma, infection, psychological).
  • Inhibitors: hyperglycemia, REM sleep.
  • Hypothalamus: GHRH (releases) vs GIH/somatostatin (inhibits).
  • Pulsatile — frequency/size peak at puberty, decline with aging.

Actions:

TissueInsulin-like effectsAnti-insulin effects
Muscle↑aa uptake, ↑RNA, ↑protein synthesis↓glucose uptake
Liver↑protein synthesis, ↑somatomedin (IGF)↑gluconeogenesis
Adipose↑glucose uptake↑lipolysis

Most growth-promoting effects are indirect via IGFs (insulin-like growth factors, somatomedins) made by the liver — drive linear bone growth.

Hyperglycemic mechanism (3 prongs): ↓glucose uptake by tissues; ↑gluconeogenesis in liver; ↑lipolysis (lipids replace glucose as fuel). GH also stimulates insulin secretion (directly + indirectly via hyperglycemia).

GH deficiency: in childhood → dwarfism (侏儒症); in adults → no obvious effect. Laron dwarfs have mutated GH receptors and are protected from cancer and diabetes.

GH excess: childhood → gigantism (巨人症); adult (after epiphyseal closure) → acromegaly (肢端肥大症, enlarged jaw/nose/hands).

Insulin tolerance test: inject insulin to induce hypoglycemia → if pituitary can release GH, GH should rise. Tests pituitary GH reserve. The "professional doctor" is correct.

Exam trap: GH's growth-promoting effects are mediated mainly by IGF-1 (not directly). Arginine stimulates GH. Dreaming = REM = INHIBITS GH.

6. Leptin

Peptide (16 kDa) encoded by the obese gene (ob) — produced mainly by white adipose tissue and many other tissues. From Greek leptos, "thin."

Action: influences food intake vs energy expenditure. High leptin → reduced appetite, increased energy expenditure.

Leptin receptor (LepR/OB-R): a cytokine-family receptor associated with JAK tyrosine kinases. Expressed primarily in brain — most leptin effects are indirect via CNS. Also regulates immune-cell function (CNS-mediated and direct).

Obesity paradox: obese individuals usually have HIGH leptin yet remain obese. Possible reasons:

  • More adipose mass → more leptin production.
  • Leptin resistance at receptors.
  • Perceived starvation state.
Exam trap: Obesity is usually associated with HIGH (not low) leptin — implying leptin resistance.
⚠ Exam Traps Summary
  • Adrenal medulla: catecholamines only, NOT regulated by ACTH.
  • Cortisol's primary action = gluconeogenesis (↑blood glucose).
  • Cushing's = excess cortisol; Addison's = deficient cortisol with hyperpigmentation.
  • T3/T4 do not act on brain BMR; act via sensitizing tissues to epinephrine.
  • Insulin = β cells, RTK, lowers glucose. Glucagon = α cells, raises glucose.
  • Type 1 DM has HIGH glucagon → ketoacidosis.
  • GH effects on growth are indirect via IGF-1; direct effect = anti-insulin.
  • Dreaming (REM) INHIBITS GH.
  • Leptin is elevated in obesity (resistance).

Top things to memorise

  1. Adrenal layers: medulla (catecholamines) vs cortex (mineralocorticoids/glucocorticoids/androgens).
  2. CRH → ACTH → cortisol axis + negative feedback.
  3. Three actions of cortisol: gluconeogenesis, vascular reactivity, immune suppression.
  4. TRH → TSH → T3/T4; iodine deficiency = goiter.
  5. Insulin (β, lowers glucose, RTK) vs glucagon (α, raises glucose) and their regulation.
  6. Type 1 (β-cell loss) vs type 2 DM (receptor defect).
  7. GH: anabolic, via IGF-1, stimulated by sleep/arginine/hypoglycemia, inhibited by hyperglycemia/REM.
  8. Gigantism (child) vs acromegaly (adult).
  9. Leptin from adipose; obesity = high leptin + resistance.

  2. Which is NOT a feature of type 1 DM? — Low glucagon (it is HIGH).
  3. How does hyperthyroidism increase cardiac contractility? — By sensitizing tissues to epinephrine.
  4. What stimulates GH? — Arginine, deep sleep, hypoglycemia.
  5. Most growth-promoting effects of GH go through what? — IGF-1.

Lecture 4: Hormones Regulating Water & Electrolyte Balance

Lecture 4 zooms in on water and electrolyte balance — three hormones that work together to keep blood volume and osmolarity in range. ADH (vasopressin) drives water reabsorption when plasma is too concentrated or blood pressure drops. Aldosterone (the major mineralocorticoid) drives Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ excretion. Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) opposes both — it drops blood volume when the heart senses overload. Knowing the trigger, target, and net effect of each is the whole game.

1. Antidiuretic hormone (ADH / vasopressin / AVP)

9-aa cyclic peptide synthesized in the supraoptic nucleus (SON) and secreted by the posterior pituitary.

Actions:

  • Kidney: ↑water reabsorption by inserting aquaporin (水通道蛋白) into collecting duct surface.
  • Vascular smooth muscle: direct vasoconstriction → ↑blood pressure (hence "vasopressin").

Regulation — two physiological inputs:

  1. Osmotic regulation — even 5-10 mOsm change is sensed (normal 280 mOsm). Osmoreceptors in anterior hypothalamus (possibly the SON neurons themselves). ↑Plasma osmolarity → ↑ADH → water retention → ↓osmolarity. Eating salty food triggers this.
  2. Volume/pressure regulation — requires >10% blood volume change (e.g. hemorrhage). Stretch receptors (TRP channels) in cardiovascular neurons signal via cranial nerves. ↓Volume/pressure → ↑ADH → vasoconstriction + water reabsorption.

Alcohol potently inhibits ADH release → polyuria and thirst.

Diabetes insipidus (尿崩症): ADH deficiency. Polyuria + thirst but NO sugar in urine (different from diabetes mellitus).

Exam trap: Drinking lots of water LOWERS plasma osmolarity and INHIBITS ADH — not stimulates. Salt and hemorrhage stimulate ADH.

2. Mineralocorticoids (aldosterone)

Steroids from the adrenal cortex regulating kidney electrolyte balance. Major form = aldosterone (醛固酮).

Action: in the kidney (distal tubule + collecting duct), aldosterone stimulates Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ excretion. Water follows Na⁺ → ↑extracellular fluid volume → ↑blood volume and blood pressure.

Regulation:

  1. Humoral: extracellular K⁺ concentration — even a 5% rise triggers aldosterone secretion.
  2. Hormonal:
    • Angiotensin II — the primary regulator. Renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS) activated when blood pressure drops.
    • ACTH — minor contribution.

RAAS sequence: ↓BP → kidney releases renin → cleaves angiotensinogen to angiotensin I → ACE converts to angiotensin II → adrenal cortex → aldosterone → Na⁺ + water retention → ↑BP.

Exam trap: The PRIMARY regulator of aldosterone is angiotensin II, not ACTH. Aldosterone INCREASES Na⁺ reabsorption and K⁺ excretion (note the asymmetry).

3. Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP)

Secreted by endocrine cells in the atria of the heart in response to mechanical stretch (↑blood volume).

Action: on kidneys, ↑Na⁺ excretion + ↑urine output → ↓blood volume and pressure. Counteracts ADH and aldosterone.

"Natriuretic" = causing natriuresis = excretion of sodium in urine. Receptor is a guanylate cyclase → cGMP.

Exam trap: ANP is from the HEART (atria), not the kidney. ANP DECREASES blood pressure — opposite to ADH and aldosterone.

Lecture 5: Reproductive Hormones

Lecture 5 covers reproductive hormones — the hormonal control of gamete production, sexual development, and pregnancy. The same hypothalamic-pituitary backbone (GnRH → FSH/LH) drives both sexes but the gonads and feedback patterns differ. In men, testosterone is constant; in women, estrogen and progesterone cycle on a ~28-day rhythm built from two synchronized cycles (ovarian and uterine). Pregnancy hijacks the cycle through hCG, which rescues the corpus luteum. Memorize the cell types (Leydig vs Sertoli; granulosa vs theca; follicle vs corpus luteum) and the feedback switches (negative most of the time, positive at mid-cycle), and the rest follows.

1. Overview of sex hormones

Two categories:

  1. Steroid hormones from cholesterol — pathway: cholesterol → progesterone → androgens → estrogens.
    • Androgens (雄激素) — mainly testosterone. More important in males.
    • Estrogens (雌激素) — mainly 17-β-estradiol. More important in females.
    • Progesterone (黃體酮) — more important in females.
  2. Peptide hormones from pituitary — oxytocin and prolactin (childbirth/lactation).

Both sexes have androgens AND estrogens — differing only in amount.

Exam trap: Estrogens come FROM androgens (aromatase converts). Androgens are NOT male-only.

2. Male reproductive system

External organs: penis + scrotum housing two testes. Scrotum is outside abdomen because sperm production needs <body temperature.

Testis structure:

  • Seminiferous tubules (生精小管) — site of spermatogenesis. Contain:
    • Germ cells — divide and differentiate into sperm.
    • Sertoli cells (支持細胞) — supporting cells. Functions: (a) form blood-testis barrier, (b) produce seminiferous tubular fluid that nourishes germ cells, (c) synthesize inhibin, (d) respond to FSH.
  • Leydig cells (萊狄希氏細胞) — interstitial cells between tubules. Produce testosterone in response to LH.

Average sperm count: ~60 million/ml. Fertility markedly impaired below 35 million/ml.

Spermatogenesis takes weeks; final maturation occurs in epididymis. Sperm transported via vas deferens through inguinal canal → urethra. Seminal vesicles add 60% of semen fluid (fructose, prostaglandins, coagulating proteins); prostate adds alkaline fluid neutralizing acid.

Neuroendocrine control of spermatogenesis:

  • Hypothalamus → GnRH → pituitary → FSH + LH.
  • LH → Leydig → testosterone (required for spermatogenesis + 2nd sex characteristics).
  • FSH → Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis (with testosterone).
  • Negative feedback: testosterone inhibits GnRH/LH/FSH; inhibin from Sertoli inhibits FSH specifically.

Stress, protracted aerobic exercise, visual/olfactory/tactile/psychological inputs all modulate GnRH.

Actions of testosterone:

  1. Prenatal: triggers primary male sex characteristic development.
  2. Puberty: enlargement of penis/scrotum/testes; secondary sex characteristics (facial hair, voice).
  3. Spermatogenesis.
  4. General growth — especially muscle protein synthesis (anabolic).
  5. Behavioral — aggression.
Exam trap: Leydig cells secrete androgens; do NOT secrete inhibin (that's Sertoli). Sertoli cells bind FSH not LH.

3. Female reproductive system

Organs: vulva (external) + vagina, uterus, uterine tubes (oviducts/fallopian tubes), two ovaries.

  • Ovaries (卵巢) — produce ova + estrogens + progesterone within follicles.
  • Oviduct (fallopian tube) — site of fertilization; transports ovum from ovary to uterus.
  • Uterus — muscular organ for implantation/fetal development. Endometrium = glandular layer.

Oogenesis: in mid-ovulatory cycle (~28 days) one ovum is expelled from one ovary. ~400-500 mature ova in a woman's lifetime (puberty → menopause). Follicle = oocyte + supporting cells. After ovulation, follicle becomes corpus luteum (黃體).

Exam trap: Site of fertilization is the OVIDUCT (fallopian tube), not the uterus. Uterus is for implantation.

4. The reproductive cycle (~28 days)

Two synchronized cycles:

5. Actions of estrogen and progesterone

Estrogen:

  • Growth/development: stimulates 1st and 2nd sex characteristics; stimulates long bone growth + early epiphyseal closure.
  • Hormonal: stimulates GnRH/LH/FSH release during follicular phase; inhibits same during luteal.
  • Metabolic: ↓insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues; protects against bone loss.

Gestational diabetes (妊娠糖尿病) — placental estrogen (and other hormones) reduce insulin sensitivity. Usually appears at 20-24 weeks. Pancreas normally compensates by making more insulin; gestational diabetes appears when it cannot.

Progesterone:

  • Fully develops endometrium (prepares for pregnancy).
  • Further mammary gland development.
  • Negative feedback to hypothalamus → prevents ovulation during pregnancy.
  • Antagonizes testosterone/dihydrotestosterone — prevents androgen response in females.
Exam trap: Gestational diabetes is caused by HORMONES (estrogen, etc.), NOT by eating too much. Estrogen causes early epiphyseal closure — explains why women stop growing earlier than men.

6. Contraceptives and IVF

Combined oral contraceptive ("the pill"): estrogen + progestogen. 99.7% effective. Mechanism: suppresses release of gonadotropins → no ovulation; also increases cervical mucus viscosity → blocks sperm. First-generation pill: FDA-approved 1960.

IVF (in vitro fertilization, 體外受精): Robert Edwards (Nobel 2010) and Patrick Steptoe. Louise Brown was the first IVF baby (1978). IVF protocol uses FSH for ovarian hyperstimulation (multiple follicles) and hCG to trigger final oocyte maturation. Now >8 million babies born via IVF.

Exam trap: Contraceptive pills PREVENT ovulation by suppressing gonadotropin release — they do not "kill" sperm directly.

  • Site of fertilization = oviduct, NOT uterus.
  • Estrogen at high mid-cycle = POSITIVE feedback (LH surge).
  • Inhibin from follicle/Sertoli specifically inhibits FSH only.
  • hCG rescues the corpus luteum during pregnancy.
  • Gestational diabetes = hormonal insulin resistance, not overeating.
  • Ovulation is prevented by HIGH estrogen + progesterone (i.e. "the pill").

Top things to memorise

  1. GnRH → FSH/LH; LH → Leydig → testosterone; FSH → Sertoli → spermatogenesis.
  2. Inhibin (Sertoli/follicle) inhibits FSH selectively.
  3. 4 phases of female cycle: follicular, ovulation, luteal + uterine menses/proliferative/secretory.
  4. Estrogen feedback: negative usually; positive at mid-cycle → LH surge.
  5. Corpus luteum makes progesterone (and some estrogen) in luteal phase.
  6. hCG from placenta rescues corpus luteum.
  7. Estrogen effects: bone growth + early epiphyseal closure; insulin resistance.
  8. Progesterone effects: prepare endometrium, prevent further ovulation.
  9. Pill = estrogen + progestogen, blocks gonadotropins.
  10. IVF: FSH for hyperstimulation, hCG for final maturation.

  2. What rescues the corpus luteum during pregnancy? — hCG.
  3. Where does fertilization occur? — Oviduct (fallopian tube).
  4. How do contraceptive pills prevent pregnancy? — Suppress gonadotropin release → no ovulation.
  5. What causes gestational diabetes? — Hormonal insulin resistance (placental estrogen).

Lecture 6: GI Physiology I — Overview & Upper GI

The gastrointestinal system (消化系統) is the body's nutrient processing line: it ingests food, breaks it down mechanically and chemically, absorbs the small-molecule products, and expels the rest. Every step is tightly regulated by a combination of the enteric nervous system (腸道神經系統), autonomic nerves, and GI hormones. Lecture 6 covers the big picture — GI functions, the general structure of the GI wall, salivary glands and oral digestion, swallowing, the stomach (gastric secretion, HCl production, pepsinogen, intrinsic factor), and the neurohormonal phases of gastric secretion (cephalic, gastric, intestinal). Understanding how HCl is made and regulated is a favorite exam target.

1. General Functions and Structure of the GI Tract

The GI tract (消化道) performs five main functions: motility (運動) — moving contents by peristalsis and segmentation; secretion (分泌) — enzymes, acids, bile, mucus; digestion (消化) — breaking macromolecules into absorbable monomers; absorption (吸收) — transporting nutrients into blood or lymph; and excretion (排泄) — eliminating undigested residue.

The wall of the GI tract has a consistent four-layer organization from inside out: mucosa (黏膜) — epithelium + lamina propria + muscularis mucosae; submucosa (黏膜下層) — contains Meissner's submucosal plexus (麥氏神經叢); muscularis externa (肌層外層) — inner circular + outer longitudinal smooth muscle, containing Auerbach's myenteric plexus (奥氏肌間神經叢) between the layers; serosa/adventitia (漿膜) — outer connective tissue covering.

The enteric nervous system (ENS, 腸道神經系統) is an independent neural network — ~100 million neurons — embedded in the gut wall, capable of coordinating digestion independently of the CNS. It is often called the "second brain." Parasympathetic (vagus 迷走神經, pelvic nerves) activation generally stimulates GI activity; sympathetic inhibits it.

Exam trap: Meissner's plexus is in the submucosa (controls secretion/absorption); Auerbach's plexus is between the muscle layers (controls motility). Do not mix them up.

2. Salivary Glands and Oral Digestion

Three pairs of major salivary glands (唾液腺): parotid (腮腺) — largest, produces watery serous secretion rich in amylase; submandibular (頜下腺) — mixed serous + mucous; sublingual (舌下腺) — mainly mucous secretion.

Saliva (唾液) functions: moistens food to form a bolus (食團); lubricates with mucins (黏蛋白); begins carbohydrate digestion with salivary amylase (唾液澱粉酶, optimal pH ~7); protects oral mucosa with IgA and lysozyme; buffers acids; dissolves food chemicals for taste. About 1–1.5 L/day is produced.

Salivation (分泌唾液) is controlled entirely by the autonomic nervous system — it has no hormonal control. Parasympathetic (via chorda tympani 鼓索神經 to submandibular/sublingual, and via glossopharyngeal 舌咽神經 to parotid) causes copious watery secretion. Sympathetic produces small amounts of viscous, protein-rich saliva.

Exam trap: Salivary amylase works optimally at neutral pH (~7) in the mouth. Stomach acid inactivates it — so starch digestion in the stomach is minimal.

3. Swallowing (Deglutition)

Swallowing (吞嚥) has three phases: oral (voluntary, tongue pushes bolus to pharynx); pharyngeal (involuntary reflex, soft palate closes nasopharynx, epiglottis closes larynx, upper esophageal sphincter opens); esophageal (peristaltic wave carries bolus to stomach, lower esophageal sphincter / LES 下食道括約肌 relaxes).

The swallowing center in the medulla oblongata (延髓) coordinates the pharyngeal and esophageal phases. Once initiated, swallowing is entirely involuntary. The LES normally remains closed (preventing reflux 胃食道逆流) and relaxes only when a peristaltic wave arrives.

Exam trap: The epiglottis (會厭) covers the larynx during swallowing to prevent aspiration — not the trachea directly. Failure leads to aspiration pneumonia.

4. Stomach Structure and Function

The stomach (胃) has four regions: cardia (賁門), fundus (胃底), body/corpus (胃體), and pyloric antrum (幽門竇). The inner surface is lined by rugae (胃皺襞) that flatten as the stomach fills. The stomach's main functions: store food; churn it into chyme (食糜); begin protein digestion; regulate rate of delivery to the small intestine.

5. HCl Production by Parietal Cells

Parietal cells (壁細胞) secrete HCl into the stomach lumen at a pH as low as 1–2. The mechanism involves a dedicated H⁺/K⁺-ATPase proton pump (氫鉀離子幫浦) on the apical membrane. Steps:

  1. CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ (catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase 碳酸酐酶 inside the cell)
  2. H₂CO₃ → H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
  3. H⁺ pumped into lumen by H⁺/K⁺-ATPase (K⁺ enters cell)
  4. HCO₃⁻ exits basolaterally in exchange for Cl⁻ (alkaline tide in blood)
  5. Cl⁻ exits apically through chloride channels alongside H⁺ → HCl in lumen

HCl functions: activates pepsinogen → pepsin (胃蛋白酶); denatures proteins; kills most ingested bacteria; provides optimal pH (~2) for pepsin activity; aids iron (Fe³⁺ → Fe²⁺) and calcium absorption.

Proton pump inhibitors (PPIs, 質子泵抑制劑) like omeprazole irreversibly block H⁺/K⁺-ATPase — first-line treatment for peptic ulcer disease and GERD.

Exam trap: The "alkaline tide" — after a meal, blood becomes transiently more alkaline because HCO₃⁻ floods out of parietal cells. Urine can also transiently become alkaline.

Gastrin (胃泌素) from G cells is the key hormone of the gastric phase. Stimulated by: vagal input (ACh), protein/peptides in stomach, stomach distension. Inhibited by: low luminal pH (negative feedback), somatostatin (D cells), secretin.

Histamine from ECL cells acts locally on parietal cell H₂ receptors (H₂受體) → cAMP → HCl secretion. This is why H₂-blockers (e.g. ranitidine) reduce acid output.

Exam trap: The intestinal phase starts as stimulatory but quickly becomes the main INHIBITORY brake on gastric secretion once acid, fat, and hypertonic solutions enter the duodenum.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Meissner's plexus = submucosa (secretion); Auerbach's = between muscle layers (motility).
  • Salivary amylase works at pH ~7 and is inactivated in the stomach.
  • Intrinsic factor comes from parietal cells, not chief cells.
  • HCl production by parietal cells creates an "alkaline tide" in blood.
  • The intestinal phase starts stimulatory but turns inhibitory — the net effect on gastric secretion is inhibition.
  • PPIs block H⁺/K⁺-ATPase irreversibly; H₂-blockers block histamine receptors on parietal cells.
Top things to memorize
  1. Four GI wall layers and which plexus is where (Meissner's = submucosa, Auerbach's = between muscles).
  2. Six gastric cell types and their secretions — especially parietal cells make HCl AND intrinsic factor.
  3. HCl production mechanism: H⁺/K⁺-ATPase, carbonic anhydrase, alkaline tide.
  4. Three phases of gastric secretion: cephalic (30%), gastric (60%), intestinal (10% → inhibitory).
  5. Gastrin stimulates HCl; somatostatin and low pH inhibit it; histamine acts via H₂ receptors.

  • Detailed anatomy of individual salivary gland ducts.

  • Historical names of GI physiologists.

Lecture 7: GI Physiology II — Small Intestine & Absorption

The small intestine (小腸) is where nearly all nutrient absorption happens. Its enormous absorptive surface — amplified by circular folds (環狀皺褶), villi (絨毛), and microvilli (微絨毛) — means that a 6-metre tube has an effective area of ~200 m². Lecture 7 covers the structural adaptations for absorption, pancreatic and biliary secretions that prepare chyme for digestion, the hormones that coordinate these secretions (secretin and CCK), and the specific mechanisms for absorbing carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, water, and key vitamins. The fat absorption pathway (micelle → chylomicron → lacteal) is an especially high-yield topic.

1. Small Intestine Structure and Surface Amplification

Three structural amplifications multiply the absorptive surface ~600-fold over a simple cylinder:

  1. Circular folds (環狀皺褶, plicae circulares) — large mucosal folds visible to the naked eye; increase surface ~3×
  2. Villi (絨毛) — finger-like projections of mucosa; each contains a capillary network and a central lymph vessel called a lacteal (乳糜管); increase surface ~10×
  3. Microvilli (微絨毛) on the apical surface of enterocytes — form the brush border (刷狀緣); increase surface ~20×

The brush border contains digestive enzymes: lactase, maltase, sucrase, aminopeptidases. These complete final digestion at the absorptive surface itself.

The small intestine has three segments: duodenum (十二指腸, ~25 cm) — receives chyme + bile + pancreatic juice; jejunum (空腸, ~2.5 m) — main site of nutrient absorption; ileum (迴腸, ~3.5 m) — absorbs bile salts (膽鹽) and vitamin B12.

Exam trap: Vitamin B12 absorption requires intrinsic factor and occurs specifically in the terminal ileum — not the jejunum.

2. Pancreatic and Biliary Secretions

The exocrine pancreas (外分泌胰腺) secretes ~1.5 L/day of pancreatic juice (胰液) into the duodenum via the pancreatic duct (胰管). It contains:

  • Bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) — from ductal cells; neutralizes stomach acid in duodenum; creates optimal pH (~7–8) for pancreatic enzymes
  • Digestive enzymes — from acinar cells (腺泡細胞): pancreatic amylase (胰澱粉酶), lipase (脂肪酶, requires co-lipase), colipase, nucleases, and proteases secreted as inactive zymogens (酶原): trypsinogen → trypsin (by enterokinase/enteropeptidase 腸激酶); chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, procarboxypeptidase (all activated by trypsin)

Bile (膽汁) is made continuously by the liver (~500–1000 mL/day) and stored/concentrated in the gallbladder (膽囊). Bile components: bile salts (膽鹽), cholesterol, phospholipids (lecithin), bilirubin (膽紅素), water, and electrolytes. Bile salts emulsify fats — breaking large fat droplets into smaller ones to increase the surface area for lipase action. They also form micelles (微胞) with fatty acids and monoglycerides to solubilize them for absorption.

Exam trap: Bile does NOT contain digestive enzymes — it emulsifies fats but does not digest them. Lipase is from the pancreas.

3. Hormonal Regulation: Secretin and CCK

Two major hormones from duodenal enteroendocrine cells regulate digestive secretions:

HormoneStimulusSourceKey Actions
Secretin (胰泌素)Acid (low pH) in duodenumS cells (duodenum)Stimulates pancreatic HCO₃⁻ secretion; stimulates bile secretion; INHIBITS gastric acid and motility
CCK (cholecystokinin, 縮膽囊素)Fat + protein in duodenumI cells (duodenum/jejunum)Stimulates pancreatic enzyme secretion; causes gallbladder contraction; relaxes sphincter of Oddi; INHIBITS gastric emptying

GIP (gastric inhibitory polypeptide, or glucose-dependent insulinotropic polypeptide) from K cells is released by fat and glucose. It inhibits gastric secretion and stimulates insulin release — part of the incretin effect (腸促胰素效應).

Exam trap: Secretin responds to ACID; CCK responds to FAT and PROTEIN. Mix-up of these two is a classic multiple-choice trap.

4. Carbohydrate and Protein Absorption

Carbohydrates: Salivary and pancreatic amylase cleave starch to maltose/oligosaccharides. Brush border enzymes (maltase, sucrase, lactase) hydrolyze disaccharides to monosaccharides. Glucose and galactose are absorbed by SGLT1 (sodium-glucose linked transporter, Na⁺-dependent secondary active transport 鈉依賴性主動運輸) on the apical membrane; exit basolaterally via GLUT2. Fructose enters by GLUT5 (facilitated diffusion 促進擴散) and also exits via GLUT2.

Exam trap: Glucose uses SGLT1 (active, Na⁺-coupled) to enter enterocytes. Fructose uses GLUT5 (passive). Both exit via GLUT2.

Proteins: Pepsin (partial), then pancreatic proteases (trypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, carboxypeptidase) break proteins to small peptides and amino acids. Brush border aminopeptidases and dipeptidases complete digestion. Amino acids and small peptides enter enterocytes via Na⁺-coupled amino acid transporters or H⁺-coupled peptide transporter PepT1. Inside the cell, peptidases complete hydrolysis; amino acids exit via basolateral transporters → portal blood.

5. Fat Absorption and Chylomicrons

Dietary triglycerides (三酸甘油酯) must be emulsified (by bile salts) and hydrolyzed by pancreatic lipase + colipase into fatty acids (脂肪酸) and monoglycerides (單酸甘油酯). These are incorporated into micelles (膽鹽微胞) with bile salts, cholesterol, and fat-soluble vitamins — a temporary water-soluble delivery vehicle.

At the brush border, fatty acids and monoglycerides diffuse out of micelles and across the lipid bilayer into enterocytes (lipid-soluble, no transporter needed). Inside the enterocyte, they are re-synthesized into triglycerides in the ER, then packaged with cholesterol and apolipoproteins into chylomicrons (乳糜微粒) by the Golgi. Chylomicrons are too large for capillaries — they are secreted by exocytosis into the lacteal (乳糜管), enter the lymphatic system, and eventually reach blood via the thoracic duct.

Short- and medium-chain fatty acids (fewer than ~12 carbons) are water-soluble enough to enter capillaries directly (bypassing lacteal) and travel in portal blood.

Exam trap: Long-chain fatty acids → chylomicrons → lymphatics (lacteal). Short/medium-chain fatty acids → portal blood directly. Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) travel with chylomicrons, so fat malabsorption causes these deficiencies.

6. Water, Mineral, and Vitamin Absorption

About 8–9 L of water enters the GI tract daily (1.5 L ingested + 7+ L from secretions). ~6 L is absorbed in the small intestine, ~1.5 L in the colon. Water follows osmotic gradients created by solute absorption — primarily Na⁺. Na⁺ is absorbed by multiple mechanisms: Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger, Na⁺/nutrient cotransporters (SGLT1), and in the colon by aldosterone-regulated ENaC channels.

Iron (鐵): absorbed primarily in the duodenum as Fe²⁺ (ferrous). Stomach HCl reduces Fe³⁺ to Fe²⁺; vitamin C also helps. Absorbed via DMT1 transporter. Stored as ferritin (鐵蛋白) in enterocytes; exported to blood via ferroportin regulated by hepcidin (鐵調素).

Calcium (鈣): mainly duodenum and jejunum. Active absorption requires vitamin D (1,25-dihydroxyvitamin D₃ / calcitriol 骨化三醇) which upregulates calbindin, TRPV6 channel, and Ca²⁺-ATPase. PTH indirectly raises absorption by stimulating calcitriol production in the kidney.

Vitamin B12: binds intrinsic factor (from gastric parietal cells), complex absorbed in terminal ileum via specific receptors.

Fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K): absorbed with dietary fat in micelles; travel via chylomicrons.

Exam Traps Summary
  • Bile emulsifies fat but contains NO digestive enzymes — lipase is from the pancreas.
  • Secretin → acid stimulus; CCK → fat + protein stimulus.
  • Glucose enters enterocytes via SGLT1 (Na⁺-dependent, active); fructose via GLUT5 (passive). Both exit via GLUT2.
  • Long-chain fat → chylomicrons → lacteals → lymphatics. Short/medium-chain → portal blood.
  • Vitamin B12 must bind intrinsic factor; absorbed in terminal ileum only.
  • Iron absorbed as Fe²⁺ in duodenum; vitamin D essential for calcium absorption.
Top things to memorize
  1. Three surface amplifiers of small intestine: circular folds → villi → microvilli (brush border).
  2. Secretin (acid) vs CCK (fat + protein) — stimuli and actions.
  3. Glucose/galactose: SGLT1 in; GLUT2 out. Fructose: GLUT5 in; GLUT2 out.
  4. Fat absorption pathway: emulsification → micelle → diffusion into enterocyte → chylomicron → lacteal → lymphatics.
  5. Vitamin B12 + intrinsic factor → terminal ileum. Fat-soluble vitamins with fat absorption.

  • Exact daily volumes of each secretion (bile 500–1000 mL, pancreatic juice 1.5 L).
  • Full list of pancreatic zymogens beyond trypsin/chymotrypsin.
  • Detailed bilirubin metabolism pathway.
  • Exact hepcidin-ferroportin regulatory circuit beyond conceptual level.

Lecture 8: GI Physiology III — Large Intestine, Liver & Pancreas

Lecture 8 covers the large intestine (大腸) — its role in water reclamation and fecal formation — and dives deeper into the liver (肝臟) and exocrine pancreas (外分泌胰腺). The liver is not just a digestive organ; it is the body's chief metabolic hub: detoxifying drugs and ammonia, making plasma proteins, processing nutrients from the portal blood, synthesizing bile, and storing glycogen. The exocrine pancreas ties digestion together by supplying the enzyme cocktail that digests all three macronutrient classes. Key exam themes: hepatic first-pass metabolism, bilirubin processing, liver failure consequences, and pancreatitis.

1. The Large Intestine

The large intestine (大腸) extends from the ileocecal valve (迴盲瓣) to the anus (~1.5 m). Segments: cecum (盲腸), ascending colon → transverse → descending → sigmoid colon (乙狀結腸), rectum, anal canal. The appendix (闌尾) is attached to the cecum.

Main functions of the colon:

  • Water and electrolyte absorption: ~1.5 L of the 2 L entering the colon is absorbed. Colon can absorb up to 5–6 L/day under maximum stimulation. Na⁺ absorbed via aldosterone-regulated ENaC channels; water follows osmotically.
  • Storage and compaction of feces (糞便).
  • Microbial fermentation: colonic bacteria ferment undigested carbohydrates → short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs: acetate, propionate, butyrate 丁酸) — energy source for colonocytes; also produce vitamin K and some B vitamins.
  • Defecation (排便): mass movements (集體蠕動) propel contents into rectum → rectal distension → urge → defecation reflex.

Colon motility: haustral contractions (袋狀收縮) shuffle contents; mass movements (集體蠕動) 3–4 times/day propel material rapidly. The gastrocolic reflex (胃結腸反射) is triggered by food entering the stomach → increases colonic motility.

Exam trap: The colon does NOT absorb significant nutrients — its primary role is water/electrolyte reclamation. Nutrient absorption is almost entirely complete before chyme reaches the colon.

2. Liver Structure and Metabolic Functions

The liver (肝臟) receives dual blood supply: ~75% from the portal vein (門靜脈) carrying nutrient-rich blood from the gut; ~25% from the hepatic artery (肝動脈) carrying oxygenated blood. Blood drains via hepatic veins to the inferior vena cava.

Structural unit: the hepatic lobule (肝小葉), with a central vein and portal triads at the periphery. Hepatocytes (肝細胞) are arranged in plates separated by sinusoids (肝血竇) — specialized capillaries lined with Kupffer cells (庫弗氏細胞, liver macrophages) and endothelium. Bile canaliculi (膽小管) run between adjacent hepatocytes, draining bile to bile ducts.

3. Bilirubin Metabolism

Bilirubin (膽紅素) is the breakdown product of heme (血紅素) from aged RBCs. Steps:

  1. Heme → biliverdin → unconjugated bilirubin (非結合膽紅素) in macrophages (mostly spleen)
  2. Unconjugated bilirubin is lipid-soluble, toxic, bound to albumin in blood — carried to liver
  3. Liver hepatocytes conjugate bilirubin with glucuronate (via UDP-glucuronosyltransferase) → conjugated (direct) bilirubin (結合膽紅素) — water-soluble, excreted in bile
  4. In the gut, bacteria convert conjugated bilirubin → urobilinogen (尿膽素原) → stercobilin (糞膽素, brown feces color) or reabsorbed → urobilin (尿膽素, yellow urine color)

Jaundice (黃疸) types:

  • Pre-hepatic (溶血性): excess RBC hemolysis → high unconjugated bilirubin
  • Hepatic (肝細胞性): liver cell damage → impaired conjugation → mixed
  • Post-hepatic / obstructive (阻塞性): bile duct blocked → backed-up conjugated bilirubin enters blood; pale stool, dark urine

Exam trap: Obstructive jaundice: DARK urine (conjugated bilirubin excreted by kidney) + PALE stool (no bilirubin in gut). Hemolytic jaundice: dark stool (excess urobilinogen), normal-to-yellow urine.

4. Hepatic First-Pass Metabolism and Drug Metabolism

First-pass metabolism (首過效應): drugs absorbed from the gut pass through the portal vein directly to the liver before reaching systemic circulation. The liver's cytochrome P450 (CYP) enzyme system (細胞色素P450酶系) metabolizes many drugs, reducing their bioavailability. This is why oral doses of some drugs are much higher than IV doses (e.g. morphine, nitroglycerin).

Enterohepatic circulation (腸肝循環) of bile salts: ~95% of secreted bile salts are reabsorbed in the terminal ileum and returned to the liver via portal blood. The liver re-secretes them into bile. The body's ~3–5 g pool circulates 6–10 times/day. Cholestyramine interrupts this cycle by binding bile salts in the gut.

Exam trap: Nitroglycerin is given sublingually (舌下) specifically to BYPASS first-pass metabolism. If swallowed, the liver destroys most of it before it can act on blood vessels.

5. Exocrine Pancreas and Pancreatitis

The exocrine pancreas (外分泌胰腺) consists of acinar cells (腺泡細胞) that produce enzymes, and ductal cells (導管細胞) that secrete bicarbonate-rich fluid. Trypsinogen (胰蛋白酶原) is activated by enterokinase (腸激酶, also called enteropeptidase) on the brush border of the duodenum → trypsin, which then activates all other proteases. This cascade prevents autodigestion of the pancreas itself.

Protective mechanisms against autodigestion: enzymes stored as zymogens (酶原); trypsin inhibitor (胰蛋白酶抑制劑) present in acinar cells; enzyme activation only occurs in the duodenum.

Acute pancreatitis (急性胰腺炎): premature activation of enzymes inside the pancreas → autodigestion. Causes: gallstones blocking pancreatic duct, alcohol. Signs: severe epigastric pain radiating to back, elevated serum amylase and lipase.

Exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (外分泌胰腺功能不全): insufficient enzyme secretion → fat malabsorption (steatorrhea 脂肪瀉), protein malabsorption, weight loss. Treat with oral pancreatic enzyme replacement.

Exam trap: Elevated serum amylase also occurs in salivary gland disease — so amylase alone is not specific for pancreatitis. Lipase is more specific for pancreatic inflammation.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Colon absorbs water/electrolytes, NOT nutrients (absorption is essentially complete in small intestine).
  • Liver makes albumin but NOT immunoglobulins; low albumin → edema (↓ oncotic pressure).
  • Obstructive jaundice: dark urine + pale stool. Hemolytic: normal/dark stool, not dark urine.
  • Nitroglycerin given sublingually to bypass first-pass hepatic metabolism.
  • Pancreatitis: elevated lipase is more specific than amylase for pancreatitis diagnosis.
  • Trypsin activates all other pancreatic proteases; enterokinase activates trypsinogen.
Top things to memorize
  1. Liver dual blood supply: portal vein (75%, nutrients) + hepatic artery (25%, O₂).
  2. Bilirubin types: unconjugated (pre-hepatic, lipid-soluble) vs conjugated (post-hepatic, water-soluble).
  3. Jaundice types and stool/urine color changes — especially obstructive (dark urine, pale stool).
  4. First-pass metabolism via CYP450 in liver; enterohepatic circulation of bile salts.
  5. Trypsinogen → trypsin (by enterokinase); trypsin then activates other zymogens.
Lower-yield / skip if short on time:
  • Specific CYP450 isoforms and their drug substrates.
  • Exact numbers: bile salt pool size, circulation frequency.
  • Detailed portal hypertension complications beyond varices/ascites concept.
  • Vitamin K synthesis by gut bacteria (know it happens; details not tested).

Lecture 9: Neuroscience I — Neurons, Glia & Membrane Potentials

The nervous system communicates through electrical signals — rapid, precise, and short-lived. Lecture 9 builds the foundation: the structure of neurons (神經元) and their classification, the supporting roles of glial cells (神經膠細胞), the ionic basis of the resting membrane potential (靜息膜電位), and the generation and propagation of the action potential (動作電位). These concepts underpin everything in neuroscience: how signals travel from sensory organs to the brain, how motor commands reach muscles, and how drugs like local anaesthetics and tetrodotoxin work. The Hodgkin-Huxley model of the action potential is a central exam target.

1. Neuron Structure and Classification

A neuron (神經元) has four functional regions: dendrites (樹突) — receive input; cell body / soma (細胞體) — integrates signals; axon (軸突) — conducts impulses; axon terminal / synaptic bouton (突觸小體) — transmits to the next cell. The point where axon begins is the axon hillock (軸丘) — site of action potential initiation (lowest threshold).

Structural classification:

  • Unipolar (單極神經元): one process; sensory neurons in dorsal root ganglia
  • Bipolar (雙極神經元): two processes; retina, cochlea
  • Multipolar (多極神經元): many dendrites, one axon; most CNS neurons
Functional classification: sensory (afferent 傳入), interneurons (聯絡神經元), motor (efferent 傳出).

Exam trap: The axon hillock has the lowest threshold for action potential initiation — this is where the decision to "fire" is made by summation of all inputs.

2. Glial Cells

Glial cells (神經膠細胞) are non-neuronal support cells — ~10× more numerous than neurons in some regions. They do NOT generate action potentials but are essential for neural function.

3. Resting Membrane Potential

At rest, the inside of a neuron is negative relative to outside — typically −70 mV (resting membrane potential, RMP 靜息膜電位). This is established by:

  1. Unequal ion distribution: K⁺ high inside, Na⁺/Cl⁻/Ca²⁺ high outside (maintained by Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase 鈉鉀泵 — pumps 3 Na⁺ out and 2 K⁺ in per cycle, consuming ATP).
  2. Selective permeability: the resting membrane is most permeable to K⁺ (via K⁺ leak channels 鉀離子漏通道). K⁺ diffuses out down its concentration gradient, leaving negative charges behind → inside becomes negative.
  3. Large intracellular anions (proteins, phosphates) that cannot cross the membrane contribute to negativity.

The Nernst equation (能斯特方程式) predicts equilibrium potential for a single ion. The Goldman equation (高曼方程式) integrates permeabilities of multiple ions to predict the actual membrane potential. For the RMP, K⁺ dominates — predicted ~−90 mV; Na⁺ permeability pulls it to ~−70 mV.

Exam trap: Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pumps 3 Na⁺ OUT and 2 K⁺ IN — electrogenic (contributes a small extra negative charge inside). Its main role is maintaining ion gradients over time, not instant RMP generation.

4. Action Potential — Generation

An action potential (AP, 動作電位) is a rapid, stereotyped reversal of membrane potential triggered when the membrane is depolarized to threshold (閾電位, approximately −55 mV). Phases:

  1. Resting: −70 mV. Voltage-gated Na⁺ channels (電壓依賴性鈉通道) closed.
  2. Depolarization (去極化): stimulus opens Na⁺ channels → Na⁺ rushes in → inside rapidly becomes positive (peaks ~+30 to +40 mV). Positive feedback: depolarization opens more Na⁺ channels.
  3. Repolarization (復極化): Na⁺ channels inactivate (閉鎖); voltage-gated K⁺ channels open → K⁺ rushes out → membrane returns toward negative.
  4. After-hyperpolarization (超極化): K⁺ channels slow to close → membrane briefly more negative than rest (~−80 mV) → absolute refractory period (絕對不應期) then relative refractory period (相對不應期).
  5. Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase restores ion gradients (slowly, not needed for each AP).
Exam trap: During the absolute refractory period, NO new AP can be generated regardless of stimulus strength. This ensures unidirectional propagation and limits firing frequency.

5. Action Potential Propagation and Myelination

An AP propagates along the axon by local currents: depolarization at one node depolarizes the adjacent membrane, triggering an AP there. Because of the refractory period, propagation is unidirectional (向前傳導). Speed depends on axon diameter and myelination.

Myelin sheath (髓鞘): formed by Schwann cells (PNS) or oligodendrocytes (CNS). Myelin insulates the axon, so ion exchange only happens at nodes of Ranvier (蘭氏結). Action potential "jumps" between nodes — saltatory conduction (跳躍傳導) — much faster than continuous conduction. Myelinated axons: 70–120 m/s; unmyelinated C fibers: 0.5–2 m/s.

Multiple sclerosis (MS, 多發性硬化症): autoimmune destruction of CNS myelin (oligodendrocytes) → slowed/blocked conduction → diverse neurological symptoms (vision loss, weakness, sensory loss).

Exam trap: Tetrodotoxin (TTX 河豚毒素) blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels → prevents AP generation → paralysis. Local anesthetics (lidocaine 利多卡因) also block Na⁺ channels to produce local numbness.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Axon hillock = lowest threshold = site of AP initiation (not dendrites).
  • Oligodendrocytes myelinate multiple CNS axons; Schwann cells myelinate one PNS axon segment.
  • Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pumps 3 Na⁺ out / 2 K⁺ in — electrogenic but mainly maintains gradients.
  • Absolute refractory period → no new AP possible; ensures unidirectional propagation.
  • TTX blocks voltage-gated Na⁺ channels; local anesthetics act by the same mechanism.
Top things to memorize
  1. Resting membrane potential = −70 mV; threshold = −55 mV; peak AP = +30 to +40 mV.
  2. Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase: 3 Na⁺ out, 2 K⁺ in (electrogenic).
  3. AP phases: depolarization (Na⁺ in) → repolarization (Na⁺ inactivates, K⁺ out) → after-hyperpolarization.
  4. Saltatory conduction at nodes of Ranvier — myelination speeds conduction.
  5. Glial cell functions: astrocytes (BBB, K⁺ buffering); oligodendrocytes (CNS myelin); Schwann cells (PNS myelin); microglia (immune).
Lower-yield / skip if short on time:
  • Exact Nernst/Goldman equation mathematics — understand concepts, not formula derivation.
  • All subclasses of voltage-gated ion channels beyond Na⁺ and K⁺.
  • Detailed MS pathology beyond the demyelination concept.
  • Historical Hodgkin-Huxley experiments — know the model, not the experimental details.

Lecture 10: Neuroscience II — Synaptic Transmission & Neural Circuits

Neurons talk to each other at synapses (突觸). Lecture 10 covers how electrical signals are converted to chemical signals and back: the structure of a chemical synapse, the sequence of neurotransmitter release (exocytosis driven by Ca²⁺ influx), how postsynaptic potentials summate to reach threshold, the major neurotransmitters (acetylcholine, glutamate, GABA, dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine), their receptor types, and the principles of neural circuits (convergence, divergence, reverberating loops). Understanding EPSP vs IPSP, spatial/temporal summation, and how drugs/toxins modify synaptic transmission is the high-yield core of this lecture.

1. Synapse Structure and Types

A synapse (突觸) is the junction where a neuron transmits a signal to another cell (neuron, muscle, or gland). Two types:

  • Electrical synapses (電突觸): gap junctions (間隙連接) directly connect cytoplasm of adjacent cells → instantaneous, bidirectional, rare in CNS (found in cardiac/smooth muscle, some brain regions for synchrony).
  • Chemical synapses (化學突觸): most common in CNS. Presynaptic terminal releases neurotransmitter into the synaptic cleft (突觸間隙, ~20 nm); postsynaptic receptor binds it → opens ion channels → changes membrane potential. Slower but amplifiable and modulatable.

Anatomy of a chemical synapse: presynaptic terminal contains synaptic vesicles (突觸小泡) full of neurotransmitter, docked at active zones near voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels. The postsynaptic density (突觸後緻密帶) on the receiving cell is packed with receptors.

2. Neurotransmitter Release — Calcium-Dependent Exocytosis

Steps of synaptic transmission:

  1. AP arrives at presynaptic terminal → depolarizes membrane
  2. Voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels (電壓依賴性鈣通道) open → Ca²⁺ flows in (intracellular Ca²⁺ is very low, ~100 nM at rest)
  3. Ca²⁺ binds synaptotagmin (突觸結合蛋白) on vesicles → triggers SNARE complex (可溶性NSF受體蛋白) to fuse vesicle with presynaptic membrane → exocytosis of neurotransmitter
  4. Neurotransmitter diffuses across ~20 nm cleft → binds postsynaptic receptors
  5. Termination: reuptake by transporters (再攝取), enzymatic degradation (酶降解), or diffusion away

Botulinum toxin (肉毒桿菌毒素, Botox) cleaves SNARE proteins → blocks vesicle fusion → no neurotransmitter release → flaccid paralysis. Used therapeutically for wrinkles, dystonia, hyperhidrosis.

Exam trap: Neurotransmitter release requires Ca²⁺ influx — block Ca²⁺ channels (e.g. aminoglycosides in Lambert-Eaton syndrome) → reduced neurotransmitter release → weakness.

3. Postsynaptic Potentials and Summation

Neurotransmitter binding causes postsynaptic potentials (突觸後電位) — small, graded (not all-or-none) changes in membrane potential:

  • EPSP (excitatory postsynaptic potential, 興奮性突觸後電位): depolarization (membrane becomes less negative) → moves toward threshold → increases chance of AP
  • IPSP (inhibitory postsynaptic potential, 抑制性突觸後電位): hyperpolarization (membrane more negative) or stabilization at resting potential → moves away from threshold → decreases chance of AP

A single EPSP is usually insufficient to trigger an AP. Summation (加成) is needed:

  • Temporal summation (時間加成): multiple APs arrive from the same synapse in rapid succession → EPSPs add up before the first decays
  • Spatial summation (空間加成): EPSPs from multiple synapses occur simultaneously → their effects add at the axon hillock
Exam trap: EPSPs and IPSPs are graded potentials — they decay with distance and time. They must summate at the axon hillock to exceed threshold and fire an AP.

5. Neural Circuit Principles

Neural circuits produce complex behavior through a few basic patterns:

  • Divergence (擴散): one neuron synapses onto many → amplification and distribution of signal
  • Convergence (匯聚): many neurons synapse onto one → integration of multiple inputs before output decision
  • Reverberating circuits (回響迴路): collateral branches form positive feedback loops → sustained activity after stimulus ends (e.g. breathing rhythm, short-term memory)
  • Lateral inhibition (側向抑制): active neurons inhibit neighboring neurons → sharpens contrast (used in sensory systems — e.g. touch localization, visual edge detection)

Synaptic plasticity (突觸可塑性): strength of synaptic connections can change. Long-term potentiation (LTP, 長期增益): repeated stimulation → stronger synaptic connection → cellular basis of learning and memory. Requires NMDA receptor activation (dual voltage + ligand gate, needs both glutamate AND membrane depolarization simultaneously — "coincidence detector").

Exam trap: NMDA receptor requires BOTH glutamate binding AND postsynaptic depolarization (to remove the Mg²⁺ block 鎂離子阻塞) to open. This makes it a "coincidence detector" essential for associative learning.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Botulinum toxin cleaves SNAREs → no exocytosis → flaccid paralysis (not spastic).
  • EPSPs and IPSPs are graded; they must summate at the axon hillock to trigger an AP.
  • Glutamate = main excitatory; GABA = main inhibitory CNS neurotransmitter.
  • NMDA receptor needs simultaneous glutamate + depolarization (removes Mg²⁺ block) — coincidence detector for LTP.
  • Benzodiazepines enhance GABA-A activity (increase Cl⁻ influx frequency) → sedation/anxiolysis.
Top things to memorize
  1. Ca²⁺ influx → synaptotagmin → SNARE fusion → exocytosis. Botox cleaves SNAREs.
  2. EPSP (depolarization) vs IPSP (hyperpolarization); both are graded and must summate.
  3. Spatial summation (multiple synapses) vs temporal summation (rapid repeated firing).
  4. Glutamate (excitatory, AMPA/NMDA) and GABA (inhibitory, GABA-A Cl⁻ channel).
  5. NMDA receptor = coincidence detector; underlies LTP and memory formation.
Lower-yield / skip if short on time:
  • Detailed pharmacology of all neurotransmitter receptor subtypes.
  • Exact molecular structure of SNARE complex proteins beyond synaptotagmin concept.
  • All types of synaptic plasticity beyond LTP (LTD, spike-timing-dependent).
  • Detailed reverberating circuit types (parallel after-discharge, etc.).
