Lecture 1: Endo1_2: Overview of the Endocrine System & Neuroendocrine Systems

The endocrine system (內分泌系統) is a network of glands and cells that release hormones — chemical messengers carried in the bloodstream — to regulate metabolism, growth, reproduction, homeostasis, and mood. Understanding hormones has transformed medicine through breakthroughs such as in vitro fertilization, contraceptive pills, recombinant insulin, and growth hormone, but has also raised concerns about man-made endocrine-disrupting chemicals. This lecture covers the classic and newly recognized endocrine organs, the four chemical classes of hormones and how their solubility determines their mechanism of action, and the specialized neuroendocrine systems linking the hypothalamus to both lobes of the pituitary gland. The distinction between glandular hormones of the anterior pituitary and neurohormones of the posterior pituitary is a central organizing theme.

1. General Concepts of the Endocrine System

The endocrine system (內分泌系統) is defined as a group of glands and cells that secrete hormones (荷爾蒙) to coordinate physiological processes including metabolism (代謝), growth and development (生長發育), homeostasis (恆定), reproduction (生殖), and mood. The word hormone comes from the Greek "to arouse or excite" and was defined by Ernest Starling (1905) as "chemical messengers which speeding from cell to cell along the blood stream, may coordinate the activities and growth of different parts of the body." Starling's definition established three key properties of a classical hormone: (1) secreted into the blood in very small amounts by specialized cells or glands; (2) carried to other parts of the body by the bloodstream; (3) interacts with specific receptors (受體) in target tissue cells to produce a particular biological response.

Classical endocrine glands include the hypothalamus, pituitary, thyroid, parathyroid, adrenal glands, pancreatic islets, gonads (ovaries/testes), and pineal gland. Beyond these, newly recognized endocrine organs also secrete hormones: the heart secretes atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP 心房利鈉肽); the kidneys secrete renin (腎素) and calcitriol; the liver secretes somatomedin (IGF 胰島素樣生長因子); stomach G cells secrete gastrin (胃泌素); duodenal S cells secrete secretin (促胰液素); adipocytes (脂肪細胞) secrete leptin (瘦素) and estrogen; bone secretes FGF-23, RANKL, and sclerostin; and the enteroendocrine system (腸內分泌系統) contains many newly identified hormones including glicentin, oxyntomodulin, GLP-1, and GLP-2. GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 胰高血糖素樣肽-1) acts on a G protein-coupled receptor (GPCR) to enhance insulin secretion, inhibit glucagon release, slow gastric emptying, and promote satiety — making it a major target for treating type 2 diabetes and obesity.

Hormone secretion is regulated by three types of stimuli: (1) Humoral changes (體液變化) — changes in blood composition such as Ca²⁺ concentration, blood glucose, or osmolarity; (2) Neural stimuli (神經刺激) — e.g., sympathetic nerve control of the adrenal medulla; (3) Hormonal stimuli (荷爾蒙刺激) — e.g., neurohormones from the hypothalamus controlling anterior pituitary hormone release.

Compared to the nervous system (神經系統), the endocrine system acts over longer distances and longer time scales; regulatory effects may sustain for minutes to days. However, the two systems are closely interrelated: hypothalamic neurosecretory cells release hormones that govern pituitary output, and the sympathetic nervous system directly controls the adrenal medulla. The same molecule can serve as both a neurotransmitter (神經遞質) and a hormone — for example, norepinephrine (去甲腎上腺素).

Exam trap: Students confuse endocrine vs. nervous system timescales. The endocrine system operates over minutes-to-days (slow, sustained); the nervous system operates in milliseconds (fast, brief). Both systems can use the same molecule (e.g., norepinephrine as both a neurotransmitter and a hormone).

2. Chemistry of Hormones: Four Chemical Classes

Hormones are classified into four chemical categories based on their structure and origin:

1. Amino acid derivatives (胺基酸衍生物): Synthesized from amino acids. Examples include thyroxine (甲狀腺素) and epinephrine/norepinephrine (腎上腺素/去甲腎上腺素) — both derived from tyrosine (酪胺酸) — and melatonin (褪黑激素), derived from tryptophan (色胺酸).

2. Polypeptides (多肽): Range from 3 to hundreds of amino acids. Often produced as larger precursors (前驅物) that are proteolytically cleaved (蛋白酶水解裂解) to the active form. Examples: TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone, 3 aa), insulin (胰島素, 51 aa in bovine), growth hormone (GH 生長激素, 191 aa in human). Insulin is noteworthy as it is first synthesized as a larger precursor (preproinsulin → proinsulin → insulin) and has two polypeptide chains.

3. Steroids (類固醇): All derived from cholesterol (膽固醇) and all are lipid-soluble (脂溶性). Differences in ring structure and side chains create functional diversity. Examples: cortisol (皮質醇), aldosterone (醛固酮), testosterone (睪固酮), and estradiol (雌二醇).

4. Prostaglandins (前列腺素): Synthesized in membranes from arachidonic acid (花生四烯酸, a 20-C polyunsaturated fatty acid, also called 5,8,11,14-eicosatetraenoic acid), which is produced by cleavage of membrane phospholipids (磷脂) by phospholipases (磷脂酶). Found in almost all tissues; act in endocrine or paracrine (旁分泌) fashion. Nine classes: PGA through PGI. Prostaglandins and related compounds are collectively called eicosanoids (二十烷酸類). PGH2 is a key example. NSAIDs (非類固醇消炎藥) such as aspirin and ibuprofen inhibit prostaglandin formation involved in fever, pain, and inflammation. NSAIDs are also under investigation for treating Alzheimer's disease. Lipid-soluble but bind to surface receptors coupled with cAMP signaling — unlike other lipid-soluble hormones that use intracellular receptors. This is a unique and commonly tested property.

Exam trap: Prostaglandins are lipid-soluble but do NOT act like typical lipid-soluble hormones. They bind surface receptors coupled to cAMP, not intracellular/nuclear receptors. This is the opposite of what students expect from a lipid-soluble molecule.
Exam trap: Not all amino acid derivatives are lipid-insoluble. Thyroxine is derived from tyrosine but is lipid-soluble (acts via nuclear receptors). Only thyroxine among amino acid derivatives crosses into this category — epinephrine and melatonin are lipid-insoluble.

3. Mechanisms of Hormone Action: Solubility Determines Receptor Location

Hormones are also classified by their solubility (溶解性), which determines how they reach their receptors and produce effects:

Lipid-soluble hormones (脂溶性荷爾蒙) — including steroids and thyroxine — diffuse across the plasma membrane and bind to receptors in the nucleus or cytoplasm. The hormone-receptor complex acts directly on DNA to modify transcription (轉錄). Effects are long-lasting (hours to days). Elwood Jensen (1920–2012) first described the estrogen receptor in 1958 and later discovered the superfamily of nuclear hormone receptors (核受體超家族). Exception: Prostaglandins are lipid-soluble but bind surface receptors acting like lipid-insoluble hormones.

Lipid-insoluble hormones (脂不溶性荷爾蒙) — polypeptides and most amino acid derivatives — bind to surface receptors (表面受體). Effects are mediated by second messengers (第二信使) or by intrinsic catalytic (催化) activity of receptors. Responses are rapid and short-lived.

Hormones coupled to GPCRs (G蛋白偶聯受體) and cAMP signaling include:
— Stimulatory: epinephrine (via beta-adrenoceptors), TSH, vasopressin (kidney), glucagon (升糖素), serotonin (血清素), PGI
— Inhibitory: epinephrine via alpha-2 adrenoceptors
Hormones coupled to GPCRs via IP3/DAG signaling: vasopressin (liver), growth factors, TRH

Enzyme-coupled receptors (酶偶聯受體): Receptor tyrosine kinases (受體酪氨酸激酶) bind insulin and growth factors and have intrinsic kinase (激酶) activity. Upon ligand binding, they phosphorylate (磷酸化) tyrosine residues on cytoplasmic proteins and also autophosphorylate (自身磷酸化) — a positive feedback mechanism increasing activity. ANP receptor is a receptor guanylate cyclase (鳥苷酸環化酶); ligand binding triggers cGMP formation.

Exam trap: Insulin receptor is NOT coupled to a G protein — it is a receptor tyrosine kinase. Students commonly confuse this. Insulin is water-soluble, uses membrane receptors, but these are enzyme-coupled, not GPCR-coupled.
Exam trap: When preparing a lipid-soluble hormone stock solution (e.g., cortisol) in the lab, you must use an organic solvent such as ethanol — NOT water, saline, or acidic solutions. This is a practical application of solubility principles.

4. Neuroendocrine System: General Concepts

Neurosecretory (neuroendocrine) cells (神經分泌細胞) are specialized neurons that secrete hormones called neurohormones (神經荷爾蒙). They form the interface between the nervous and endocrine systems. They are found mainly in two locations:
(I) In the hypothalamus (下丘腦):
  (a) Magnocellular neurons (大細胞神經元) in the supraoptic nucleus (視上核, SON) and paraventricular nucleus (室旁核, PVN) — project to the posterior pituitary gland.
  (b) Parvocellular neurons (小細胞神經元) — project to hypophyseal portal capillaries (垂體門脈毛細血管).
(II) In the adrenal medulla (腎上腺髓質): chromaffin cells (嗜鉻細胞), which share the same embryological origin (胚胎學起源) as neural tissue.

Compared to ordinary neurons, neurosecretory cells differ structurally and functionally:
Structural: Secretory granules (分泌顆粒) are 100–400 nm — much larger than synaptic vesicles (突觸小泡, 30–60 nm). They terminate in clusters near a bed of capillaries called the neurohemal organ (神經血管器).
Functional: They respond to sensory inputs like regular neurons but release hormones into the bloodstream rather than neurotransmitters into a synapse. They are still excitable neurons (可興奮神經元).

The pituitary gland (hypophysis 垂體) is an endocrine gland at the base of the brain, connected to the hypothalamus. "Hypophysis" comes from the Greek for "attachment underneath." It has two distinct lobes:

The hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system (下丘腦-垂體門脈系統) consists of two capillary beds directly joined by portal blood vessels. Parvocellular neurons release releasing hormones and release-inhibiting hormones into this portal system, which carries them to the anterior pituitary to stimulate or inhibit glandular hormone secretion.

Exam trap: The posterior pituitary does NOT synthesize neurohormones — it only stores and releases them. The actual synthesis occurs in hypothalamic magnocellular neurons (SON and PVN). The anterior pituitary is true endocrine tissue, not neural tissue. Students often confuse which lobe is which.
Exam trap: Neurosecretory cells are excitable neurons — they are NOT "non-excitable." A common distractor in quiz questions is to say they are non-excitable; this is FALSE.

5. Glandular Hormones of the Anterior Pituitary

The anterior pituitary secretes two categories of glandular hormones (腺垂體荷爾蒙):

Direct-acting hormones (直接作用荷爾蒙) act on somatic, non-endocrine target tissues:
— Growth Hormone (GH 生長激素): 191 amino acids; stimulates body growth via IGF/somatomedin.
— Prolactin (PRL 泌乳素): originally named for stimulating milk protein synthesis and mammary gland growth; also produced by uterus, breasts, immune cells, and prostate. Stimulated by TRH and estrogen; inhibited by dopamine (多巴胺), also called prolactin inhibiting hormone. Has many effects beyond lactation.
— Melanocyte-Stimulating Hormone (MSH 促黑素細胞激素): secreted by pars intermedia; acts on pigment cells (melanocytes 黑色素細胞) in skin to produce more melanin (黑色素), causing skin darkening. Generated by cleavage of pro-opiomelanocortin (POMC 前阿黑皮素原), which also yields ACTH. Also produced by skin keratinocytes (角質形成細胞) in response to UV light. Elevated by estrogen (during pregnancy) → melasma (黃褐斑, "mask of pregnancy").

Tropic hormones (促激素) act on other endocrine tissues (they stimulate other endocrine glands):
— ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone 促腎上腺皮質激素): stimulates adrenal cortex.
— FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone 促卵泡素): acts on gonads.
— LH (luteinizing hormone 黃體生成素): acts on gonads.
— TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone 促甲狀腺素): stimulates thyroid gland.
"-tropic" (= trophic) means growth-promoting; stimulating the activity of another endocrine gland.

The anterior pituitary is regulated by releasing hormones and release-inhibiting hormones (抑制激素) secreted by parvocellular neurons, transported via the hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system. Key mnemonic: All direct-acting and tropic hormones have corresponding releasing hormones (from the hypothalamus). Only direct-acting hormones also have release-inhibiting hormones. (Note: MSH and ACTH are derived from the same precursor POMC, so they share CRH as the main hypothalamic signal.)

Causes of elevated MSH: (1) Hypothalamic level — Addison's disease (艾迪生病) reduces adrenal cortex hormones → no negative feedback on CRH → high CRH → high MSH; (2) Pituitary level — pregnancy-driven estrogen/progesterone elevates MSH → melasma; birth control pills can also cause this; (3) Receptor level — mutations in MSH receptors. TB (tuberculosis 肺結核) can damage the adrenal gland, also reducing cortisol feedback.

Exam trap: "Tropic" and "trophic" are often used interchangeably in endocrinology — both mean growth-promoting/stimulating. ACTH, FSH, LH, and TSH are tropic hormones (act on other endocrine glands), while GH and prolactin are direct-acting (act on non-endocrine somatic tissues).
Exam trap: MSH and ACTH are both derived from POMC — they are not independent. This means conditions causing high CRH (like Addison's disease) raise both ACTH and MSH simultaneously, explaining skin darkening in Addison's patients.

6. Neurohormones of the Posterior Pituitary

The posterior pituitary stores and releases two neurohormones (神經荷爾蒙) synthesized in hypothalamic magnocellular neurons of SON and PVN:

Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH, vasopressin, AVP — arginine vasopressin 精氨酸加壓素):
— Target: kidney — regulates water retention (水分保留). Mechanism: ADH receptors are coupled with adenylate cyclase (腺苷酸環化酶); increase surface expression of aquaporins (水通道蛋白, water channels). Peter Agre won the 2003 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for discovering aquaporins.
— Target: vascular smooth muscle — regulates blood pressure by direct vasoconstriction (血管收縮).

Oxytocin (催產素) — reproduction-related:
— Target: mammary gland — stimulates milk ejection (噴乳反射) during nursing.
— Target: uterus — stimulates uterine contractions (子宮收縮) during and after parturition (分娩).
— As a neurotransmitter in the brain, oxytocin is called the "hormone of love and bonding" — involved in social recognition, bonding, trust, and generosity. Released during hugging, touching, and orgasm in both sexes.

ADH and oxytocin are structurally very similar — both are 9-amino-acid nonapeptides with a disulfide bond (二硫鍵). Argipressin (AVP/ADH) sequence: Cys-Tyr-Phe-Gln-Asn-Cys-Pro-Arg-Gly. Oxytocin: Cys-Tyr-Ile-Gln-Asn-Cys-Pro-Leu-Gly. They differ at positions 3 and 8. The vertebrate vasopressin family and vertebrate oxytocin family are evolutionarily conserved across species (marsupials, birds, reptiles, fish).

Exam trap: Prolactin is often confused with oxytocin because both relate to lactation. However, prolactin (from anterior pituitary) stimulates milk synthesis, while oxytocin (from posterior pituitary/neurohormone) stimulates milk ejection. They are from different lobes, and prolactin is a glandular hormone, not a neurohormone.
Exam trap: ADH acts on two different targets via different mechanisms: in the kidney (water retention via aquaporin upregulation), and in blood vessels (vasoconstriction). Students often only remember the kidney action.

  • Thyroxine is the only amino acid derivative that is lipid-soluble; epinephrine and melatonin are lipid-insoluble amino acid derivatives.
  • Glucose derivatives are NOT used as hormones in the body.
  • Cortisol (lipid-soluble) must be dissolved in ethanol (not water or saline) for lab stock solutions.
  • Insulin receptor is a receptor tyrosine kinase (enzyme-coupled), NOT a GPCR. Insulin is water-soluble and uses membrane receptors, but these are not G-protein coupled.
  • Neurosecretory cells ARE excitable neurons — stating they are "non-excitable" is FALSE.
  • The posterior pituitary does NOT synthesize neurohormones — synthesis occurs in SON/PVN of the hypothalamus; the posterior pituitary only stores and releases them.
  • Anterior pituitary = true endocrine tissue (adenohypophysis); posterior pituitary = neural tissue (neurohypophysis, axon terminals of hypothalamic neurons).
  • Prolactin (anterior pituitary, glandular hormone) stimulates MILK SYNTHESIS; oxytocin (posterior pituitary, neurohormone) stimulates MILK EJECTION — different hormones, different lobes, different mechanisms.

Lecture 2: Endo3_4: Metabolic & Developmental Hormones + Water/Electrolyte Balance

The body maintains moment-to-moment energy balance and long-term growth through a coordinated network of metabolic and developmental hormones, each acting on specific tissues to either mobilise or store fuel, promote growth, or adjust metabolic rate. Rapid stress responses are handled by catecholamines (腎上腺素類) from the adrenal medulla and glucocorticoids (糖皮質素) from the adrenal cortex, while thyroid hormones (甲狀腺激素) set the baseline metabolic rate for nearly all cells. Blood glucose is continuously fine-tuned by the antagonistic pair insulin (胰島素) and glucagon (升糖素), whereas growth hormone (生長激素) and leptin (瘦素) govern long-term body composition and energy intake. Finally, a trio of hormones — ADH (抗利尿激素), aldosterone (醛固酮), and atrial natriuretic peptide (心房鈉尿肽, ANP) — maintains water and electrolyte balance by acting on the kidneys and vasculature.

1. Epinephrine & Norepinephrine (腎上腺素與去甲腎上腺素)

Catecholamines (兒茶酚胺) are stress hormones built on a catechol (苯二酚) ring. Both epinephrine (腎上腺素, adrenaline) and norepinephrine (去甲腎上腺素, noradrenaline) are secreted by the adrenal medulla (腎上腺髓質) — the inner layer of the adrenal gland. Epinephrine makes up ~80 % of the secretion; norepinephrine is the biosynthetic precursor (前驅物) and is converted to epinephrine by the enzyme phenylethanolamine-N-methyltransferase (PNMT), a step that requires cortisol stimulation.

Adrenal anatomy (腎上腺解剖): The adrenal gland has two functionally distinct layers. The inner adrenal medulla (腎上腺髓質) is neural (ectodermal) in origin and secretes catecholamines. The outer adrenal cortex (腎上腺皮質) is non-neural (mesodermal) and secretes mineralocorticoids (礦質皮質素), glucocorticoids, and androgens such as DHEA (dihydroepiandrosterone, 脫氫表雄酮). Glucocorticoids from the cortex are NOT secreted by the medulla.

Chromaffin cells (嗜鉻細胞) of the adrenal medulla are neurosecretory cells anatomically analogous to postganglionic (節後) neurons of the sympathetic nervous system. There are two types — one producing epinephrine, one producing norepinephrine. They are activated by preganglionic (節前) sympathetic fibers that release acetylcholine (乙醯膽鹼). The adrenal medulla is therefore considered an extension (延伸) of the sympathetic nervous system.

Catecholamine synthesis pathway (合成路徑): Dopamine (多巴胺) → Norepinephrine (via dopamine β-hydroxylase, sympathetic stimulation) → Epinephrine (via PNMT, cortisol stimulation). Secretion is regulated directly by neural input — there is no dedicated tropic hormone (the answer is "none of the above" in the lecture quiz).

Activation stimuli (激活刺激): Cold (寒冷), stress (壓力), hemorrhage (出血), and exercise (運動) — the same stimuli that activate the sympathetic nervous system. The catecholamines reinforce and prolong the fight-or-flight response (戰或逃反應) to increase the body's capacity for vigorous muscle activity.

Actions of epinephrine (作用) — Table 14-4:
Cardiovascular: increased cardiac output (心輸出量↑), increased heart rate (心率↑), increased strength of cardiac contractions (收縮力↑), vasodilation (血管舒張) in skeletal muscle, vasoconstriction (血管收縮) in internal organs and skin.
Other tissues: relaxation of smooth muscle (平滑肌) in GI tract, urinary bladder, and lung airways; increased mental alertness (警覺性↑).
Metabolism: increased glycogenolysis (糖原分解) in liver and muscle, increased lipolysis (脂解) in adipose tissue (脂肪組織), decreased insulin secretion (胰島素分泌↓), increased glucagon secretion (升糖素分泌↑).

Adrenoreceptor signaling (腎上腺素受體訊號): α₁ receptors → Phospholipase C (磷脂酶C) → phosphoinositides → IP₃ + DAG → tissue response. β₁ and β₂ receptors → adenylate cyclase (腺苷酸環化酶) activation → cAMP↑ → tissue response. α₂ receptors → adenylate cyclase inhibition → cAMP↓. Key agonists/antagonists: phenylephrine (α₁-agonist), clonidine (α₂-agonist), phentolamine (mixed α-antagonist), prenalterol (β₁-agonist), practolol (β₁-antagonist), salbutamol (β₂-agonist, used in asthma), butoxamine (β₂-antagonist), isoproterenol (non-selective β-agonist).

Clinical — Pheochromocytoma (嗜鉻細胞瘤): An adrenal medulla tumour causing excess adrenaline. Symptoms: periodic hypertension (高血壓), heart palpitations (心悸), sweating (出汗), headaches (頭痛), anxiety (焦慮). May lead to persistently high blood pressure. Hematidrosis (血汗症, blood sweating) is an extreme stress-related condition caused by rupture of capillary blood vessels.

Clinical — Anaphylaxis (過敏性休克) treatment: Epinephrine is the first-aid medication of choice. It works via vasoconstriction to prevent laryngeal edema (喉頭水腫), hypotension (低血壓), and shock (休克), plus bronchodilation (支氣管擴張) and cardiac inotropic (心肌收縮力) and chronotropic (心率) effects. Delivered via autoinjector (自動注射器, e.g., EpiPen).

Exam trap: The adrenal medulla secretes catecholamines, NOT glucocorticoids. Glucocorticoids come from the adrenal cortex (皮質), not the medulla (髓質). Also, catecholamine secretion from the adrenal medulla is regulated by neural input only — there is no specific tropic hormone like ACTH for catecholamines.
Exam trap: Epinephrine decreases insulin secretion (not increases it) and increases glucagon secretion. A question asking "which is NOT an effect of epinephrine" would list "increased insulin secretion" as the correct (NOT) answer.

2. Glucocorticoids (糖皮質素)

Glucocorticoids (糖皮質素), also called corticosteroids (皮質類固醇), are steroid hormones (類固醇激素) secreted by the adrenal cortex (腎上腺皮質). The major form in humans is cortisol (皮質醇), also known as hydrocortisone (氫化可的松). Other forms include cortisone (可的松) and corticosterone (皮質酮). The 1950 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine was awarded for discoveries relating to adrenal cortex hormones.

Regulation of glucocorticoid release (調節): Stress (including hypoglycemia, 低血糖) and diurnal rhythm (晝夜節律) are key stimuli. The pathway: stress/neural input → CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone, 促腎上腺皮質素釋放激素) from hypothalamic neurosecretory cells → ACTH (adrenocorticotropic hormone, 促腎上腺皮質素) from the anterior pituitary → glucocorticoid secretion from the adrenal cortex. Glucocorticoids exert negative feedback (負回饋) on both hypothalamus and pituitary. Cortisol follows a diurnal rhythm — plasma cortisol peaks in the early morning (around 8:00 AM) and is lowest around midnight.

The primary effect is to provoke gluconeogenesis (糖質新生) — forming glucose from non-carbohydrate molecules (fats and proteins, 脂肪與蛋白質) under stress, important for sustaining energy supply to critical tissues like the brain during starvation. Glucocorticoids also enhance vascular reactivity (增強血管反應性) and at higher doses suppress inflammation (抑制發炎) and the immune system (免疫系統). This makes them useful as immunosuppressants (免疫抑制劑) after organ transplantation and as lympholytics (淋巴溶解劑) in chemotherapy.

Cushing's Syndrome (庫欣氏症候群): Caused by excessive cortisol — from glucocorticoid drugs, or from tumors secreting cortisol, ACTH, or CRH. Major symptoms: rapid weight gain (體重快速增加), moodiness/depression (情緒不穩/抑鬱), muscle weakness (肌肉無力), immune suppression (免疫抑制), capillary dilation (微血管擴張), skin thinning (皮膚變薄), baldness (禿頭).

Exam trap: Cortisol = hydrocortisone — these are two names for the same compound, the major glucocorticoid in humans. Do NOT confuse cortisol with cortisone or corticosterone. Hydrocortisone was used with Ribavirin to treat SARS patients because it suppresses the immune system, not because it raises glucose or amino acids directly.

3. Thyroid Hormones (甲狀腺激素)

Thyroid hormones are formed from two iodinated (碘化的) tyrosine (酪氨酸) precursors in the thyroid gland (甲狀腺). Synthesis requires dietary iodine (碘); deficiency causes goiter (甲狀腺腫大) — visible in historical art and still common in iodine-poor regions. The two hormones are:

  • T4 / Thyroxine (四碘甲狀腺素, tetraiodothyronine) — the major form released (2 tyrosines + 4 iodines).
  • T3 / Triiodothyronine (三碘甲狀腺素) — more potent (效力更強); T4 is converted to T3 in target tissues (靶組織) (2 tyrosines + 3 iodines).
Thyroid hormone receptors are nuclear receptors (核受體) that act as transcription factors (轉錄因子). They also have non-genomic actions (非基因組作用) that do not directly affect gene expression.

Regulation of thyroid hormone release (調節): Inputs from higher CNS centers and the temperature-regulating center in the hypothalamus (e.g., prolonged cold, increased energy needs in pregnancy) → TRH (thyrotropin-releasing hormone, 促甲狀腺素釋放激素) from hypothalamus → TSH (thyroid-stimulating hormone, 促甲狀腺素) from anterior pituitary → thyroid hormone secretion. TSH also stimulates thyroid gland growth (TSH亦促進甲狀腺生長). Thyroid hormones exert negative feedback on hypothalamus and pituitary.

Actions of thyroid hormones (at whole-body/tissue level):

  1. Stimulate cellular respiration (細胞呼吸) to increase basal metabolic rate (基礎代謝率, BMR) and heat production (產熱). Acts on all tissues except the brain (腦), spleen (脾臟), testes/uterus (睪丸/子宮), and thyroid itself.
  2. Sensitize tissues to epinephrine (使組織對腎上腺素敏感) — explains why hyperthyroidism (甲狀腺機能亢進) causes increased heart rate and strength of cardiac contractions.
  3. Promote development and maturation (發育與成熟) of skeletal (骨骼) and nervous systems (神經系統). This action requires the presence of growth hormone (GH, 生長激素).

Exam trap: T4 is the major form released by the thyroid, but T3 is more potent and is the active form in target tissues after conversion. Also, thyroid hormones do NOT act on the brain, spleen, testes/uterus, or thyroid itself when stimulating cellular respiration. The cardiac effects of hyperthyroidism are due to sensitization to epinephrine — not direct catecholamine secretion from the adrenal medulla.
Exam trap: TSH not only stimulates thyroid hormone secretion — it also stimulates thyroid gland growth (增生). This is why iodine deficiency (low T3/T4) → high TSH (no negative feedback) → goiter (甲狀腺腫大).

4. Insulin & Glucagon — Blood Glucose Regulation (胰島素與升糖素)

Insulin (胰島素) and glucagon (升糖素) are peptide hormones with opposite effects secreted by the islets of Langerhans (蘭氏小島) in the pancreas (胰臟). Insulin comes from the Latin insula = island. Beta (β) cells secrete insulin (2 chains, 51 amino acids); Alpha (α) cells secrete glucagon (single chain, 29 amino acids).

Regulation of insulin secretion (胰島素分泌調節):

  1. Humoral ("hormone of nutrient abundance"): Elevated blood glucose (major stimulus, 主要刺激); elevated amino acids and fatty acids in blood.
  2. Hormonal: Stimulated by GIP (gastric inhibitory peptide, 胃抑制肽) — signals meal ingestion. Other hyperglycemic hormones (glucagon, epinephrine, glucocorticoids, GH) have direct or indirect effects by raising blood glucose.
  3. Neural: Sympathetic NS activation → insulin release inhibited (抑制); Parasympathetic NS activation → insulin release stimulated (促進).

Major effects of insulin (胰島素主要作用):

  • Carbohydrate metabolism (碳水化合物代謝): Increases uptake and storage of glucose (as glycogen 糖原) in liver, muscle, and adipose tissue (脂肪組織).
  • Fat metabolism (脂肪代謝): Stimulates lipogenesis (脂肪生成) in liver and adipose tissue.
  • Protein metabolism (蛋白質代謝): Stimulates amino acid (aa) uptake in liver and muscle, and incorporation of aa into proteins (蛋白質合成).
Insulin also increases transport of glucose and amino acids into all tissues, induces glycogen synthesis in muscle, suppresses glycogenolysis in liver, and increases glucose absorption from kidney filtrate.

Regulation of glucagon secretion (升糖素分泌調節) — mainly humoral:

  • Inhibited by elevated blood glucose and fatty acids (different from insulin — fatty acids stimulate insulin but inhibit glucagon). The regulation of glucagon by blood glucose requires the presence of insulin.
  • Stimulated by elevated blood amino acids (similar to insulin), but glucagon promotes conversion of amino acids to glucose (糖質新生).

Diabetes mellitus (糖尿病): From Greek "siphon" (excessive urination) + Latin "mellitus" = honey (sweet urine). Distinct from diabetes insipidus (尿崩症) which is due to ADH deficiency. Both cause excessive thirst and urination — called "消渴症" in traditional Chinese medicine.

Types of diabetes:

  • Type 1 (insulin-dependent, 胰島素依賴型, 10% cases): Loss of pancreatic beta cells → decreased insulin production.
  • Type 2 (insulin-independent, 非胰島素依賴型, 90% cases): Defective insulin receptors (受體缺陷).
  • Gestational diabetes (妊娠糖尿病): Occurs in pregnant women.
Diabetes is the 7th leading cause of death globally; 14% of adults worldwide had it as of 2022; 10–12% in Hong Kong. Global economic cost: US$1.3 trillion (2024).

Acute complications (急性併發症):

  • Hyperglycemia (高血糖) → glycosuria (尿糖, glucose spills into urine) → increased urination → dehydration (脫水).
  • In Type 1: absence of insulin removes inhibitory effect on glucagon → high glucagon → increased ketone (酮體) formation by liver → ketoacidosis (酮酸中毒) → electrolyte imbalance (電解質失衡) → dehydration → coma/death.
Chronic complications (慢性併發症):
  • Vascular: Narrowing of large blood vessels in brain, heart, and lower extremities → stroke (中風), heart attack (心臟病發), limb loss.
  • Vision/kidney (視力/腎臟): Lesions in small vessels and capillaries in kidney (nephropathy, 腎病) and retina (retinopathy, 視網膜病變) → kidney disease, blindness.
  • Neural (神經): Damaged autonomic NS fibers → bladder/GI dysfunction, impotence; damaged peripheral sensory nerves → loss of sensation in lower limbs (diabetic foot, 糖尿病足).

Exam trap: Insulin is secreted by beta (β) cells, not alpha cells. Glucagon is secreted by alpha (α) cells. A common wrong option states "both are secreted by alpha cells" — this is FALSE. Also: in Type 1 diabetes, glucagon level is HIGH (not low) because insulin normally suppresses glucagon, and this suppression is absent.
Exam trap: Both insulin and glucagon secretion are stimulated by elevated blood amino acids — but their responses to glucose and fatty acids are opposite. Fatty acids stimulate insulin but inhibit glucagon.

5. Growth Hormone & Leptin (生長激素與瘦素)

Growth hormone (GH, 生長激素) is a peptide hormone (191 amino acids in humans) produced in the anterior pituitary (腦垂體前葉). Its overall effect is to promote tissue growth (促進組織生長) and it is regarded as an anabolic hormone (合成代謝激素).

Control of GH secretion (分泌調控): The hypothalamus receives inputs from stress centers and sleep centers in the brain, as well as humoral stimuli. It releases:

  • GRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone, 生長激素釋放激素) — stimulates GH secretion (+)
  • GIH / Somatostatin (體抑素, growth hormone-inhibiting hormone) — inhibits GH secretion (−)
GH acts on the liver to produce somatomedins / IGFs (insulin-like growth factors, 類胰島素生長因子), which feed back negatively on GH secretion. GH is secreted in a phasic (脈衝式) manner — peak occurs about 1 hour after sleep onset (睡眠後1小時). Pulses also occur 2–4 hours after a meal. Pulse frequency and size increase at puberty (青春期) and decline with aging (老化).

GH secretion stimulators (刺激因子): Low blood glucose (hypoglycemia, 低血糖); amino acids (especially arginine, 精氨酸); deep sleep (深度睡眠, non-REM); stress (physical trauma, infection, psychological stress).

GH secretion inhibitors (抑制因子): High blood glucose (高血糖, hyperglycemia); REM (rapid eye movement) sleep (快速眼動睡眠, i.e., dreaming stage).

Direct and indirect effects of GH (直接與間接作用): GH has both insulin-like (類胰島素) and anti-insulin (抗胰島素) effects.

Three mechanisms of hyperglycemic effect of GH: (1) Decreased glucose uptake in all tissues; (2) Increased gluconeogenesis in liver; (3) Increased lipolysis in adipose (lipids replace glucose as energy source, freeing glucose). Indirect growth effects via IGFs (somatomedins): Most growth-promoting effects of GH are mediated by IGFs produced by the liver.

  • IGF-I (IGF-1): Promotes linear growth of the skeleton (骨骼線性生長).
  • IGF-II (IGF-2): Promotes growth of many tissues and size of many organs.
Overall effect summary: Direct → increased muscle mass + decreased fat storage → accumulation of lean body mass (瘦體重). Indirect (via somatomedins) → linear skeletal growth + growth of many organs and tissues.

GH deficiency (生長激素缺乏): Can result from (1) defect in pituitary function or (2) defect in IGF-I production by the liver / inability of target tissues to respond to IGF-I. Deficiency in childhood → dwarfism (侏儒症). Deficiency in adults (after normal bone growth) → no evident effect (無明顯影響). Laron dwarfs in Ecuador carry a mutation inactivating the GH receptor — remarkably protected from cancer and diabetes.

GH excess (生長激素過多):

  • In childhood → Gigantism / Giantism (巨人症)
  • In adulthood → Acromegaly (肢端肥大症) — enlarged jaw, nose, hands and feet (bone plates already fused, so no increase in height).

Leptin (瘦素) — from Greek leptos = "thin" (薄/瘦). A peptide hormone (16 kDa) encoded by the obese gene (ob gene, 肥胖基因). Produced primarily by white adipose tissue (白色脂肪組織), but also by many other tissues. Influences food intake relative to energy expenditure (影響食物攝入量與能量消耗). When leptin levels are high → appetite decreases (食慾↓) and energy expenditure increases (能量消耗↑).

Leptin receptor (瘦素受體, LepR / OB-R): Expressed primarily in the brain; most of leptin's effects on end organs are indirect via the CNS (中樞神經系統). LepR is a cytokine family receptor (細胞因子家族受體), a tyrosine kinase (JAKs)-associated receptor (JAK激酶相關受體). Leptin also regulates immune cell function both via the CNS and directly.

Leptin in obesity (肥胖): Obese individuals paradoxically have higher leptin levels. Possible reasons: (1) higher percentage body fat → more leptin produced; (2) leptin resistance (瘦素抵抗) at the site of action — partial resistance means more leptin is needed for the same effect; (3) obese individuals are in a state of perceived starvation (感知飢餓狀態).

Exam trap: Deep sleep (non-REM) stimulates GH secretion, but REM (dreaming) sleep inhibits GH. The lecture quiz answer for "stimulus of GH secretion" is arginine — NOT dreaming (which is REM, an inhibitor). High blood glucose inhibits GH; low blood glucose stimulates it.
Exam trap: GH deficiency in adults causes no evident effect because bone growth has already concluded. GH deficiency during childhood causes dwarfism. GH excess in childhood = gigantism; in adulthood = acromegaly (no increased height, only enlargement of extremities). Injecting insulin → hypoglycemia → stimulates GH release → this is a legitimate clinical test of pituitary GH secretory capacity.
Exam trap: GH generally exerts its metabolic effects via somatomedins/IGFs — the lecture quiz answer is IGF-1 (for metabolic effects on liver, muscle, and adipose tissue). Do not confuse IGF-1 (skeletal growth) with IGF-2 (organ growth).

6. Hormones Regulating Water & Electrolyte Balance (水與電解質平衡激素)

Three key hormones regulate body water and electrolyte balance: ADH (antidiuretic hormone, 抗利尿激素), aldosterone (醛固酮), and ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide, 心房鈉尿肽). ADH and aldosterone act to retain water/raise blood pressure, while ANP opposes them by promoting sodium and water excretion.

ADH / Vasopressin (抗利尿激素/血管加壓素): A cyclic (環形) peptide hormone of 9 amino acids, synthesized in the supraoptic nucleus (視上核) of the hypothalamus, and secreted by the posterior pituitary (腦垂體後葉).
Actions:

  • Increases water reabsorption (水重吸收) by the kidney — by increasing surface expression of aquaporin (水通道蛋白) channels.
  • Regulates blood pressure by direct action on vascular smooth muscle (血管平滑肌) causing vasoconstriction (血管收縮).

Regulation of ADH secretion (ADH分泌調節):

  1. Osmotic regulation (滲透壓調節): Increased plasma osmolality (血漿滲透壓↑) → sensed by osmoreceptors (滲透壓感受器) in the anterior hypothalamus (下丘腦前部) (possibly the supraoptic neurons themselves) → ADH secretion↑ → water reabsorption by kidney↑ → plasma osmolality↓. A change of just 5–10 mOsm from the normal 280 mOsm can be sensed. Eating salty food (高鹽飲食) raises osmolality → stimulates ADH. Drinking a lot of water lowers osmolality → inhibits ADH.
  2. Volume and blood pressure regulation (血容量與血壓調節): Hemorrhage (出血) of >10% blood volume → decreased blood volume and pressure → sensed by stretch receptors (牽張感受器, TRP channels) in cardiovascular system neurons → signals transmitted to pituitary via cranial nerves (腦神經) → ADH secretion↑ → vasoconstriction + water reabsorption → blood volume and pressure restored.
Alcohol (酒精) potently inhibits ADH release → water reabsorption decreases → urine volume increases and thirst develops.

Mineralocorticoids (礦質皮質素): Steroid hormones secreted by the adrenal cortex (腎上腺皮質). The major form is aldosterone (醛固酮).
Action of aldosterone: Stimulates Na⁺ reabsorption (鈉離子重吸收) and K⁺ excretion (鉀離子排泄) in the kidney, with water following Na⁺. Net effect: increased extracellular fluid (ECF, 細胞外液) volume → increased blood volume (血容量) and blood pressure (血壓).

Regulation of aldosterone secretion (醛固酮分泌調節):

  1. Humoral: Extracellular K⁺ concentration — even a 5% change can trigger aldosterone secretion.
  2. Hormonal:
    • Angiotensin II (血管緊張素II) — the PRIMARY hormonal regulator. Angiotensin II secretion is stimulated by blood pressure decrease → activates the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS, 腎素-血管緊張素-醛固酮系統): decreased renal perfusion → juxtaglomerular apparatus (球旁器) secretes renin (腎素) → angiotensinogen (血管緊張素原) → Angiotensin I (via renin) → Angiotensin II (via ACE, angiotensin-converting enzyme, 血管緊張素轉換酶, in lung/kidney endothelium) → aldosterone secretion + vasoconstriction + ADH secretion + sympathetic activity. Net result: water and salt retention, increased circulating volume.
    • ACTH (促腎上腺皮質素) — also stimulates aldosterone secretion.

Atrial Natriuretic Peptide (ANP, 心房鈉尿肽):

  • Secreted by endocrine cells in the atria of the heart (心房) in response to mechanical stretch (機械牽張), e.g., blood volume increase.
  • Acts on the kidneys to increase Na⁺ excretion and urine production (natriuresis, 尿鈉排泄).
  • Counteracts (對抗) the effects of ADH and aldosterone.
  • The word "natriuretic" comes from "natriuresis" — from natrium (Latin for sodium, 鈉), meaning excretion of excessive sodium in urine.

Lecture 3: Endo5 — Reproductive Hormones

Reproductive hormones coordinate two fundamental tasks: producing gametes (配子) and regulating sexual development. The gonads (生殖腺) — ovary (卵巢) and testis (睾丸) — act as both gamete factories and endocrine glands, secreting steroid hormones derived from a single biosynthetic pathway starting at cholesterol (膽固醇). In males, a tonic neuroendocrine axis drives continuous sperm production and testosterone secretion, held in check by dual negative feedback loops through testosterone and inhibin. In females, the same hypothalamic-pituitary axis orchestrates a cyclic 28-day programme in which feedback switches between negative and positive, triggering ovulation at mid-cycle and preparing the uterus for implantation. Understanding who secretes what, where, and which direction the feedback runs is the key to mastering this lecture.

1. Overview of Reproductive Organs and Hormones

Human sex is organised into two layers. Primary sex characteristics (第一性徵) are directly tied to reproduction and include the genitalia. Secondary sex characteristics (第二性徵) distinguish the sexes without being directly reproductive, such as voice quality, facial hair, and breast development.

The reproductive system contains two organ types. Gonads (性腺) — ovary and testis — are the primary sex organs; they produce gametes (配子) and secrete sex hormones that regulate the reproductive process and drive development of other reproductive organs. Secondary sex organs (次要性器官) such as the uterus (子宮) and penis (陰莖) transport, protect, and nourish gametes, embryos, or the young.

There are two chemical classes of reproductive hormones. First, steroid hormones (類固醇激素) derived from cholesterol follow the biosynthetic sequence: cholesterol → progesterone (黃體素) → androgens (雄激素) → estrogens (雌激素). The main androgen is testosterone (睾固酮), dominant in males; the main estrogen is 17β-estradiol (雌二醇), dominant in females; progesterone is especially important in females. Second, two peptide hormones (肽激素) — oxytocin (催產素) and prolactin (催乳素) — are produced in the pituitary and function in childbirth and lactation.

Key entries from the hormone table: estradiol-17β originates from the ovarian follicle (卵泡), corpus luteum (黃體), and adrenal cortex (腎上腺皮質); its regulation is stimulated by increased FSH and LH. Progesterone originates from the corpus luteum and adrenal cortex; increased LH and prolactin stimulate its secretion. Testosterone originates from the testes (Leydig cells 萊迪氏細胞) and adrenal cortex; increased LH stimulates secretion. Oxytocin (後葉腦垂體 posterior pituitary) promotes smooth muscle contraction and milk ejection; high progesterone inhibits its release. Prolactin (anterior pituitary 前葉腦垂體) increases milk synthesis; normally blocked by prolactin-inhibiting hormone (PIH 催乳激素抑制激素), but increased estrogen and decreased PIH permit release.

Exam trap: Not all reproductive hormones are steroids — oxytocin (nonapeptide 九肽) and prolactin (peptide) are peptide hormones, not steroids. Likewise, not all steroids are reproductive hormones (e.g. cortisol). Androgens are present in both sexes, not males only; adrenal cortex produces androgens in females too.

2. Male Reproductive System and Spermatogenesis

The male sex organs are external and consist of the penis (陰莖, copulatory organ and common exit for urinary and reproductive systems) and the scrotum (陰囊), which houses the testes outside the abdominal cavity because sperm production requires a temperature below normal body temperature. Inside the scrotum: (a) two testes (睾丸) where spermatogenesis occurs in the seminiferous tubules (精細管; semin- = seed, -ferous = producing) and where Leydig cells produce testosterone; (b) the epididymis (附睾), the site of final sperm maturation taking several weeks; (c) the vas deferens (輸精管) for sperm transport through the inguinal canal to the abdominal cavity and out via the urethra.

Accessory glands add fluids to semen along the route. The seminal vesicles (精囊) add 60% of semen fluid, containing fructose (as an energy source for sperm), prostaglandins (前列腺素), and proteins that coagulate semen in the vagina. The prostate gland (前列腺) adds seminal fluid that neutralises the acidic environment of the urethra and vagina. The bulbourethral gland (尿道球腺) also contributes fluid.

Spermatogenesis (精子發生) is the process by which germ cells (生殖細胞) undergo a series of divisions and differentiations to become mature sperm. It occurs entirely in the seminiferous tubules, which contain two cell types: (1) germ cells (生殖細胞) that produce sperm, and (2) Sertoli cells (賽爾托利細胞) that support the process. Normal adult males produce about 60 million sperm/mL; fertility is markedly impaired when counts fall below 35 million/mL.

Sertoli cells have three key functions: (1) form the blood-testes barrier (血睾屏障), protecting developing sperm from immune attack; (2) produce seminiferous tubular fluid (精細管液) that stimulates and nourishes developing germ cells; (3) synthesise inhibin (抑制素), a peptide hormone that specifically feeds back to inhibit FSH release.

Leydig cells (萊迪氏細胞), also called interstitial cells (間質細胞), sit outside the seminiferous tubules. They respond to LH and produce testosterone. The distinction between Leydig cells (testosterone, respond to LH, outside tubules) and Sertoli cells (inhibin, seminiferous fluid, blood-testes barrier, respond to FSH, inside tubules) is a classic exam target.

Exam trap: Sertoli cells do NOT bind or respond to LH — that is Leydig cells. Sertoli cells respond to FSH. Also, inhibin is secreted by Sertoli cells in males (and by follicles in females), not by Leydig cells. "Secrete inhibin" is a FALSE statement about Leydig cells.

3. Neuroendocrine Control of Male Reproduction

The hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal axis (下丘腦-垂體-性腺軸) in males operates as follows. The hypothalamus (下丘腦) releases GnRH (促性腺激素釋放激素, gonadotropin-releasing hormone), which stimulates the anterior pituitary (前葉腦垂體) to release both LH (黃體生成素, luteinising hormone) and FSH (促卵泡素, follicle-stimulating hormone). Both LH and FSH are required for spermatogenesis.

LH targets Leydig cells and drives testosterone synthesis. Testosterone then exerts negative feedback (負回饋) on both the hypothalamus (inhibiting GnRH) and the anterior pituitary (inhibiting LH and FSH release). FSH targets Sertoli cells and directly stimulates spermatogenesis, but this process also requires testosterone. Inhibin, secreted by Sertoli cells, provides specific negative feedback on FSH release only.

GnRH secretion is also modulated by sensory inputs. Visual, olfactory (嗅覺), or tactile (觸覺) stimuli and thoughts and moods can all influence GnRH release. Prolonged stress, such as protracted aerobic exercise (持久有氧運動), suppresses GnRH secretion in both sexes, leading to inhibition of sperm production in males and disruption of menstruation in females.

Actions of testosterone: (1) In prenatal (產前) development, triggers development of primary male sex characteristics (genitalia). (2) At puberty (青春期), drives enlargement of male sex organs (penis, scrotum, testes) and development of secondary sex characteristics (voice changes, facial hair). (3) Maintains spermatogenesis. (4) Promotes general growth and protein synthesis, especially muscle proteins (肌肉蛋白), producing greater muscle mass in males. (5) Behavioural changes, including increased aggression (攻擊性). Note: testosterone is a steroid secreted primarily by Leydig cells but also by the adrenal cortex; it acts on many tissues, not only Sertoli cells.

Exam trap: Testosterone is NOT secreted only by Leydig cells — the adrenal cortex also produces testosterone and other androgens. Testosterone promotes protein synthesis (合成), not degradation. It acts on many target tissues (seminiferous tubules, somatic tissues, brain, primary and secondary sex organs), not only Sertoli cells.

4. Female Reproductive System and Oogenesis

Female sex organs include external genitalia (外生殖器, collectively the vulva 外陰) and four internal organs: (1) vagina (陰道), a muscular canal for receiving the penis and passage of the infant at birth; (2) the uterus (子宮), a muscular organ for implantation and fetal development, whose inner layer, the endometrium (子宮內膜), is a secretory tissue of glands and connective tissue; (3) the uterine tube (輸卵管, also called oviduct or fallopian tube 法羅皮管), the site of fertilisation (受精) of the ovum and transport from ovary to uterus; (4) two ovaries (卵巢), the primary sex organs, which produce ova and secrete estrogens and progesterone, with all processes occurring inside the follicles (卵泡). Note: unlike males, females have separate exits for the urinary and reproductive systems.

Oogenesis (卵子發生) is the formation, development, and maturation of ova (卵子; singular: ovum = egg). Each ovulatory cycle (~28 days), one ovum is expelled by one of the two ovaries. A woman's reproductive lifespan (puberty to menopause 更年期) yields about 400–500 mature ova total.

Ova develop in primary follicles (初級卵泡) in the ovary. A follicle = oocyte (卵母細胞) + supporting cells that provide nutrients, chemical signals, and a barrier (analogous to Sertoli cells in males). After ovulation (排卵), the ruptured follicle transforms into the corpus luteum (黃體, literally "yellow body"), which secretes progesterone and some estrogen. If no fertilisation and implantation occur, the corpus luteum degenerates and hormone levels fall.

Exam trap: The oviduct (fallopian tube 輸卵管) is the site of fertilisation, not implantation. Implantation (著床) occurs in the endometrium of the uterus. The ovary is the site of ova production, not fertilisation.

5. The Female Reproductive Cycle

The ~28-day human reproductive cycle (生殖週期) actually consists of two parallel cycles: the ovarian cycle (卵巢週期) and the uterine cycle (子宮週期). Their phases align as follows:

DayOvarian cycle phaseUterine cycle phaseKey events

Day 0–10 (early follicular / menses): The previous corpus luteum degenerates (no implantation), removing its negative feedback on the hypothalamus. GnRH secretion rises, triggering FSH and LH release. FSH stimulates follicle growth, which begins producing estrogen. Simultaneously, the falling progesterone/estrogen triggers menstruation (月經) — sloughing of the endometrium lasting ~5 days.

Day 10–12 (late follicular): Rising estrogen from the growing follicle initially provides positive feedback (正回饋) to the hypothalamus, amplifying GnRH/LH/FSH release. Estrogen also drives proliferation of the endometrium.

Day 12–14 (ovulation): The LH and FSH surge (峰值) triggered by high estrogen causes ovulation — release of the egg around day 14.

Day 14–28 (luteal phase): The ruptured follicle becomes the corpus luteum, which secretes progesterone and some estrogen. High progesterone/estrogen now switches feedback back to negative, suppressing FSH and LH. Progesterone and estrogen also maintain and enhance endometrium secretions (secretory phase). Inhibin released at ovulation also inhibits FSH. If no implantation, the corpus luteum degenerates, hormones fall, and the next cycle begins.

If pregnancy occurs: Fertilisation typically takes place in the fallopian tube several days after ovulation. The fertilised ovum implants in the endometrium. Within a day of implantation, the placenta (胎盤) begins secreting chorionic gonadotropin (CG / hCG) (人絨毛膜促性腺激素), which "rescues" the corpus luteum, preventing its degeneration. The corpus luteum continues producing progesterone and estrogen through the first trimester (三個月); thereafter the placenta takes over hormone production.

Exam trap: During the follicular phase, estrogen stimulates GnRH/LH/FSH release (positive feedback 正回饋). During the luteal phase, the same estrogen plus progesterone inhibit GnRH/LH/FSH (negative feedback 負回饋). The direction of estrogen's effect on the hypothalamus depends on the phase — this is a very common exam trap. Also: "ovulation is prevented by follicular estrogen" is FALSE — high follicular estrogen actually TRIGGERS the LH surge and ovulation.

6. Female Hormones — Effects, Contraception, and IVF

Estrogens (雌激素) include three main forms: estradiol (雌二醇), estrone (雌酮), and estriol (雌三醇); 17β-estradiol is the most potent. Effects:
Growth and development at puberty: stimulates growth of primary sex organs (first sex characteristics), stimulates secondary sex characteristics, promotes growth of long bones but also causes early closure of the epiphyses (骨骺), limiting final height.
Hormonal: phase-dependent dual feedback on GnRH/LH/FSH (stimulates in follicular phase, inhibits in luteal phase); decreases sensitivity of peripheral tissues (周邊組織) to insulin (胰島素).
Metabolic: protects against bone loss (骨質疏鬆) in women — hence increased osteoporosis risk after menopause.

Gestational diabetes (妊娠糖尿病): estrogen decreases insulin sensitivity. As the placenta grows during pregnancy it produces more estrogen, increasing insulin resistance (胰島素阻抗). The pancreas compensates with additional insulin, but if maximal pancreatic output cannot overcome placental hormone effects, gestational diabetes results. It typically develops around weeks 20–24 of pregnancy.

Progesterone (黃體素) effects: (1) Stimulates full development of the endometrium, preparing it for implantation. (2) Stimulates further development of mammary glands (乳腺). (3) Provides negative feedback to the hypothalamus to prevent further ovulation during pregnancy. (4) Antagonises the effects of testosterone and dihydrotestosterone (DHT 二氫睾酮), preventing target tissues from responding to androgens in the female.

Contraceptive pill (避孕藥) combines estrogen and progestogen (合成黃體素): 99.7% effective. Mechanism: suppresses gonadotropin (促性腺激素) release (preventing ovulation) and increases cervical mucus (子宮頸黏液) viscosity to inhibit sperm mobility. The most common side effect is "breakthrough bleeding." The first generation pill was FDA-approved in 1960.

In vitro fertilisation (IVF, 體外受精) was invented by Robert Edwards (Nobel Prize 2010) and Patrick Steptoe; Louise Brown (1978) was the first "test tube baby" and more than 8 million IVF babies have been born since. IVF requires ovarian hyperstimulation (卵巢過度刺激) using FSH to generate multiple eggs, plus human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) for final oocyte (卵母細胞) maturation. Unused embryos sometimes go to stem cell (幹細胞) research, which raises ethical controversy.

Exam trap: Gestational diabetes is caused by high estrogen (from the growing placenta) decreasing insulin sensitivity — NOT by progesterone and NOT by excess carbohydrate intake. Also, estrogen causes early epiphyseal closure, so it limits, not extends, final bone growth.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Not all reproductive hormones are steroids — oxytocin (nonapeptide) and prolactin (peptide) are NOT steroids.
  • Androgens are present in BOTH sexes (adrenal cortex produces them in females too).
  • Sertoli cells respond to FSH and secrete inhibin; Leydig cells respond to LH and secrete testosterone. Sertoli cells do NOT secrete inhibin according to being Leydig cells — "Leydig cells secrete inhibin" is FALSE.
  • Testosterone promotes protein SYNTHESIS, not degradation; it acts on many tissues, not only Sertoli cells.
  • Testosterone is secreted by both Leydig cells AND the adrenal cortex, not exclusively by Leydig cells.
  • Fertilisation occurs in the fallopian tube (oviduct); implantation occurs in the uterine endometrium.
  • Estrogen's feedback direction depends on the cycle phase: POSITIVE feedback in follicular phase (drives LH surge), NEGATIVE feedback in luteal phase. "Follicular estrogen prevents ovulation" is FALSE — it triggers ovulation.
  • Gestational diabetes is caused by high ESTROGEN (from placenta) reducing insulin sensitivity — not progesterone and not diet.
  • Contraceptive pills prevent ovulation by suppressing gonadotropins, not by blocking estrogen receptors.
  • Inhibin from Sertoli cells (males) or follicle (females) specifically inhibits FSH only, not LH.
  • Corpus luteum is rescued by hCG from the placenta — not by FSH or LH from the pituitary.
Top things to memorize
  1. Biosynthetic pathway: Cholesterol → Progesterone → Androgens → Estrogens
  2. Leydig cells (LH → testosterone); Sertoli cells (FSH → spermatogenesis + inhibin → inhibits FSH only)
  3. Three functions of Sertoli cells: blood-testes barrier, seminiferous tubular fluid, inhibin synthesis
  4. Testosterone actions: prenatal primary sex characteristics, puberty sex organ growth + 2° characteristics, spermatogenesis, muscle protein synthesis, aggression
  5. Stress (e.g. prolonged exercise) inhibits GnRH in both sexes → stops sperm production / disrupts menstruation
  6. Ovarian cycle phases: Follicular (day 0–14) → Ovulation (~day 14) → Luteal (day 14–28)
  7. Uterine cycle phases: Menses → Proliferative → Secretory (mirrors ovarian phases)
  8. Estrogen: POSITIVE feedback in follicular phase (triggers LH surge), NEGATIVE feedback in luteal phase
  9. After ovulation: follicle → corpus luteum → secretes progesterone + estrogen → negative feedback on FSH/LH
  10. hCG from placenta rescues corpus luteum in pregnancy; placenta takes over hormone production after first trimester
  11. Gestational diabetes: placental estrogen → insulin resistance (~weeks 20–24)

Lecture 4: GI1 — Overview of the Digestive System

The digestive system converts food into molecules the body can absorb — ingesting, digesting, absorbing, and excreting in a tightly coordinated sequence from mouth to anus. Understanding the four-segment plan (headgut, foregut, midgut, hindgut) and the layered wall structure explains why each region does what it does. Motility is not simple muscle movement but a regulated behaviour — peristalsis propels, segmentation mixes — both governed by the enteric nervous system, the "little brain in the gut."

1. General Concepts — Types of Digestion & Nutrient Classes

The digestive system (消化系統) consists of the alimentary canal (消化道) plus accessory digestive organs (附屬消化器官), working together for ingestion (攝食), digestion (消化), and absorption (吸收).

Two types of digestion (消化):

  • Mechanical digestion (機械性消化) — solid food masses are torn apart, ground up, and vigorously mixed with digestive secretions (消化液) to prepare food for chemical digestion. Examples: chewing (咀嚼), mixing by tongue, churning (攪拌) in stomach, segmentation (分節, rhythmic local constrictions of intestine).
  • Chemical digestion (化學性消化) — complex food molecules are broken down to their chemical building blocks by enzymes (酶) secreted by various digestive glands.

3. Microscopic Structure of the GI Wall

Four concentric layers from lumen outward:

  • Mucosa (黏膜) — innermost; mucous membrane with epithelial (上皮), connective (結締), and smooth muscle (平滑肌) tissues; contains glands and mucosa-associated lymphoid tissue (MALT)
  • Submucosa (黏膜下層) — loose connective tissue with glands; contains the submucosal plexus (Meissner's plexus 梅氏神經叢) of the enteric nervous system — controls secretion
  • Muscularis (肌層) — longitudinal (縱行) and circular (環行) smooth muscle fibres; the myenteric plexus (Auerbach's plexus 奧氏神經叢) lies between these two layers — controls motility
  • Serosa (漿膜) — outermost; visceral peritoneum (腹膜)
Exam trap: The myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus lies between the longitudinal and circular muscle layers — not in the submucosa. The submucosal (Meissner's) plexus is in the submucosa.

4. Types of GI Motility — Peristalsis & Segmentation

Three functions of GI movement (運動): (1) translocate food from point A to B, (2) mechanical breakdown of food, (3) mix food for continual renewal of material in contact with secreting and absorbing surfaces. Food moves down the GI tract even in the absence of gravity — GI mobility requires muscle involvement.

5. Control of Motility — Enteric Nervous System & Innervation

Two levels of control (控制):

  • Intrinsic control (內在控制) — mediated by the enteric nervous system (ENS 腸道神經系統); modulated by stretching of the GI tract and substances in the chyme (食糜, partly digested food in the stomach)
  • Extrinsic control (外在控制): (a) GI peptide hormones (胜肽激素) secreted by the GI tract in response to chemical stimulants; (b) sympathetic (交感神經, inhibitory 抑制) and parasympathetic (副交感神經, excitatory 興奮) nervous systems

The ENS — nicknamed the "little brain in the gut" — contains ~100 million neurons (more than a mouse brain), organized as an independent integrative system with two plexuses:

  • Myenteric plexus (肌層神經叢 / Auerbach's plexus) — between muscle layers; controls motility
  • Submucosal plexus (黏膜下神經叢 / Meissner's plexus) — in submucosa; controls secretion and absorption

ENS neuron types: sensory neurons (感覺神經元) — mechanoreceptors (機械感受器), chemoreceptors (化學感受器), thermoreceptors (溫度感受器); interneurons (中間神經元) — reflexes, motor programs; motor neurons (運動神經元) — inhibitory & excitatory → targets: muscle, secretory epithelium (分泌上皮), endocrine cells, blood vessels.

Extrinsic innervation (外在神經支配):

  • Sympathetic (交感): preganglionic cholinergic (膽鹼能) → postganglionic adrenergic (腎上腺素能) / cholinergic → inhibits GI motility and secretion
  • Parasympathetic (副交感): vagus nerve (迷走神經) — preganglionic cholinergic → postganglionic cholinergic/adrenergic → excites GI motility and secretion

Hirschsprung's disease (先天性巨結腸症): in children, nerves fail to form in all or part of the large intestine; waste cannot pass through the colon lacking nerve tissue; the normal colon swells with blocked stool.

Exam trap: Sympathetic = inhibitory for GI; parasympathetic = excitatory. "Fight or flight" shuts the gut down; "rest and digest" activates it. Many students mistakenly flip these.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Stomach has no absorption — absorption begins in the small intestine (midgut).
  • Segmentation uses circular muscle only — longitudinal muscle is NOT involved (peristalsis uses both).
  • Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus is between muscle layers; submucosal (Meissner's) plexus is in the submucosa — do not swap them.
  • Sympathetic is inhibitory for GI motility; parasympathetic is excitatory ("rest and digest").
Top things to memorize
  1. Two digestion types: mechanical (tears/grinds/mixes) vs. chemical (enzymes break bonds)
  2. Three nutrients and end-products: protein → amino acids; carbs → monosaccharides (glucose/fructose/galactose); fats → glycerol + fatty acids
  3. Four GI segments: headgut (mouth/pharynx) → foregut (esophagus/stomach) → midgut (small intestine/pancreas/liver) → hindgut (colon/rectum/anus)
  4. Four wall layers (lumen outward): mucosa → submucosa → muscularis → serosa
  5. Two plexuses: myenteric (between muscle layers, motility) vs. submucosal (in submucosa, secretion/absorption)
  6. Peristalsis: both muscles, propulsion, aboral direction; Segmentation: circular muscle only, mixing, no net propulsion
  7. ENS = ~100 million neurons; sensory + interneurons + motor; operates independently
  8. Sympathetic inhibits GI; parasympathetic excites GI
  9. Hirschsprung's disease = absent enteric neurons in colon → stool blockage + colon swelling

Lecture 5: GI2 — Digestion: Mouth, Esophagus, Stomach, Pancreas & Liver

GI Lecture 2 traces the path of food through each digestive organ from mouth to duodenum, covering the mechanical and chemical processes at each site. Every structure — from salivary amylase to the parietal cell's proton pump — is precisely specialized, and understanding their secretions and regulation explains why clinical conditions such as GERD, H. pylori ulcers, and gallstones arise exactly where they do.

1. Mouth & Salivary Glands

The mouth (口腔 / buccal cavity 頰腔) contains three main structures:

  • Teeth (牙齒) — biting, tearing, grinding (mechanical digestion)
  • Tongue (舌頭) — tasting and manipulating food bolus (食團); involved in swallowing
  • Salivary glands (唾液腺) — three pairs: parotid (腮腺), sublingual (舌下腺), submandibular (頜下腺); secrete multifunctional saliva (~1.5 L/day)

Two cell types in salivary glands:

Three functions of saliva (唾液):

  • A. Moistening & lubrication (潤滑) — essential for swallowing and taste
  • B. Protect teeth against dental caries (蛀牙): contains enzyme proteins (peroxidase 過氧化物酶, lysozyme 溶菌酶) and antibacterial proteins (defensins 防禦素); saliva is slightly alkaline (弱鹼性) — neutralizes acids from mouth bacteria and acid refluxed from stomach
  • C. Amylase — begins starch digestion: starch → maltose (麥芽糖, 2 glucose), maltotriose (麥芽三糖, 3 glucose), oligosaccharides (寡醣, >3 glucose)

Defensins (防禦素): secreted by immune cells and epithelial cells; amphipathic (兩親性) and cationic (陽離子); mechanism: disrupt pathogen membrane (破壞病原體細胞膜), block viral receptors and signaling.

Regulation of saliva: salivary centers in brain stem receive two types of stimuli: (A) touch (pressoreceptors 壓力感受器), smell, and taste (taste buds 味蕾); (B) psychological stimuli (e.g. thought of food = conditioned reflex 條件反射); fear inhibits; sleep, fatigue (疲勞), and dehydration (脫水) also inhibit (→ dry mouth). Salivary glands controlled by autonomic nerves (自主神經):

  • Parasympathetic (facial & glossopharyngeal nerves) — promotes moderate amount of watery saliva; keeps mouth, lips, tongue moist
  • Sympathetic — also stimulates secretion but less than parasympathetic; saliva is richer in mucins (mucin-rich 富黏蛋白), more viscous; sympathetic stimulus → dry mouth sensation

Magnitudes of GI secretion (L/day): Saliva 1.5 | Gastric juice 1–3 | Pancreatic juice 2 | Bile 0.6–1.2 | Intestinal secretions 2 | Total 7–10 L/day. Water lost in feces: ~100 ml/day (the rest is reabsorbed).

Exam trap: Sympathetic stimulation of salivary glands still produces saliva, but it is more viscous and mucin-rich — not absent. The subjective sensation is dry mouth because of less total fluid.

2. Pharynx, Esophagus & Swallowing

The pharynx (咽) has three parts: nasopharynx (鼻咽, air only), oropharynx (口咽), laryngopharynx (喉咽). It is a common passageway for food, fluids, and air; the external muscle layer contains two skeletal muscle layers (pharyngeal constrictor muscles 咽縮肌) involved in the swallowing reflex.

Swallowing reflex (吞嚥反射 / deglutition 吞嚥) — sequential steps:

  1. Tongue moves bolus to rear of mouth → presence of object activates swallowing reflex in pharynx
  2. Soft palate (軟顎) closes nasal passage; epiglottis (會厭) moves over glottis (聲門) to close larynx (喉) and trachea (氣管)
  3. Upper esophageal sphincter (上食道括約肌) relaxes → food bolus propelled into esophagus → sphincter re-contracts

The esophagus (食道) is a collapsible muscular tube (~10 inches / ~25 cm) posterior to the trachea; conducts food to stomach by peristalsis (蠕動). Key features:

  • Begins at inferior end of laryngopharynx; passes through diaphragm (橫膈膜) via esophageal hiatus (食道裂孔)
  • Mucosa = stratified squamous epithelium (多層扁平上皮) / smooth muscle (平滑肌)
  • Upper esophageal sphincter (上食道括約肌) — controls passage from laryngopharynx into esophagus
  • Lower esophageal (cardiac) sphincter (下食道括約肌) — controls passage from esophagus into stomach

GERD (胃食道逆流病): failure of lower esophageal sphincter to close after food enters stomach → acid backflow → "heartburn" (胃灼熱) sensation. Affects ~10–20% of Western populations. Risk factors: obesity (肥胖), pregnancy (懷孕), smoking (吸煙).

Exam trap: The epiglottis covers the glottis (trachea opening) — not the esophagus — during swallowing. The esophageal sphincter relaxes to allow food entry. These two events happen simultaneously but are mechanically distinct.

3. Stomach — Structure & Gastric Juice

The stomach (胃) is a J-shaped enlargement of the GI tract connecting the esophagus to the duodenum (十二指腸). Four functions: (1) food reservoir (食物儲存庫), (2) mechanical fragmentation (機械性分解), (3) mix food with gastric secretion → chyme (食糜), (4) empty gastric contents into small intestine at a controlled rate.

Gross structure (大體結構):

  • Cardia (賁門, "near the heart") — entry from esophagus
  • Fundus (胃底)
  • Body (胃體) — midportion
  • Pyloric region (幽門區, funnel-shaped): pyloric antrum (幽門竇 "cave"), pyloric canal (幽門管), pylorus (幽門) with pyloric sphincter (幽門括約肌) that controls stomach emptying

Rugae (胃皺褶): large folds in the mucosa of the empty stomach; enable expansion (膨脹) and distension (伸展).

Gastric juice — five components:

  • 2. Pepsinogen → pepsin (胃蛋白酶) — converted at pH < 5; cleaves proteins (蛋白質) → small peptides; maximal activity at pH ~2; pepsin can autocatalyze its own formation from pepsinogen.

  • 4. Intrinsic factor (內因子) — glycoprotein (糖蛋白) that binds vitamin B12; essential for B12 absorption in the ileum (迴腸); needed for RBC production (紅血球生成).

  • 1. H⁺/K⁺ ATPase (proton pump 質子幫浦) — apical; pumps H⁺ into lumen, K⁺ into cell (active, ATP-driven)
  • 2. Cl⁻/HCO₃⁻ exchanger (anion exchanger 陰離子交換蛋白) — basolateral; HCO₃⁻ exits to blood, Cl⁻ enters cell
  • 3. Cl⁻ channel — apical; Cl⁻ exits into lumen alongside H⁺ → HCl formed in lumen
  • Source of H⁺: CO₂ + H₂O → H₂CO₃ → HCO₃⁻ + H⁺, catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase (碳酸酐酶, CA)
Exam trap: Pepsinogen requires pH < 5 for conversion to pepsin, but pepsin's optimal activity is at pH ~2. Do not confuse conversion pH with optimal activity pH.

5. Pancreas & Liver/Bile

The pancreas (胰腺) is a soft oblong gland ~6 in. long / 1 in. thick; posterior to the greater curvature of the stomach; connected to the duodenum by pancreatic (duct of Wirsung 胰管) and accessory ducts (duct of Santorini) via the sphincter of Oddi (奧迪括約肌).

Exocrine pancreas — two secretory cell types:

  • Acinar cells (腺泡細胞) — secrete small volume of digestive enzymes

Gallstones (膽結石, cholesterol stones): possible causes — enzyme defect, decreased secretion of bile salts, or increased cholesterol synthesis → cholesterol precipitates.

Lecture 6: GI3 — Absorption: Small Intestine & Large Intestine

GI Lecture 3 focuses on absorption — the transfer of digested nutrients from the intestinal lumen into the bloodstream or lymph. The small intestine is extraordinarily specialized for this, amplifying its surface area ~600-fold through circular folds, villi, and microvilli. Each nutrient class uses a distinct molecular strategy: sugars ride a sodium gradient, amino acids and dipeptides use active transporters, and lipids are packaged into micelles before being reassembled as chylomicrons for lymphatic export.

1. Small Intestine — Structure & General Absorption

Absorption (吸收) is the process by which molecules are transported through the epithelial cells (上皮細胞) lining the GI tract → enter the blood (血液) or lymph (淋巴). Key principles:

  • Occurs only after complete chemical digestion — ingested molecules must be cleaved into smaller units first
  • Nutrients (carbohydrates, fats, proteins), water, ions, and some vitamins absorbed primarily in the small intestine (小腸)
  • Absorption of fluid and certain ions also occurs in the large intestine (大腸)

Small intestine (SI): ~6 m long, ~4 cm diameter; accounts for ¾ of GI tract length; chyme takes 2–4 hours to pass through. Three segments:

Ileum (迴腸)3.5 mMore absorption; joins large intestine; B12 absorbed here (with intrinsic factor)

2. Carbohydrate Absorption

Dietary carbohydrates (碳水化合物) in two categories:

  • Digestible (可消化的): Major = plant starch (植物澱粉, a polysaccharide 多醣; e.g. rice, bread). Minor = disaccharides (雙醣): sucrose (蔗糖 = 1 glucose + 1 fructose), lactose (乳糖 = 1 glucose + 1 galactose).
  • Indigestible (不可消化的): cellulose (纖維素) = dietary fiber (膳食纖維); excreted in feces.

Digestion pathway: starch → maltose (麥芽糖), maltotriose (麥芽三糖), oligosaccharides (寡醣) by salivary + pancreatic amylases. Only monosaccharides (單醣) can be absorbed — brush border enzymes complete hydrolysis (水解):

  • Glucose & galactose — secondary active transport (次級主動運輸) via SGLT1 (sodium-glucose transporter 1 鈉葡萄糖共同運輸蛋白); downhill Na⁺ flux (順梯度) energizes uphill glucose/galactose entry; Na⁺ then pumped out basolaterally by Na⁺/K⁺ ATPase; glucose/galactose exit across basolateral membrane via GLUT2 (passive)

ORT reduced risk of death from diarrhea by 93%; on WHO Essential Medicine list.

Exam trap: ORT works because of SGLT1 — glucose is essential to drive Na⁺ (and therefore water) absorption. A solution of water + salt alone without glucose is far less effective because it cannot use SGLT1-mediated cotransport.

4. Lipid Absorption

Dietary lipids: triglycerides (三酸甘油酯, TG) account for ~90%; the rest = cholesterol esters (膽固醇酯), phospholipids (磷脂), fat-soluble vitamins (脂溶性維生素 A, D, E, K).

Digestion: TG emulsified (乳化) by bile salts → digested by gastric lipase + pancreatic lipase → free fatty acids (游離脂肪酸, FFA) + monoglycerides (單酸甘油酯, MG).

Mixed micelles (混合微胞): FFA + MG (and other lipid-soluble molecules) form mixed micelles with amphipathic bile salts (兩親性膽鹽). Micelles keep hydrophobic lipids in aqueous solution. The molecules in mixed micelles are in dynamic equilibrium (動態平衡) with single free molecules — micelles serve as a reservoir, replenishing free molecules as they are absorbed at the brush border.

Two routes depending on fatty acid chain length:

  • Short-chain fatty acids (<12 carbons) — absorbed directly into portal vein (門靜脈) → liver
  • Long-chain fatty acids + monoglycerides (>12 carbons) — passively diffuse into absorptive cells → resynthesized in ER (內質網) into TG → packaged in Golgi (高基氏體) with other lipids + apoproteins into chylomicrons (乳糜微粒, 100–500 nm lipoprotein particles) → expelled by exocytosis (胞吐) into central lacteal (中央乳糜管) — too large for capillaries → lymph → venous circulation

Large intestine (大腸/結腸): primarily absorbs water (水) and ions (離子) and some vitamins; accumulates and dehydrates digestion waste; stores feces for defecation (排便). No significant nutrient absorption occurs here.

Exam trap: Chylomicrons enter the lacteal (lymph), not the portal blood — they are too large for capillaries. Short-chain FAs go directly to the portal vein. Many students assume all lipids take the same route.

  • ORT requires glucose to work — glucose drives SGLT1-mediated Na⁺ and water absorption; salt water alone is far less effective.
  • Dipeptides and tripeptides are the major (faster) protein absorption pathway — not free amino acids.
  • Chylomicrons (long-chain fats) → lacteal/lymph; short-chain FAs (<12C) → portal vein directly.
  • Fructose is absorbed passively; glucose and galactose require SGLT1 (Na⁺ cotransport, active).
  • Carbohydrate absorption is complete by the end of the jejunum — not the ileum.

  2. Surface area: 0.33 m² → ~200 m²; circular folds ×3, villi ×10, microvilli ×20

Lecture 7: Physiology of Nerve Cells — Ion Channels, Membrane Potential & Action Potentials

Neurons communicate by controlling the flow of ions (離子) across the cell membrane through specialised pore-forming proteins called ion channels (離子通道). The selective permeability of the membrane to different ions, especially K+, establishes a negative resting membrane potential (靜息膜電位) of about −70 mV. When a depolarising (去極化) stimulus pushes the membrane potential above threshold (閾值), voltage-gated Na+ channels open in a positive-feedback loop that generates a brief all-or-none action potential (動作電位). The action potential then propagates along the axon — passively decrementing in subthreshold signals, but regeneratively and unidirectionally in the AP — and ultimately triggers neurotransmitter release at the synapse (突觸) to influence the next cell.

1. Ion Channels — Structure, Properties & Gating

Ion channels (離子通道) are pore-forming membrane proteins that allow specific ions to pass through the cell membrane. Unlike carrier proteins (載體蛋白), channels move ions purely by passive transport — they never couple to ATP or co-transport mechanisms. Two hallmark features set them apart from other transporters:

  1. Extremely fast ion flux: >106 ions per second.
  2. Ions travel downhill along their electrochemical gradient (電化學梯度) — no metabolic energy required.

Ion channels serve four major physiological roles:

  • Establishing the resting membrane potential (靜息膜電位).
  • Shaping action potentials and other electrical signals.
  • Controlling ion flow across secretory and epithelial cells.
  • Regulating cell volume.

Membrane transport proteins (膜轉運蛋白) fall into two broad families:

  • Channels: always passive; ions move along electrochemical gradient (downhill / facilitated diffusion).
  • Carriers: can be passive or active.
    • Secondary active transport — coupled carriers (e.g., symporters 同向轉運體, antiporters 反向轉運體) use the gradient of one ion to move another uphill.
    • Primary active transport — ATP-driven pumps (e.g., Na+/K+-ATPase) directly hydrolyse ATP.
The key rule: the electrochemical gradient determines the direction of all passive transport of charged molecules (Electrochemical gradient = membrane potential + concentration gradient).

Exam trap: Channels are always passive; carriers can be active or passive. Do not say "ion channels can perform active transport."

Gating (閘控) means the control of channel open and closed states. A single channel fluctuates stochastically between these two conformations. Channels can be gated in different ways:

  • Voltage-gated (電壓閘控): open/close in response to changes in membrane potential.
  • Ligand-gated (配體閘控): open/close when a specific molecule (neurotransmitter, second messenger) binds.
  • Mechanically gated: respond to mechanical deformation of the membrane.
The patch-clamp technique (膜片鉗技術), developed by Erwin Neher and Bernd Sakmann (Nobel Prize 1991), allows recording of single-channel currents from a tiny membrane patch — the gold standard for studying channel gating, pharmacology, and biophysics.

Tetrodotoxin (河豚毒素, TTX), produced in puffer fish and certain other animals, is a potent selective blocker of voltage-gated Na+ channels. It is a classic pharmacological tool used to isolate Na+ channel contributions in electrophysiological experiments.

Exam trap: TTX blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels, not all Na+ transport or other channel types. It does not block K+ channels.

2. Membrane Potential & the Nernst / GHK Equations

Two conditions are required to build a membrane potential (膜電位):

  1. A semi-permeable membrane — different ions have different permeabilities.
  2. An ion concentration gradient (離子濃度梯度) across the membrane (K+out/K+in ≠ 1).
By convention, the extracellular side is the reference (ground = 0 mV), so the inside of a neuron at rest is negative (~−70 mV in mammalian neurons). The resting potential reflects the membrane being most permeable to K+ at rest.

Key ion concentration gradients (mammalian neuron):

Exam trap: In the Nernst equation, the reference (outside) concentration goes on top of the logarithm by convention: E = (RT/Fz) × ln([ion]out/[ion]in). Putting inside on top gives the wrong sign.

The Nernst equation (能斯特方程) calculates the equilibrium potential (平衡電位) for a single ion — the membrane voltage at which there is no net flow of that ion:
E = (RT / Fz) × ln([ion]out / [ion]in)
At body/room temperature this simplifies to ~58 mV/z × log([ion]out/[ion]in).

  • EK (squid): 58/1 × log(20/400) = −75 mV → this equals the resting potential when the membrane is selectively permeable to K+.
  • ENa (squid): 58/1 × log(440/50) = +55 mV → strongly positive, driving Na+ inward during AP.

Exam trap: The Nernst equilibrium potential is the voltage at which net flux is zero — it is not the same as the resting membrane potential unless that ion is the only one carrying current. The resting potential is set by the relative permeabilities of all ions (GHK equation).

The Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz (GHK) equation (高曼-霍奇金-卡茨方程) accounts for the contribution of multiple ions (K+, Na+, Cl−) weighted by their relative membrane permeabilities (相對膜通透性, p):

  • At rest in a typical neuron: pK : pNa : pCl = 1 : 0.05 : 0.45.
  • Because pK dominates at rest, Vrest is close to EK (but not exactly, because pNa and pCl pull it slightly positive).
  • Permeability values are reported as relative (unitless) with pK = 1 as reference.

The Na+/K+-ATPase (鈉鉀泵) pumps 3 Na+ out and 2 K+ in per ATP hydrolysed. It is not directly responsible for the moment-to-moment resting potential (that is set by ion channel permeabilities and gradients), but it is essential for long-term maintenance of the ion concentration gradients that make the resting potential possible.

Exam trap: The Na+/K+-ATPase is electrogenic (net outward positive charge = slight hyperpolarisation), but its primary role is gradient maintenance, not moment-to-moment potential generation.

3. Action Potential — Generation & Phases

The action potential (動作電位, AP) is a rapid, stereotyped voltage transient lasting ~1–2 ms. Its phases are:

  1. Resting potential (~−70 mV): voltage-gated Na+ and K+ channels closed.
  2. Depolarisation (去極化) — rising phase: voltage-gated Na+ channels open → Na+ rushes in → membrane rapidly swings toward +55 mV (ENa).
  3. Repolarisation (復極化) — falling phase: Na+ channels inactivate (not simply close); delayed voltage-gated K+ channels open → K+ rushes out → membrane returns toward rest.
  4. Afterhyperpolarisation (後超極化): K+ channels remain open briefly longer than needed → membrane overshoots below resting potential.
  5. Return to resting potential as K+ channels close.

Three key characteristics of the AP:

  • All-or-none (全或無) — once threshold is crossed, the AP always has the same amplitude and shape; stimuli below threshold produce no AP.
  • Short duration — ~1–2 ms.
  • Requires a trigger depolarisation above threshold (閾值) — typically ~15–20 mV depolarisation from rest.

Threshold (閾值) represents a critical tipping point driven by a positive-feedback loop:

  1. Depolarisation exceeds the rate of membrane repolarisation.
  2. Na+ influx exceeds K+ efflux.
  3. More depolarisation → more voltage-gated Na+ channels open.
  4. More open Na+ channels → more depolarisation → runaway feedback → AP fires.

Exam trap: Threshold is a dynamic, regenerative event — once Na+ influx exceeds K+ efflux, the process is self-sustaining. A stimulus that is "just below threshold" produces only a graded depolarisation, not an AP.

4. Refractory Periods & AP Propagation

After an AP, voltage-gated Na+ channels pass through an inactivated state (distinct from simply closed). This produces two refractory phases (不應期):

  • Absolute refractory period (絕對不應期): Na+ channels are fully inactivated → threshold is effectively infinite → no second AP can be generated regardless of stimulus strength.
  • Relative refractory period (相對不應期): Na+ channels are partially recovering from inactivation; afterhyperpolarisation means membrane is more negative than rest → threshold is elevated → an AP can be generated but only with a stronger than normal stimulus, and the resulting AP has a smaller amplitude.
  • Normal excitability returns when channels fully recover and membrane returns to resting potential.
The refractory period sets an upper limit on spike frequency and, together with inactivation, ensures unidirectional propagation (the region just behind an advancing AP is refractory).

Exam trap: Na+ channel inactivation ≠ simply "closing." Inactivated channels cannot be opened by depolarisation alone — they must first recover (de-inactivate) by returning to hyperpolarised potentials.

Passive (subthreshold) propagation (被動傳導): local current spreads electrotonically but:

  • Amplitude decays exponentially with distance (determined by the length constant, λ).
  • Signal slows over distance.
  • Spreads in all directions.
Active (AP) propagation (主動傳導):
  • Regenerative — no decrement in amplitude.
  • Unidirectional — the region behind the AP is in the absolute refractory period.
  • Fast conduction.

In myelinated axons (有髓軸突), the myelin sheath (髓鞘) insulates the internodal membrane and dramatically increases the effective length constant. APs can only be regenerated at the nodes of Ranvier (朗維爾結) — gaps in the myelin. The AP appears to "jump" from node to node: this is called saltatory conduction (跳躍式傳導). Benefits: much faster conduction velocity at lower metabolic cost compared to unmyelinated axons of equal diameter.

Exam trap: Saltatory conduction is faster AND more energetically efficient than conduction in unmyelinated axons — both points are often tested. The AP itself is not different; only the spatial pattern of where it regenerates changes.

5. Synaptic Transmission — Chemical Synapses

At a chemical synapse (化學突觸), information is converted from electrical to chemical and back to electrical. The five steps are:

  1. Synthesis (合成): neurotransmitter (神經遞質) is synthesised in the cell body and/or terminal.
  2. Storage (儲存): packed into synaptic vesicles (突觸小泡) and transported to the terminal.
  3. Release / Exocytosis (釋放 / 胞吐): AP depolarises the terminal → voltage-gated Ca2+ channels open → Ca2+ influx → vesicle fusion with presynaptic membrane → transmitter released into synaptic cleft (突觸間隙).
  4. Postsynaptic effects (突觸後效應): transmitter binds postsynaptic receptor → ion channels open/close → electrical change (EPSP or IPSP).
  5. Inactivation (失活): transmitter is removed by (a) diffusion away from cleft, (b) enzymatic degradation (e.g., acetylcholinesterase for ACh), (c) presynaptic re-uptake (e.g., monoamine transporters).

Exam trap: Ca2+ influx is the trigger for vesicle fusion and transmitter release — it is the link between the electrical AP in the terminal and the chemical signal. Blocking Ca2+ channels abolishes release even if the AP fires normally.

EPSP (興奮性突觸後電位, excitatory post-synaptic potential): transmitter binding opens channels that depolarise the postsynaptic membrane (e.g., non-selective cation channels → Na+ in). The membrane moves toward threshold.

IPSP (抑制性突觸後電位, inhibitory post-synaptic potential): transmitter binding opens channels that hyperpolarise the membrane or clamp it below threshold (e.g., K+ channels open, or Cl− channels open → membrane toward ECl). The nature of the postsynaptic receptor determines whether the effect is excitatory or inhibitory, not the transmitter identity per se.

Exam trap: The same transmitter can produce an EPSP at one synapse and an IPSP at another, depending on which receptor and which ion channel it acts on. The receptor type — not the transmitter identity — determines the sign of the postsynaptic response.

Synaptic integration (突觸整合) occurs at the axon hillock (軸丘), the site of AP initiation. EPSPs and IPSPs are summed spatially (from different inputs arriving simultaneously) and temporally (from the same input firing repeatedly). Only when the net depolarisation crosses threshold does an AP fire — the neuron acts as a coincidence detector.

Termination of postsynaptic transmitter action occurs by:

  • Diffusion away from the cleft (common for peptide neurotransmitters).
  • Enzymatic degradation — e.g., acetylcholinesterase (乙醯膽鹼酯酶) breaks down acetylcholine (ACh).
  • Presynaptic re-uptake (回收) — e.g., monoamine transporters (DAT, SERT, NET) recycle dopamine, serotonin, noradrenaline.
  • Combinations of the above.

Exam Traps Summary
  • Ion channels are always passive — they never use ATP; carriers can be active or passive.
  • In the Nernst equation the outside concentration goes on top of the logarithm (extracellular = reference by convention).
  • The Nernst equilibrium potential is NOT the resting membrane potential; it is the voltage at which net flux of that single ion is zero.
  • The GHK equation (not Nernst) gives the actual resting potential by weighting all ions by their relative permeabilities (pK : pNa : pCl = 1 : 0.05 : 0.45 at rest).
  • Repolarisation is due to Na+ channel inactivation (not simply closing) AND K+ channel opening — both contribute.
  • Na+ channel inactivation ≠ channel closing: inactivated channels cannot be re-opened by depolarisation; they need hyperpolarisation to recover.
  • Absolute refractory period: no AP possible (Na+ channels fully inactivated). Relative refractory period: AP possible but requires stronger stimulus and produces smaller AP.
  • Saltatory conduction is both faster AND more metabolically efficient than unmyelinated conduction.
  • TTX blocks only voltage-gated Na+ channels, not K+ channels.
  • The nature of the postsynaptic receptor (not the transmitter identity) determines EPSP vs. IPSP.
  • Ca2+ influx at the presynaptic terminal triggers vesicle fusion; without Ca2+, release does not occur even if the AP fires.
  • The Na+/K+-ATPase maintains long-term ion gradients; it does not directly generate the resting potential moment-to-moment.
Top Things to Memorize
  1. Ion channels: passive only; >106 ions/sec; down electrochemical gradient; no ATP.
  2. Resting membrane potential requires (1) semi-permeable membrane + (2) concentration gradient; ~−70 mV in mammalian neurons.
  3. Nernst equation: E = (RT/Fz) × ln([ion]out/[ion]in); EK ≈ −75 mV (squid); ENa ≈ +55 mV (squid).
  4. GHK equation permeability ratios at rest: pK : pNa : pCl = 1 : 0.05 : 0.45.
  5. AP phases: resting → depolarisation (Na+ in) → repolarisation (Na+ inactivates + K+ out) → afterhyperpolarisation → rest; duration ~1–2 ms; all-or-none.
  6. AP threshold: positive-feedback loop — Na+ influx > K+ efflux → runaway depolarisation.
  7. Absolute refractory (Na+ fully inactivated, no AP possible) vs. relative refractory (partially inactivated, AP possible with stronger stimulus).
  8. Saltatory conduction in myelinated axons: AP jumps node-to-node; faster + more efficient.
  9. TTX = selective voltage-gated Na+ channel blocker from puffer fish.
  10. Chemical synapse 5 steps: synthesis → storage → release (Ca2+-triggered) → postsynaptic effects → inactivation.
  11. Postsynaptic receptor type determines EPSP (depolarisation) or IPSP (hyperpolarisation).
  12. Transmitter termination: diffusion (peptides), enzymatic degradation (ACh → acetylcholinesterase), re-uptake (monoamines).

Lecture 8: Neuro2 — Properties of Sensory Cells and Sensory Systems

Sensory receptors (感受器) are specialised cells — neural or non-neural — that detect a specific environmental stimulus and convert it into an electrical signal the CNS can interpret, a process called sensory transduction (換能). The strength and duration of the resulting generator or receptor potential are graded with stimulus intensity, and the CNS reads intensity as action-potential frequency and duration. The somatosensory system handles four modalities — touch, proprioception, temperature, and nociception — each mediated by distinct receptor types and molecular channels including the Nobel Prize-winning Piezo2, TRPV1, and TRPM8. Hearing is a special sense that relies on cochlear hair cells to convert mechanical vibrations into electrical signals; inner hair cells (IHC, 內毛細胞) are the primary signal transmitters while outer hair cells (OHC, 外毛細胞) act as mechanical amplifiers via the motor protein prestin. Place theory explains how frequency is encoded spatially along the cochlea (耳蝸), with each location responding maximally to a particular frequency — a principle established by Georg von Békésy.

1. Sensory Receptors and Transduction

A sensory receptor (感受器) is a specialised cell — neural or non-neural — that detects a specific environmental stimulus and converts it into an electrical signal. Four major types exist:

Receptor TypeExamples of Stimuli
Chemoreceptors (化學感受器)O2, pH, glucose and other organic molecules
Mechanoreceptors (機械感受器)Pressure (baroreceptors), cell stretch (osmoreceptors), vibration, acceleration, sound
Photoreceptors (光感受器)Photons of light
Thermoreceptors (溫度感受器)Varying degrees of heat

Sensory transduction (換能) is the conversion of stimulus energy into information the CNS can process. The environmental stimulus depolarises the membrane by opening ion channels directly or indirectly. Two broad receptor categories differ in how they generate downstream signals:

  • Neural sensory receptors: depolarisation produces a generator potential (發生器電位), which triggers action potentials (AP) in the same cell.
  • Non-neural sensory receptors: depolarisation produces a receptor potential (感受器電位), which drives neurotransmitter release, generating APs in downstream (postsynaptic) neurons.
Three basic transmission patterns (Type 1, Type 2, Type 3) describe how sensory signals reach the CNS, with Type 3 being relevant to non-neural receptors.

Exam trap: Do not confuse generator potential (in neural receptors — fires its own AP) with receptor potential (in non-neural receptors — triggers NT release to excite a downstream neuron). Both are graded, but the downstream mechanism differs.

Sensory coding (感覺編碼) translates stimulus properties into neural signals:

  • The strength of the generator/receptor potential → frequency of APs (or amount of NT released).
  • The duration of the generator/receptor potential → duration of the AP train (or NT secretion).
This means a stronger stimulus makes neurons fire faster, not harder (APs are all-or-none).

Exam trap: Stimulus intensity is encoded as AP frequency, not AP amplitude. AP amplitude does not change with stimulus strength.

2. The Somatosensory System

The somatosensory system (體感系統) covers four modalities: touch (觸覺), proprioception (本體感覺), temperature (溫度覺), and nociception (傷害感覺 / 痛覺 and itching). Each modality has dedicated receptor types.

The somatosensory pathway (體感傳導路徑) follows a three-neuron chain:

  1. Primary sensory neurons — activated by receptor stimulation; carry signals from the periphery.
  2. Secondary sensory neurons — interneurons that cross the midline of the body, so left-body sensations are processed in the right hemisphere and vice versa.
  3. Tertiary sensory neurons — receive input in the thalamus (丘腦) and project to the somatosensory cortex (體感皮層) of the cerebral cortex.

Exam trap: It is the secondary sensory neurons (not primary or tertiary) that cross the midline. Also, all pathways relay in the thalamus before reaching cortex.

The somatosensory cortex (體感皮層) maps the body topographically — each body region corresponds to a specific cortical area. More sensitive body regions have proportionally larger cortical representations. Importantly, the size of a region's cortical representation is not fixed; it can change with usage (cortical plasticity 皮層可塑性).

Touch receptors (觸覺感受器) respond to stretch, steady pressure, fluttering, vibration, and texture. They include free nerve endings and complex encapsulated receptors. The Piezo2 channel is a mechanosensitive ion channel that mediates gentle touch; it is also responsible for proprioception (本體感覺), described as the "sixth sense" — the sense of self-movement and body position.

Temperature receptors (溫度感受器) are free nerve endings in two functional classes:

  • Cold receptors: respond to temperatures below body temperature; use the TRPM8 channel (a Transient Receptor Potential channel). TRPM8 also senses cooling compounds like menthol (薄荷醇) and eucalyptol (桉葉油醇). TRPM8-deficient mice are insensitive to cold.
  • Warm receptors: respond from body temperature to ~45°C; other TRP channels handle intermediate ranges. TRPV1 responds to noxious heat above 43°C and also to capsaicin (辣椒素) from chilli peppers.
Discovery of TRPV1 and TRPM8 as temperature sensors, and Piezo channels as touch sensors, earned the 2021 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

Exam trap: TRPV1 senses noxious heat (above 43°C) and capsaicin — not all warm temperatures. TRPM8 senses cold and menthol/eucalyptol. Do not assign capsaicin to TRPM8 or menthol to TRPV1.

Nociceptors (傷害感受器), also called pain receptors, are free nerve endings that respond to strong noxious stimuli — chemical, mechanical, or thermal — that cause or risk tissue damage. They activate two pathways:

  • A. Reflexive protective responses integrated at the spinal cord level (fast, unconscious).
  • B. Ascending pathways to the cortex producing conscious pain sensation.

Exam trap: Nociceptors activate two pathways — a fast spinal reflex arc AND a slower ascending conscious pathway. Pain is not purely cortical.

3. Hearing: Properties of Sound

Hearing (聽覺) is the perception of energy carried by sound waves, characterised by three properties: amplitude (振幅), frequency (頻率), and complexity (複雜性 / 音色). Disabling hearing loss affects 5.3% of the world population and one third of people over 65. The molecular identity of the mechanotransduction channel in auditory hair cells is still not fully established, making it the "last frontier" of sensory neurobiology.

The three basic properties of sound:

  • Amplitude: perceived as loudness, measured in decibels (dB). Normal conversation is ~60 dB. Sounds above 80 dB can damage hair cells.
  • Frequency: perceived as pitch, measured in Hz. The average human ear hears 20–20,000 Hz, with most acute hearing at 1–3 kHz.
  • Complexity (Timbre, 音色): what makes two instruments sound different even at the same pitch and loudness — determined by the mix of overtones in the sound wave.

Exam trap: The most acute human hearing is at 1–3 kHz, NOT across the full 20–20,000 Hz range. Also, hearing range narrows with age.

4. Cochlear Anatomy and Auditory Transduction

The cochlea (耳蝸) houses the organ of Corti (科蒂氏器), which contains two key cell types:

  • Inner hair cells (IHC, 內毛細胞): 3,000 per cochlea; arranged in one row. They are the primary sensory cells that transmit auditory signals to the brain.
  • Outer hair cells (OHC, 外毛細胞): 12,000 per cochlea; arranged in three rows. They are mechanical amplifiers.
Hair cells bear stereocilia (靜纖毛) connected by tip links (尖端連結), which are crucial for mechanotransduction.

Auditory transduction (聽覺換能) — mechano-electrical transduction (機電換能) — occurs in the cochlear hair cells. Sound waves cause the basilar membrane to vibrate, deflecting the stereocilia. This deflection pulls on tip links, opening mechanically gated ion channels. The resulting ion influx generates a receptor potential, triggering neurotransmitter release onto the cochlear nerve fibers.

Innervation asymmetry: The cochlear nerve contains 30,000+ fibers. There is a nearly 10:1 fiber-to-IHC ratio — meaning each IHC receives massive innervation. By contrast, very few afferent fibers innervate OHCs. A small number of efferent fibers descend from the brain to OHCs (for modulation). This confirms that IHCs are the primary signal senders to the brain.

Exam trap: Despite there being 4× more OHCs (12,000) than IHCs (3,000), the vast majority of afferent cochlear nerve fibers innervate IHCs, not OHCs. OHCs receive mainly efferent (descending) input.

OHC mechanical amplification: OHCs contain prestin (普雷斯汀), a transmembrane motor protein that changes shape (contracts and elongates) in response to membrane voltage changes (electromotility, 電動性). This electromotility amplifies the vibration of the basilar membrane, increasing sensitivity to incoming sound frequencies and amplifying the signal — a process called cochlear amplification (耳蝸放大).

Exam trap: Prestin drives OHC motility in response to voltage changes (electromotility), not directly to sound. Prestin is in OHCs, not IHCs. IHCs transmit the signal; OHCs amplify the mechanical wave.

5. Sensory Coding for Sound Frequency — Place Theory

Place theory (位置理論) states that each location along the cochlea responds maximally to a specific sound frequency — thus the cochlea performs a spatial frequency analysis. Low frequencies are encoded near the apex; high frequencies near the base. This was established by Georg von Békésy (1899–1972), who won the Nobel Prize for this work. The analogy is that different keys on a piano correspond to different positions along the cochlea.

Exam trap: Place theory means frequency is encoded by which cochlear neurons are activated (location), not by the firing rate of a single neuron. Do not confuse with rate/frequency coding.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Generator potential (neural receptors) ≠ receptor potential (non-neural receptors); both are graded but differ in what they drive.
  • Stimulus intensity is encoded as AP frequency, not AP amplitude (APs are all-or-none).
  • It is secondary sensory neurons that cross the midline, not primary or tertiary.
  • Somatosensory cortical map size is not fixed — it changes with body part usage.
  • TRPV1 = noxious heat (>43°C) AND capsaicin; TRPM8 = cold AND menthol/eucalyptol. Do not swap these.
  • Nociceptors activate two pathways: spinal reflex arc (unconscious) AND ascending cortical pathway (conscious).
  • Most acute human hearing is at 1–3 kHz, not across the full 20–20,000 Hz range.
  • Despite 4× more OHCs than IHCs, most afferent cochlear nerve fibers innervate IHCs (~10:1 fiber-to-IHC ratio).
  • Prestin drives OHC electromotility in response to voltage changes — it is in OHCs, not IHCs.
  • Place theory = frequency encoded by cochlear location, not by AP firing rate of a single neuron.
  • The molecular identity of the mechanotransduction channel in hair cells is still not fully established.
  • Piezo2 mediates both gentle touch AND proprioception (the "sixth sense").
Top things to memorize
  1. Four receptor types: chemo-, mechano-, photo-, thermoreceptors with their stimuli.
  2. Neural receptor → generator potential → AP in same cell; non-neural receptor → receptor potential → NT release → AP in downstream neuron.
  3. Stimulus intensity = AP frequency; stimulus duration = AP train duration.
  4. Three-neuron somatosensory pathway: primary → secondary (crosses midline) → tertiary → somatosensory cortex via thalamus.
  5. Piezo2: gentle touch AND proprioception; Nobel 2021.
  6. TRPV1: noxious heat (>43°C) + capsaicin; TRPM8: cold + menthol/eucalyptol; both Nobel 2021.
  7. IHC: 3,000; one row; primary signal transmitter; ~10 afferent fibers each. OHC: 12,000; three rows; mechanical amplifier.
  8. Prestin: motor protein in OHC; electromotility (contracts/elongates with Vm changes) → cochlear amplification.
  9. Tip links connect stereocilia in hair cells; deflection opens mechanotransduction channels.
  10. Sound amplitude >80 dB damages hair cells; most acute hearing at 1–3 kHz.
  11. Place theory (Békésy): each cochlear location responds maximally to one frequency.
  12. Mechanotransduction channel identity in auditory hair cells remains the "last frontier" of sensory neurobiology.
