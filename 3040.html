Endo1_2: Overview of the Endocrine System & Neuroendocrine Systems

The endocrine system (內分泌系統) is the body's slow-acting chemical messaging network. Specialized glands and cells secrete hormones (荷爾蒙) directly into the bloodstream, which carry them to distant target tissues where they bind specific receptors to regulate metabolism, growth, development, reproduction, water balance, and mood. This lecture covers the core concepts: what a hormone is, the four chemical classes, and the two big mechanisms of action. It also introduces the neuroendocrine system — how the hypothalamus and pituitary gland form a command center linking the nervous and endocrine systems.

1. Overview and General Concepts

Ernest Starling (1866-1927) coined the word "hormone" (from Greek: to arouse or excite) in 1905 after discovering secretin with William Bayliss in 1902. The endocrine system consists of glands and cells that release hormones to regulate physiology.

Working definition of a classical hormone:

  • Secreted in very small amounts by specialized cells or glands
  • Carried in the bloodstream to distant tissues
  • Binds specific receptors in target cells to produce a biological response

Classical endocrine glands: hypothalamus, pituitary, thyroid, adrenal, pancreatic islets, gonads, pineal. Endocrine cells in other tissues: heart (ANP), kidney (renin, calcitriol), liver (IGF), stomach G cells (gastrin), duodenal S cells (secretin), adipocytes (leptin), bone (FGF-23), enteroendocrine system (GLP-1, GLP-2).

Three categories of stimuli for hormone secretion: (1) Humoral — blood chemical changes (glucose, Ca²⁺, osmolarity); (2) Neural — sympathetic control of adrenal medulla; (3) Hormonal — hypothalamic neurohormones controlling pituitary.

The endocrine and nervous systems are closely linked. Both regulate physiology, but the nervous system acts in milliseconds; the endocrine system acts over minutes to days. The same molecule can be both (e.g., norepinephrine/去甲腎上腺素).

Exam trap: Hormones travel via BLOOD (endocrine). Secreting via a duct = exocrine, NOT endocrine.

3. Mechanisms of Hormone Action

Lipid-soluble hormones (steroids, thyroxine): Cross the membrane → bind cytoplasmic or nuclear receptors → hormone-receptor complex acts directly on DNA to modify transcription. Long-lasting effects (hours to days). Elwood Jensen (1920-2012) discovered the estrogen receptor in 1958.

Lipid-insoluble hormones (polypeptides, catecholamines): Bind surface receptors → effects via second messengers (cAMP, IP3/DAG) or intrinsic receptor kinase activity. Rapid, short-lived responses.

Exam trap: Insulin uses receptor tyrosine kinase, NOT a GPCR. ANP uses receptor guanylate cyclase (→ cGMP), NOT adenylate cyclase (→ cAMP).

4. Neuroendocrine Systems — Hypothalamus and Pituitary

Neurosecretory cells (神經分泌細胞): Specialized neurons that release hormones. Larger secretory granules (100-400 nm) vs. ordinary synaptic vesicles (30-60 nm). Terminate in capillary beds (neurohemal organs).

Two types in the hypothalamus: (1) Magnocellular neurons in SON and PVN — project to posterior pituitary, release ADH and oxytocin. (2) Parvocellular neurons — release into hypothalamic-hypophyseal portal system.

Pituitary lobeTissueHormones
Anterior (adenohypophysis/腺垂體)True glandular endocrine tissueGH, PRL, MSH, ACTH, FSH, LH, TSH
Posterior (neurohypophysis/神經垂體)Neural tissue (axons of SON/PVN neurons)ADH (vasopressin), Oxytocin
Exam trap: The ANTERIOR pituitary is glandular tissue connected to hypothalamus via the PORTAL SYSTEM (not nerve axons). The POSTERIOR pituitary IS neural tissue (axons from hypothalamus). These are frequently swapped in exam questions.

5. Anterior Pituitary Hormones

Direct-acting hormones (act on non-endocrine tissues): GH, Prolactin (PRL), MSH

Tropic hormones (act on other endocrine glands): ACTH, FSH, LH, TSH

MSH: Acts on melanocytes → more melanin → skin darkening. Generated from POMC (same precursor as ACTH). High ACTH/MSH states: Addison's disease (no cortisol → no negative feedback → high CRH → high POMC cleavage → high MSH → hyperpigmentation). Melasma in pregnancy: estrogen → high MSH.

Prolactin: Stimulated by TRH and estrogen. Inhibited by dopamine (= prolactin-inhibiting hormone). Anti-psychotics block dopamine → hyperprolactinemia.

Hypothalamic neurohormones for anterior pituitary: All direct and tropic hormones have releasing hormones. Only direct hormones (GH, PRL) also have release-inhibiting hormones (somatostatin/GIH for GH; dopamine for PRL).

6. Posterior Pituitary Hormones

ADH (antidiuretic hormone/vasopressin): 9-aa cyclic peptide from SON. Kidney: V2 receptor → cAMP → aquaporin insertion → water reabsorption. Blood vessels: direct vasoconstriction → raises BP. Inhibited by alcohol.

Oxytocin: From PVN. Targets: mammary gland (milk ejection), uterus (contractions during/after birth). In brain: bonding/trust/love hormone. Note: prolactin SYNTHESIZES milk; oxytocin EJECTS it.

Exam trap: ADH and oxytocin differ in only 2 of 9 amino acids. ADH is a NEUROHORMONE (from neurosecretory cells). GH and prolactin are NOT neurohormones — they are from glandular cells.

Endo3_4: Metabolic & Developmental Hormones + Water/Electrolyte Balance

This lecture covers the hormones that control metabolism, development, and water/electrolyte balance — the body's core survival regulators. The adrenal medulla and cortex, thyroid, pancreatic islets, pituitary (GH), adipose (leptin), posterior pituitary (ADH/vasopressin), and cardiac atria (ANP) are all covered. The common thread: each hormone has a feedback loop, a specific receptor mechanism, and a clinical consequence of excess or deficiency that frequently appears in exams.

1. Adrenal Medulla — Epinephrine and Norepinephrine

The adrenal medulla (腎上腺髓質) is the inner layer of the adrenal gland. It is neural (ectodermal) in origin — analogous to sympathetic postganglionic neurons. It contains chromaffin cells that release catecholamines: epinephrine (80%) and norepinephrine (20%).

Regulation: Directly by sympathetic preganglionic neurons — NOT by ACTH or other pituitary hormones.

Adrenal medulla effectsDetail
Glycogenolysis in liverReleases glucose
Lipolysis in adiposeReleases free fatty acids
Inhibits insulin secretionVia α2 receptors on beta cells
Stimulates glucagon secretionRaises blood glucose
Bronchodilation, tachycardiaFight-or-flight
Exam trap: The adrenal MEDULLA is regulated by the sympathetic nervous system, NOT by ACTH. The adrenal CORTEX is regulated by ACTH (via HPA axis). Epinephrine INHIBITS insulin, not stimulates it.

2. Adrenal Cortex — Glucocorticoids (Cortisol)

Major human glucocorticoid: cortisol = hydrocortisone. Secreted by zona fasciculata of adrenal cortex. Axis: Stress → CRH (hypothalamus) → ACTH (anterior pituitary) → cortisol (adrenal cortex) → negative feedback on hypothalamus and pituitary.

Cortisol actionLiverMuscleAdipose
Primary effect↑ Gluconeogenesis↓ Protein synthesis, ↑ protein breakdown↑ Lipolysis, ↓ glucose uptake

Other cortisol actions: Enhances vascular reactivity; suppresses inflammation and immune system (clinical use in autoimmune disease, transplants, SARS treatment).

Cushing's syndrome (庫欣氏症候群): Excess cortisol. Causes: glucocorticoid drugs, cortisol-secreting tumors, ACTH-secreting tumors, CRH-secreting tumors. Symptoms: rapid weight gain, muscle weakness, immune suppression, thin skin, moon face.

Exam trap: Cortisol DECREASES glucose uptake in muscle and adipose (anti-insulin), and INCREASES gluconeogenesis in liver. Cortisol has diurnal rhythm — peaks in the morning.

3. Thyroid Hormones (T3 and T4)

Formed from 2 iodinated tyrosine precursors in the thyroid. T4 (thyroxine/tetraiodothyronine) is the major released form. T3 (triiodothyronine) is more potent — T4 is converted to T3 in target tissues. Both bind nuclear receptors (transcription factors).

Regulation: Cold/energy need → TRH → TSH → T3/T4 → negative feedback. TSH also stimulates thyroid growth.

Actions: (1) Stimulate cellular respiration → ↑ basal metabolic rate (ALL tissues EXCEPT brain, spleen, testes/uterus, thyroid itself); (2) Sensitize tissues to epinephrine; (3) Required for normal development of skeletal and nervous systems (with GH).

Iodine deficiency → reduced T3/T4 → no negative feedback → high TSH → thyroid hypertrophy = goiter (甲狀腺腫).

Exam trap: Hyperthyroidism increases heart rate by SENSITIZING tissues to epinephrine — not by directly stimulating the heart. Thyroid hormones do NOT stimulate brain metabolism.

4. Insulin and Glucagon

Both are peptides from islets of Langerhans (蘭氏小島) in the pancreas. Beta cells: insulin (51 aa, 2 chains). Alpha cells: glucagon (29 aa, single chain). They have OPPOSITE effects on blood glucose.

InsulinGlucagon
Stimulated byHigh blood glucose (main), high AA, GIP, parasympatheticLow blood glucose, high AA (not FA)
Inhibited bySympathetic (α2), glucagon (indirectly)High glucose + FA, insulin (must have insulin to suppress glucagon)

ReceptorReceptor tyrosine kinaseGPCR → cAMP

Diabetes mellitus (糖尿病): Type 1 (10%): beta cell destruction → no insulin → high glucagon unopposed → ketoacidosis. Type 2 (90%): insulin resistance (defective receptors). Both cause hyperglycemia, polyuria, thirst.

Exam trap: In Type 1 DM, glucagon is HIGH (not low) because insulin cannot suppress it. Low glucagon is the wrong answer. Ketoacidosis occurs because high glucagon drives hepatic ketone formation in the absence of insulin.

5. Growth Hormone and Leptin

Growth Hormone (GH): 191 aa polypeptide from anterior pituitary. Both insulin-like AND anti-insulin effects. GH stimulates liver to release IGF-1 (insulin-like growth factor 1, somatomedin) which mediates most growth-promoting effects.

GH stimulatorsGH inhibitors
Hypoglycemia, arginine, deep sleep, stressHyperglycemia, REM sleep, somatostatin (GIH), high IGF-1

GH deficiency in childhood → dwarfism (侏儒症). Excess GH in childhood → gigantism (巨人症). Excess GH in adulthood → acromegaly (肢端肥大症) — enlarged jaw, nose, hands.

Leptin (from Greek leptos = thin): Peptide from white adipose tissue (ob gene). High leptin = reduced appetite + increased energy expenditure. Leptin receptor (LepR) is a cytokine family receptor (JAK-associated), mainly in brain. Obese individuals: HIGH leptin but leptin resistance.

Exam trap: GH stimulates insulin secretion both directly (on beta cells) and indirectly (via hyperglycemic effect). Injecting insulin → hypoglycemia → GH release — this is the insulin tolerance test for pituitary GH reserve.

6. ADH, Mineralocorticoids, and ANP — Water/Electrolyte Balance

ADH (antidiuretic hormone/vasopressin): 9-aa cyclic peptide from hypothalamus (SON), released from posterior pituitary. Actions: (1) Kidney collecting duct: V2 receptor → cAMP → aquaporin (水通道蛋白) insertion → ↑ water reabsorption. (2) Blood vessels: direct vasoconstriction.

ADH stimulusMechanismThreshold
↑ Plasma osmolalityOsmoreceptors in anterior hypothalamusVery sensitive: 5-10 mOsm change
↓ Blood volume/pressureStretch receptors (TRP channels) in cardiovascular systemRequires >10% blood volume loss

Alcohol potently inhibits ADH → polyuria + thirst. Diabetes insipidus (尿崩症) = ADH deficiency → voluminous dilute urine.

Aldosterone (mineralocorticoid): Steroid from adrenal cortex (zona glomerulosa). Primary regulator: Angiotensin II (via RAAS when BP falls). Also: extracellular K+ (5% change sufficient), ACTH. Action: ↑ Na+ reabsorption + ↑ K+ excretion in distal tubule/collecting duct → ↑ extracellular fluid volume → ↑ blood pressure.

ANP (atrial natriuretic peptide): From cardiac atria in response to stretch (↑ blood volume). Action: ↑ Na+ excretion + ↑ urine production. Receptor = receptor guanylate cyclase (cGMP). Counteracts ADH and aldosterone.

Exam trap: ADH and aldosterone RAISE blood pressure; ANP LOWERS it. Drinking water DECREASES ADH (lower osmolality). Only ANP has a receptor guanylate cyclase (like ANP's brother in the endocrine world).
Exam Traps Summary
  • Adrenal medulla: regulated by sympathetic NS, NOT by ACTH. Secretes catecholamines only
  • Adrenal cortex: regulated by ACTH. Secretes glucocorticoids, mineralocorticoids, androgens
  • Cushing's = EXCESS cortisol. Addison's = DEFICIENCY of cortisol
  • T4 is converted to more potent T3 in target tissues
  • Thyroid hormones don't stimulate brain/spleen/testes/uterus metabolism
  • In Type 1 DM, glucagon is HIGH (not low)
  • Insulin: RTK receptor. Glucagon: GPCR (cAMP)
  • Deep sleep stimulates GH; REM sleep inhibits GH
  • Leptin resistance explains why obese individuals have HIGH, not low, leptin
  • ADH: osmoreceptors are very sensitive (5-10 mOsm); volume receptors need >10% loss
  • ANP counteracts ADH and aldosterone
Top things to memorize
  1. Adrenal medulla = epinephrine + norepinephrine; regulated by sympathetic neurons directly
  2. Cortisol: gluconeogenesis in liver, anti-insulin in muscle/adipose; inhibits immune system
  3. HPA axis: Stress → CRH → ACTH → cortisol (negative feedback)
  4. T4 converted to T3 (more potent); thyroid sensitizes tissues to epinephrine
  5. Insulin: RTK receptor, lowers glucose; glucagon: GPCR, raises glucose; both from islets
  6. Type 1 DM: no insulin → high glucagon → ketoacidosis
  7. GH: deep sleep stimulates; IGF-1 mediates growth effects
  8. ADH: V2 → cAMP → aquaporins; aldosterone: Na+ reabsorption; ANP: Na+ excretion
Lower-yield / skip if short on time
  • Exact structure of catecholamine synthesis pathway steps
  • Specific adrenoreceptor subtype signaling details beyond α2 vs β
  • Laron dwarf genetics details
  • Specific prostaglandin subclasses in the eicosanoid family

Endo5: Reproductive Hormones

This lecture covers how the male and female reproductive systems are hormonally controlled — from gamete production to the cyclic changes of the menstrual cycle, pregnancy, and contraception. Testosterone, estrogen, and progesterone are all steroids from cholesterol. Their regulation involves the classic hypothalamus-pituitary-gonad axis, with both negative and (in the female) positive feedback loops. Understanding the feedback switches at different cycle phases is the most important conceptual challenge.

1. Overview of Reproductive Hormones

Two types of reproductive hormones: (1) Steroid hormones — all from cholesterol. Biosynthetic pathway: Cholesterol → progesterone → androgens → estrogens (via aromatase). (2) Peptide hormones from the pituitary: oxytocin (posterior) and prolactin (anterior).

Androgens: mainly testosterone, more important in males but present in both sexes. Estrogens: mainly 17β-estradiol, three forms (estradiol, estrone, estriol). Progesterone: important in females, especially in pregnancy.

Primary sex characteristics: directly related to reproduction (genitalia). Secondary sex characteristics: other distinguishing features (voice, facial hair, breast development).

Exam trap: Not all reproductive hormones are steroids — oxytocin and prolactin are peptides. Androgens are present in BOTH sexes, not males only. Estrogens are converted from androgens by aromatase.

2. Male Reproductive Hormones

Testes anatomy: Seminiferous tubules (spermatogenesis). Leydig cells (interstitial, outside tubules) → testosterone in response to LH. Sertoli cells (inside tubules) → support germ cells, seminiferous fluid, inhibin.

Cell typeStimulated byProduces
Leydig cellsLHTestosterone
Sertoli cellsFSHInhibin, seminiferous fluid, support for germ cells

Neuroendocrine control: GnRH → LH + FSH → testosterone (LH on Leydig) + spermatogenesis (FSH + testosterone on Sertoli). Testosterone provides negative feedback on GnRH, LH, FSH. Inhibin selectively inhibits FSH.

Actions of testosterone: (1) Prenatal: primary male sex characteristics; (2) Puberty: secondary sex characteristics + penile/testicular growth; (3) Spermatogenesis; (4) Muscle protein synthesis → greater muscle mass; (5) Behavioral: aggression.

Scrotum houses testes OUTSIDE the abdominal cavity because spermatogenesis requires temperature ~34°C (below core 37°C).

Exam trap: LH stimulates LEYDIG cells (testosterone). FSH stimulates SERTOLI cells (spermatogenesis support). Sertoli cells secrete inhibin — NOT Leydig cells. Inhibin inhibits FSH selectively (not LH).

3. Female Reproductive Hormones and Menstrual Cycle

Ovarian organs: Two ovaries (egg production + estrogen/progesterone). Uterine tubes/fallopian tubes = oviducts (fertilization site + egg transport). Uterus (implantation + development). Vagina (passage for birth + reception).

Oogenesis (排卵): One mature ovum released per cycle. About 400-500 ova over reproductive lifespan (puberty to menopause). Follicle = oocyte + supporting cells. After ovulation, follicle → corpus luteum.

DaysOvarian cycleUterine cycleHormones
1-5Old corpus luteum degeneratingMenstruationLow E2 + P4 → GnRH/FSH/LH rise
5-12Follicular phase — follicle growsProliferative phase — endometrium rebuildsRising estrogen from follicle

If fertilization occurs: placenta secretes hCG within 1 day of implantation → rescues corpus luteum → maintains P4/E2 for first trimester. If no fertilization: corpus luteum degenerates → P4/E2 fall → menstruation.

Exam trap: In the FOLLICULAR phase, rising estrogen provides POSITIVE feedback (triggers LH surge). In the LUTEAL phase, high estrogen + progesterone provide NEGATIVE feedback (suppress FSH/LH). These two phases use OPPOSITE feedback signs — a classic exam question.

4. Effects of Estrogen and Progesterone

Estrogen effects: Growth of sex organs at puberty; secondary sex characteristics; bone growth then EARLY epiphyseal closure (explains why women stop growing earlier); stimulates GnRH/LH/FSH in follicular phase (positive feedback); inhibits in luteal phase (negative feedback); DECREASES peripheral tissue insulin sensitivity.

Progesterone effects: Full endometrial development (preparing for pregnancy); mammary gland development; negative feedback to prevent further ovulation during pregnancy; antagonizes androgens in females.

Gestational diabetes (妊娠糖尿病): Growing placenta produces increasing estrogen → decreased insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues → if pancreas cannot compensate → gestational diabetes. Develops ~20-24 weeks. Resolves after birth.

Contraceptive pills: Estrogen + progestogen → continuous high levels → negative feedback on hypothalamus → suppressed GnRH → no LH surge → no ovulation. Also increases cervical mucus viscosity to inhibit sperm. 99.7% effective. FDA approved 1960.

IVF: Robert Edwards (Nobel 2010) + Patrick Steptoe. FSH for ovarian hyperstimulation (multiple follicles). hCG for final oocyte maturation induction. First "test tube baby" Louise Brown, 1978.

Exam trap: Estrogen DECREASES insulin sensitivity (not increases). Gestational diabetes is caused by HIGH ESTROGEN-driven insulin resistance, not excessive carbohydrate intake. Ovulation in the cycle is TRIGGERED by high estrogen (positive feedback) — it is NOT prevented by it at that stage.
Exam Traps Summary
  • LH → Leydig cells → testosterone. FSH → Sertoli cells → spermatogenesis support
  • Inhibin from Sertoli cells inhibits FSH selectively, NOT LH
  • Follicular phase: rising E2 = POSITIVE feedback → LH surge → ovulation
  • Luteal phase: high P4 + E2 = NEGATIVE feedback → suppresses FSH/LH
  • Oviduct = site of fertilization, NOT implantation (that is the uterus)
  • hCG rescues corpus luteum in pregnancy — it does NOT degenerate despite hCG
  • Estrogen DECREASES insulin sensitivity → gestational diabetes
  • Prolactin = milk SYNTHESIS; Oxytocin = milk EJECTION
  • Contraceptive pill prevents ovulation by negative feedback suppressing LH surge
  • Sperm count below 35 million/mL → markedly impaired fertility
Top things to memorize
  1. Steroid biosynthesis: Cholesterol → progesterone → androgens → estrogens
  2. LH → Leydig cells (testosterone); FSH → Sertoli cells (spermatogenesis, inhibin)
  3. Testosterone: negative feedback on GnRH, LH, FSH; inhibin: only on FSH
  4. Ovarian cycle phases: follicular → ovulation (LH surge day 14) → luteal
  5. Positive feedback (E2 in follicular phase) vs negative feedback (P4+E2 in luteal phase)
  6. hCG rescues corpus luteum in early pregnancy
  7. Gestational diabetes: placental estrogen → insulin resistance
  8. Contraceptive pill = suppress GnRH/LH via negative feedback

GI1: Overview of the Digestive System

The digestive system is the body's food processing factory — a 9-meter tube from mouth to anus that mechanically and chemically breaks down food, absorbs nutrients, and excretes waste. This first GI lecture establishes the big picture: the anatomy of the GI tract (headgut, foregut, midgut, hindgut), two types of digestion, the four-layer wall structure, the enteric nervous system (the "little brain" with 100 million neurons), and two types of motility (peristalsis for propulsion; segmentation for mixing). Everything in later lectures builds on this scaffold.

1. Overview of the Digestive System

The digestive system (消化系統) = alimentary canal + accessory digestive organs (pancreas, liver, gallbladder). Responsible for ingestion, digestion, and absorption of food.

Two types of digestion:

  • Mechanical: Physical breakdown — chewing, mixing, churning, segmentation (rhythmic local contractions)
  • Chemical: Enzyme-catalyzed breakdown of complex molecules to their building blocks

Nutrient breakdown: Proteins → amino acids. Carbohydrates → monosaccharides (glucose, fructose, galactose). Fats (triglycerides) → glycerol + free fatty acids.

GI segmentFunction
Headgut (mouth, pharynx)Food intake, primary processing; saliva + chewing
Foregut (esophagus, stomach)Food conduction, storage, partial digestion
Midgut (small intestine + accessory organs)Main digestion and absorption
Hindgut (large intestine, rectum)Water/ion absorption, waste excretion

2. GI Tract Anatomy and Microscopic Structure

The portal vein carries nutrient-rich blood from the digestive organs, spleen, pancreas, and gallbladder to the liver first — important for first-pass metabolism of absorbed substances.

Layer (inside → outside)Contents
MucosaEpithelial cells + connective tissue + smooth muscle (innermost)

Exam trap: Myenteric (Auerbach's) plexus is between the two muscle layers — controls motility. Submucosal (Meissner's) plexus is in the submucosa — controls secretion/absorption. These are frequently confused.

3. Enteric Nervous System — The Little Brain in the Gut

The enteric nervous system (ENS / 腸道神經系統) contains ~100 million neurons — more than a mouse brain. Includes sensory neurons, interneurons, motor neurons organized as an independent integrative system capable of autonomous reflex activity.

Two plexuses: Myenteric plexus (Auerbach's) — between circular and longitudinal muscle — controls motility. Submucosal plexus (Meissner's) — in submucosa — controls secretion and absorption.

Hirschsprung's disease: Failure of enteric neurons to develop in part/all of the large intestine → waste cannot pass → normal colon swells above the aganglionic segment.

4. GI Motility — Peristalsis and Segmentation

Peristalsis (蠕動)Segmentation (節段運動)

Muscles involvedBoth circular AND longitudinalCircular ONLY (no longitudinal)

Food moves through the GI tract independent of gravity — peristaltic contractions are sufficient (you can swallow upside down).

Control of motility: (1) Intrinsic: enteric nervous system (ENS) — modulated by stretch and chyme chemistry. (2) Extrinsic hormonal: GI peptide hormones. (3) Extrinsic neural: sympathetic = inhibitory; parasympathetic = excitatory.

Exam trap: Segmentation does NOT use the longitudinal muscle layer. Peristalsis does. Sympathetic is INHIBITORY to GI; parasympathetic is EXCITATORY.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Mechanical digestion = physical; chemical digestion = enzymes
  • Muscularis has two layers; myenteric plexus is BETWEEN them
  • ENS = 100 million neurons; myenteric = motility; submucosal = secretion
  • Peristalsis = circular + longitudinal (propulsion); segmentation = circular only (mixing)
  • Sympathetic inhibits GI motility; parasympathetic stimulates it
  • Portal vein takes absorbed nutrients to liver first (first-pass metabolism)
  • Pancreas is both endocrine (islets, 1%) AND exocrine (acini, 99%)
Top things to memorize
  1. Four GI tract layers from inside out: mucosa, submucosa, muscularis, serosa
  2. Two neural plexuses: myenteric (motility) vs submucosal (secretion)
  3. ENS has ~100 million neurons — autonomous
  4. Peristalsis (propulsion, both muscle layers) vs segmentation (mixing, circular only)
  5. GI segments and their functions: headgut → foregut → midgut → hindgut
  6. Portal vein delivers absorbed nutrients to liver first

GI2: Digestion — Mouth, Esophagus, Stomach, Pancreas & Liver

This lecture covers how food is chemically digested — from the mouth through the esophagus, stomach, pancreas, and liver. Each organ adds its own secretion to the mix: saliva (amylase + antimicrobials), gastric juice (HCl + pepsin + intrinsic factor), pancreatic juice (bicarbonate + all digestive enzymes), and bile (emulsifiers + excretory pigments). The regulation of gastric acid secretion — its three synergistic stimuli and three phases — is the most exam-dense topic in this lecture.

1. Mouth and Saliva

Three key mouth structures: Teeth (biting, tearing, grinding), Tongue (tasting, manipulating food bolus, swallowing), Salivary glands (secrete multifunctional saliva).

Daily saliva volume: ~1.5 L. Serous cells release watery secretion with electrolytes, salivary amylase (begins starch digestion), and antibacterial proteins. Mucous cells release mucins (glycoproteins) → viscous mucus.

Functions of saliva: (1) Moistening and lubrication; (2) Antibacterial (peroxidase, lysozyme, defensins — amphipathic cationic peptides that disrupt pathogen membranes); (3) Amylase breaks starch to maltose, maltotriose, oligosaccharides; (4) Slightly alkaline — neutralizes oral bacteria acids and refluxed stomach acid.

Control of saliva: Purely autonomic — no hormonal control. Parasympathetic: moderate volume, watery saliva. Sympathetic: less, more viscous, mucin-rich saliva → dry mouth sensation.

Exam trap: Salivary amylase is inactivated by low stomach pH — carbohydrate digestion largely stops in the stomach. Saliva does NOT contain pepsin. Sympathetic = dry mouth; parasympathetic = wet mouth.

2. Pharynx, Esophagus and Swallowing

Swallowing reflex: Tongue moves bolus to rear → pharyngeal swallowing reflex triggered → soft palate closes nasal passage + epiglottis covers glottis (prevents aspiration) + upper esophageal sphincter relaxes → bolus enters esophagus.

Nasopharynx is ONLY for air — food cannot pass through it.

Lower esophageal sphincter (LES): Normally closed; relaxes when peristaltic wave arrives; closes after food passes. Failure → GERD (gastroesophageal reflux disease/胃食道逆流) — acid reflux causing "heartburn." Affects 10-20% of Western populations.

3. Stomach — Gastric Juice and Regulation

Stomach functions: (1) Food reservoir; (2) Mechanical mixing → chyme; (3) Partial chemical digestion; (4) Controlled emptying into duodenum via pyloric sphincter.

Cell typeSecretionFunction
Mucous surface/neck cellsMucus + HCO3-Protects mucosa (maintains pH near cell surface near neutral despite lumen pH ~2)
Chief (zymogenic) cellsPepsinogen, gastric lipasePepsinogen activated to pepsin (protease) at pH <5; lipase starts fat digestion (10-30% of triglycerides)
Parietal cellsHCl + intrinsic factorHCl: denatures proteins, kills microbes, activates pepsinogen; intrinsic factor: B12 absorption in ileum
G cells (antrum)GastrinStimulates HCl secretion, gastric gland growth, gastric motility

Three synergistic stimuli for HCl: Histamine (from ECL cells, paracrine), Acetylcholine (vagal, neurotransmitter), Gastrin (from G cells, endocrine). All act through cAMP or Ca²⁺ on H⁺/K⁺-ATPase. They are SYNERGISTIC — blocking one reduces acid substantially.

Cimetidine: H2 receptor blocker on parietal cells → reduces acid. PPIs: irreversibly block H⁺/K⁺-ATPase → most potent acid inhibitors.

Three phases of gastric secretion: (1) Cephalic (sight/smell/taste/thought of food — nervous); (2) Gastric (food in stomach — distension + amino acids/peptides → gastrin); (3) Intestinal (food in intestine — initially stimulatory then inhibitory).

H. pylori produces urease to neutralize local acid → survives in stomach → increases risk of gastric ulcers. Infects ~50% of world population.

Exam trap: Pepsinogen is activated at pH <5 (NOT at high/neutral pH). Intrinsic factor comes from PARIETAL cells, not chief cells. Three stimuli for HCl are synergistic — inhibiting one alone (H2 blocker) can substantially reduce acid. Amylase is NOT in gastric juice.

4. Pancreas — Exocrine Secretion

Exocrine pancreas: Produces 1-2 L/day of pancreatic juice. Two cell types: (1) Duct epithelial cells: large volume of NaHCO3-rich watery juice to neutralize acidic chyme and create alkaline pH for enzyme activity. (2) Acinar cells: small volume containing inactive digestive zymogens.

Pancreatic zymogens activation cascade: Enteropeptidase (brush border of duodenum) → converts trypsinogen to trypsin → trypsin activates all other zymogens (chymotrypsinogen, proelastase, procarboxypeptidases). This cascade in the intestinal lumen prevents autodigestion of the pancreas.

Enzyme classExamplesSubstrate
ProteolyticTrypsin, chymotrypsin, elastase, carboxypeptidasesProteins → peptides + amino acids
CarbohydrateAmylaseStarch → oligosaccharides, maltose, maltotriose
LipidLipase, phospholipase, cholesterol esteraseTriglycerides → fatty acids + monoglycerides

Exam trap: Enteropeptidase is in the BRUSH BORDER of the duodenum (not a pancreatic enzyme). It activates trypsinogen. Trypsin then activates all other zymogens. All proteolytic enzymes are secreted as inactive zymogens to prevent autodigestion.

5. Liver and Bile

The liver is the largest gland in the body. Major exocrine function: bile production (~1 L/day by hepatocytes). Bile composition: H2O (97%), NaCl/NaHCO3, bile salts (from cholesterol), cholesterol, phospholipids, bile pigments (bilirubin).

Bile salts (膽鹽): Amphipathic, made from cholesterol in liver. (A) Emulsify fats → smaller droplets for lipase access; (B) Solubilize cholesterol and phospholipids in bile (preventing gallstones). Between meals: bile stored and concentrated in gallbladder (water removed). During meal: gallbladder contracts → bile released via cystic duct → common bile duct → sphincter of Oddi → duodenum.

Bile pigments: Bilirubin = breakdown product of hemoglobin → gives bile yellow-green color. High blood bilirubin → jaundice (黃疸). Gallstones (膽結石): cholesterol stones most common — from decreased bile salt secretion or increased cholesterol synthesis.

Exam trap: Bile contains NO digestive enzymes — enzymes come from the pancreas. Bile SALTS emulsify fat (don't digest it). Jaundice = high bilirubin, NOT bile salts.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Salivary amylase digests starch in mouth — inactivated by stomach acid
  • Pepsinogen activated by pH <5; pepsin optimal at pH ~2
  • Intrinsic factor from PARIETAL cells (not chief cells)
  • Three HCl stimuli: histamine + ACh + gastrin — all SYNERGISTIC via H+/K+ ATPase
  • Three gastric secretion phases: cephalic (neural) → gastric (hormonal+neural) → intestinal
  • Enteropeptidase in brush border activates trypsinogen → trypsin activates all others
  • Bile = no enzymes; emulsifies fat via bile salts
  • Gallstones = cholesterol stones (too little bile salts or too much cholesterol)
Top things to memorize
  1. Saliva: amylase + antimicrobials; purely autonomic control; 1.5 L/day
  2. Parietal cells: HCl + intrinsic factor. Chief cells: pepsinogen + gastric lipase. G cells: gastrin
  3. Three stimuli for HCl: histamine, ACh, gastrin — synergistic
  4. Phases: cephalic (thought/smell) → gastric (food in stomach) → intestinal
  5. Enteropeptidase activates trypsinogen; trypsin activates all other zymogens
  6. Pancreas: duct cells (NaHCO3, large volume) + acinar cells (enzymes, small volume)
  7. Bile salts: amphipathic, from cholesterol, emulsify fat, prevent gallstones
  8. Bilirubin = heme breakdown product; high = jaundice

GI3: Absorption — Small Intestine & Large Intestine

Absorption is where the GI system delivers — nutrients, water, ions, and vitamins are transported from the gut lumen into blood or lymph. The small intestine is the star: ~200 m² of absorptive surface (circular folds × villi × microvilli) handles carbohydrates, proteins, lipids, vitamins, and most of the fluid. The large intestine finishes by absorbing remaining water and forming feces. The key concepts are the transporters (SGLT1, GLUT5, PepT1), the routes (portal vein vs. lymph for lipids), and how oral rehydration therapy exploits SGLT1.

1. Overview of Absorption

Absorption = transport of digested molecules through intestinal epithelial cells into blood or lymph. Occurs only after COMPLETE chemical digestion.

All nutrients in intestinal blood enter the portal vein → liver first (first-pass metabolism). Lymph from lacteals eventually drains via thoracic duct into venous circulation.

Daily intestinal transport capacity: Water ~18 L/day (1 million mmoles), Glucose 20,000 mmoles/day, Amino acids 5,000 mmoles, Triglycerides 900 mmoles. But only ~100 mL water is lost in feces — the GI tract reabsorbs nearly everything it secretes.

2. Small Intestine Structure and Surface Area

Small intestine: ~6 m long, ~4 cm diameter. Three segments: Duodenum (26 cm, 5%) — main digestion zone; Jejunum (2.5 m, 40%) — main absorption zone; Ileum (3.5 m, 55%) — more absorption + B12 + bile salt reabsorption.

StructureSizeSurface area increase
Circular folds10 mm high ridges3×
Villi0.5-1.0 mm finger-like projections10×
Microvilli (brush border)On absorptive cells20×
TotalGross surface 0.33 m² → True surface ~200 m²~600×

Each villus has a central lacteal (lymph capillary) for lipid absorption and blood capillaries for other nutrients.

Exam trap: Rugae (大皺褶) are in the STOMACH (for expansion). Circular folds, villi, and microvilli are in the SMALL INTESTINE. Microvilli provide the GREATEST individual amplification (20×).

3. Carbohydrate Absorption

Starch → maltose/maltotriose/oligosaccharides (salivary + pancreatic amylase). Brush border enzymes: Isomaltase (oligosaccharides → glucose), Maltase (maltose → glucose), Sucrase (sucrose → fructose + glucose), Lactase (lactose → galactose + glucose). Only monosaccharides can be absorbed.

MonosaccharideTransporter (apical)Mechanism
Glucose and galactoseSGLT1 (sodium-glucose transporter 1)Secondary active transport: Na+ gradient drives uphill glucose/galactose uptake
FructoseGLUT5Passive (facilitated diffusion)
All three exit basolateralGLUT2Facilitated diffusion into blood

Oral Rehydration Therapy (ORT): NaCl + glucose + water. Glucose drives Na+ uptake via SGLT1; water follows osmotically. Reduced diarrheal deaths by 93%. WHO Essential Medicine list.

Lactose intolerance: Insufficient lactase → undigested lactose remains → (a) osmotic fluid accumulation → diarrhea; (b) bacterial fermentation → lactic acid + CO2 + H2 + methane → gas, bloating, cramps.

Exam trap: Glucose and galactose use SGLT1 (active). Fructose uses GLUT5 (passive). ORT uses glucose to enhance Na+ and water absorption via SGLT1 — not osmosis alone.

4. Protein and Lipid Absorption

Protein absorption: Dipeptides and tripeptides are the MAJOR pathway — absorbed by H+-coupled PepT1 transporter (active), then converted intracellularly to amino acids. Free amino acids absorbed more slowly by Na+-dependent transporters. Most absorption in duodenum and jejunum.

Lipid absorption: Products of digestion (fatty acids + monoglycerides) form mixed micelles with bile salts + cholesterol + phospholipids → keeps hydrophobic lipids in solution → diffuse passively into absorptive cells.

Chain lengthRoute after absorption
Short-chain FA (<12C)Directly into portal blood capillaries
Long-chain FA (>12C)Resynthesized into triglycerides in ER → packaged into chylomicrons (100-500 nm) in Golgi → exocytosis into central LACTEAL → lymphatics → thoracic duct → blood
Exam trap: Long-chain fatty acids go via LYMPH (lacteals → thoracic duct), NOT portal vein. Short-chain go via portal vein. Chylomicrons are too large for blood capillaries — they MUST go through lacteals.

5. Vitamin, Mineral, and Water Absorption

Vitamins: Fat-soluble (A, D, E, K) — absorbed with lipids in mixed micelles. Hydrophilic (B complex, C) — absorbed via specific carrier proteins. Vitamin B12 — must bind intrinsic factor (from gastric parietal cells) → absorbed in TERMINAL ILEUM only.

Sodium: Absorbed along entire intestine via channels, cotransporters, exchangers; actively expelled by Na+/K+ ATPase at basolateral membrane. Na+/K+ ATPase maintains low intracellular Na+ to drive all secondary active transport (SGLT1, amino acid cotransporters, etc.).

Calcium: Two routes: (1) High dietary intake: paracellular passive diffusion throughout SI; (2) Low dietary intake: transcellular in duodenum/upper jejunum — regulated by calcitriol (active vitamin D3) which controls apical Ca2+ channels, intracellular calbindin, and basolateral Ca2+ ATPase.

Water: Absorbed by osmosis following solute (Na+, glucose, amino acids) absorption. Can pass through aquaporins or tight junctions. Little absorption in duodenum; mainly in jejunum.

6. Large Intestine — Function

Large intestine = cecum + colon (ascending, transverse, descending, sigmoid) + rectum. Functions: (1) Absorbs remaining water + ions → dehydrates chyme into feces; (2) Secretes mucus to protect mucosa; (3) Ascending/transverse colon = active absorption; (4) Descending/sigmoid colon = feces storage; (5) Bilirubin decomposition → fecal color.

Once feces reaches the rectum, the defecation reflex is triggered. Large intestine does NOT perform significant nutrient digestion.

Exam trap: Starch/carbohydrate digestion and absorption is COMPLETE by the end of the jejunum — NOT in the large intestine. The large intestine absorbs water, not nutrients (mainly).
Exam Traps Summary
  • Surface area amplifiers: circular folds (3×), villi (10×), microvilli (20×) — rugae are in the STOMACH
  • Glucose/galactose = SGLT1 (active). Fructose = GLUT5 (passive). All exit via GLUT2
  • Dipeptides/tripeptides = MAJOR protein absorption pathway via PepT1 (H+-coupled)
  • Long-chain FA → chylomicrons → lacteal → lymph → thoracic duct (NOT portal vein)
  • Short-chain FA → portal vein directly
  • Vitamin B12 absorption: TERMINAL ILEUM only, needs intrinsic factor
  • Na+/K+ ATPase on basolateral side maintains low intracellular Na+ → drives all secondary active transport
  • Carb digestion complete by end of jejunum; large intestine has no major nutrient digestion
Top things to memorize

  2. SGLT1 for glucose/galactose; GLUT5 for fructose; GLUT2 for basolateral exit
  3. ORT: glucose drives Na+ via SGLT1, water follows osmotically
  4. Dipeptides/tripeptides via H+-PepT1 = major protein absorption route
  5. Long-chain FA: ER → Golgi → chylomicrons → lacteals → lymph
  6. Short-chain FA: portal vein directly
  7. Vitamin B12: intrinsic factor required; absorbed only in terminal ileum
  8. Large intestine: absorbs water and ions; forms solid feces

Neuro1: Physiology of Nerve Cells — Ion Channels, Membrane Potential & Action Potentials

Neurons communicate using electrical signals — action potentials — and chemical signals at synapses. This lecture establishes the physical basis of neuronal signaling: ion channel properties, how concentration gradients and membrane permeability create the resting potential, how voltage-gated Na+ channels generate the action potential, how APs propagate, and how synaptic transmission converts electrical to chemical to electrical signals. The Nernst equation and Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation provide the quantitative framework. These concepts are foundational for understanding all of neuroscience and sensory physiology.

1. Ion Channels and Membrane Transport

Ion channels (離子通道): Pore-forming membrane proteins. Two distinctive features vs. other transporters: (1) Rate >10⁶ ions/sec; (2) Ions move PASSIVELY — downhill along electrochemical gradient, no energy input.

Transport typeMechanismEnergy
Ion channelsAlways passive (downhill)None
Carrier — facilitated diffusionPassive (downhill)None
Secondary active transportIon gradient of one ion drives uphill transport of anotherIndirect (from Na+ gradient built by Na+/K+ ATPase)
Primary active transport (pump)ATP-driven against gradientDirect ATP

Electrochemical gradient = concentration gradient + electrical gradient (membrane potential). Both must be considered to determine direction of ion movement.

Exam trap: Ion channels are ALWAYS passive — they CANNOT pump ions uphill. Secondary active transport (e.g., SGLT1) is "active" in that it uses the Na+ gradient — which itself was built by Na+/K+ ATPase using ATP.

2. Membrane Potential and Resting Potential

Two requirements for a membrane potential: (1) Semi-permeable membrane; (2) Ion concentration gradient across it.

IonInside (mammalian neuron)OutsideEquilibrium potential
K+140 mM (HIGH)5 mM~-90 mV (drives resting potential)
Na+5-15 mM145 mM (HIGH)~+55 mV
Cl-4-30 mM110 mM (HIGH)~-70 mV
Ca²+0.0001 mM1-2 mM (HIGH)Very positive

Nernst equation: E = (58 mV / z) × log([ion]outside / [ion]inside). For K+ in squid: E = 58/1 × log(20/400) = -75 mV. This is close to the resting potential because the membrane is most permeable to K+ at rest.

Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation: Accounts for relative permeabilities of K+, Na+, and Cl-. At rest: pK:pNa:pCl = 1:0.05:0.45. K+ dominates but Na+ and Cl- shift the resting potential to ~-70 mV (less negative than pure K+ equilibrium).

Na+/K+ ATPase: 3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in per ATP — maintains long-term ion gradients that underlie resting potential.

Exam trap: The resting potential is NOT zero — it is approximately -70 mV (inside negative). K+ is HIGH inside; Na+ and Ca²+ are HIGH outside. The Na+/K+ ATPase is required for LONG-TERM maintenance; single APs do not collapse the gradient immediately.

3. Action Potential Generation and Propagation

Phases of the action potential:

  • Resting: -70 mV; Na+ channels closed
  • Depolarization (rising phase): threshold reached → voltage-gated Na+ channels open → Na+ rushes IN (driven by both concentration AND electrical gradient) → positive feedback loop → membrane approaches +55 mV
  • Repolarization (falling phase): Na+ channels inactivate; voltage-gated K+ channels open → K+ flows OUT → membrane returns to negative
  • Afterhyperpolarization (undershoot): K+ channels remain open briefly → membrane overshoots below resting potential

Action potentials are all-or-none: fixed amplitude regardless of stimulus strength. Stimulus intensity is encoded by AP FREQUENCY.

Refractory periods: Absolute refractory: Na+ channels inactivated — no AP possible regardless of stimulus. Relative refractory: Na+ channels partially inactivated — higher threshold, smaller AP.

AP propagation: Unidirectional because Na+ channels behind the AP are in absolute refractory period. In myelinated axons: saltatory conduction — AP jumps from node to node (Nodes of Ranvier) — much faster and energy-efficient. TTX (tetrodotoxin) from puffer fish blocks voltage-gated Na+ channels → blocks AP.

Exam trap: APs are all-or-none — amplitude is fixed. Intensity is coded by frequency. Unidirectional propagation = because BEHIND the AP, Na+ channels are inactivated. Saltatory conduction is in MYELINATED axons only.

4. Synaptic Transmission

Steps in chemical synaptic transmission:

  1. Synthesis: in cell body, transported to terminal
  2. Storage: in synaptic vesicles
  3. Release/exocytosis: AP → depolarization → voltage-gated Ca²+ channels open → Ca²+ influx → vesicle fusion → neurotransmitter released into cleft
  4. Postsynaptic effects: transmitter binds receptor → electrical changes (EPSP or IPSP)
  5. Inactivation: diffusion, enzymatic degradation (e.g., acetylcholinesterase), or presynaptic re-uptake (monoamines)

EPSP (excitatory postsynaptic potential): depolarization — brings membrane toward threshold. IPSP (inhibitory): hyperpolarization — moves membrane away from threshold.

The nature of the postsynaptic effect (excitation or inhibition) is determined by the TYPE OF POSTSYNAPTIC RECEPTOR — not the neurotransmitter itself. Same neurotransmitter can be excitatory at one synapse and inhibitory at another.

Integration: axon hillock sums all EPSPs and IPSPs spatially (from different synapses) and temporally (rapid sequential inputs). AP fires only when net depolarization reaches threshold.

Patch-clamp technique (Neher + Sackmann, Nobel 1991): records single ion channel currents from membrane patches.

Exam trap: Ca²+ influx (not Na+) triggers neurotransmitter release at the presynaptic terminal. The postsynaptic RECEPTOR type determines EPSP vs IPSP — not which neurotransmitter is released.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Ion channels = always passive. Secondary active transport uses Na+ gradient (not ATP directly)
  • K+ is HIGH inside; Na+ and Ca²+ are HIGH outside the neuron
  • Resting potential ~-70 mV — maintained mainly by K+ permeability
  • APs are all-or-none; intensity coded by frequency, not amplitude
  • Rising phase = Na+ in; falling phase = K+ out + Na+ inactivation
  • Unidirectional propagation = refractory period behind the AP
  • Ca²+ triggers vesicle release at presynaptic terminal — NOT Na+
  • EPSP = depolarization; IPSP = hyperpolarization
  • Postsynaptic receptor TYPE determines EPSP or IPSP — not neurotransmitter identity
Top things to memorize
  1. Ion channels = passive. Na+/K+ ATPase = primary active (3 Na+ out, 2 K+ in)
  2. K+ high inside → drives resting potential toward -70 mV
  3. Na+ high outside → ENa = +55 mV → drives AP rising phase
  4. AP phases: depolarization (Na+ in) → repolarization (K+ out) → afterhyperpolarization
  5. Absolute refractory: Na+ channels inactivated; Relative: partially inactivated
  6. Saltatory conduction in myelinated axons: faster + energy efficient
  7. Synaptic release requires Ca²+ influx → vesicle exocytosis
  8. EPSP = depolarization; IPSP = hyperpolarization; receptor type determines which
Lower-yield / skip if short on time
  • Exact GHK equation formula and calculation
  • Specific values of relative permeabilities (pK:pNa:pCl = 1:0.05:0.45)
  • Length constant and time constant mathematics
  • Classification of all neurotransmitter classes

Neuro2: Properties of Sensory Cells and Sensory Systems

Sensory systems convert environmental stimuli into neural signals the brain can interpret. This lecture covers sensory transduction mechanisms (how stimuli become electrical signals), the somatosensory pathway, and the special senses of touch, temperature, and hearing. The key molecular discoveries — TRP channels (TRPV1 for heat/capsaicin, TRPM8 for cold/menthol) and Piezo channels (touch/proprioception) — earned the 2021 Nobel Prize. Cochlear hair cell transduction is a rich topic covering the organ of Corti, inner vs outer hair cells, prestin electromotility, and place theory of frequency coding.

1. Sensory Receptors and Transduction

A sensory receptor is a specialized cell (neural or non-neural) that receives a specific environmental stimulus and converts it into an electrical signal for the CNS.

Receptor typeStimulus
Chemoreceptors (化學感受器)O2, pH, glucose, organic molecules
Mechanoreceptors (機械感受器)Pressure, stretch, vibration, sound
Photoreceptors (光感受器)Photons of light
Thermoreceptors (溫度感受器)Temperature (heat and cold)
Nociceptors (傷害性感受器)Noxious stimuli — pain and itching

Sensory transduction: Stimulus → opens channels directly or indirectly → membrane depolarizes → generator potential (in neural receptors) or receptor potential (in non-neural receptors).

Transmission typeMechanism
Type 1 and 2 (neural receptors)Stimulus → generator potential → AP in the sensory neuron itself
Type 3 (non-neural, e.g., cochlear hair cells)Stimulus → receptor potential → neurotransmitter release → AP in downstream neuron

Sensory coding: Stimulus intensity → AP frequency (or neurotransmitter release amount). Stimulus duration → duration of AP train. APs are all-or-none so intensity CANNOT be coded by AP amplitude.

2. Somatosensory Pathway

Four somatosensory modalities: Touch, Proprioception, Temperature, Nociception (pain/itching).

Three-neuron somatosensory pathway:

  1. Primary sensory neuron: receptor → spinal cord/brainstem
  2. Secondary sensory neuron: crosses midline (decussation) → synapses in thalamus
  3. Tertiary sensory neuron: thalamus → somatosensory cortex

All secondary neurons cross the midline → sensations from the LEFT body are processed in the RIGHT hemisphere.

Somatosensory cortex: Maps each body region. Size of cortical area ∝ sensory sensitivity (not physical size of body part). Hands and lips have very large representations. Map is PLASTIC — changes with use.

Exam trap: Secondary sensory neurons cross the midline — not primary neurons. Cortical representation size = sensory sensitivity, NOT physical body size (hands are small but have huge cortical maps).

3. Touch and Temperature Receptors — Molecular Sensors

Touch receptors: Respond to stretch, steady pressure, fluttering, vibration, texture. Come as free nerve endings and complex encapsulated receptors. Piezo2 channel mediates gentle touch AND proprioception (body position sense — the "sixth sense").

Temperature receptors: Free nerve endings.

ChannelStimulusNotes
TRPV1Noxious heat (>43°C) + capsaicin (辣椒素)Cannot distinguish heat from capsaicin — why spicy food "feels hot"
TRPM8Cold (below body temp) + menthol (薄荷醇) + eucalyptol (桉葉油醇)TRPM8-knockout mice insensitive to cold

Discovery of TRPV1, TRPM8, and Piezo channels as temperature and touch sensors led to the 2021 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine.

Nociceptors: Free nerve endings responding to noxious chemical, mechanical, or thermal stimuli. Activate two pathways: (1) Spinal cord reflex (fast, protective withdrawal); (2) Ascending to cortex (conscious pain perception).

Exam trap: TRPV1 responds to BOTH heat (>43°C) AND capsaicin — because it cannot distinguish them. TRPM8 responds to cold AND menthol. Piezo2 mediates BOTH gentle touch AND proprioception — not just one.

4. Hearing — The Cochlea and Auditory Transduction

Hearing = perception of sound wave energy (frequency/amplitude/complexity). Human range: 20-20,000 Hz; most acute at 1-3 kHz. Sounds above 80 dB can damage cochlear hair cells. Disabling hearing loss: 5.3% of world population; 1/3 of persons over 65.

Hair cell typeCountInnervationRole
Inner hair cells (IHC)3,000Nearly 10:1 fiber-to-IHC ratio (primary sensory)Primary auditory transduction → brain
Outer hair cells (OHC)12,000Sparse afferent; mainly efferent from brainCochlear amplification via electromotility

Auditory transduction: Sound → basilar membrane vibration → stereocilia bundle deflection → tip links stretch → mechanotransduction channels open → K+ + Ca²+ influx → depolarization → IHC releases neurotransmitter → AP in cochlear nerve fiber.

Cochlear amplification (OHCs): The protein prestin in OHC membrane contracts/elongates in response to voltage changes (electromotility) → amplifies basilar membrane vibration up to ~1000× → increases sensitivity and frequency selectivity.

Place theory (Georg von Bekesy, Nobel 1961): Each location along the basilar membrane corresponds to a specific sound frequency. Base = high frequency; apex = low frequency. Tonotopic organization.

Timbre (音色): The quality distinguishing instruments at same pitch/loudness — determined by harmonic complexity of the sound wave.

Exam trap: IHCs (3,000) are the PRIMARY sensory transducers — despite being fewer. OHCs (12,000) are for AMPLIFICATION via prestin electromotility. The molecular identity of the hair cell mechanotransduction channel is still not fully established — the "last frontier" of sensory neurobiology.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Generator potential (neural receptors) vs receptor potential (non-neural): both are GRADED, not all-or-none
  • Stimulus intensity → AP frequency, NOT AP amplitude
  • All secondary sensory neurons cross the midline — left body → right cortex
  • Cortical map size = sensitivity, not body size
  • TRPV1: heat >43°C AND capsaicin. TRPM8: cold AND menthol. Piezo2: touch AND proprioception
  • 2021 Nobel: TRPV1, TRPM8, Piezo channels
  • IHCs (3,000) = primary transducers; OHCs (12,000) = amplifiers (prestin)
  • Place theory: cochlear location → specific frequency perception
  • Sounds >80 dB can permanently damage hair cells
Top things to memorize
  1. Five receptor types: chemo, mechano, photo, thermo, nociceptor
  2. Transduction types: neural (generator potential → AP) vs non-neural (receptor potential → NT release → AP)
  3. Somatosensory: 3 neurons; secondary crosses midline; left body → right hemisphere
  4. TRPV1 = heat + capsaicin; TRPM8 = cold + menthol; Piezo2 = touch + proprioception
  5. 2021 Nobel Prize for these molecular sensors
  6. IHC = sensory transduction; OHC = cochlear amplification (prestin)
  7. Place theory: basilar membrane location = frequency; base = high freq, apex = low freq
  8. >80 dB damages hair cells (irreversible in mammals)
Lower-yield / skip if short on time
  • Exact tip link molecular composition
  • All TRP channel subclasses beyond TRPV1 and TRPM8
  • Detailed anatomy of the outer/middle ear structures
  • Temporal theory of frequency coding (alternative to place theory)
