Lecture 11: The Nucleus II

The nucleus is sealed off from the cytoplasm by the nuclear envelope, yet it must constantly exchange proteins and RNA through the nuclear pore complex (核孔複合體). This lecture explains how the cell selectively imports nuclear proteins and exports RNA by using signal sequences, receptor proteins called importins and exportins, and the small GTPase Ran to set the direction of transport. Understanding this machinery matters because misregulation of nuclear transport underlies diseases ranging from viral infections to cancer.

1. Nuclear Transport: Overview and Directionality

  • The nucleus separates the genome (基因組) from the site of mRNA translation 翻譯 in the cytoplasm 細胞質 — this is its defining function.
  • Two classes of molecules must cross the nuclear envelope 核膜 in opposite directions:
    • RNA (合成於核內) — exported outward to the cytoplasm for translation.
    • Proteins (合成於細胞質) — imported inward; examples include histones 組蛋白, DNA polymerases DNA聚合酶, RNA polymerase RNA聚合酶, and transcription factors 轉錄因子.
  • All traffic passes through the nuclear pore complex (NPC) 核孔複合體; the central challenge is achieving selectivity 選擇性 and directionality 方向性.
  • The NPC structure (from Lecture 10) includes: cytosolic fibrils 細胞質纖維, scaffold nucleoporins 支架核孔蛋白, channel nucleoporins 通道核孔蛋白 (with disordered FG-repeat regions FG重複區), membrane ring proteins 膜環蛋白, and the nuclear basket 核籃.

2. Experimental Methods to Study Nuclear Import

The lecture presents three experimental strategies used to dissect the mechanism of nuclear transport, following a hypothesis-driven scientific approach: Question → Hypothesis → Experimental design → Results → Conclusions → Textbook knowledge.

Method 1: Electron microscopy (電子顯微鏡) with gold particles

  • Gold particles 金粒子 were injected into the cytosol of living cells; their position was tracked by EM at different time points after injection.
  • Observation 1: Uncoated gold particles must be <8–10 nm in diameter to enter the nucleus by passive diffusion 被動擴散.
  • Observation 2: When coated with a nuclear protein, particles as large as 20 nm in diameter can enter the nucleus — much larger than the passive diffusion limit.
  • Observation 3: Trypsin digestion 胰蛋白酶消化 of the protein coat on the 20 nm particle prevents nuclear entry.
  • Conclusion: Nuclear proteins carry a specific protein motif 序列基序 that is required to facilitate passage through the NPC beyond passive diffusion limits.

Method 2: In vitro reconstitution 體外重建 of nuclear transport (Adam and Gerace, JCB, 1990)

  • Digitonin 洋地黃皂苷 was used to permeabilize 使通透 the plasma membrane, creating semi-intact cells 半完整細胞 while leaving the nuclear envelope intact.
  • Fluorescently labelled 螢光標記 nuclear proteins were added in the presence or absence of cytoplasmic lysate 細胞質裂解液.
  • Observation: No nuclear transport occurred without cell lysate; transport occurred normally with lysate.
  • Conclusion: Soluble cytoplasmic proteins in the lysate are required for nuclear transport — transport is not spontaneous.

Identification of the NLS receptor from cytosol:

  • Strategy: purify cytosolic proteins that specifically bind the NLS, then test if they stimulate nuclear import.
  • Results: cytosol alone gave 9× import; cytosol + receptor gave 23× import; buffer + receptor gave 0× import.
  • Conclusion: The purified receptor binds the NLS and stimulates nuclear import — but it requires additional cytosolic factors.

Method 3: Direct visualization using GFP 綠螢光蛋白 fusion proteins

  • GFP fused to a nuclear protein can be tracked in live cells by fluorescence microscopy 螢光顯微鏡.
  • Roger Tsien pioneered the engineering of multiple fluorescent protein variants (EBFP, ECFP, EGFP, Citrine, mOrange, mCherry, mPlum, etc.) enabling multicolour imaging; the "Brainbow" technique uses this to label individual neurons in transgenic mice with distinct colours.
  • Insight from live imaging: Nuclear transport is a regulated and often dynamic process — proteins can move rapidly between nucleus and cytoplasm in response to signals.

Exam trap: Trypsin digestion of the gold-particle protein coat is a control proving the protein motif (not just any coating) is responsible for nuclear entry — do not confuse this with a non-specific size effect.
Exam trap: The in vitro reconstitution experiment shows transport requires cytosolic factors, NOT just the nuclear envelope or the NLS alone. Buffer + receptor gives 0× transport, proving receptor alone is insufficient without cytosol.

3. Nuclear Localization Signals (NLS) and Importins

  • Proteins destined for the nucleus carry specific amino acid sequences called nuclear localization signals (NLS) 核定位訊號.
  • NLS are short stretches 短片段 of positively charged 帶正電荷 amino acids (e.g. Lys 賴胺酸, Arg 精胺酸).
  • The NLS amino acids are often close together in the 3D protein structure 三維蛋白質結構 but not necessarily immediately adjacent 緊鄰 in the primary sequence.
  • Classic example — SV40 T-antigen NLS: Pro–Pro–Lys–Lys–Lys–Arg–Lys–Val; mutation of a single Lys in this motif abolishes nuclear localization.

Proof that NLS is necessary AND sufficient:

  • Necessary: Wild-type protein A (with NLS) → nucleus; mutant protein A lacking NLS (ΔNLS) → cytoplasm.
  • Sufficient: Pyruvate kinase 丙酮酸激酶 (normally cytoplasmic) fused to the SV40 T-antigen NLS → accumulates in the nucleus.

Importins 輸入蛋白 are the receptors that carry cargo into the nucleus:

  • Soluble cytosolic proteins 可溶性細胞質蛋白.
  • Bind the NLS on the cargo protein in the cytoplasm.
  • Interact with the NPC by binding FG repeats FG重複序列 — the disordered regions 無序區域 of channel nucleoporins 通道核孔蛋白.
  • The receptor–cargo complex travels through the NPC by repeated binding, dissociating, and rebinding to adjacent FG repeats.
  • Once inside the nucleus, the importin dissociates from the cargo and returns to the cytoplasm.

Exam trap: NLS is NOT cleaved after import — unlike signal peptides for the ER, the NLS remains permanently on the protein. Do not confuse these two targeting mechanisms.
Exam trap: NLS amino acids are positively charged (Lys, Arg), NOT hydrophobic. ER signal sequences are hydrophobic. Students frequently confuse these two.
Importin binds cargo (NLS) in cytoplasm → translocates through NPC via FG repeats → releases cargo in nucleus → returns to cytoplasm

4. Nuclear Export and the Role of Ran GTPase in Directionality

Nuclear export 核輸出 mirrors import but in reverse:

  • Export requires nuclear export signals (NES) 核輸出訊號 on the cargo.
  • Export receptors called exportins 輸出蛋白 bind cargo inside the nucleus and carry it out through the NPC.
  • Key asymmetry question: what determines the direction of transport for importins vs. exportins?

GTPases GTP酶 are molecular switches 分子開關 that cycle between a GTP-bound active state and a GDP-bound inactive state. Three examples:

  • Ran GTPases — facilitate transport of proteins to/from the nucleus.
  • Rab GTPases — regulate vesicle trafficking 囊泡運輸.
  • Ras GTPases — cell differentiation 細胞分化 and growth signalling 生長訊號傳遞.

Ran GTPase 核轉運GTP酶 cycle:

  • Ran is a small GTPase 小型GTP酶 that cycles between Ran-GTP (active) and Ran-GDP (inactive).
  • Ran-GEF (guanine nucleotide exchange factor 鳥嘌呤核苷酸交換因子) — promotes exchange of GDP for GTP; located in the nucleus.
  • Ran-GAP (GTPase activating protein GTP酶激活蛋白) — promotes hydrolysis 水解 of GTP → GDP + Pi; located in the cytoplasm.
  • Ran can diffuse freely 自由擴散 between nucleus and cytoplasm through the NPC.
  • Result: most Ran in the nucleus is Ran-GTP; most Ran in the cytoplasm is Ran-GDP — a steep gradient 梯度 across the nuclear envelope.

How Ran-GTP gradient provides directionality to nuclear IMPORT:

  1. Free importin binds cargo (via NLS) in the cytoplasm where Ran-GDP predominates.
  2. The importin–cargo complex translocates through the NPC into the nucleus.
  3. Inside the nucleus, Ran-GTP (abundant) binds importin → causes rapid dissociation of cargo from importin.
  4. Importin returns to cytoplasm bound to Ran-GTP.
  5. In the cytoplasm, Ran-GAP promotes GTP hydrolysis → Ran-GDP dissociates from importin, freeing importin for another cycle.
  6. Ran-GDP diffuses back to nucleus where Ran-GEF recharges it to Ran-GTP.
  7. The whole cycle is energetically driven by GTP hydrolysis.

How Ran-GTP gradient provides directionality to nuclear EXPORT:

  1. Inside the nucleus, Ran-GTP + exportin + cargo (with NES) form a stable ternary complex 三元複合體.
  2. Exportin allows the complex to traverse the NPC to the cytoplasm.
  3. In the cytoplasm, Ran-GAP promotes GTP hydrolysis by Ran → Ran-GDP.
  4. This GTP hydrolysis causes dissociation of the Ran/exportin/cargo complex, releasing cargo into cytoplasm.

Ran-GTP (nucleus) vs Ran-GDP (cytoplasm) gradient — the engine of directional transport
Exam trap: Ran-GEF is in the NUCLEUS (makes Ran-GTP); Ran-GAP is in the CYTOPLASM (converts Ran-GTP → Ran-GDP). Students frequently reverse these locations.
Exam trap: Ran-GTP promotes RELEASE of importin cargo in the nucleus AND STABILIZES the exportin–cargo complex in the nucleus — opposite effects depending on import vs. export receptor. A drug blocking GEF (blocking GDP→GTP exchange) would prevent importins from releasing cargo in the nucleus.

5. Regulation of Nuclear Transport

Transport through the NPC can be regulated by three general mechanisms:

  • Phosphorylation 磷酸化 of amino acids close to the NLS or NES — can mask or expose the signal, altering access of importin/exportin.
  • Inhibitory proteins 抑制蛋白 that mask the signal sequences — bind to and cover the NLS or NES, preventing receptor recognition.
  • Anchoring proteins 錨定蛋白 that retain proteins in the cytosol or another compartment — physically prevent the cargo from reaching the NPC.

Example 1: Dorsal protein in Drosophila embryo

  • The transcription regulatory protein 轉錄調控蛋白 Dorsal is excluded from nuclei at the dorsal side (top) of the embryo but concentrated in nuclei at the ventral side (bottom).
  • This spatial regulation of nuclear import creates a transcription factor gradient that patterns the embryo along the dorsal-ventral axis 背腹軸.

Example 2: SREBP regulation of cholesterol biosynthesis

  • SREBP (sterol regulatory element-binding protein 固醇調控元件結合蛋白) is a transcription regulator 轉錄調控子 controlling expression of cholesterol biosynthetic enzymes 膽固醇生物合成酶.
  • High cholesterol 高膽固醇: SREBP is anchored in the ER membrane by interaction with SCAP, an ER membrane protein 內質網膜蛋白 that binds cholesterol — nuclear import is blocked.
  • Low cholesterol 低膽固醇:
    1. The cholesterol binding site on SCAP is empty → SCAP changes conformation 構象.
    2. SCAP + SREBP are packaged into transport vesicles 運輸囊泡 and delivered to the Golgi apparatus 高爾基體.
    3. At the Golgi, proteases 蛋白酶 (site 1 and site 2 proteases) cleave SREBP, freeing its cytosolic domain 胞質域 from the membrane.
    4. The released SREBP domain moves into the nucleus to activate transcription of cholesterol biosynthesis genes.
  • This is an example of regulation by anchoring (ER membrane) combined with proteolytic release 蛋白水解釋放.

Example 3: NF-AT and T-cell activation

  • NF-AT (nuclear factor of activated T cells 活化T細胞核因子) is a transcription regulatory protein that resides in the cytosol in a phosphorylated state 磷酸化狀態 in resting T cells — NLS is masked by phosphate groups, NES is exposed → protein stays in cytoplasm.
  • Upon T-cell activation, cytoplasmic Ca²⁺ 鈣離子 rises → calcineurin 鈣調磷酸酶 (a protein phosphatase 蛋白磷酸酶) is activated → dephosphorylates 去磷酸化 NF-AT.
  • Dephosphorylated NF-AT: NLS exposed, NES masked → imported into nucleus → activates gene transcription 基因轉錄 → T cell activation.
  • Some immunosuppressive drugs 免疫抑制藥物 (e.g. cyclosporin) inhibit calcineurin → NF-AT remains phosphorylated and cytoplasmic → T cell activation is blocked.

Exam trap: SREBP is regulated by anchoring in the ER (not direct NLS masking) — its nuclear import requires physical cleavage at the Golgi. Students may misclassify this as simple phosphorylation regulation.
Exam trap: In resting T cells NF-AT is phosphorylated → cytoplasmic (NES exposed). Upon activation, calcineurin dephosphorylates NF-AT → NLS exposed → nuclear. Phosphorylation here promotes EXPORT (not import) — the opposite of what students may assume.

6. Visualizing Ran-GTP: FRET-Based Molecular Probe

Fluorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) 螢光共振能量轉移 is a technique to detect molecular proximity 分子接近性:

  • Two fluorophores 螢光團 are used: CFP (cyan fluorescent protein 青色螢光蛋白) and YFP (yellow fluorescent protein 黃色螢光蛋白).
  • Excitation 激發 at 435 nm hits CFP.
  • If CFP and YFP are far apart: CFP emits cyan signal; no FRET.
  • If CFP and YFP are close together (within ~10 nm): energy is transferred from CFP to YFP → reduced CFP signal + increased YFP signal = FRET occurs.
  • The ratio IFRET/ICFP reports the level of FRET and thus proximity of the two fluorophores.

Ran-GTP FRET probe (Kalab et al., Science, 2002):

  • Probe design: YFP–RBD–CFP construct, where RBD = Ran-GTP binding domain 結合域.
  • Without Ran-GTP: RBD adopts a compact conformation → YFP and CFP are close → FRET occurs → reduced CFP, enhanced YFP (IFRET/ICFP is high).
  • With Ran-GTP: Ran-GTP binds RBD → opens/extends conformation → YFP and CFP move apart → no FRET → CFP and YFP signals unchanged (IFRET/ICFP is low).
  • Low IFRET/ICFP ratio = high Ran-GTP level in that location.

Experiment 1 — Ran-GTP in interphase nucleus (Xenopus 非洲爪蟾 sperm nuclei in interphase egg extracts):

  • Observation: IFRET/ICFP ratio was lowest inside the nucleus.
  • Control: adding RanT24N (a Ran-GEF inhibitor 抑制劑) abolished the nuclear enrichment.
  • Conclusion: Ran-GTP is highly enriched in the nucleus — confirming the model that Ran-GEF in the nucleus generates a high Ran-GTP zone.

Experiment 2 — Ran-GTP during mitosis (Xenopus sperm nuclei in mitotic egg extracts):

  • Observation: IFRET/ICFP ratio was lowest in the central spindle region 中央紡錘體 around chromosomes 染色體.
  • Conclusion: Ran-GTP is highly enriched around the central spindle during mitosis — suggesting Ran-GTP plays roles beyond nuclear transport, including spindle assembly.

Exam trap: A LOW IFRET/ICFP ratio means HIGH Ran-GTP (probe is open, no FRET). Students may intuitively assume high signal = high Ran-GTP — the logic is inverted for this probe.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Trypsin digestion of gold-particle protein coat proves the protein motif, not particle size alone, is responsible for nuclear entry.
  • In vitro reconstitution: buffer + receptor alone = 0× import — cytosol is also required; receptor alone is insufficient.
  • NLS is NOT cleaved after import (unlike ER signal peptides); it remains permanently on the protein.
  • NLS amino acids are positively charged (Lys, Arg) — NOT hydrophobic like ER signal sequences.
  • Ran-GEF is in the NUCLEUS (generates Ran-GTP); Ran-GAP is in the CYTOPLASM (converts Ran-GTP → Ran-GDP). Do not reverse these.
  • Ran-GTP causes cargo RELEASE from importin (in nucleus) but STABILIZES exportin–cargo complex (in nucleus) — opposite effects for import vs. export.
  • A drug blocking Ran-GEF (blocking GDP→GTP exchange) → no Ran-GTP in nucleus → importins cannot release cargo in nucleus.
  • SREBP regulation involves ER anchoring + Golgi proteolytic cleavage, not simple NLS phosphorylation.
  • NF-AT phosphorylation promotes cytoplasmic retention (NES exposed); dephosphorylation by calcineurin exposes NLS and drives nuclear import — phosphorylation here causes EXPORT, not import.
  • Low IFRET/ICFP in FRET probe = HIGH Ran-GTP concentration (probe is open, FRET cannot occur). The relationship is inverse.
  • During mitosis, Ran-GTP is enriched around chromosomes/central spindle — not just inside the nucleus.
Top things to memorize
  1. NLS = short, positively charged amino acid sequence (Lys/Arg-rich) — necessary AND sufficient for nuclear import; remains on protein after import.
  2. Importin binds NLS in cytoplasm → translocates through NPC via FG-repeat interactions → releases cargo in nucleus when Ran-GTP binds.
  3. Exportin binds cargo (NES) + Ran-GTP inside nucleus → stable ternary complex → traverses NPC → Ran-GAP in cytoplasm hydrolyses GTP → complex dissociates.
  4. Ran-GEF in nucleus → Ran-GTP high in nucleus; Ran-GAP in cytoplasm → Ran-GDP high in cytoplasm — this gradient is the engine of directionality.
  5. Gold particle EM experiment: uncoated <8–10 nm passive; coated nuclear protein up to 20 nm; trypsin removes coat → no entry.
  6. In vitro reconstitution: digitonin permeabilizes plasma membrane → semi-intact cells; transport requires cytoplasmic lysate; cytosol alone = 9×, cytosol + receptor = 23×, buffer + receptor = 0×.
  7. SV40 T-antigen NLS: Pro–Pro–Lys–Lys–Lys–Arg–Lys–Val; single Lys mutation abolishes localization; NLS is sufficient to redirect cytoplasmic pyruvate kinase to the nucleus.
  8. Three mechanisms of regulation: (1) phosphorylation near signal, (2) inhibitory protein masking signal, (3) anchoring in cytosol/compartment.
  9. SREBP: anchored in ER by SCAP (high cholesterol) → low cholesterol → SCAP changes conformation → transported to Golgi → proteolytic cleavage → released domain enters nucleus.
  10. NF-AT: phosphorylated (resting T cell) → cytoplasmic; calcineurin dephosphorylates (high Ca²⁺, activated T cell) → nuclear; immunosuppressants block calcineurin.
  11. FRET probe logic: no Ran-GTP → FRET (probe closed, low IFRET/ICFP is NOT the case here — reversed: low ratio = high Ran-GTP = probe open = no FRET). Ran-GTP enriched in nucleus (interphase) and around spindle (mitosis).
  12. GTPase family: Ran (nuclear transport), Rab (vesicle trafficking), Ras (cell differentiation/growth signalling).

Lecture 12: Vesicular Traffic, Secretion and Endocytosis-I

Eukaryotic cells are divided into membrane-bound compartments, creating a logistical challenge: how do proteins and molecules move from where they are made to where they are needed? This lecture introduces vesicular transport — the cell's delivery system — and establishes the secretory pathway roadmap (ER → Golgi → plasma membrane) discovered by George Palade. It then dissects the molecular principles underlying each step: how coat proteins deform membranes to bud vesicles, how dynamin pinches them off, how microtubules serve as tracks, and how Rab proteins, tethering factors, and SNAREs ensure vesicles dock and fuse only with the correct target compartment.

1. Introduction: Why Vesicular Transport Matters

Compartmentalization (區室化) — eukaryotic cells contain many membrane-enclosed organelles; proteins synthesized in the cytoplasm must be delivered to the correct compartment to function.

  • Problem 1: Nuclear proteins (transcription factors, DNA polymerases) synthesized in cytoplasm must reach the nucleus — solved by Nuclear Pore Complex (核孔複合體) mediated gated transport.
  • Problem 2: Secreted proteins (growth factors, insulin, hormones) must travel from inside the cell to outside — solved by the secretory transport pathway (分泌運輸途徑).
  • Problem 3: Extracellular molecules (nutrients, cholesterol, ions) must be brought into the cell — solved by the endocytic transport pathway (胞吞運輸途徑).

Transport vesicles (運輸囊泡) — the carriers mediating both secretory and endocytic pathways:

  • Small, spherical (球形), membrane-enclosed (膜包被) structures.
  • Enriched with specific cargo proteins (貨物蛋白) destined for a target compartment.
  • Physiological importance: pancreatic acinar cells (胰臟腺泡細胞) secrete digestive enzymes from zymogen granules (酶原顆粒, ZG); neurons store neurotransmitters in synaptic vesicles (突觸囊泡) released at the synapse.

Exam trap: The secretory pathway and endocytic pathway are both mediated by vesicular carriers — do not confuse them. Secretory = inside to outside (or to organelles); endocytic = outside to inside.

2. The Secretory Pathway Roadmap

George Palade (喬治·帕拉德) — "Father of modern cell biology"; Nobel Prize in Physiology and Medicine, 1974; established the secretory pathway route: ER → Golgi → Plasma membrane.

  • Cargo proteins enter the endoplasmic reticulum (內質網, ER) lumen, travel through Golgi cisternae (高爾基體扁囊), and exit via secretory vesicles (分泌囊泡) / zymogen granules to the plasma membrane or extracellular space.
  • Endocytic arm: plasma membrane → endocytic vesicle → early endosome (早期核內體) → recycling endosome (回收核內體) → late endosome (晚期核內體) → lysosome (溶酶體).
  • Fluorescent tagging of cargo proteins allowed direct visualization of trafficking along the secretory pathway.

Yeast genetic screen (酵母菌遺傳篩選) — Novick & Schekman (1979, 1980) used temperature-sensitive mutants of Saccharomyces cerevisiae to identify genes required for secretion:

  • At restrictive temperature, secretory mutants accumulated secretory proteins at defined steps rather than secreting them.
  • Secretory mutant A: protein accumulates in ER (block at ER exit).
  • Secretory mutant B: protein accumulates in Golgi apparatus (block at Golgi exit).
  • Secretory mutant C: protein accumulates in transport vesicles (block at vesicle fusion with plasma membrane).
  • Wild-type yeast can be separated from secretory mutants by density gradient centrifugation (密度梯度離心) — mutants are denser because they accumulate vesicles/organelles.
  • Identified 23 complementation groups (互補群) (SEC genes) required for post-translational events in the secretory pathway (Cell, 1980).
  • Randy Schekman shared the 2013 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine for this work.

Exam trap: Secretory mutant A accumulates protein in the ER (not Golgi); mutant B in the Golgi; mutant C in transport vesicles — each reflects where the block occurs in the sequential pathway.

3. Essential Steps of Vesicular Transport

Every vesicular transport event has three essential steps (三個關鍵步驟):

  • Step 1 — Vesicle budding (囊泡出芽) and cargo packaging (貨物包裝): coat proteins deform the donor membrane; cargo receptors select cargo; dynamin pinches the vesicle off.
  • Step 2 — Vesicle transport (囊泡運輸): vesicles are delivered along microtubule tracks (微管軌道) from donor to acceptor compartment.
  • Step 3 — Vesicle fusion (囊泡融合) with target organelle: vesicle docks and fuses with the correct target membrane; cargo is released.

Exam trap: All three steps — budding, transport, and fusion — are required for vesicular traffic. Missing any one step blocks cargo delivery entirely.

4. Vesicle Budding: Coat Proteins, Clathrin, and Dynamin

Coat proteins (外被蛋白) — polymerize on the cytosolic face of donor membranes to force membrane deformation into a curved bud:

  • Clathrin (囊格蛋白) — mediates endocytosis from plasma membrane; also Golgi → endosome transport. Forms a cage-like lattice structure visible by EM.
  • COPI (COP I 蛋白) — mediates Golgi → ER (retrograde, 逆行) transport and intra-Golgi transport.
  • COPII (COP II 蛋白) — mediates ER → Golgi (anterograde, 順行) transport. Scale bar ~100 nm.
  • Mechanism of membrane deformation: coat proteins polymerize into concave-shaped cage structures (凹形籠狀結構) that physically force the lipid bilayer to curve.
  • Cargo selection: cargo receptors (貨物受體) on the donor membrane recognize specific cargo molecules and concentrate them into the forming vesicle.

Clathrin-coated vesicle assembly sequence (有網格蛋白外被囊泡裝配順序):

  1. Cargo proteins recognized by cargo receptor (貨物受體).
  2. Cargo receptor recruits adaptin (接合蛋白) — adaptor protein linking cargo receptor to clathrin.
  3. Adaptin recruits clathrin (囊格蛋白).
  4. Clathrin polymerizes to form first a clathrin-coated membrane patch (有網格蛋白外被膜片), then a clathrin-coated pit (有網格蛋白外被小窩).
  5. Clathrin-coated pit pinches off from the plasma membrane — requires dynamin (液泡素).
  6. Clathrin coat is released from the vesicle (uncoating, 去外被), producing a naked transport vesicle (裸露運輸囊泡).
EM observation: Clathrin-coated pits (~0.2 µm) visible on inner surface of plasma membrane of cultured skin cells.

Dynamin (液泡素) — a GTPase (GTP 水解酶) that pinches clathrin-coated vesicles off the donor membrane:

  • Forms a dynamin helix (液泡素螺旋) around the membrane neck of the budding vesicle.
  • GTP hydrolysis (GTP 水解) drives conformational change in the dynamin ring, constricting and severing the membrane neck.
  • Genetic evidence: Temperature-sensitive mutations in dynamin (in Drosophila) cause accumulation of clathrin-coated pits that fail to pinch off — visible by EM as long membrane tubules with attached pits.
  • In vivo evidence: Dynamin mutant Drosophila are paralyzed at high temperature (25°C) because synaptic vesicle recycling fails; wild-type flies are unaffected.

Exam trap: Dynamin is specifically required for the scission (切斷) step — not for coat assembly or cargo selection. Dynamin mutants accumulate coated pits that never fully pinch off.
Exam trap: COPI mediates retrograde (Golgi to ER) traffic; COPII mediates anterograde (ER to Golgi) traffic; clathrin mediates endocytosis and Golgi-to-endosome traffic. Do not mix up directions.

5. Vesicle Transport and Fusion: Rab Proteins, Tethering Factors, and SNAREs

Vesicle transport along microtubules (沿微管的囊泡運輸):

  • Microtubules (微管) serve as intracellular highways — cells contain a dense network of microtubule tracks.
  • Vesicles (green fluorescence) can be visualized moving along microtubule tracks (red fluorescence) by live-cell fluorescence microscopy.
  • Motor proteins (馬達蛋白) on vesicle surface walk along microtubule tracks to deliver vesicles directionally.

Targeting and docking of vesicles (囊泡靶向與停靠) — three key molecular players ensure specificity:

  • Rab proteins (Rab 蛋白) — small GTPases (小型 GTP 酶) on the vesicle surface; each Rab marks a specific vesicle/compartment identity; recruits tethering proteins.
  • Tethering proteins (栓繫蛋白) — on the target membrane; interact with active (GTP-bound) Rab to initially capture the vesicle from a distance; also assemble SNAREs.
  • SNAREs (SNARE 蛋白) — transmembrane proteins that catalyze lipid bilayer fusion:
    • v-SNARE (囊泡SNARE) — on the transport vesicle membrane.
    • t-SNARE (靶膜SNARE) — on the target membrane.
    • Complementary v-SNARE and t-SNARE pairs intertwine (zip together) to pull the two membranes into close contact, causing bilayer fusion.
    • Different v-SNARE/t-SNARE pairs are compartment-specific, ensuring vesicles fuse only with the correct target.

SNARE-mediated membrane fusion sequence (SNARE 介導的膜融合過程):

  1. Tethering (栓繫): Rab-GTP on vesicle recruits tethering protein on target membrane — vesicle is captured at a distance.
  2. Docking (停靠): v-SNARE and t-SNARE begin to zipper together — membranes are pulled close.
  3. Membrane coalescence (膜聚合): membranes contact and begin to merge.
  4. Lipid bilayer fusion (脂雙層融合): complete fusion — cargo protein released into target compartment lumen or membrane.
Nobel recognition: James Rothman shared the 2013 Nobel Prize for discovering the SNARE mechanism of vesicle fusion.

SNARE specificity (SNARE 特異性) — ensures correct vesicle targeting:

  • Each compartment expresses a unique set of v-SNAREs and t-SNAREs.
  • A vesicle carrying v-SNARE "type A" only fuses efficiently with a target membrane bearing the matching t-SNARE "type A" — cargo a and cargo b are thus sorted to different compartments.
  • This combinatorial code prevents mis-targeting of vesicles to wrong organelles.

Example: Autophagosome–vacuole fusion (自噬體–液泡融合) in yeast (research example from lecture):

  • Rab GTPase: Ypt7; GEF (guanine nucleotide exchange factor, 鳥嘌呤核苷酸交換因子): Mon1-Ccz1.
  • Tethering complex: HOPS — binds Ypt7-GTP and also assembles SNAREs.
  • v-SNARE on autophagosome: Ykt6 (recruited from the PAS, pre-autophagosomal structure).
  • Sequence: Atg8 targets Mon1-Ccz1 to autophagosome → Mon1-Ccz1 activates Ypt7 → Ypt7-GTP binds HOPS on vacuole → HOPS assembles SNAREs → fusion. (Gao et al., JCB 2018; eLife 2018; EMBO R 2020)

Exam trap: SNARE pairing is specific — v-SNARE on the vesicle must match the t-SNARE on the target. This specificity, not just physical proximity, determines which vesicle fuses with which compartment.

6. Summary: Complete Vesicular Transport Machinery

The full 7-step cycle of vesicular transport (Bonifacino and Glick, 2004):

  1. Initiation (起始): transmembrane cargo protein and v-SNARE associate with coat proteins at donor membrane.
  2. Budding (出芽): coat proteins polymerize, deforming the membrane into a bud.
  3. Scission (切斷): dynamin severs the vesicle neck (GTP hydrolysis).
  4. Uncoating (去外被): coat proteins dissociate from the vesicle, releasing them back to the cytosol.
  5. Tethering (栓繫): Rab-GTP on vesicle contacts tethering factor on acceptor membrane.
  6. Docking (停靠): v-SNARE and t-SNARE zipper together.
  7. Fusion (融合): lipid bilayers merge; cargo is delivered.

Lecture 13: Vesicular Traffic, Secretion and Endocytosis-II

Vesicular transport moves proteins through two major highways: the secretory pathway (ER → Golgi → plasma membrane) and the endocytic pathway (plasma membrane → endosomes → lysosome). Coat proteins — COPII, COPI, and clathrin — select cargo and sculpt vesicle buds at specific membrane compartments, while sorting signals encoded in protein sequences direct each cargo to its correct destination. The trans-Golgi network (TGN 反式高爾基體網路) acts as the central dispatch hub from which cargoes diverge to lysosomes, secretory vesicles, or the cell surface. Endocytosis, exemplified by clathrin-mediated uptake of LDL-cholesterol, allows cells to efficiently import extracellular molecules using receptor-based selectivity.

1. Coat Proteins and Sorting Signals in the Secretory Pathway

  • Vesicle coat proteins (囊泡外被蛋白) — serve two functions: (1) deform the lipid bilayer to form a bud; (2) select and package specific cargo proteins into budding vesicles
  • Three major coat types used at distinct trafficking steps:
    CoatDirection / LocationKey function
    COPIIER → Golgi (anterograde 正向)Export cargo from ER
    COPIGolgi → ER (retrograde 逆向); intra-GolgiRetrieval of ER-resident proteins; intra-Golgi transport
    Clathrin (網格蛋白)TGN → endosome/lysosome; plasma membrane → endosomeReceptor-mediated endocytosis; lysosomal targeting
  • Dynamin (動力蛋白) — GTPase that pinches off (scission 切斷) vesicles from the donor membrane
  • Rab proteins (Rab蛋白) & tethering factors (拴繫因子) — mediate vesicle targeting to the correct acceptor compartment
  • SNAREs (可溶性NSF附著蛋白受體) — v-SNARE on vesicle pairs with t-SNARE on target membrane to drive membrane fusion (融合)
  • Sorting signal (分揀信號 / targeting signal) — a short amino acid sequence, a signal patch on the folded structure, or a covalent modification that acts as an "address label" recognised by coat proteins; examples: KDEL sequence (soluble ER residents), mannose-6-phosphate (溶酶體酶)

Exam trap: COPII moves cargo forward (ER→Golgi); COPI moves cargo backward (Golgi→ER). Students often swap these. COPI also mediates intra-Golgi transport — it is not exclusively retrograde.

2. COPII-Mediated ER-to-Golgi Transport

  • COPII coat assembly — initiated by GTP-bound Sar1 (小GTP酶) inserting into the ER membrane:
    1. Sar1-GDP (inactive) in cytosol is activated by a GEF → Sar1-GTP inserts amphipathic helix into ER membrane
    2. Sar1-GTP recruits the inner layer: Sec23/24 complex — Sec24 recognises and captures transmembrane cargo proteins
    3. Outer layer: Sec13/31 complex polymerises (聚合) over Sec23/24 to complete the coat and deform the membrane into a bud
    4. Scission releases the COPII vesicle; Sar1 GTP hydrolysis triggers coat disassembly (uncoating 去外被)
  • Destination — COPII vesicles travel to the vesicular tubular cluster (囊泡管狀簇, VTC), an intermediate compartment between ER and Golgi (also called ERGIC in mammals)
  • COPII vesicle morphology — ~60–90 nm diameter; can be visualised by electron microscopy as coated buds on the ER membrane
  • Cargo selectivity — transmembrane cargo proteins contain cytoplasmic tail signals recognised by Sec24; soluble luminal proteins are co-packaged by binding to transmembrane cargo receptors

Exam trap: Sar1 GTP hydrolysis causes coat disassembly, not assembly. If Sar1 cannot hydrolyse GTP, the coat remains — vesicles bud but cannot uncoat, blocking further transport.

3. Golgi Apparatus Structure and COPI Retrieval

  • Golgi apparatus (高爾基體) — discovered by Camillo Golgi (Nobel Prize 1906); consists of a stack of flattened sacs called cisternae (囊腔), ~200 nm diameter as seen by EM
  • Polarity (極性) — cargo flows from cis (順式) → medial (中間) → trans (反式) cisternae; each cisterna is enriched with distinct glycosylation (糖基化) enzymes that sequentially modify proteins
  • Trans Golgi network (TGN 反式高爾基體網路) — the trans face; acts as the primary cargo-sorting hub dispatching vesicles to: lysosomes, secretory vesicles, plasma membrane, recycling endosomes
  • COPI functions:
    1. Intra-Golgi transport (between cisternae)
    2. Retrograde retrieval of escaped ER-resident proteins back to the ER
  • KDEL retrieval mechanism:
    • Soluble ER-resident proteins (e.g., BiP, PDI) carry a C-terminal KDEL motif (KDEL序列)
    • If these proteins escape to the Golgi, the KDEL receptor in the Golgi/VTC membrane recognises the KDEL sequence and loads the escaped protein into a COPI vesicle
    • COPI vesicle carries the KDEL receptor + bound luminal protein retrograde back to the ER
    • In the ER lumen (neutral pH), the receptor releases the protein (low affinity at neutral pH)
  • Rothman's in vitro Golgi transport assay (1986–1989) — used VSV-G protein (水泡性口炎病毒G蛋白) and ³H-GlcNAc transfer as a readout of inter-Golgi vesicle transport; key results:
    Condition[³H] GlcNAc incorporated into VSV-G (cpm)
    Complete reaction3500
    − ATP50
    − Cytosol75
    − Golgi membranes95
    + GTPγS (10 μM, non-hydrolysable GTP)420
  • GTPγS (非可水解GTP類似物) — GTP analogue that cannot be hydrolysed; addition causes accumulation of coated vesicles (unable to uncoat), confirming that a GTPase controls coat disassembly; vesicles purified this way led to discovery of COPI coat proteins (Malhotra et al., Cell 1989)
  • Defective TGN sorting → mistargeting → physiological defect: e.g., HIV protein Nef hijacks TGN sorting machinery to reroute MHC-I (主要組織相容性複合體I類) away from the plasma membrane, redirecting it to the lysosome for degradation — allowing HIV-infected cells to evade cytotoxic T cells

Exam trap: GTPγS addition in Rothman's assay gives lower transport signal (420 cpm) than the complete reaction (3500 cpm) because vesicles form but cannot fuse — they are trapped as coated vesicles. This confirms GTP hydrolysis is required for vesicle uncoating/fusion, not for budding itself.

4. Golgi-to-Plasma Membrane: TGN as Sorting Hub and Polarised Cells

  • TGN as cargo dispatch centre — from the TGN, vesicles are sorted to:
    • Late endosome (晚期內體) / lysosome (溶酶體) — via clathrin-coated vesicles, e.g., lysosomal hydrolases tagged with mannose-6-phosphate
    • Secretory vesicle (分泌囊泡) → constitutive or regulated exocytosis to plasma membrane
    • Early endosome (早期內體) / recycling endosome (再循環內體)
    • Apical or basolateral plasma membrane in polarised epithelial cells
  • Polarised epithelial cells (極性上皮細胞) — e.g., intestinal absorptive cells:
    • Apical surface (頂面) — faces gut lumen; imports nutrients via microvilli (微絨毛)
    • Basal surface (基底面) — faces basal lamina; exports nutrients to blood
    • Different membrane proteins must be sorted to apical vs. basolateral domains — TGN sorting ensures correct targeting
  • HIV Nef example of mistargeting:
    • Normal: MHC-I travels ER → Golgi → TGN → plasma membrane, displaying viral peptides to cytotoxic T lymphocytes (CTL)
    • With Nef: Nef protein associates with MHC-I at the TGN/Golgi and redirects it into vesicles going to the late endosome → lysosome → degradation
    • Consequence: CTL cannot recognise infected cells → viral immune evasion (免疫逃避)

Exam trap: The TGN is part of the Golgi apparatus (trans face) but functions as an independent sorting station — it dispatches cargo in multiple directions simultaneously. Do not confuse TGN with the trans cisterna; they are distinct sub-compartments.

5. Endocytic Pathway: Types, Road Map, and LDL-Cholesterol Uptake

  • Endocytosis (內吞作用) major functions: uptake of nutrients; recycling of lipid membranes; cellular signalling; cell adhesion and migration; immune defence
  • Two types of endocytosis:
    TypeMechanismCargo size
    Pinocytosis (胞飲作用)Ingestion of small molecules; clathrin-coated vesicles (~0.1 μm); EM shows progressive membrane invagination → pinchingSmall molecules, proteins
    Phagocytosis (吞噬作用)Ingestion of large particles; pseudopods (偽足) engulf bacteria or dead cells; performed by macrophages & neutrophilsBacteria, cells (~5 μm)
  • Road map of the endocytic pathway:
    1. Clathrin-coated pit (網格蛋白包被坑) at plasma membrane → clathrin-coated vesicle
    2. Uncoating → early endosome (早期內體): slightly acidic lumen; receptor-ligand dissociation begins
    3. From early endosome: receptors recycled back to plasma membrane via recycling endosome (再循環內體)
    4. Ligand/cargo progresses to late endosome (晚期內體): more acidic; multivesicular body (多囊體)
    5. Late endosome fuses with lysosome (溶酶體): hydrolytic enzymes degrade cargo

Exam trap: Phagocytosis is distinct from pinocytosis: phagocytosis involves active pseudopod extension around large particles and is restricted to specialised immune cells; pinocytosis (including clathrin-mediated endocytosis) occurs in virtually all cells.

6. Receptor-Mediated Endocytosis of Cholesterol (LDL Pathway)

  • LDL particle (低密度脂蛋白顆粒) structure (~22 nm):
    • Core: cholesteryl ester molecules (膽固醇酯) + free cholesterol molecules
    • Outer shell: phospholipid monolayer (磷脂單層) + apolipoprotein B (載脂蛋白B) on surface
  • LDL receptor (LDLR 低密度脂蛋白受體) — transmembrane protein on plasma membrane; binds apolipoprotein B on LDL surface
  • Clathrin-mediated endocytosis of LDL — step-by-step:
    1. LDL binds LDLR at the cell surface; LDLR concentrates into clathrin-coated pits
    2. Clathrin-coated vesicle forms (dynamin-dependent scission) and is internalised
    3. Vesicle uncoats → fuses with early endosome
    4. Acidic pH of early endosome causes LDL to dissociate from LDLR
    5. LDLR is recycled back to the plasma membrane via recycling endosome (receptor reused ~200 times)
    6. LDL is delivered to lysosome → hydrolytic enzymes release free cholesterol
    7. Free cholesterol exits lysosome and is used by the cell for membrane synthesis or stored
  • Advantages of receptor-mediated endocytosis (受體介導的內吞作用):
    • Efficiency (效率) — molecules at very low extracellular concentration can still be captured
    • Selectivity (選擇性) — only molecules recognised by specific receptor proteins are internalised
  • Familial hypercholesterolaemia (家族性高膽固醇血症) — caused by loss-of-function mutations in LDLR gene:
    • LDL cannot be cleared from the blood → 6–10-fold elevation in blood LDL levels
    • Clinical consequences: atherosclerosis (動脈硬化), heart attacks in early childhood
    • Treatment: statins (他汀類藥物) — inhibit a key enzyme in the cholesterol biosynthesis pathway, reducing blood cholesterol; discovered by Goldstein and Brown (Nobel Prize 1985)
  • Exam-relevant drug scenario — drug H5434 prevents cholesterol from dissociating from LDLR in acidic conditions: receptor remains bound to LDL in the endosome → LDLR cannot be recycled → remains on endosomal membrane → cytosolic cholesterol levels decrease (answer: B); receptor will not return to plasma membrane (not answer C — LDLR is still internalised)

Exam trap: The LDL receptor is recycled back to the plasma membrane (not degraded with the LDL cargo). Mutations that impair receptor recycling — or drugs that prevent LDL-receptor dissociation in the endosome — will reduce cytosolic cholesterol because LDL cannot be delivered to the lysosome and degraded.

7. How SARS-CoV-2 Hijacks the Trafficking Machinery (not tested in final exam)

  • SARS-CoV-2 structure — spherical coronavirus, diameter 80–120 nm; surface decorated with spike (S) glycoprotein (棘突蛋白) giving club-like projections; single-strand positive-sense RNA genome sharing ~80% identity with SARS-CoV
  • Viral structural proteins: Spike (S), Membrane (M), Envelope (E), Nucleocapsid (N)

    1. Lecture 14: Mechanisms of Cellular Homeostasis

Cells constantly produce proteins that must eventually be degraded — whether they are misfolded, damaged, short-lived regulators, or entire organelles that have become dysfunctional. Two major cellular machines handle this task: the proteasome, a barrel-shaped protease complex distributed throughout the nucleus and cytoplasm, and the lysosome, an acidic organelle that receives cargo via endocytosis, phagocytosis, and autophagy. A small protein tag called ubiquitin acts as the universal "expired" stamp that routes proteins to either destination. Failure of these systems leads to accumulation of toxic protein aggregates and underlies devastating neurodegenerative diseases such as Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and ALS.

1. Why Protein Degradation Matters

  • Quality control (品質管控) — destroy misfolded (錯誤折疊), aggregated (聚集), and damaged (受損) proteins before they harm the cell
  • Protein turn-over (蛋白質更新) — regular replacement of functional proteins; lifespan varies enormously by protein type and cellular context:
    • Structural proteins of bones and muscles — lifespan: months to years
    • Metabolic enzymes — lifespan: days to hours
    • Cell growth / division regulators — lifespan: seconds to minutes
  • Digest invading bacteria (入侵細菌) or damaged organelles (受損胞器) — immune cells engulf pathogens and deliver them to lysosomes

Consequences of failed protein degradation — neurodegenerative diseases (神經退化性疾病) caused by proteinaceous lesion (蛋白質病變) accumulation:

  • Alzheimer's disease (阿茲海默症) — Amyloid-β (澱粉樣蛋白-β) deposits spread progressively through the brain cortex
  • Parkinson's disease (帕金森症) — α-Synuclein (α-突觸核蛋白) inclusions (Lewy bodies 路易體); symptoms: tremor at rest (靜止性震顫), muscle rigidity (肌肉僵直), bradykinesia (運動遲緩), stooped posture, poor balance; famous patients: Muhammad Ali, Michael J. Fox
  • ALS — amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (肌萎縮性脊髓側索硬化症 / 漸凍人症) — TDP-43 inclusions spread along motor pathways; famously associated with Stephen Hawking

Exam trap: Option (b) in the lecture's practice question states "Protein degradation is NOT important for regular protein turn-over" — this is FALSE. Protein degradation IS essential for regular turn-over; choose it as the false statement.

2. Two Major Sites of Protein Degradation

  • Proteasome (蛋白酶體) — large protein complex; distributed throughout nucleus + cytoplasm; degrades misfolded and short-lived proteins
  • Lysosome (溶酶體) — intracellular organelle; distributed in cytoplasm; degrades proteins, bacteria, damaged organelles, viruses, and unhealthy cells

Protein quality control overview (Shahheydari et al., 2017):

  • Newly synthesised proteins enter the ER; chaperone proteins (伴隨蛋白) attempt correct folding
  • Correctly folded proteins → Golgi apparatus → secretion (ER/Golgi trafficking)
  • Misfolded proteins → three fates:
    • Ubiquitination (泛素化) → Proteasome (ERAD) or → Autophagolysosome (Macroautophagy)
    • Aggregation → Stress Granule (壓力顆粒)
    • Hsc70 recognition → Lysosome (Chaperone-mediated autophagy, CMA)

3. The Ubiquitin–Proteasome System

Ubiquitin (泛素, Ub) — the "expired" tag for protein degradation:

  • Small protein of 76 amino acids
  • Exists in all eukaryotic cells; highly conserved — human and yeast Ub share 96% sequence identity
  • Conjugated to a lysine (賴氨酸) residue on the target protein via an isopeptide bond (異肽鍵) (between Ub's C-terminal Gly and substrate Lys)
  • Contains 7 lysine residues itself (e.g., Lys48, Lys63) → Ub can be ubiquitinated → forms polyubiquitin chains (多聚泛素鏈)
  • Lys48-linked chains → proteasomal degradation; Lys63-linked → endocytic/lysosomal routing

The three-enzyme ubiquitination cascade (泛素化酶促級聯):

Proteasome (蛋白酶體) structure — the 26S proteasome:

  • 20S core particle (核心顆粒) — central cylinder containing proteolytic (蛋白水解) active sites; performs the actual cleavage
  • 19S regulatory cap (調節帽) — "unfoldase ring (解摺疊環)" at each end; contains:
    • Ubiquitin receptor — recognises polyubiquitinated protein
    • ATPase subunits — use ATP hydrolysis to unfold the target protein
    • Deubiquitinase (去泛素酶) — removes ubiquitin for recycling
  • Location — found throughout cytoplasm and nucleoplasm; concentrated at the nuclear envelope–ER network

Steps of proteasomal degradation:

  1. 19S cap ubiquitin receptor recognises the polyubiquitin chain on the target protein
  2. Deubiquitinase removes and recycles the Ub tags
  3. 19S ATPases use ATP hydrolysis to unfold the protein
  4. Unfolded protein is threaded through the narrow 20S cylinder
  5. Proteases in the 20S core cut the protein into small peptide fragments
  6. Peptide products exit and are further degraded to amino acids by cytosolic peptidases

Exam trap: Ubiquitin itself has 7 Lys residues and can itself be ubiquitinated — forming a chain. Do not confuse the E3 ligase (recognises the substrate) with E1 (activates Ub with ATP) or E2 (carries Ub). E3 does NOT use ATP directly.

4. The Lysosome and Delivery Pathways

Lysosome (溶酶體) — structure and composition:

  • Size: 0.2–0.5 μm diameter
  • Lumen pH: ~5.0 (acidic; cytosol pH ~7.2)
  • Contains >50 types of acid hydrolases (酸性水解酶) including: nucleases, proteases, glycosidases, lipases, phosphatases, sulfatases, phospholipases
  • Equivalents: vacuole (液泡) in yeast and plants

Lysosomal membrane proteins (溶酶體膜蛋白) — two key functions:

  • H⁺ pump (質子泵) — uses ATP hydrolysis to pump H⁺ into the lumen → maintains pH ~5.0
  • Transport proteins (轉運蛋白) — export final digestion products (amino acids, nucleotides) to the cytosol
  • Both pumps and transporters are heavily glycosylated (高度糖基化) on the luminal face to protect them from the very proteases inside

  • Ubiquitinated membrane receptors (e.g., receptor tyrosine kinases) are sorted into the MVB (multivesicular body 多囊體) pathway
  • ESCRT complexes (ESCRT-I, -II, -III) recognise Ub tags and sort cargo into intraluminal vesicles

Exam trap: The lysosome receives cargo from multiple routes — do not say only "endocytosis." Autophagy, phagocytosis, and the secretory pathway are equally valid delivery routes. The H⁺ pump uses ATP; if ATP is depleted the lysosome acidification fails.

5. Autophagy — Three Types and Macroautophagy in Detail

Autophagy (自噬 / "self-eating") — discovered by Yoshinori Ohsumi (Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, 2016):

  • Quality control — remove damaged organelles and large protein complexes
  • Reconstruction — normal cell growth and development
  • Adaptive stress response — starvation, infection

Three principal types of autophagy (自噬的三種類型):

Process of macroautophagy (巨自噬過程) — 4 key steps:

  1. Vesicle nucleation (囊泡成核) — isolation membrane (隔離膜) / phagophore (吞噬泡) forms; requires PI3P (phosphatidylinositol-3-phosphate) and ATG proteins
  2. Vesicle elongation (囊泡延伸) — phagophore expands and curves around cargo (damaged mitochondria, aggregated proteins, pathogens); ATG8/LC3 coats the membrane
  3. Docking and fusion (停靠與融合) — completed autophagosome (雙層膜囊泡) fuses with lysosome/vacuole → autolysosome (自溶酶體); machinery: Rab GTPase Ypt7, GEF Mon1-Ccz1, tethering complex HOPS, SNAREs (Ykt6)
  4. Vesicle breakdown and degradation (囊泡分解與降解) — lysosomal hydrolases digest the inner autophagosomal membrane and cargo → metabolites recycled to cytosol

Autophagosome–vacuole/lysosome fusion machinery (in yeast model):

  • Atg8 (LC3 in mammals) — autophagosome membrane marker; recruits Mon1-Ccz1 complex
  • Mon1-Ccz1 — GEF (鳥嘌呤核苷酸交換因子) that activates Rab GTPase Ypt7 (mammal: Rab7)
  • Ypt7-GTP — binds HOPS tethering complex on the vacuole
  • HOPS — tethers (拴繫) the autophagosome to the vacuole; assembles SNARE proteins
  • SNAREs (Ykt6 and others) — drive membrane fusion (膜融合)
  • HOPS mutants: autophagosome accumulates outside the vacuole (shown by Atg8 dots not entering the vacuole lumen)

Exam trap: "Autophagy" alone usually means macroautophagy in exam questions. CMA uses the Hsc70 chaperone and LAMP-2A channel — it does NOT form an autophagosome. Microautophagy is direct engulfment by the lysosomal membrane.

6. Autophagy and Cancer — a Double-Edged Role

  • Autophagy acts as a tumour suppressor (腫瘤抑制因子) — removes damaged organelles/proteins that could trigger malignant transformation (惡性轉化); prevents tumour initiation
  • Autophagy also acts as a survival pathway (存活途徑) — established tumour cells use autophagy to endure metabolic stress and resist death by chemotherapeutic agents (化療藥物)
  • Key protein: Beclin1 (貝克林1) — core autophagy regulator; functions in vesicle nucleation; acts as a tumour suppressor

Experimental evidence — Liang et al., Nature 1999 (MCF7 breast carcinoma cells):

  • MCF7 cells lacking Beclin1 (no autophagy): under starvation, cells survive without forming autophagic vacuoles; when injected into mice (orthotopic injection 原位注射), ~89% (8/9) of mice grew tumours by day 5; high tumour incidence at days 7, 37, 38
  • MCF7 cells stably transfected with Beclin1 (autophagy restored): starvation induces significantly higher level of autophagic vacuoles; tumour incidence reduced when compared to control
  • Conclusion: defects in autophagy (失能自噬) promote tumour growth; Beclin1 is a haploinsufficient tumour suppressor

Exam trap: Autophagy is a context-dependent process. In early tumourigenesis it is tumour-suppressive; in established tumours it is pro-survival. Do not state simply that "autophagy prevents cancer" — both effects exist.

Lecture 15: The Cytoskeleton and Cell Movement-I

The cytoskeleton (細胞骨架) is an intricate network of protein filaments that extends throughout the cytoplasm of eukaryotic cells, providing structural support, shape, and the machinery for intracellular transport and cell movement. Three distinct filament systems — intermediate filaments, microtubules, and actin filaments — each contribute unique mechanical and functional properties. Motor proteins (馬達蛋白) such as kinesin and dynein walk along microtubule tracks to ferry cargo across the cell, while specialized microtubule arrays in cilia and flagella generate coordinated beating motions essential for mucus clearance and sperm motility.

1. Overview of the Three Cytoskeletal Filaments

  • Cytoskeleton (細胞骨架) — an intricate network of protein filaments extending throughout the cytoplasm; found in all eukaryotic cells
  • Three principal filament types in multicellular eukaryotes:
    FilamentDiameterSubunitKey location/function
    Intermediate filaments (中間纖維)~10 nmFibrous proteins (e.g. keratin, lamin)Mechanical strength; nuclear lamina
    Microtubules (微管)~25 nmα/β-tubulin heterodimerTransport highways; mitotic spindle; cilia
    Actin filaments (肌動蛋白絲)~7 nmG-actin monomerCell cortex; cell motility; muscle contraction
  • Scale bar for all three filament types in cells: ~25 µm (cell level); ~25 nm (filament level)
  • All three are dynamic — they can polymerize and depolymerize in response to cell signals

Exam trap: The cytoskeleton is present only in eukaryotes; bacteria (e.g. E. coli) lack true intermediate filaments and microtubules. Do not confuse filament diameter: microtubules (~25 nm) are the largest, actin (~7 nm) is the smallest, intermediate filaments (~10 nm) are in between.

2. Intermediate Filaments: Structure, Assembly, and Function

  • Intermediate filaments (中間纖維, IF) — rope-shaped filaments ~10 nm in diameter formed by lateral bundling of fibrous protein subunits; no nucleotide binding or hydrolysis required for assembly
  • Assembly — subunits wind together into coiled-coils (螺旋捲曲), then laterally bundle into the characteristic "rope shape" visible by EM
  • Polarity — IFs are non-polar (無極性); unlike actin and microtubules, they have no plus or minus end
  • Primary function — mechanical strength; enable cells to withstand mechanical stress (機械應力) when stretched
  • Anchorage at desmosomes (橋粒):
    • IFs are anchored to the plasma membrane at desmosomes (cell-cell junctions)
    • This links the IF networks of adjacent cells, distributing tensile forces across a tissue
    • Also anchored at hemidesmosomes (半橋粒) to the basal lamina (基底膜)
  • Mechanical stress experiment: a sheet of cells with IFs remains intact when stretched; the same sheet without IFs ruptures
  • Nuclear lamina (核纖層) — a specialized IF meshwork 10–40 nm thick on the nucleoplasmic surface of the inner nuclear membrane; composed of lamins (核纖層蛋白); provides structural integrity and mechanical stability to the nucleus
  • Disease: Epidermolysis bullosa simplex (大皰性表皮松解症) — mutations in keratin (角蛋白) genes → defective keratin filament network in basal cells of epidermis → blistered skin (水疱) upon mechanical friction; hemidesmosomes fail to anchor basal cells to basal lamina
  • Around 30 different diseases are associated with mutations in IF protein genes

Exam trap: Intermediate filaments do NOT use ATP or GTP for assembly, and they are non-polar — motor proteins cannot walk along them. Keratin mutations cause blistered skin, not muscle disease; confusing keratin with other IF types is a common error.

3. Microtubules: Structure, Polarity, and Dynamic Instability

  • Microtubule (微管) — hollow cylindrical tube ~25 nm in diameter; wall formed by 13 protofilaments (原纖絲) arranged in parallel
  • Subunit — α/β-tubulin heterodimer (管蛋白異二聚體); the basic building block added during polymerization
  • Polarity — microtubules are polar (有極性):
    • Plus end (正端) — fast-growing end; β-tubulin exposed; points toward cell periphery in interphase
    • Minus end (負端) — slow-growing end; α-tubulin exposed; anchored at MTOC (e.g. centrosome) in interphase
  • GTP cap (GTP帽) — growing microtubules maintain a cap of GTP-tubulin dimers at the plus end; GTP on β-tubulin is exchangeable; GTP on α-tubulin is non-exchangeable

  • Dynamic instability (動態不穩定性) — a single microtubule end oscillates stochastically between growing and shrinking states, allowing rapid exploration of cellular space
  • Catastrophe (崩塌):
    • If GTP hydrolysis proceeds faster than tubulin addition, the GTP cap is lost
    • GDP-tubulin (GDP管蛋白) in the lattice is curved and destabilized → rapid depolymerization (shrinking)
  • Rescue (拯救):
    • GTP-tubulin subunits may still add to a shrinking end
    • If enough GTP-tubulin accumulates to re-form a cap, growth resumes
  • Functional significance — dynamic instability allows cells to build microtubule arrays with specific shapes by selectively stabilizing or destabilizing microtubules at particular locations
  • GDP-only conditions → no GTP cap possible → microtubules shrink; non-hydrolyzable GTP analog → permanent cap → stable, continuously growing microtubules

Exam trap: It is the β-tubulin subunit that carries exchangeable GTP — not α-tubulin. If only GDP is present (no GTP), microtubules cannot maintain a cap and will shrink. If a non-hydrolyzable GTP analog is used, no catastrophe can occur and microtubules grow continuously.

4. Microtubule Nucleation: MTOC, Centrosome, and γ-TuRC

  • MTOC (microtubule organizing center, 微管組織中心) — any structure that nucleates microtubule assembly; controls the number, polarity, and location of microtubules
  • Centrosome (中心體) — the main MTOC in animal cells; located near the nucleus; consists of:
    • A pair of centrioles (中心粒) — short cylindrical structures built from triplet microtubules
    • Pericentriolar matrix (centrosome matrix) — protein-rich cloud surrounding the centrioles; contains nucleating sites
  • Nucleating sites — γ-tubulin ring complexes (γ-TuRC, γ-管蛋白環狀複合體) embedded in the centrosome matrix
  • γ-TuRC assembly:
    • γ-TuSC (γ-tubulin small complex, γ-管蛋白小複合體) — formed by 2 copies of γ-tubulin + a pair of accessory proteins
    • Seven copies of γ-TuSC assemble into a spiral structure exposing 13 γ-tubulin subunits in a circular orientation matching the 13 protofilaments of a microtubule
    • This "template" nucleates polymerization, skipping the rate-limiting nucleation step that free tubulin requires
  • Minus end orientation — the minus end of every microtubule is anchored at the centrosome; plus ends radiate outward toward the cell periphery
  • Interphase organization — microtubules radiate from the centrosome near the nucleus, reaching the cell cortex (plus ends marked +)
  • Mitosis organization — centrosomes duplicate and separate to form the two poles of the mitotic spindle (有絲分裂紡錘體); kinetochore microtubules attach to chromosomes at their plus ends

Exam trap: γ-tubulin (gamma-管蛋白) is NOT incorporated into the microtubule lattice — it forms the ring template at the minus end only. The minus end stays buried in the centrosome; only the plus end grows outward. Confusing γ-TuSC with γ-TuRC: γ-TuSC is the small subunit; 7 × γ-TuSC = one γ-TuRC.

5. Motor Proteins: Kinesin and Dynein

  • Motor proteins (馬達蛋白) — proteins that use ATP hydrolysis to move along cytoskeletal tracks; both kinesin and dynein walk along microtubules using their globular head domains
  • Structure — each motor has:
    • Globular head (球形頭部) — ATPase domain that contacts the microtubule
    • Tail domain (尾部) — binds cargo (vesicles, organelles, chromosomes, etc.)
  • Kinesin (驅動蛋白):
    • Moves toward the plus end (positive-end directed) — i.e., from centrosome toward cell periphery in interphase
    • Anterograde transport (順向運輸) of vesicles and organelles away from the cell body
  • Dynein (動力蛋白):
    • Moves toward the minus end (negative-end directed) — i.e., from cell periphery toward centrosome
    • Retrograde transport (逆向運輸) of vesicles and organelles back toward the cell body
  • Different kinesin family members and dyneins transport different cargo types (vesicles, mitochondria, melanosomes, chromosomes) along the same microtubule highway
  • Melanosome (黑色素體) movement example — melanosomes disperse outward (kinesin, plus-end directed) or aggregate centrally (dynein, minus-end directed), producing pigment distribution changes in fish skin cells

Exam trap: Kinesin moves to the plus end (periphery); dynein moves to the minus end (centrosome). A common mistake is reversing these. Note that some kinesin family members are minus-end directed — conventional kinesin-1 is the canonical plus-end motor.

6. Cilia and Flagella: Structure, Function, and Disease

  • Cilia (纖毛) and flagella (鞭毛) — microtubule-based organelles that project from the cell surface; powered by axonemal dynein (軸絲動力蛋白)
  • Motile vs immobile:
    • Motile cilia — back-and-forth beating; move fluid (e.g., mucus in respiratory tract) or propel cells
    • Flagella — propeller-like rotation; drive sperm motility; longer and fewer per cell than cilia
    • Immobile (primary) cilia — act as the cell's antenna (細胞天線); contain receptors in their membrane/axoneme; transduce environmental signals (e.g. renal cilia sense urine flow; olfactory cilia sense odorants)
  • Axoneme (軸絲) structure — the core of a cilium/flagellum:
    • 9+2 arrangement: 9 outer doublet microtubules (雙聯微管) surrounding 2 central singlet microtubules
    • Each doublet has outer dynein arms and inner dynein arms projecting toward neighboring doublets
  • Basal body (基體):
    • At the base of every cilium; structurally identical to a centriole (9 triplet microtubules)
    • Acts as the MTOC for ciliary axoneme assembly; transition fibers and terminal plate connect basal body to the ciliary membrane
  • Mechanism of bending (how dynein causes flagellum to bend):
    • In isolated doublets + ATP: axonemal dynein slides adjacent doublets relative to each other (microtubule sliding, 微管滑動)
    • In intact flagellum: linking proteins (connexin-like) resist sliding → force is converted to bending (彎曲) of the entire axoneme
  • Primary ciliary dyskinesia (PCD, 原發性纖毛運動障礙) — caused by defects in cilia lining respiratory tract:
    • ~30 different diseases caused by cilia defects
    • Symptoms: reduced mucus clearance from lungs, chronic recurrent respiratory infections, nasal/sinus problems, infertility (male and female)
    • Affected organs: nasal cavity/sinuses, trachea, bronchial tubes, lungs, middle ear, reproductive system

Exam trap: The basal body and centriole have the same 9-triplet microtubule structure; the axoneme has a 9+2 arrangement (doublets, not triplets). Dynein in cilia/flagella is axonemal dynein — separate from cytoplasmic dynein that transports cargo. Sliding without linking proteins produces only translation, not bending; linking proteins are required to convert sliding into bending.
Exam Traps Summary

  • Filament diameter order: actin (~7 nm) < intermediate filaments (~10 nm) < microtubules (~25 nm).
  • Intermediate filaments are non-polar and do not use ATP/GTP for assembly; motor proteins cannot walk on them.
  • Keratin gene mutations → Epidermolysis bullosa simplex (blistered skin); the problem is at hemidesmosomes, not desmosomes between cells.
  • Nuclear lamina is made of lamin (a type of IF), not actin or tubulin.
  • Exchangeable GTP binds β-tubulin only; α-tubulin GTP is non-exchangeable and irrelevant to dynamic instability.
  • GDP-only solution → microtubule shrinkage; non-hydrolyzable GTP analog → permanent cap → stable growth.
  • γ-tubulin forms the ring template at the minus end but does NOT enter the microtubule lattice; 7 × γ-TuSC = 1 γ-TuRC = 13 γ-tubulin subunits.
  • Kinesin moves to the plus end (periphery); cytoplasmic dynein moves to the minus end (centrosome).
  • Axoneme = 9+2 doublet arrangement; basal body = 9 triplet microtubules; confusing the two is a common error.
  • Dynein sliding alone (isolated doublets) causes translational sliding; linking proteins convert this to bending of the cilium/flagellum.

Lecture 16: The Cytoskeleton and Cell Movement-II

This lecture focuses on actin filaments as the third major cytoskeletal component, covering their structure, assembly dynamics, and the actin-binding proteins that regulate them. It then traces how actin polymerization drives cell migration through lamellipodia and filopodia, and how myosin motor proteins interact with actin to power contraction. The lecture concludes with the molecular events of skeletal muscle contraction, from sarcomere organization to calcium-triggered sliding filament mechanics controlled by the troponin-tropomyosin system.

1. Actin Filament Structure and Assembly Dynamics

  • 肌動蛋白絲 (actin filament) / microfilament (微絲) — thinnest cytoskeletal element; diameter ~7 nm; visible at 25 nm scale bar under EM
  • G-actin (球狀肌動蛋白, globular actin) — cytosolic monomeric form; each monomer binds one ATP; asymmetric shape gives polarity upon polymerization (head-to-tail assembly)
  • F-actin (絲狀肌動蛋白, filamentous actin) — double-stranded helical polymer; helical repeat ~37 nm; two strands wind around each other
  • Plus end (正端) — faster-growing barbed end; minus end (負端) — slower-growing pointed end; polarity is structurally fixed by asymmetric monomer orientation
  • Nucleation (核化) — rate-limiting step; unstable oligomeric nucleus must form before rapid elongation begins; spontaneous nucleation in the cell is slow without nucleation factors
  • ATP hydrolysis: G-actin binds ATP; upon incorporation into filament, ATP is hydrolyzed to ADP + Pi; ADP-actin subunits have weaker inter-subunit bonds and are prone to disassembly

Three kinetic regimes governed by relative rates of monomer addition vs. ATP hydrolysis:

  • Addition at plus end > addition at minus end > ATP hydrolysis rate → both ends grow (filament elongates)
  • ATP hydrolysis rate > addition at plus end > addition at minus end → both ends shrink (filament depolymerizes)
  • Addition at plus end > ATP hydrolysis rate > addition at minus end → treadmilling (踏車運動): ATP-actin adds to plus end; ADP-actin falls off minus end; net filament length is constant but subunits translocate through the filament from plus to minus end

Exam trap: ATP hydrolysis does NOT provide energy for actin polymerization — it weakens bonds to promote depolymerization and enable treadmilling. This parallels GTP hydrolysis in tubulin. A statement saying "ATP hydrolysis promotes actin polymerization" is FALSE.

3. Actin-Based Cell Migration

  • Lamellipodium (片足) — flat, broad protrusion at leading edge; contains branched actin web nucleated by Arp2/3; plus ends face the plasma membrane and polymerize to push it forward
  • Filopodium (絲足) — thin, finger-like projection extending from lamellipodium; contains parallel actin bundles (nucleated by formin); acts as a chemosensory probe
  • Actin cortex (肌動蛋白皮質層) — dense actin meshwork lining the inner plasma membrane; maintained under tension by myosin-II
  • Focal adhesion (黏著斑) / focal contact — integrin (整合素)-containing anchor linking actin cytoskeleton to extracellular matrix; transmits traction force; essential for directed cell movement

Four-step cell crawling cycle:

  1. Protrusion: Arp2/3-driven actin polymerization at plus ends pushes lamellipodium forward
  2. Attachment: Lamellipodium adheres to substratum via integrins forming focal contacts
  3. Contraction: Myosin-II in actin cortex slides actin filaments; cell body moves forward; unpolymerized actin flows toward leading edge for reuse
  4. Retraction: Rear focal adhesions release; trailing edge retracts; cycle repeats

  • Intracellular bacteria such as Listeria monocytogenes hijack host actin machinery to propel themselves through the cytoplasm
  • Bacterial surface protein ActA activates Arp2/3 at one bacterial pole; branched actin assembles as an actin "comet tail" with plus ends pressing against the bacterium
  • Cofilin depolymerizes older (ADP-actin) regions at the minus ends; capping protein stabilizes plus ends of lateral branches — net polymerization at the bacterial pole generates propulsive force
  • Key conclusion: actin polymerization alone, without myosin, can produce directional force sufficient to move objects

Exam trap: Listeria actin-based motility does not require myosin; the propulsive force is entirely from actin polymerization. Do not apply myosin-based logic to this system.

4. Myosin Motor Proteins on Actin

  • Myosin (肌球蛋白) — actin-based motor; moves toward the plus (+) end of actin filaments; ATPase activity in head domain drives conformational change (power stroke, 動力衝程)
  • Myosin-I (肌球蛋白-I) — single-headed; length ~70 nm; tail attaches to membranes or vesicles; transports cargo (e.g., vesicles) along actin toward the plus end; can also move actin filaments relative to a surface
  • Myosin-II (肌球蛋白-II) — double-headed; length ~150 nm; two heavy chains form coiled-coil tail; two globular head domains each bind actin and hydrolyze ATP; the motor for muscle contraction and cytokinesis

  • Myosin-II self-assembles into bipolar filaments: molecules associate tail-to-tail; heads point outward at both ends; central bare region (裸區) ~0.2 µm contains only tails; total bipolar filament length ~1 µm
  • Sliding mechanism: heads on one side of the bipolar filament walk along one actin filament (pulling it toward center); heads on the other side walk along an antiparallel actin filament (pulling it toward the opposite center) — the two actin filaments slide past each other, shortening the distance between their anchoring Z discs
  • In non-muscle cells: myosin-II generates tension in the actin cortex; contractile ring (收縮環) containing myosin-II pinches the cell during cytokinesis

Exam trap: Myosin moves toward the plus end of actin. In the sarcomere, the plus ends of thin filaments are anchored at Z discs; myosin heads pull actin inward (toward the center), shortening the sarcomere. The filaments do not shorten — the sarcomere does.

5. Skeletal Muscle Structure: From Fiber to Sarcomere

  • Muscle fiber (肌纖維) / skeletal muscle cell — multinucleate; innervated by a motor neuron (運動神經元) at the neuromuscular junction
  • Myofibril (肌原纖維) — cylindrical bundle of sarcomeres running the length of the cell; many myofibrils pack the cell in parallel; responsible for the striated (條紋) appearance
  • Sarcomere (肌節) — fundamental contractile unit; length ~2.2 µm at rest; bounded by Z discs on both sides

  • Z disc (Z盤) — protein disc at each sarcomere boundary; anchors plus ends of thin filaments from adjacent sarcomeres; defines the sarcomere border
  • Thin filament (細肌絲) — actin filament + tropomyosin + troponin complex; plus end anchored at Z disc; minus end points toward sarcomere center
  • Thick filament (粗肌絲) — bipolar myosin-II filament (~1 µm); located in center of sarcomere; bare region at center where no heads are present
  • Overlap region (重疊區) — zone where thick and thin filaments interdigitate; cross-bridges form here
  • During contraction: thick filaments stay fixed in position; thin filaments slide inward; Z discs move closer; sarcomere shortens

  • T tubule (橫管, transverse tubule) — deep invagination of plasma membrane; lumen is continuous with extracellular space; conducts action potential rapidly to the interior of the large muscle cell; runs perpendicular to myofibrils
  • Sarcoplasmic reticulum (肌漿網, SR) — specialized smooth ER ensheathing each myofibril; Ca²⁺ storage organelle; membrane contains Ca²⁺-ATPase pumps to reload Ca²⁺ after contraction
  • T tubule and SR are closely apposed with a ~35 nm gap; voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel (dihydropyridine receptor, DHPR) in T-tubule membrane is physically coupled to the Ca²⁺-release channel (ryanodine receptor, RyR, 蘭尼鹼受體) in the SR membrane
  • Simultaneous contraction of all sarcomeres in the fiber is ensured because T tubules conduct the action potential to every sarcomere at the same moment

Exam trap: The lumen of T tubules is extracellular space, not cytosol. The Ca²⁺ that triggers contraction comes from the sarcoplasmic reticulum (into cytosol), not from the T tubule lumen. These two spaces are physically adjacent but functionally distinct.

6. Calcium Regulation and the Sliding Filament Mechanism

Excitation-contraction coupling (興奮-收縮耦聯) — step by step:

  1. Motor neuron fires → action potential propagates along muscle cell plasma membrane
  2. Action potential travels down T tubule membrane; DHPR (voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channel in T-tubule) changes conformation
  3. DHPR physically gates RyR (Ca²⁺-release channel) in SR membrane across the 35 nm gap; Ca²⁺ released from SR lumen into cytosol
  4. Cytosolic [Ca²⁺] rises → Ca²⁺ binds troponin C (旋轉肌鈣蛋白C) of the troponin complex on thin filament → conformational change in troponin I → tropomyosin shifts ~1–1.5 nm laterally in actin groove → myosin-binding sites on actin exposed
  5. Myosin heads bind exposed actin sites; ATP-driven cross-bridge cycling begins; thin filaments slide inward; sarcomere shortens; muscle contracts
  6. Relaxation: SR Ca²⁺-ATPase pumps Ca²⁺ back into SR lumen; cytosolic [Ca²⁺] falls; troponin reverts; tropomyosin re-covers binding sites; cross-bridges cannot form; muscle relaxes

  • Tropomyosin (原肌球蛋白) — rod-shaped coiled-coil protein; runs along the groove of the actin double helix; one tropomyosin spans ~7 actin monomers (~10 nm); in resting muscle physically blocks myosin-binding sites
  • Troponin complex (旋轉肌鈣蛋白複合體) — three subunits, one complex per tropomyosin molecule:
    • Troponin I (抑制亞基) — inhibitory subunit; holds tropomyosin in blocking position in the absence of Ca²⁺
    • Troponin C (鈣結合亞基) — Ca²⁺-binding subunit; binds Ca²⁺; conformational change pulls troponin I away from tropomyosin
    • Troponin T (原肌球蛋白結合亞基) — anchors entire troponin complex to tropomyosin along the filament
  • Result of Ca²⁺ binding: troponin C grips troponin I; troponin I releases tropomyosin; tropomyosin shifts; myosin-binding sites on actin exposed; contraction proceeds
  • Without Ca²⁺: tropomyosin blocks myosin; no cross-bridges even if ATP is present

Three cytoskeletal filaments — comparative summary:

Filament Diameter Subunit / nucleotide Motor Key roles
Intermediate filaments (中間絲) ~10 nm Keratins, lamins, vimentin; no nucleotide None Mechanical strength, nuclear lamina
Microtubules (微管) ~25 nm α/β-tubulin heterodimer; GTP Kinesin, dynein Intracellular transport, mitosis, cilia/flagella
Actin filaments (肌動蛋白絲) ~7 nm G-actin monomer; ATP Myosin Migration, muscle contraction, cytokinesis, microvilli

Exam trap: Not all eukaryotic cells have intermediate filaments — but all have actin and microtubules. The cytoskeleton is dynamic, not rigid or permanent. The three filament systems collaborate, not act independently.
Exam Traps Summary

  • Treadmilling condition: plus-end addition rate > ATP hydrolysis rate > minus-end addition rate. Both other conditions either grow or shrink both ends.
  • Formin: linear filaments, stays at plus end. Arp2/3: branched filaments, stays at minus end of branch. Do not swap.
  • Listeria / bacterial actin motility uses Arp2/3 and cofilin but no myosin — pure polymerization force.
  • T tubule lumen = extracellular space. Ca²⁺ released into cytosol comes from SR lumen, not T tubule lumen.
  • Tropomyosin physically blocks myosin-binding sites on actin. Troponin C is the Ca²⁺ sensor that moves tropomyosin. Ca²⁺ does not directly bind actin.
  • Sliding filament model: filaments themselves do not shorten — they slide past each other; the sarcomere length decreases.
  • Sarcomere length at rest ≈ 2.2 µm. Plus ends of thin filaments are at Z discs; minus ends point inward toward sarcomere center.

  2. Plus end grows faster than minus end; treadmilling = ATP-actin added at plus, ADP-actin lost at minus, net length constant

  4. Thymosin sequesters monomers; profilin speeds plus-end elongation; cofilin depolymerizes ADP-actin regions; gelsolin severs and caps plus end; tropomodulin caps minus end; tropomyosin stabilizes filament side
  5. Lamellipodia (Arp2/3, branched actin, flat sheet) vs. filopodia (formin, parallel bundles, thin spike); plus ends face membrane
  6. Four-step crawling cycle: protrusion (actin polymerization) → attachment (integrins/focal adhesions) → contraction (myosin-II) → retraction

Lecture 17: Mechanisms of Cellular Communication-I

Cells constantly exchange information to coordinate survival, growth, differentiation, and death. This lecture establishes the conceptual framework of signal transduction (訊號傳導): how an extracellular chemical signal is received by a target cell, relayed and amplified through intracellular signaling cascades, and converted into a specific cellular response. Four modes of intercellular communication are distinguished by distance, and three classes of cell-surface receptors are introduced alongside intracellular receptors used by lipid-soluble hormones. The HIPPO pathway provides a concrete example of how signaling controls organ size in vivo.

1. Why Cell Communication Matters and Four Modes of Signaling

  • Cell communication (細胞通訊) is required for: sensing and responding to the environment (環境), sending orders to other cells, wound healing (傷口癒合), and developmental patterning (發育模式形成).
  • Even unicellular organisms use it: yeast (酵母菌) mating-type cells a and α respond to each other's mating factor (交配因子), elongating toward the partner before fusing.
  • Signal transduction (訊號傳導) — extracellular signals are converted to intracellular responses; an extracellular signal molecule A binds a receptor on the target cell, triggering intracellular signaling molecule B, which produces a response.

Contact-dependent signaling (接觸依賴性訊號傳遞)

  • Signaling cell displays a membrane-bound signal molecule (膜結合訊號分子) that binds a receptor on the directly adjacent target cell; no secreted ligand involved.
  • Example: Delta–Notch signaling (側向抑制 lateral inhibition) — one epithelial cell expressing the inhibitory signal protein Delta activates Notch on all neighbours, suppressing them from adopting the same fate; result: cell specialization from a field of equivalent cells.

Paracrine signaling (旁分泌訊號傳遞) — short distance

  • Signal molecules diffuse locally, remaining in the neighbourhood of the secreting cell; these signals are called local mediators (局部介質).
  • Functions: regulate inflammation (發炎) at an infection site; control cell proliferation (細胞增殖) in a healing wound.
  • Autocrine signaling (自分泌) is a variant where the cell responds to its own secreted signal.

Endocrine signaling (內分泌訊號傳遞) — long distance

  • Endocrine cells secrete hormones (激素) into the bloodstream (血液循環); signal reaches distant target cells that carry the matching receptor.
  • Classic example: the pancreas (胰臟) secretes insulin (胰島素) into the blood; insulin binds its receptor on cells throughout the body, opening glucose channels (葡萄糖通道) and stimulating glucose uptake (葡萄糖攝取).
  • Slow to reach targets but affects many cell types simultaneously.

Synaptic signaling (突觸訊號傳遞) — long distance, high specificity

  • A neuron (神經元) transmits an electrical signal along its axon (軸突) to the nerve terminal (神經末梢), where it triggers release of neurotransmitters (神經傳遞物質) into the synapse (突觸).
  • Neurotransmitters diffuse across the narrow synaptic cleft and bind receptors on the target cell; delivery is fast and spatially precise — "private lines."

Exam trap: Paracrine is NOT the same as endocrine. Paracrine signals stay local (no bloodstream entry); endocrine hormones travel via the bloodstream to distant targets. Do not write "paracrine = hormone."
Exam trap: Delta–Notch is contact-dependent, not paracrine. Delta is membrane-bound and never secreted — physical cell-to-cell contact is required.

2. Three Phases of Signal Transduction

  • Reception (接收) — the target cell detects the extracellular signal; a receptor protein (受體蛋白) binds the signal molecule with high specificity (特異性).
  • Transduction (轉導) — conversion of the extracellular signal into a form capable of producing a specific cellular response; involves a cascade of intracellular relay molecules (接力分子).
  • Response (反應) — the specific cellular outcome: altered metabolism (代謝), altered cell shape or movement, or altered gene expression (基因表現).
  • Relay molecules form a signal transduction pathway (訊號傳導路徑) that connects receptor activation to effector proteins (效應蛋白): metabolic enzymes, cytoskeletal proteins, or transcription regulators (轉錄調節因子).
  • Pathway functions during transduction: relay (接力), amplify (放大), integrate (整合), distribute (分配), feedback control (回饋控制).

Speed of response depends on mechanism:

  • Fast responses (< sec to mins) — signal activates pre-existing proteins directly, altering their function without new gene expression; examples: muscle contraction (肌肉收縮), secretion of digestive enzymes.
  • Slow responses (mins to hrs) — signal travels to nucleus, alters transcription (轉錄), new proteins synthesized; examples: cell growth (細胞生長), cell differentiation (細胞分化).

Same signal, different responses:

  • The same signal molecule (e.g., acetylcholine 乙醯膽鹼) produces different responses in different cell types, determined by which receptors and intracellular pathways are present.
  • Heart pacemaker cell (心臟起搏細胞): acetylcholine → decreased rate of firing (放電頻率降低).
  • Salivary gland cell (唾液腺細胞): acetylcholine → secretion (分泌).
  • Skeletal muscle cell (骨骼肌細胞): acetylcholine → contraction (收縮).

Signal combinations determine fate:

  • Different combinations (組合) of extracellular signals cause different target cell responses: survive, grow and divide (生長與分裂), differentiate (分化), or undergo apoptosis (細胞凋亡).
  • A cell integrates inputs from multiple pathways simultaneously before committing to a response.

Exam trap: "Transduction" is not just relaying the signal — it specifically means converting it into a form that drives a response. Students often conflate transduction with reception.
Exam trap: Fast responses alter existing protein function; slow responses require new protein synthesis via altered gene expression. These are mechanistically distinct — do not describe both as "signal transduction" without specifying pathway type.

3. Reception: Intracellular vs. Cell-Surface Receptors

  • Extracellular signal molecules bind either to cell-surface receptors (細胞表面受體) or to intracellular receptors (細胞內受體), depending on whether they can cross the plasma membrane (質膜).
  • Cell-surface receptors: used by large, charged, or hydrophilic (親水性) signal molecules that cannot cross the bilayer; binding triggers intracellular signaling molecules inside the cytosol.
  • Intracellular receptors: used by small, hydrophobic (疏水性) signal molecules (e.g., steroid hormones 類固醇激素) that diffuse freely through the membrane.

Cortisol (皮質醇) — intracellular receptor example:

  • Cortisol is a steroid hormone; it crosses the plasma membrane freely due to its hydrophobic nature.
  • Binds the nuclear receptor protein (核受體蛋白) in the cytosol; binding causes a conformational change (構形改變) that activates the receptor.
  • The activated receptor-cortisol complex translocates (移位) into the nucleus.
  • Complex binds the regulatory region (調控區) of target genes and activates transcription (轉錄).
  • Outcome: altered metabolism of proteins, carbohydrates, and lipids in most tissues — a slow, genome-level response.

Exam trap: Intracellular receptors are inside the cell but the signal molecule itself comes from outside — students mistakenly call the receptor "extracellular." The receptor is intracellular; the ligand (配體) crosses the membrane.
Exam trap: Cortisol's receptor is described as a "nuclear receptor" but it is found in the cytosol before ligand binding — it only moves to the nucleus after forming the active complex. Do not say the receptor starts in the nucleus.

4. Three Classes of Cell-Surface Receptors

Receptor Class Mechanism Speed Example
Ion-channel-coupled (離子通道耦聯受體) Signal binding opens an ion channel (離子通道) in the receptor protein itself; ions flow down electrochemical gradient (電化學梯度) Very fast (ms) Neuromuscular junction acetylcholine receptor
G-protein-coupled (G蛋白耦聯受體, GPCR) Signal activates receptor → receptor activates trimeric G protein (三聚體G蛋白); G protein exchanges GDP for GTP (鳥苷三磷酸) and releases active subunit that triggers downstream effectors Fast–moderate Adrenaline receptor, odorant receptors
Enzyme-coupled (酶耦聯受體) Signal (often a dimer 二聚體) causes receptor dimerization (二聚化), activating an intrinsic catalytic domain (催化結構域) or recruiting an associated enzyme; commonly receptor tyrosine kinases (受體酪氨酸激酶) Moderate–slow Insulin receptor, growth factor receptors

  • Ion-channel-coupled receptors (離子通道耦聯受體): the receptor protein itself forms the channel; ligand (配體) binding causes conformational change (構形改變) that opens the pore, allowing ions (e.g., Na⁺, Ca²⁺) to enter the cytosol and immediately change membrane potential (膜電位) or trigger downstream effects.
  • GPCRs: 7 transmembrane-helix (跨膜螺旋) receptor; signal activates the coupled G protein, converting it from GDP-bound (inactive) to GTP-bound (active); GTP hydrolysis (水解) returns G protein to OFF state — an intrinsic molecular timer.
  • Enzyme-coupled receptors: two main subtypes: (a) receptor with intrinsic catalytic domain — dimerization activates the kinase domain; (b) receptor associated with a separate enzyme that becomes activated upon ligand binding.

Exam trap: GPCRs do NOT have intrinsic kinase activity — the G protein is the switch, not a kinase. Enzyme-coupled receptors are kinases. Students frequently swap these two classes.
Exam trap: For enzyme-coupled receptors, the signal molecule often acts as a dimer to cross-link two receptor monomers; a monomer signal cannot activate the receptor — specificity depends on this dimerization requirement.

5. Intracellular Signaling: Molecular Switches, Relay, and Feedback

Molecular switches (分子開關):

  • Two main switch mechanisms exist in intracellular signaling proteins:
  • Phosphorylation switch (磷酸化開關): protein kinase (蛋白激酶) transfers phosphate from ATP to the target protein (ON state); protein phosphatase (蛋白磷酸酶) removes the phosphate, returning to OFF state. Reversible and fast.
  • GTP-binding switch (GTP結合開關): GTP binding activates the protein (ON); intrinsic GTPase (GTP水解酶) activity hydrolyzes GTP to GDP + Pᵢ, returning to OFF. Ras is the canonical example.

Molecular relay and amplification (放大):

  • Cell-surface receptor activation triggers a cascade: receptor → scaffold proteins (支架蛋白) → multiple intracellular signaling molecules → small intracellular messenger molecules (小型細胞內訊息分子, second messengers) → effector proteins.
  • Cascade functions: primary transduction (初級轉導) at receptor; relay (接力); transduce and amplify; integrate; distribute to multiple effectors; feedback regulation (回饋調節).
  • Each amplification step can activate many downstream molecules — one receptor activation can trigger thousands of intracellular events (domino effect 骨牌效應).
  • Effector proteins: metabolic enzyme (代謝酶) → altered metabolism; cytoskeletal protein (細胞骨架蛋白) → altered cell shape/movement; transcription regulator → altered gene expression.

Pathway functions in detail:

  • Relay — passes signal from one protein to the next in sequence.
  • Amplify (放大) — each activated molecule activates multiple next-step molecules; signal strength grows exponentially.
  • Integrate (整合) — convergent inputs from multiple pathways merge at a single protein; the cell senses combinations of signals, not just individual ones.
  • Distribute (分配) — a single receptor can branch to activate multiple downstream pathways simultaneously.
  • Feedback control (回饋控制): product Y of pathway inhibits upstream component X (negative feedback 負回饋); stabilizes output magnitude; prevents runaway signaling.

Exam trap: Protein kinase adds phosphate (ON); protein phosphatase removes it (OFF). Students sometimes reverse which enzyme does what. Also: GTP-binding proteins turn ON with GTP binding, not with phosphorylation — do not confuse the two switch types.
Exam trap: Feedback regulation is part of transduction, not a separate phase. It occurs within the signaling cascade, not after the response is complete.

6. The HIPPO Pathway: A Case Study in Enzyme-Coupled Signaling

  • The HIPPO pathway (希波訊號路徑) is a conserved signaling cascade that controls organ size by regulating cell proliferation (增殖) and apoptosis.
  • Key transcriptional co-activators: YAP (Yes-associated protein) and TAZ — when active in the nucleus, they drive expression of pro-growth transcription factors (促生長轉錄因子), promoting cell growth and proliferation.
  • Low cell density (低細胞密度): membrane-bound receptors DCH S1/2 and FAT4 are not fully engaged; YAP/TAZ remain unphosphorylated and enter the nucleus → transcription of pro-growth genes → cell proliferation.
  • High cell density (高細胞密度): cell-cell contacts activate membrane-bound regulators (DCH S1/2, FAT4) → downstream kinase cascade phosphorylates (磷酸化) YAP/TAZ → phosphorylated YAP/TAZ are retained in the cytoplasm by 14-3-3 proteins → cannot enter the nucleus → pro-growth gene expression blocked → proliferation halted.
  • This mechanism implements contact inhibition (接觸抑制): when a tissue reaches sufficient density, cells stop dividing.

In vivo evidence:

  • Fruit fly (果蠅) wing imaginal discs: HIPPO pathway mutation causing high pathway activation leads to massive tissue overgrowth — wing disc grows many times larger than wild-type (Huang et al., 2005, Cell).
  • Mouse liver experiment (Jixin Dong et al., Cell, 2007): Inducible expression of an activated YAP transgene (using doxycycline 強力黴素) in mouse liver increased liver/body weight ratio from ~5% to ~17% within 2 weeks. Growth was reversible upon turning off the transgene. At 8 weeks, tumors (腫瘤) developed — linking HIPPO pathway dysregulation to cancer.
  • Reversibility of organ size demonstrates that proliferation vs. arrest is a dynamic, signal-dependent decision, not a permanent state.

Exam trap: In HIPPO signaling, HIGH cell density ACTIVATES the kinase cascade that INACTIVATES YAP/TAZ. Students often reverse this: they say "high density activates YAP" — the opposite is true. High density = HIPPO on = YAP/TAZ off = no proliferation.
Exam trap: The mouse liver data shows the effect is reversible — removing the YAP signal stops liver overgrowth. This is used to argue that HIPPO signaling is a genuine growth-control switch, not irreversible transformation. Do not say the enlarged liver is permanent unless the 8-week tumor stage is discussed.
Exam Traps Summary
  • Paracrine signals stay local and never enter the bloodstream — endocrine hormones do. Do not use them interchangeably.
  • Delta (in Delta-Notch) is membrane-bound; it is not secreted. Contact-dependent signaling requires physical cell-cell contact.
  • "Transduction" means converting the signal — not merely passing it on. Passing it on is relay, which is one function within transduction.
  • Fast responses (< sec to mins) alter existing protein function; slow responses (mins to hrs) require altered gene expression and new protein synthesis.
  • Intracellular receptors are inside the cell; the hydrophobic signal molecule itself crosses the membrane. Do not call the receptor extracellular.
  • Cortisol's nuclear receptor starts in the cytosol, not the nucleus — it only moves to the nucleus after binding cortisol.
  • GPCRs do NOT have kinase activity. Enzyme-coupled receptors have kinase (or associated enzyme) activity. These are two distinct classes.
  • Protein kinase = ON (adds phosphate); protein phosphatase = OFF (removes phosphate). GTP-binding proteins = ON with GTP, OFF after hydrolysis.
  • Feedback regulation is part of transduction — not a fourth phase of signal transduction.
  • High cell density ACTIVATES the HIPPO kinase cascade → INACTIVATES YAP/TAZ → NO proliferation. High density does NOT activate YAP.
  • Mouse liver HIPPO data shows the size change is reversible — enlarged liver is not permanent without the continuing YAP signal.
Top things to memorize
  1. Four signaling modes: contact-dependent (Delta-Notch), paracrine (local mediators), endocrine (hormones via blood), synaptic (neurotransmitters via axon).
  2. Three phases: Reception → Transduction (relay, amplify, integrate, distribute, feedback) → Response.
  3. Two receptor locations: cell-surface (hydrophilic signals) vs. intracellular (hydrophobic signals, e.g., steroids).
  4. Three cell-surface receptor classes: ion-channel-coupled, G-protein-coupled (GPCR), enzyme-coupled.
  5. GPCR mechanism: GDP (OFF) → GTP binding (ON) → GTP hydrolysis → GDP (OFF).
  6. Two molecular switch types: phosphorylation (kinase ON / phosphatase OFF) and GTP-binding (GTP ON / GDP OFF).
  7. Cortisol: hydrophobic → crosses membrane → binds cytosol nuclear receptor → receptor-cortisol complex enters nucleus → activates transcription.
  8. Acetylcholine example: same signal, different responses in pacemaker (decreased firing), salivary gland (secretion), skeletal muscle (contraction) — determined by receptor/pathway identity in each cell.
  9. HIPPO pathway logic: low density → YAP/TAZ nuclear → proliferation; high density → YAP/TAZ phosphorylated/cytoplasmic → arrest.
  10. YAP/TAZ are transcriptional co-activators; 14-3-3 proteins sequester phosphorylated YAP/TAZ in the cytoplasm.
  11. Enzyme-coupled receptors often require signal dimerization to cross-link two receptor monomers and activate the catalytic domain.
  12. Mouse liver experiment: doxycycline-induced YAP → liver/body ratio rises from ~5% to ~17% in 2 weeks; reversible; tumors appear at 8 weeks if unchecked.

Lecture 18: Mechanisms of Cellular Communication-II

This lecture continues the cell signaling framework by dissecting two major classes of cell-surface receptors: G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs) and enzyme-coupled receptors, with a focus on receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs). Each receptor class activates a distinct set of intracellular cascades — GPCRs work through heterotrimeric G proteins to raise second messengers such as cAMP and Ca²⁺, while RTKs dimerize upon ligand binding and recruit signaling complexes via phosphotyrosine docking sites. Both pathways converge on profound changes in cell behavior, and their dysregulation underpins major diseases including cancer, making them prime therapeutic targets.

1. G-Protein-Coupled Receptors (GPCRs): Structure and Activation

  • GPCR (G蛋白偶聯受體) structure — seven-pass transmembrane protein (七次跨膜蛋白); the seven helices (螺旋) span the lipid bilayer, with an extracellular ligand-binding face and a cytosolic (細胞質) G-protein-coupling face.
  • Family size — >700 members in humans; ~400 functional olfactory (嗅覺) receptor genes; includes rhodopsin (視紫質, photoreceptor), adrenaline receptors (腎上腺素受體) that regulate heart rate and glycogen (糖原) breakdown in muscle.
  • Clinical relevance — ~30% of all drugs in use today act through GPCRs; Nobel Prize 2012 awarded to Prof. Robert J. Lefkowitz for GPCR work.
  • Signal flow analogy — described as a "domino effect" (骨牌效應): one ligand binding event triggers a molecular relay (A → B → C → D) culminating in cell response.

  • Heterotrimeric G protein (異三聚體G蛋白) — three subunits: α, β, γ; β and γ are tethered to the plasma membrane by short lipid tails (脂質尾).
  • Activation switch — α subunit carries GDP (inactive, 非活化) or GTP (active, 活化); in the resting state the trimer is GDP-bound and associated with the receptor's cytosolic face.
  • Activation sequence: ligand binds GPCR → receptor changes conformation (構型改變) → GDP dissociates (解離) from Gα → GTP binds Gα → Gα-GTP dissociates from Gβγ → both Gα-GTP and free Gβγ become active effector-activating units.
  • Diversity — >20 different types of G proteins in mammals; each couples to distinct effectors (效應蛋白).
  • Signal duration — determined by the intrinsic GTPase (鳥苷三磷酸酶) activity of Gα, which hydrolyzes GTP → GDP, returning Gα to the inactive state and allowing reassociation with Gβγ. (Exam-relevant: answer to practice MCQ = option d.)

Exam trap: GPCRs are seven-pass transmembrane proteins, not single-pass. Confusing them with RTKs (single-pass) is a common error. Also: both Gα-GTP AND Gβγ are active after dissociation — students often forget Gβγ activates effectors too.
Exam trap: Signal duration is set by Gα's own GTPase (鳥苷三磷酸酶) activity, NOT by phosphatases or protein degradation. Cholera toxin locks Gα in GTP-bound active state by blocking this GTPase activity.

2. GPCR Downstream Effector Pathways

Three major effectors activated by G proteins:

  • Ion channels (離子通道) — Gβγ opens K⁺ channels directly; example: acetylcholine (乙醯膽鹼) binds muscarinic GPCR in heart pacemaker cells (心臟節律細胞) → activated Gβγ complex opens K⁺ channel → K⁺ efflux → hyperpolarization (超極化) → slows heart rate.
  • Adenylyl cyclase (腺苷酸環化酶) — activated by Gα-GTP of stimulatory G protein (Gs); converts ATP → cyclic AMP (環腺苷酸, cAMP); cAMP is the second messenger (第二信使).
  • Phospholipase C (磷脂酶C, PLC) — cleaves the membrane phospholipid PIP₂ into two second messengers: IP₃ (肌醇三磷酸, inositol trisphosphate) and diacylglycerol (二酰甘油, DAG).

cAMP/PKA pathway detail:

  • Adrenaline (腎上腺素) → adrenergic receptor (GPCR) → Gs protein → adenylyl cyclase activated → ATP → cAMP.
  • cAMP binds and activates Protein Kinase A (蛋白激酶A, PKA); inactive PKA is a tetramer of 2 regulatory + 2 catalytic subunits; cAMP binding to regulatory subunits releases active catalytic subunits.
  • PKA effects (effector activation 效應物活化): increase heart rate (心率); glycogen (糖原) breakdown in muscle cells; transcription (轉錄) of target genes in neuronal cells to regulate long-term memory (長期記憶).

PLC/Ca²⁺/PKC pathway detail:

  • Signal → GPCR → G protein → PLC activated → PIP₂ cleaved into IP₃ + DAG.
  • IP₃ (水溶性, water-soluble) diffuses to ER membrane → opens Ca²⁺ channels → Ca²⁺ release from ER into cytosol; Ca²⁺ concentration gradient (濃度梯度): ER ~1 mM, cytosol ~10⁻⁴ mM at steady state (靜止狀態).
  • DAG (脂溶性, lipid-soluble) stays in membrane → activates Protein Kinase C (蛋白激酶C, PKC) together with elevated Ca²⁺.
  • PKC phosphorylates (磷酸化) various target proteins → effector activation → cell response.
  • Biological example: fertilization (受精) of an egg by sperm triggers a Ca²⁺ wave across the egg, visualized by fluorescent Ca²⁺ indicators.

Exam trap: PLC produces TWO second messengers — IP₃ (triggers Ca²⁺ release) AND DAG (activates PKC). Students often name only one. Also: Ca²⁺ comes from the ER, not from outside the cell, as the primary initial source.
Exam trap: cAMP activates PKA, not PKC. PKC is activated by DAG + Ca²⁺ from the PLC pathway, not the adenylyl cyclase pathway.

3. Enzyme-Coupled Receptors: Structure and Types

  • Enzyme-coupled receptors (酶聯受體) — single-pass transmembrane proteins (單次跨膜蛋白); extracellular ligand-binding domain + intracellular enzyme/enzyme-associated domain.
  • Activation mechanism — ligand (usually a dimer, 二聚體) causes two receptor monomers (單體) to dimerize (二聚化); dimerization activates the intracellular catalytic domain (催化域).
  • Alternatively: receptor recruits and activates a separate associated enzyme upon ligand binding.
  • Ligands — primarily growth factors (生長因子) that regulate growth (生長), proliferation (增殖), differentiation (分化), and survival (存活) of animal cells.
  • Response — typically leads to changes in gene expression (基因表達).
  • Disease link — major role in development of cancer (癌症).

Three main subtypes:

  • Tyrosine kinase receptors (酪氨酸激酶受體) — the largest and best-studied class; includes receptor tyrosine kinases (RTKs); relevant to oncology and metabolic disorders.
  • Serine/threonine kinase receptors (絲氨酸/蘇氨酸激酶受體) — phosphorylate (磷酸化) Ser/Thr residues; examples include TGF-β receptors.
  • Guanylyl cyclase-linked receptors (鳥苷酸環化酶聯受體) — produce cGMP (環鳥苷酸) as second messenger.

Exam trap: Enzyme-coupled receptors are single-pass (單次跨膜), not seven-pass like GPCRs. Mixing up transmembrane topology between GPCR and RTK is a very common error.

4. Receptor Tyrosine Kinases (RTKs): Activation and Signaling Complexes

Activation mechanism:

  • Inactive state — RTK monomers (單體) sit in membrane with inactive intracellular kinase domain (激酶域); tyrosine (酪氨酸) residues on the cytoplasmic tail (細胞質尾部) are unphosphorylated.
  • Ligand binding (配體結合) — growth factor (typically a dimer) bridges two RTK monomers, causing receptor dimerization (受體二聚化).
  • Transautophosphorylation (交叉自磷酸化) — dimerization stimulates kinase activity; each kinase domain phosphorylates multiple tyrosine residues on the adjacent (相鄰) receptor tail; multiple phosphotyrosines (磷酸酪氨酸) are generated.
  • Docking sites — each phosphotyrosine serves as a specific docking site (停靠位點) for a different intracellular signaling protein; different proteins recognize different phosphotyrosine sequences, allowing one RTK to activate multiple parallel pathways simultaneously.
  • Result — assembly of an intracellular signaling complex (訊號複合體) → activation of downstream pathways → altered cell behavior.

Experimental approach — mutagenesis (突變分析) to map docking sites:

  • Individual tyrosine (酪氨酸) residues (Y1, Y2, Y3) mutated one at a time to alanine (丙氨酸, which cannot be phosphorylated).
  • If mutating Y2 to alanine causes loss of binding of a specific signaling protein, then phospho-Y2 is the docking site for that protein.
  • This mutational analysis (突變分析) establishes which phosphotyrosine recruits which downstream effector.
  • Overactive (constitutively active, 持續活化) form of Ras is used to determine the order of proteins in a pathway: cells lacking protein X but expressing constitutively active Ras help deduce whether X acts upstream or downstream of Ras.

Exam trap: RTK dimerization is induced by ligand binding — the receptor does NOT dimerize first and then bind ligand. Also: each phosphotyrosine is a specific docking site for a DIFFERENT protein, enabling pathway multiplexing from one receptor.

5. RTK Downstream Pathways: Ras and PI3-Kinase

Ras (Ras蛋白) pathway:

  • Ras — a monomeric GTPase (單體GTP酶) anchored to the inner plasma membrane leaflet; inactive when GDP-bound, active when GTP-bound; analogous in principle to Gα.
  • Activation cascade: activated RTK (phosphotyrosines) → adaptor protein (接頭蛋白, e.g. Grb2) docks on a phosphotyrosine → adaptor recruits Ras-GEF (Ras鳥苷酸交換因子, guanine nucleotide exchange factor, e.g. SOS) → Ras-GEF promotes GDP dissociation from Ras and GTP loading → Ras-GTP = active Ras.
  • Active Ras transmits signal onward through the MAP kinase cascade (絲裂原活化蛋白激酶級聯): Ras → Raf → MEK → MAPK → nucleus → gene expression changes → altered cell behavior (proliferation, differentiation, survival).
  • Cancer link — 30% of human cancers contain activating mutations in Ras (Ras活化突變) that lock it in GTP-bound constitutively active (持續活化) form, bypassing need for upstream signal.
  • Experimental logic — overactive constitutively active form of Ras is used to determine the order of proteins in a pathway; if constitutively active Ras rescues signaling in a cell lacking protein X, then X acts upstream of Ras.

PI3-kinase (磷脂醯肌醇3-激酶) / Akt pathway:

  • Activated RTK → PI3-kinase recruited and activated → phosphorylates inositol phospholipid (肌醇磷脂) in the inner membrane leaflet → generates phosphorylated inositol phospholipid (PIP₃).
  • PIP₃ acts as a docking site (停靠位點) at the membrane → recruits Protein Kinase 1 (PDK1) and Akt (蛋白激酶B) to the membrane.
  • PDK1 phosphorylates and activates Akt → activated Akt phosphorylates multiple downstream targets → promotes cell growth (生長) and survival (存活) of many cell types.
  • Signal flow: Signal molecule → RTK → PI3-kinase → phosphorylated inositol phospholipid → protein kinase 1 (PDK1) → Akt kinase → cell response.
  • PTEN (磷酸酶) opposes PI3-kinase by dephosphorylating PIP₃ back to PIP₂; PTEN is a major tumor suppressor (腫瘤抑制基因).

Exam trap: Ras-GEF promotes GTP LOADING onto Ras (it is not a kinase). Ras itself has GTPase activity that shuts off the signal — exactly parallel to Gα. Confusing GEF function with a kinase is common.
Exam trap: PI3-kinase phosphorylates a membrane LIPID (inositol phospholipid), not a protein. The product PIP₃ is a lipid-based second messenger that recruits Akt to the membrane.

6. EGFR as a Cancer Target and Therapeutic Strategies

EGFR (表皮生長因子受體, Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor) pathway overview:

  • EGFR is an RTK; upon EGF (ligand) binding → dimerization → autophosphorylation → activates both Ras/MAPK branch (via Grb2-SOS/Ras-GEF) and PI3K/AKT branch simultaneously.
  • Downstream outcomes (下游效應): cell survival (存活), proliferation (增殖), invasion (侵潤), metastasis (轉移), angiogenesis (血管生成).
  • PTEN opposes the PI3K arm; loss of PTEN is common in cancer.
  • SOS — the Ras-GEF recruited by the Grb2 adaptor protein in the EGFR-Ras pathway.

EGFR overexpression and cancer:

  • EGFR is overexpressed (過度表達) in cancers of the head and neck, ovary, cervix, and other epithelial tissues.
  • Many epithelial cancers carry mutations that constitutively activate (持續活化) EGFR without ligand.
  • EGFRvIII mutant — a deletion mutant lacking the extracellular domain; constitutively active; common in glioblastoma (神經膠質母細胞瘤).

Inhibition strategies (抑制策略):

  • EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKI, 酪氨酸激酶抑制劑) — small molecules that competitively block the ATP-binding site of the intracellular kinase domain, preventing autophosphorylation.
  • Anti-EGFR monoclonal antibody (單株抗體, mAb) inhibitors — bind the extracellular domain of EGFR, blocking ligand binding and/or receptor dimerization.

Generations of EGFR TKI therapy and acquired resistance (獲得性耐藥):

  • 1st/2nd generation TKIs (e.g. Gefitinib, Erlotinib, Afatinib) — target EGFR activating mutations; tumors acquire resistance via T790M mutation (position 790 substitution).
  • 3rd generation TKIs (e.g. AZD9291/Osimertinib, CO-1686, HM61713) — designed to overcome T790M resistance; tumors then acquire C797S mutation.
  • 4th generation TKI (EAI045) — designed to overcome C797S mutation; field is still evolving.
  • Clinical data — T790M-positive patients have significantly shorter progression-free survival (無進展生存期) vs. T790M-negative patients (Kaplan-Meier curves, p<0.001).
  • Progression-free survival (無進展生存期) — length of time during and after treatment that a patient lives with disease but it does not worsen.

Exam trap: There are TWO distinct EGFR inhibition strategies — TKIs act intracellularly on the kinase domain; mAbs act extracellularly on the ligand-binding domain. They work at different sites on the same receptor.
Exam trap: Acquired resistance mutations (T790M, C797S) arise AFTER treatment — they are not present at baseline in most patients. The arms race of drug generations reflects sequential resistance acquisition.
Exam Traps Summary
  • GPCRs are seven-pass transmembrane proteins; RTKs are single-pass — never mix these up.
  • Both Gα-GTP AND free Gβγ are active effector-activating units after G protein dissociation.
  • Signal duration for GPCR signaling is set by the intrinsic GTPase activity of Gα (answer d in practice MCQ), not phosphatases or degradation.
  • PLC produces TWO second messengers: IP₃ (releases ER Ca²⁺) and DAG (activates PKC); naming only one is a common error.
  • cAMP activates PKA; DAG + Ca²⁺ activate PKC — these are separate pathways with separate kinases.
  • RTK dimerization is ligand-induced; each phosphotyrosine is a specific docking site for a different downstream protein.
  • Ras-GEF promotes GTP loading on Ras — it is not a kinase; Ras shuts off via its own GTPase activity.
  • PI3-kinase phosphorylates a membrane lipid (inositol phospholipid), not a protein; PIP₃ recruits Akt to the membrane.
  • 30% of human cancers have constitutively activating Ras mutations.
  • EGFR inhibitors: TKIs block the intracellular kinase domain; anti-EGFR mAbs block the extracellular domain.
  • T790M mutation confers resistance to 1st/2nd generation EGFR TKIs; C797S confers resistance to 3rd generation.
Top things to memorize
  1. GPCR = 7-pass transmembrane; heterotrimeric G protein (α, β, γ); Gα-GDP inactive → Gα-GTP active; signal off by Gα GTPase activity.
  2. Three GPCR effectors: ion channels (Gβγ opens K⁺), adenylyl cyclase (Gα → cAMP → PKA), phospholipase C (Gα → IP₃ + DAG → Ca²⁺ + PKC).
  3. Ca²⁺ steady-state: ER ~1 mM; cytosol ~10⁻⁴ mM — large gradient drives Ca²⁺ release when IP₃ opens ER channels.
  4. RTK = single-pass; ligand (dimer) → receptor dimerization → transautophosphorylation of tyrosines → phosphotyrosines as specific docking sites.
  5. RTK → Ras pathway: adaptor protein (Grb2) + Ras-GEF (SOS) → Ras-GTP → Raf → MEK → MAPK → gene expression.
  6. RTK → PI3K pathway: PI3K → PIP₃ → PDK1 + Akt recruited → Akt promotes survival; PTEN opposes PI3K.
  7. 30% of human cancers: activating Ras mutations (constitutively active, locked GTP-bound).
  8. EGFR activates both Ras/MAPK and PI3K/Akt simultaneously via different phosphotyrosine docking sites.
  9. EGFR TKI generations and resistance: 1st/2nd gen → T790M resistance → 3rd gen (AZD9291) → C797S resistance → 4th gen (EAI045).
  10. ~30% of all drugs act through GPCRs; >700 GPCR members in humans; ~400 olfactory receptor genes.
  11. Enzyme-coupled receptor subtypes: tyrosine kinase receptors (RTKs), serine/threonine kinase receptors, guanylyl cyclase-linked receptors.
  12. Mutagenesis approach: mutate individual tyrosine to alanine → loss of specific signaling protein binding → identifies which phosphotyrosine is the docking site for that protein.
